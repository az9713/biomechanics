0. Motivation — the force that has to be built

You reach down, grip a grocery bag, and lift it with your forearm horizontal. In Module 1 we solved the statics of exactly this pose: the elbow must supply a torque $\tau=F_m\,d_m$, and because the biceps inserts only about $d_m\approx3\ \mathrm{cm}$ from the joint while the bag hangs about $r_L\approx35\ \mathrm{cm}$ out along the forearm, torque balance forces the muscle to pull with roughly $F_m\approx(r_L/d_m)\,W\approx12\times$ the bag's weight. The human arm is a third-class lever: it trades force for reach and speed, and the muscle pays the tax.

But Module 1 took that muscle force as given — a number that simply appears on the free-body diagram. Module 3 was even more explicit: when it set up the joint as a constrained mechanical interface, it left the applied muscle force $Q$ as a literal blank, a placeholder to be filled by "a Hill-type muscle with force–length–velocity behaviour, activation dynamics, and pennation." This module fills that blank. Where does the pulling force come from, what sets its size, and why can the nervous system dial it up and down at will?

The moment you ask, the cable picture falls apart — a muscle is nothing like a rope you can pull tight on demand:

  • It has a rise time. Decide to lift and the force does not appear instantly; it ramps up over roughly a tenth of a second, and it lingers briefly after you "let go." Force lags the command.
  • It depends on length. The same muscle that easily holds $5\ \mathrm{kg}$ with the elbow near a right angle can barely produce force when fully stretched or fully bunched up.
  • It depends on speed. Try to shorten quickly and the muscle weakens; resist while being forcibly lengthened (lowering the bag) and it is stronger than when still.
  • It fatigues. Hold the bag long enough and the very same neural command yields less and less force.

A cable does none of these things. Any honest account of "muscle force" has to reproduce all four — a rise time, a length dependence, a velocity dependence, and fatigue. Each is a clue about the machinery underneath, and each becomes a term in the model we build.

The resolution is the idea in the module's title: a muscle is a chemo-electro-mechanical actuator. A command that begins as a nerve signal is transduced — electrically, then chemically, then mechanically — into force, through a short chain of steps, each of which we can write down and quantify. It is cleanest to picture the whole module as a single pipeline:

To make each arrow quantitative we need three layers of physics, each built on the one before:

  • The generator itself — how muscle is built (fascicles, pennation, and the maximal force set by cross-sectional area), and the sarcomere, whose overlapping filaments give the force its dependence on length (§1–§2).
  • Turning a command into activation — the action potential at the neuromuscular junction, the calcium release that switches the machinery on, and how the nervous system grades whole-muscle drive by recruiting motor units and firing them faster — all collapsed into a first-order activation equation (§3–§5).
  • The force laws and the whole model — the dependence of force on shortening velocity (Hill's hyperbola), assembled with length and activation into the Hill-type muscle model, then converted to joint torque through a moment arm that varies with angle, with agonist and antagonist acting across the joint (§6–§8).

Two computational labs then put the model to work — predicting joint torque from a neural command through a movement, and showing how that torque fades under sustained effort (§9) — before we take honest stock of what the model captures and misses and close with problems (§10). By the end you will be able to hand the model a neural command and get back the torque it produces at the joint, moment by moment, and understand why the same command produces less when the muscle is tired. We begin with the hardware: how a muscle is built to make force.

1. Muscle architecture: the force-producing hierarchy

Before we can ask how a nerve signal controls force (§3–§5), we need to know what sets the force a muscle can produce at all. That ceiling is fixed by architecture — how the tissue is built and arranged. So begin by zooming in, from the whole muscle you can see under the skin down to the molecular unit that actually generates tension.

A skeletal muscle is a nested bundle. Define each level of the hierarchy the first time it appears:

  • A whole muscle (say the biceps) is wrapped in connective tissue and tapers at each end into a tendon — the tough cord that transmits its force to bone.
  • Inside, the muscle is divided into fascicles: visible bundles of fibres, each sheathed in connective tissue (the perimysium). The arrangement of these fascicles relative to the tendon is what we will call pennation, below.
  • Each fascicle is a bundle of muscle fibres. A muscle fibre is a single cell — unusually, a long, cylindrical, multinucleated one (10–100 μm across, up to many centimetres long), striped (striated) with a regular cross-banding.
  • Each fibre is packed with hundreds of myofibrils: thread-like strands running its full length.
  • Each myofibril is a chain of sarcomeres laid end to end. The sarcomere is the contractile unit — the smallest piece that shortens and pulls. Its banding is the source of the fibre's stripes, and its internal machinery (thick and thin filaments) is where force is generated, developed in §2.

Two architectural numbers set the maximal force. First, how much contractile material the muscle carries across its section. Second, at what angle the fibres pull on the tendon. Take them in turn.

Specific tension and physiological cross-section

Every fibre is built from the same molecular machinery (the crossbridges of §2), packed at nearly the same density. So the force a fibre can exert is set simply by how many filaments lie side by side across its cross-section — i.e. by its cross-sectional area. The proportionality constant is a material property of muscle:

Definition 1.1 (specific tension). The specific tension $\sigma$ is the maximal isometric ("iso" = same, "metric" = length: contracting at fixed length) force a muscle produces per unit cross-sectional area of fibre. It is a stress (force / area), and across mammalian skeletal muscle it is remarkably constant, $$\sigma \approx 0.3\ \mathrm{MPa} = 30\ \mathrm{N\,cm^{-2}},\tag{1.1}$$ with a measured range of about $0.2$–$0.35\ \mathrm{MPa}$. Its near-universality is the first hint that all muscle runs on one shared mechanism.

But a fibre in a pennate muscle does not run straight down the muscle's long axis, so the muscle's anatomical cross-section (a cut perpendicular to the whole muscle) slices fibres obliquely and undercounts them. The area that matters is the one taken perpendicular to the fibres themselves, summed over every fibre:

Definition 1.2 (physiological cross-sectional area, PCSA). The physiological cross-sectional area is the total cross-sectional area of all the muscle's fibres, measured perpendicular to their length. Since a bundle of fibres of length $L_f$ and total volume $V$ satisfies $V = \mathrm{PCSA}\times L_f$, $$\mathrm{PCSA} = \frac{V}{L_f} = \frac{m}{\rho\,L_f},\tag{1.2}$$ where $m$ is muscle mass and $\rho\approx1.06\ \mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}}$ is muscle density. PCSA counts contractile material, not girth: a fat muscle of long fibres can have a smaller PCSA than a slim muscle of short, densely packed ones.

The force a muscle's fibres generate along their own direction is then just specific tension times the physiological cross-section, $F_{\text{fibre}} = \sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}$. What reaches the tendon depends on the pennation angle, which we quantify next.

Pennation: angling the fibres

Definition 1.3 (pennation angle). The pennation angle $\theta_p$ is the angle between a muscle's fibres and its tendon (its line of action). A muscle whose fibres run parallel to the tendon ($\theta_p\approx0$) is parallel (or fusiform); one whose fibres attach obliquely, like the barbs of a feather (Latin penna), is pennate.

Angling the fibres has an immediate mechanical cost: only the component of a fibre's force along the tendon actually pulls on the bone. Resolve the fibre force $F_{\text{fibre}}$ into a component along the tendon and one perpendicular to it:

So each fibre delivers $F_t=F_f\cos\theta_p$ along the tendon (eq. below). This looks like a pure loss — yet the most powerful muscles in the body (the quadriceps, the calf) are strongly pennate. The resolution is a trade-off: angling the fibres frees up room, so a pennate muscle can pack many more (necessarily shorter) fibres into the same volume. By (1.2), halving the fibre length doubles the PCSA. That gain in PCSA scales faster than the modest $\cos\theta_p$ loss (at $\theta_p=25^\circ$, $\cos\theta_p=0.91$ — only a 9% cut), so pennate muscles are high-force designs. The price they pay is elsewhere: short fibres shorten by only a short distance, so pennate muscles have small range and high force, while parallel muscles have large range and lower force. Architecture is a choice between force and excursion.

Collecting the two effects gives the maximal isometric force a muscle delivers to its tendon:

Proposition 1.1 (maximal isometric force from architecture). A muscle of specific tension $\sigma$, physiological cross-sectional area $\mathrm{PCSA}$, and pennation angle $\theta_p$ delivers a maximal isometric tendon force $$\boxed{\;F_{\max} \;=\; \sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}\,\cos\theta_p\;}\tag{1.3}$$ This is the ceiling the neural control of §3–§7 can approach but never exceed; every later force term is a fraction of it.

Put in numbers. With $\sigma=30\ \mathrm{N\,cm^{-2}}$, a muscle's maximal force in newtons is just $30\times\mathrm{PCSA}\,[\mathrm{cm^2}]\times\cos\theta_p$. The table spans the range from a small parallel flexor to the big pennate extensors:

Validation against §0. In §0 the elbow flexors had to pull with roughly $600\ \mathrm N$ to hold a $5\ \mathrm{kg}$ bag at arm's length. The biceps alone reaches only $\sim135\ \mathrm N$ — but it is one of several flexors (brachialis, brachioradialis add more), and together they produce several hundred newtons: the right order of magnitude. The mismatch is itself the lesson — holding $5\ \mathrm{kg}$ on a fully outstretched forearm really is near the limit of the flexor group, which is why it is so quickly tiring. The calf and thigh numbers ($\sim10^3\ \mathrm N$) likewise match the joint reactions of Module 3, where standing loaded the hip to $\sim1700\ \mathrm N$.

Equation (1.3) fixes the size of the force. But a muscle almost never works at $F_{\max}$: the force it actually makes depends on how stretched it is, how fast it is shortening, and how strongly it is switched on. The first of these — the dependence on length — is written into the very geometry of the sarcomere we just drew, and it is where we turn next (§2).

2. The sarcomere: crossbridges and the length–tension law

Section 1 fixed the ceiling on force, $F_{\max}=\sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}\cos\theta_p$. But a muscle almost never delivers $F_{\max}$. The force it actually produces at any instant is that ceiling scaled by three dimensionless factors, each between 0 and 1:

$F_{\text{active}} \;=\; \underbrace{a(t)}_{\text{how switched on}}\;\cdot\; \underbrace{f_L(\ell)}_{\text{how stretched}}\;\cdot\; \underbrace{f_V(v)}_{\text{how fast}}\;\cdot\; F_{\max}$

Activation $a$ is the story of §3–§5; the velocity factor $f_V$ is §6. This section derives the middle one — the length factor $f_L(\ell)$ — and the remarkable thing is that it falls straight out of the geometry of the sarcomere we already drew in §1 (Fig. 3). Force is made by molecular crossbridges; a crossbridge can only pull where a thin filament lies beside it; how much thin filament lies beside the bridges is set by how long the sarcomere is. So force must depend on length — and we can compute exactly how.

The sliding-filament model

Recall the sarcomere from §1: central thick (myosin) filaments interdigitating with thin (actin) filaments anchored to the Z-discs at each end. Define the one length this section turns on:

Definition 2.1 (sarcomere length). The sarcomere length $\ell$ is the distance between the two Z-discs that bound one sarcomere — the length of the repeating contractile unit. At rest in human muscle $\ell\approx2.6$–$2.8\ \mu\mathrm m$.

How does a muscle shorten? Not by the filaments themselves getting shorter. In the sliding-filament model (A. F. Huxley and Hanson, 1954) the thick and thin filaments keep their lengths and instead slide past one another: the thin filaments are drawn deeper in between the thick ones, hauling the Z-discs together and shrinking $\ell$. The tell-tale evidence is that the thick-filament band stays a fixed width under the microscope while the sarcomere shortens — only the overlap changes. Everything below follows from that one fact. (An animation of this sliding is the capstone figure of the section, once we know what it does to force.)

The crossbridge cycle — where force and ATP meet

Projecting from each thick filament are hundreds of myosin heads, or crossbridges: tiny motor domains that reach out, grab the neighbouring thin filament, and pull. Each head runs a four-state cycle, burning exactly one molecule of ATP (adenosine triphosphate, the cell's energy currency) per turn (Fig. 5):

Two consequences matter for the rest of the module. First, one ATP is hydrolysed per crossbridge per cycle — the chemical price of the mechanical work, the "chemo-" in this module's title. Second, and more immediately, the heads act as a parallel array of tiny, independent force generators: at any instant the sarcomere's active force is the sum of the pulls of all the heads that happen to be attached, so

active force $\;\propto\;$ number of crossbridges attached at that instant.

Force follows overlap

A head can only attach where there is a thin filament beside it to grab. The heads are spread (at nearly uniform density) along the thick filament everywhere except a central bare zone that carries no heads. So the number of heads that can attach is set by how much thin filament lies alongside the head-bearing part of the thick filament — the overlap. To turn "∝ attached heads" into "∝ overlap length" we make one modelling assumption explicit, because it is exactly what makes the derivation work:

Modelling assumption 2.2 (independent, uniform crossbridges). The myosin heads are (i) distributed at uniform density along the head-bearing zones of the thick filament, and (ii) independent and identical force generators. Then the number of attachable heads — and hence the active force — is proportional to the length of overlap between the thin filaments and the head-bearing zone: $$F_{\text{active}} \;\propto\; (\text{overlap length}).\tag{2.1}$$ Uniform density is what makes this proportionality linear; it is the hinge of the whole length–tension law.

Sarcomere geometry sets the curve

Now it is pure bookkeeping of lengths. Take representative human filament dimensions (each varies a little between muscles and people — the spread is given):

Watch the overlap change as we lengthen the sarcomere from fully shortened to fully stretched. Four snapshots (Fig. 6) tell the whole story (the force under each is computed from (2.1)):

Each snapshot is a breakpoint of the curve, and each is a clean statement about lengths (all measured Z-to-Z). Reading from long to short:

  • Descending-limb zero — the thin filaments have slid completely clear of the thick filament, so overlap and force reach zero. This happens when the sarcomere length equals the thick length plus both thin filaments: $\ell = A + 2\,a_{\text{thin}} = 1.60 + 2(1.30) = 4.20\ \mu\mathrm m.$
  • Plateau (optimal length $\ell_0$) — as the sarcomere shortens from there, overlap grows until every head is engaged. That is first true when the thin-filament tips just reach the edge of the bare zone: $\ell_0 = 2\,a_{\text{thin}} + B = 2.60 + 0.10 = 2.70\ \mu\mathrm m.$ Shorten a touch more and the thin tips slide across the (head-free) bare zone without gaining or losing any engaged heads — so force stays flat until the tips meet at the centre, at $\ell = 2\,a_{\text{thin}} = 2.60\ \mu\mathrm m$. The plateau width equals the bare-zone width $B$: the head-free centre is exactly what buys a muscle a range of lengths at full force.
  • Ascending limb — below $2.60\ \mu\mathrm m$ the thin filaments overshoot the centre and enter the opposite half, where they lie the wrong way among the far heads and interfere (the orange double overlap); force declines. At $\ell = A = 1.60\ \mu\mathrm m$ the thick filaments run into the Z-discs and begin to crumple, and force drops steeply, extrapolating to zero near $\ell\approx1.27\ \mu\mathrm m$.

That last zero deserves a caution. Unlike the descending limb — whose zero at $4.2\ \mu\mathrm m$ is pure thin/thick geometry — the ascending zero near $1.27\ \mu\mathrm m$ is an empirical extrapolation set by the thick filament buckling against the Z-discs, a property of the (nearly species-invariant) thick filament rather than a length we can derive by overlap alone. So we treat the descending limb and plateau as exact geometry and the ascending limb as a measured, piecewise-linear approximation.

Derivation of the descending limb (linear). On the descending limb every attached head lies in the head-bearing zone, and by (2.1) force is proportional to the overlap between one thin filament and one head-bearing half of the thick filament. Measuring from the sarcomere centre, that half runs out to $A/2$, and the thin filament's inner tip sits at $\ell/2 - a_{\text{thin}}$. The overlap is therefore $$\text{overlap}(\ell) \;=\; \frac{A}{2} - \Big(\frac{\ell}{2} - a_{\text{thin}}\Big),$$ which shrinks linearly as $\ell$ grows. Normalising by its maximum, the head-bearing length $\tfrac{A-B}{2}=0.75\ \mu\mathrm m$, gives the straight descending limb from $f_L=1$ at $\ell_0=2.70$ to $f_L=0$ at $4.20\ \mu\mathrm m$. ∎

Collecting all four regions gives the active length–tension law — the first mechanical law of the module:

Proposition 2.1 (active length–tension relation). With sarcomere length $\ell$ in micrometres, the normalized active force is $$\boxed{\;f_L(\ell)=\begin{cases}0, & \ell\le 1.27 \ \text{ or }\ \ell\ge 4.20,\\[3pt]\text{ascending (double overlap; thick-filament}\\[-1pt]\quad\text{compression) }\nearrow, & 1.27\lt\ell\lt2.60,\\[3pt]1, & 2.60\le\ell\le 2.70\quad(\text{plateau}),\\[3pt]\dfrac{4.20-\ell}{4.20-2.70}, & 2.70\lt\ell\lt4.20.\end{cases}\;}$$ The plateau sits at the optimal length $\ell_0\approx2.7\ \mu\mathrm m$; force falls to zero at $\ell\approx1.27$ and $4.20\ \mu\mathrm m$.

Our three landmarks match the human values quoted at the start — plateau at $\ell_0\approx2.7\ \mu\mathrm m$, zeros near $1.27$ and $4.2\ \mu\mathrm m$ — a geometry-only prediction validated against measurement.

What does it mean for a working muscle? Sarcomeres in the body are held, by their tendons and skeletal geometry, to operate mostly on the plateau and the near flanks around $\ell_0$ — they rarely wander out to the zeros, where they would be useless. But they do move off the plateau: this is why your grip is weakest with the wrist fully flexed or fully extended (finger-flexor sarcomeres pushed onto the ascending or descending limb), and strongest in between.

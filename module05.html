0. Motivation — the force that has to be built

You reach down, grip a grocery bag, and lift it with your forearm horizontal. In Module 1 we solved the statics of exactly this pose: the elbow must supply a torque $\tau=F_m\,d_m$, and because the biceps inserts only about $d_m\approx3\ \mathrm{cm}$ from the joint while the bag hangs about $r_L\approx35\ \mathrm{cm}$ out along the forearm, torque balance forces the muscle to pull with roughly $F_m\approx(r_L/d_m)\,W\approx12\times$ the bag's weight. The human arm is a third-class lever: it trades force for reach and speed, and the muscle pays the tax.

But Module 1 took that muscle force as given — a number that simply appears on the free-body diagram. Module 3 was even more explicit: when it set up the joint as a constrained mechanical interface, it left the applied muscle force $Q$ as a literal blank, a placeholder to be filled by "a Hill-type muscle with force–length–velocity behaviour, activation dynamics, and pennation." This module fills that blank. Where does the pulling force come from, what sets its size, and why can the nervous system dial it up and down at will?

The moment you ask, the cable picture falls apart — a muscle is nothing like a rope you can pull tight on demand:

  • It has a rise time. Decide to lift and the force does not appear instantly; it ramps up over roughly a tenth of a second, and it lingers briefly after you "let go." Force lags the command.
  • It depends on length. The same muscle that easily holds $5\ \mathrm{kg}$ with the elbow near a right angle can barely produce force when fully stretched or fully bunched up.
  • It depends on speed. Try to shorten quickly and the muscle weakens; resist while being forcibly lengthened (lowering the bag) and it is stronger than when still.
  • It fatigues. Hold the bag long enough and the very same neural command yields less and less force.

A cable does none of these things. Any honest account of "muscle force" has to reproduce all four — a rise time, a length dependence, a velocity dependence, and fatigue. Each is a clue about the machinery underneath, and each becomes a term in the model we build.

The resolution is the idea in the module's title: a muscle is a chemo-electro-mechanical actuator. A command that begins as a nerve signal is transduced — electrically, then chemically, then mechanically — into force, through a short chain of steps, each of which we can write down and quantify. To make each arrow quantitative we need three layers of physics, each built on the one before:

  • The generator itself — how muscle is built (fascicles, pennation, and the maximal force set by cross-sectional area), and the sarcomere, whose overlapping filaments give the force its dependence on length (§1–§2).
  • Turning a command into activation — the action potential at the neuromuscular junction, the calcium release that switches the machinery on, and how the nervous system grades whole-muscle drive by recruiting motor units and firing them faster — all collapsed into a first-order activation equation (§3–§5).
  • The force laws and the whole model — the dependence of force on shortening velocity (Hill's hyperbola), assembled with length and activation into the Hill-type muscle model, then converted to joint torque through a moment arm that varies with angle, with agonist and antagonist acting across the joint (§6–§8).

Two computational labs then put the model to work — predicting joint torque from a neural command through a movement, and showing how that torque fades under sustained effort (§9) — before we take honest stock of what the model captures and misses and close with problems (§10). By the end you will be able to hand the model a neural command and get back the torque it produces at the joint, moment by moment, and understand why the same command produces less when the muscle is tired. We begin with the hardware: how a muscle is built to make force.

1. Muscle architecture: the force-producing hierarchy

Before we can ask how a nerve signal controls force (§3–§5), we need to know what sets the force a muscle can produce at all. That ceiling is fixed by architecture — how the tissue is built and arranged. So begin by zooming in, from the whole muscle you can see under the skin down to the molecular unit that actually generates tension.

A skeletal muscle is a nested bundle. Define each level of the hierarchy the first time it appears:

  • A whole muscle (say the biceps) is wrapped in connective tissue and tapers at each end into a tendon — the tough cord that transmits its force to bone.
  • Inside, the muscle is divided into fascicles: visible bundles of fibres, each sheathed in connective tissue (the perimysium). The arrangement of these fascicles relative to the tendon is what we will call pennation, below.
  • Each fascicle is a bundle of muscle fibres. A muscle fibre is a single cell — unusually, a long, cylindrical, multinucleated one (10–100 μm across, up to many centimetres long), striped (striated) with a regular cross-banding.
  • Each fibre is packed with hundreds of myofibrils: thread-like strands running its full length.
  • Each myofibril is a chain of sarcomeres laid end to end. The sarcomere is the contractile unit — the smallest piece that shortens and pulls. Its banding is the source of the fibre's stripes, and its internal machinery (thick and thin filaments) is where force is generated, developed in §2.

Two architectural numbers set the maximal force. First, how much contractile material the muscle carries across its section. Second, at what angle the fibres pull on the tendon. Take them in turn.

Specific tension and physiological cross-section

Every fibre is built from the same molecular machinery (the crossbridges of §2), packed at nearly the same density. So the force a fibre can exert is set simply by how many filaments lie side by side across its cross-section — i.e. by its cross-sectional area. The proportionality constant is a material property of muscle:

Definition 1.1 (specific tension). The specific tension $\sigma$ is the maximal isometric ("iso" = same, "metric" = length: contracting at fixed length) force a muscle produces per unit cross-sectional area of fibre. It is a stress (force / area), and across mammalian skeletal muscle it is remarkably constant, $$\sigma \approx 0.3\ \mathrm{MPa} = 30\ \mathrm{N\,cm^{-2}},\tag{1.1}$$ with a measured range of about $0.2$–$0.35\ \mathrm{MPa}$. Its near-universality is the first hint that all muscle runs on one shared mechanism.

But a fibre in a pennate muscle does not run straight down the muscle's long axis, so the muscle's anatomical cross-section (a cut perpendicular to the whole muscle) slices fibres obliquely and undercounts them. The area that matters is the one taken perpendicular to the fibres themselves, summed over every fibre:

Definition 1.2 (physiological cross-sectional area, PCSA). The physiological cross-sectional area is the total cross-sectional area of all the muscle's fibres, measured perpendicular to their length. Since a bundle of fibres of length $L_f$ and total volume $V$ satisfies $V = \mathrm{PCSA}\times L_f$, $$\mathrm{PCSA} = \frac{V}{L_f} = \frac{m}{\rho\,L_f},\tag{1.2}$$ where $m$ is muscle mass and $\rho\approx1.06\ \mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}}$ is muscle density. PCSA counts contractile material, not girth: a fat muscle of long fibres can have a smaller PCSA than a slim muscle of short, densely packed ones.

The force a muscle's fibres generate along their own direction is then just specific tension times the physiological cross-section, $F_{\text{fibre}} = \sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}$. What reaches the tendon depends on the pennation angle, which we quantify next.

Pennation: angling the fibres

Definition 1.3 (pennation angle). The pennation angle $\theta_p$ is the angle between a muscle's fibres and its tendon (its line of action). A muscle whose fibres run parallel to the tendon ($\theta_p\approx0$) is parallel (or fusiform); one whose fibres attach obliquely, like the barbs of a feather (Latin penna), is pennate.

So each fibre delivers $F_t=F_f\cos\theta_p$ along the tendon (eq. below). This looks like a pure loss — yet the most powerful muscles in the body (the quadriceps, the calf) are strongly pennate. The resolution is a trade-off: angling the fibres frees up room, so a pennate muscle can pack many more (necessarily shorter) fibres into the same volume. By (1.2), halving the fibre length doubles the PCSA. That gain in PCSA scales faster than the modest $\cos\theta_p$ loss (at $\theta_p=25^\circ$, $\cos\theta_p=0.91$ — only a 9% cut), so pennate muscles are high-force designs. The price they pay is elsewhere: short fibres shorten by only a short distance, so pennate muscles have small range and high force, while parallel muscles have large range and lower force. Architecture is a choice between force and excursion.

Collecting the two effects gives the maximal isometric force a muscle delivers to its tendon:

Proposition 1.1 (maximal isometric force from architecture). A muscle of specific tension $\sigma$, physiological cross-sectional area $\mathrm{PCSA}$, and pennation angle $\theta_p$ delivers a maximal isometric tendon force $$\boxed{\;F_{\max} \;=\; \sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}\,\cos\theta_p\;}\tag{1.3}$$ This is the ceiling the neural control of §3–§7 can approach but never exceed; every later force term is a fraction of it.

Put in numbers. With $\sigma=30\ \mathrm{N\,cm^{-2}}$, a muscle's maximal force in newtons is just $30\times\mathrm{PCSA}\,[\mathrm{cm^2}]\times\cos\theta_p$. The table spans the range from a small parallel flexor to the big pennate extensors:

Equation (1.3) fixes the size of the force. But a muscle almost never works at $F_{\max}$: the force it actually makes depends on how stretched it is, how fast it is shortening, and how strongly it is switched on. The first of these — the dependence on length — is written into the very geometry of the sarcomere we just drew, and it is where we turn next (§2).

2. The sarcomere: crossbridges and the length–tension law

Section 1 fixed the ceiling on force, $F_{\max}=\sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}\cos\theta_p$. But a muscle almost never delivers $F_{\max}$. The force it actually produces at any instant is that ceiling scaled by three dimensionless factors, each between 0 and 1:

$F_{\text{active}} \;=\; \underbrace{a(t)}_{\text{how switched on}}\;\cdot\; \underbrace{f_L(\ell)}_{\text{how stretched}}\;\cdot\; \underbrace{f_V(v)}_{\text{how fast}}\;\cdot\; F_{\max}$

Activation $a$ is the story of §3–§5; the velocity factor $f_V$ is §6. This section derives the middle one — the length factor $f_L(\ell)$ — and the remarkable thing is that it falls straight out of the geometry of the sarcomere we already drew in §1 (Fig. 3). Force is made by molecular crossbridges; a crossbridge can only pull where a thin filament lies beside it; how much thin filament lies beside the bridges is set by how long the sarcomere is. So force must depend on length — and we can compute exactly how.

The sliding-filament model

Recall the sarcomere from §1: central thick (myosin) filaments interdigitating with thin (actin) filaments anchored to the Z-discs at each end. Define the one length this section turns on:

Definition 2.1 (sarcomere length). The sarcomere length $\ell$ is the distance between the two Z-discs that bound one sarcomere — the length of the repeating contractile unit. At rest in human muscle $\ell\approx2.6$–$2.8\ \mu\mathrm m$.

How does a muscle shorten? Not by the filaments themselves getting shorter. In the sliding-filament model (A. F. Huxley and Hanson, 1954) the thick and thin filaments keep their lengths and instead slide past one another: the thin filaments are drawn deeper in between the thick ones, hauling the Z-discs together and shrinking $\ell$. The tell-tale evidence is that the thick-filament band stays a fixed width under the microscope while the sarcomere shortens — only the overlap changes. Everything below follows from that one fact. (An animation of this sliding is the capstone figure of the section, once we know what it does to force.)

The crossbridge cycle — where force and ATP meet

Projecting from each thick filament are hundreds of myosin heads, or crossbridges: tiny motor domains that reach out, grab the neighbouring thin filament, and pull. Each head runs a four-state cycle, burning exactly one molecule of ATP (adenosine triphosphate, the cell's energy currency) per turn (Fig. 5):

Two consequences matter for the rest of the module. First, one ATP is hydrolysed per crossbridge per cycle — the chemical price of the mechanical work, the "chemo-" in this module's title. Second, and more immediately, the heads act as a parallel array of tiny, independent force generators: at any instant the sarcomere's active force is the sum of the pulls of all the heads that happen to be attached, so

active force $\;\propto\;$ number of crossbridges attached at that instant.

Force follows overlap

A head can only attach where there is a thin filament beside it to grab. The heads are spread (at nearly uniform density) along the thick filament everywhere except a central bare zone that carries no heads. So the number of heads that can attach is set by how much thin filament lies alongside the head-bearing part of the thick filament — the overlap. To turn "∝ attached heads" into "∝ overlap length" we make one modelling assumption explicit, because it is exactly what makes the derivation work:

Modelling assumption 2.2 (independent, uniform crossbridges). The myosin heads are (i) distributed at uniform density along the head-bearing zones of the thick filament, and (ii) independent and identical force generators. Then the number of attachable heads — and hence the active force — is proportional to the length of overlap between the thin filaments and the head-bearing zone: $$F_{\text{active}} \;\propto\; (\text{overlap length}).\tag{2.1}$$ Uniform density is what makes this proportionality linear; it is the hinge of the whole length–tension law.

Each snapshot is a breakpoint of the curve, and each is a clean statement about lengths (all measured Z-to-Z). Reading from long to short:

  • Descending-limb zero — the thin filaments have slid completely clear of the thick filament, so overlap and force reach zero. This happens when the sarcomere length equals the thick length plus both thin filaments: $\ell = A + 2\,a_{\text{thin}} = 1.60 + 2(1.30) = 4.20\ \mu\mathrm m.$
  • Plateau (optimal length $\ell_0$) — as the sarcomere shortens from there, overlap grows until every head is engaged. That is first true when the thin-filament tips just reach the edge of the bare zone: $\ell_0 = 2\,a_{\text{thin}} + B = 2.60 + 0.10 = 2.70\ \mu\mathrm m.$ Shorten a touch more and the thin tips slide across the (head-free) bare zone without gaining or losing any engaged heads — so force stays flat until the tips meet at the centre, at $\ell = 2\,a_{\text{thin}} = 2.60\ \mu\mathrm m$. The plateau width equals the bare-zone width $B$: the head-free centre is exactly what buys a muscle a range of lengths at full force.
  • Ascending limb — below $2.60\ \mu\mathrm m$ the thin filaments overshoot the centre and enter the opposite half, where they lie the wrong way among the far heads and interfere (the orange double overlap); force declines. At $\ell = A = 1.60\ \mu\mathrm m$ the thick filaments run into the Z-discs and begin to crumple, and force drops steeply, extrapolating to zero near $\ell\approx1.27\ \mu\mathrm m$.

That last zero deserves a caution. Unlike the descending limb — whose zero at $4.2\ \mu\mathrm m$ is pure thin/thick geometry — the ascending zero near $1.27\ \mu\mathrm m$ is an empirical extrapolation set by the thick filament buckling against the Z-discs, a property of the (nearly species-invariant) thick filament rather than a length we can derive by overlap alone. So we treat the descending limb and plateau as exact geometry and the ascending limb as a measured, piecewise-linear approximation.

Derivation of the descending limb (linear). On the descending limb every attached head lies in the head-bearing zone, and by (2.1) force is proportional to the overlap between one thin filament and one head-bearing half of the thick filament. Measuring from the sarcomere centre, that half runs out to $A/2$, and the thin filament's inner tip sits at $\ell/2 - a_{\text{thin}}$. The overlap is therefore $$\text{overlap}(\ell) \;=\; \frac{A}{2} - \Big(\frac{\ell}{2} - a_{\text{thin}}\Big),$$ which shrinks linearly as $\ell$ grows. Normalising by its maximum, the head-bearing length $\tfrac{A-B}{2}=0.75\ \mu\mathrm m$, gives the straight descending limb from $f_L=1$ at $\ell_0=2.70$ to $f_L=0$ at $4.20\ \mu\mathrm m$. ∎

Collecting all four regions gives the active length–tension law — the first mechanical law of the module:

Proposition 2.1 (active length–tension relation). With sarcomere length $\ell$ in micrometres, the normalized active force is $$\boxed{\;f_L(\ell)=\begin{cases}0, & \ell\le 1.27 \ \text{ or }\ \ell\ge 4.20,\\[3pt]\text{ascending (double overlap; thick-filament}\\[-1pt]\quad\text{compression) }\nearrow, & 1.27\lt\ell\lt2.60,\\[3pt]1, & 2.60\le\ell\le 2.70\quad(\text{plateau}),\\[3pt]\dfrac{4.20-\ell}{4.20-2.70}, & 2.70\lt\ell\lt4.20.\end{cases}\;}$$ The plateau sits at the optimal length $\ell_0\approx2.7\ \mu\mathrm m$; force falls to zero at $\ell\approx1.27$ and $4.20\ \mu\mathrm m$.

What does it mean for a working muscle? Sarcomeres in the body are held, by their tendons and skeletal geometry, to operate mostly on the plateau and the near flanks around $\ell_0$ — they rarely wander out to the zeros, where they would be useless. But they do move off the plateau: this is why your grip is weakest with the wrist fully flexed or fully extended (finger-flexor sarcomeres pushed onto the ascending or descending limb), and strongest in between. 3. Excitation–contraction coupling: from action potential to calcium

Section 2 taught that a crossbridge pulls only where a thin filament lies beside it. But it needs one more thing: the actin binding site must be exposed. At rest those sites are physically covered, so even a sarcomere at optimal overlap, flush with ATP, makes no force. Overlap is necessary but not sufficient — the muscle also needs a go signal.

That signal is calcium, dumped into the muscle cell the instant the motor nerve fires. Tracing how a nerve impulse becomes a calcium pulse is the electro- of this "chemo-electro-mechanical" module, and it hands us the last missing quantity: activation $a$ — the dimensionless switch, between 0 and 1, that §5 will model as an ODE and §7 will multiply into the force. The whole cascade, nerve to filament, is one figure (Fig. 9):

The neuromuscular junction: nerve impulse to muscle impulse

A skeletal muscle fibre is commanded by exactly one motor neuron, which meets it at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ) — the synapse between nerve and muscle. When an action potential (a self-propagating wave of membrane depolarisation) reaches the nerve terminal, it opens voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels there; the inrushing Ca²⁺ triggers release of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine (ACh) into the narrow synaptic cleft. ACh binds receptors on the motor end-plate (the specialised patch of muscle membrane under the terminal), opening ion channels that depolarise it.

That depolarisation is large enough to ignite a fresh, all-or-none muscle action potential in the sarcolemma (the muscle fibre's cell membrane), which then races along the fibre in both directions at a few metres per second. The NMJ is a high-safety-factor relay: unlike the many-to-one synapses of the brain, one nerve impulse reliably yields exactly one muscle impulse. So the CNS controls a fibre entirely by when and how often it fires — a fact §4 builds on.

From voltage to calcium: the T-tubule and the sarcoplasmic reticulum

To reach the contractile machinery packed throughout the fibre's interior, the muscle action potential travels down the T-tubules — tubular invaginations of the sarcolemma that plunge deep into the cell, carrying the voltage signal inward. Each T-tubule runs alongside the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR), an internal membrane sac that stores Ca²⁺ at high concentration. Where a T-tubule meets two SR sacs sits the triad, and there the coupling happens:

  • Embedded in the T-tubule membrane is the voltage sensor DHPR (the dihydropyridine receptor). When the action potential's voltage reaches it, the DHPR changes shape.
  • The DHPR is physically tethered to the RyR (ryanodine receptor), the Ca²⁺-release channel in the SR membrane. The DHPR's conformational change mechanically pulls the RyR open.

This mechanical gating is the signature of skeletal muscle: the voltage sensor opens the release channel by direct physical linkage, not by calcium triggering more calcium (the calcium-induced-calcium-release mechanism of cardiac muscle). The instant the RyR opens, Ca²⁺ floods from the SR into the cytosol, and the free calcium concentration leaps from its resting $\sim0.1\ \mu\mathrm M$ to $\sim1$–$2\ \mu\mathrm M$ within a few milliseconds.

The pulse is transient because the SR immediately fights to reclaim its calcium. Studded through the SR membrane is the SERCA pump (a Ca²⁺-ATPase) that spends one ATP to drag Ca²⁺ back inside — another entry in the module's chemical-energy ledger. Release then re-uptake makes the calcium transient: a sharp rise as the RyR dumps Ca²⁺, and a decay of tens of milliseconds as SERCA pumps it away.

Calcium unblocks the sites — and defines activation

Now the payoff. In a resting fibre the actin binding sites are masked by tropomyosin, a long strand lying along the thin filament, held in its blocking position by the three-part troponin complex. No calcium, no attachment, no force. When Ca²⁺ rises, it binds the troponin, which swivels and drags the tropomyosin down into the groove of the actin helix — uncovering the sites so the crossbridges of §2 can grab on and cycle (Fig. 10):

Definition 3.1 (activation). The activation $a$ is the fraction of actin binding sites made available — equivalently, the fraction of the crossbridge machinery switched on — ranging from $a=0$ (fully blocked, relaxed) to $a=1$ (all sites exposed, maximal). It is the factor that scales the crossbridge count of §2, and it enters the muscle force as $$F_{\text{active}} = a\,f_L(\ell)\,f_V(v)\,F_{\max}.$$ (Symbol note: this activation $a$ is unrelated to the thin-filament length $a_{\text{thin}}$ of §2, which always carries its subscript.)

How does $a$ depend on the calcium level? Each troponin binds more than one Ca²⁺, and neighbouring troponin–tropomyosin units cooperate — once one lets go of the tropomyosin, its neighbours follow. That cooperativity makes the site-availability a steep, switch-like function of calcium, well captured by a Hill relation:

Proposition 3.1 (steady-state activation). Under a fixed (clamped) calcium level, the equilibrium fraction of exposed sites is $$a_\infty([\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}]) \;=\; \frac{[\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}]^{\,n}}{Ca_{50}^{\,n} + [\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}]^{\,n}},$$ with cooperativity (Hill) exponent $n\approx2$–$4$ and half-activation $Ca_{50}\approx0.5$–$1\ \mu\mathrm M$. It is $\approx0$ at the resting $0.1\ \mu\mathrm M$ and saturates toward $1$ well above $Ca_{50}$ (plotted in Fig. 11).

The single twitch

The sigmoid is the steady-state answer — where activation settles under a held calcium level. But a single nerve impulse gives only a brief calcium spike, and the thin filament cannot follow it instantly: binding calcium and sliding tropomyosin take time. So activation relaxes toward its steady-state target with a time constant $\tau_a$, $$\frac{da}{dt} = \frac{a_\infty\!\big([\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}](t)\big) - a}{\tau_a},$$ which is exactly the activation dynamics §5 will formalise (there, driven directly by the neural command). Chaining calcium→$a_\infty$→$a$ and remembering that force tracks $a$ (force $\propto a$), one action potential produces a single twitch (Fig. 12):

Validation. The computed twitch has a time-to-peak of ${\sim}25$ ms and a half-relaxation of ${\sim}40$ ms — squarely in the measured range for a fast (type II) fibre (time-to-peak ${\sim}20$–$40$ ms); slow (type I) fibres are two to three times slower, a fibre-type spread that §4 puts to use. The delay of a few milliseconds between the impulse and the first rise of force is the electromechanical delay measured in the lab.

The single twitch is the atom of muscle force, but by itself it is feeble — a fifth of the way to full activation, and already fading before it peaks. The gap between one twitch and that tetanic ceiling is the entire opportunity the nervous system exploits: fire the impulses fast enough and the twitches pile on top of one another, summing toward a fused, maximal contraction. Cashing that in — twitch summation, motor-unit recruitment, and the fibre types just mentioned — is §4.

4. Motor units, recruitment, and rate coding

§3 left us with a feeble atom. One nerve impulse releases one calcium pulse, which drives activation up to only $a\approx0.22$ — under a third of the tetanic ceiling $a_\infty\approx0.73$ — and the twitch is already fading before it peaks (Fig. 12). Yet a whole muscle grades its force smoothly from the whisper that threads a needle to the surge that lifts a barbell. Between the feeble twitch and that range lies the nervous system’s control problem, and it has exactly two knobs to turn:

  • Recruitment — how many motor units it switches on, and
  • Rate coding — how fast it fires each one.

This section builds both, then collapses them into a single lumped command $u(t)\in[0,1]$ — the neural drive, or excitation, that §5 turns into the activation $a$ the force laws multiply. We start with the quantum the whole scheme is built from.

The motor unit: the quantum of contraction

A muscle fibre never fires alone. Its command arrives down an α-motoneuron — a nerve cell whose body sits in the spinal cord and whose single axon runs out to the muscle, there branching to contact many fibres. Every fibre it touches is wired to that one neuron and no other (Fig. 13):

Definition 4.1 (motor unit). A motor unit is one α-motoneuron together with all the muscle fibres it innervates. It is all-or-none: when the motoneuron fires an action potential, every fibre in the unit is excited and twitches together. The motor unit — not the single fibre — is therefore the smallest packet of force the nervous system can command. You cannot fire half a motor unit.

A muscle is a pool of many motor units sharing the tissue, and their fibres are interdigitated rather than clumped: the fibres of any one unit are scattered through the whole cross-section, so its contraction pulls evenly on the whole muscle rather than tugging one corner. Switching a unit on adds its fibres’ force to the total; that is the physical act of recruitment.

Motor units come in a graded range of sizes

Units are not identical — they span a wide, orderly range, and this is the fibre-type spread §3 flagged when the twitch turned out to be a fast-fibre twitch. Three broad classes span the range:

Henneman’s size principle: recruitment order is set by biophysics

Motor units are recruited in a fixed order — smallest first, largest last — regardless of the movement. This is the size principle (Henneman, 1957), and the remarkable thing is that the nervous system does not have to arrange it: the order falls straight out of a one-line circuit argument (Fig. 14, inset).

A motoneuron fires when a descending synaptic current $I$ charges its cell membrane past a voltage threshold. Model the membrane as a resistance $R$ — its input resistance, the voltage change produced per unit injected current. A small motoneuron has little membrane area, hence a high input resistance; a large one has more membrane and a lower resistance. By Ohm’s law the depolarisation a shared drive current produces is

$$\Delta V \;=\; I\,R,\qquad R_{\text{small}} \gg R_{\text{large}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \Delta V_{\text{small}} \gg \Delta V_{\text{large}}.$$

The same command current depolarises the high-resistance small neuron the most, so it reaches threshold first; as the command grows, progressively larger (lower-resistance) neurons cross threshold in turn. Recruitment order is not a control decision — it is forced by cell size. The consequence is a beautifully useful one (Fig. 14):

Because the small units recruited first add little force and the large units recruited last add a great deal, force is built in fine increments at low effort and coarse ones near maximum. You get your most delicate force control exactly where daily life needs it, and the fatigue-resistant S units carry the continuous postural load while the fatigable FF reserve waits for emergencies. Fine motor resolution is a free gift of $\Delta V = IR$.

Rate coding: firing faster sums the twitches

Recruitment sets how many units are on; the second knob grades the force of each unit already recruited by changing its firing rate — how many impulses per second it fires. This works because of the timescale mismatch §3 built in: the twitch outlasts the impulse that caused it. Activation peaks near $25$ ms and is still relaxing at $40$ ms and beyond, but a motoneuron can fire again far sooner. Fire the next impulse before the last twitch has relaxed and its calcium re-opens the gate while activation is still elevated — so the twitches sum.

We can compute this exactly, and consistently with §3, because the machinery is already in hand. Each impulse fires the same calcium pulse of §3. Calcium release saturates — a fibre cannot dump more than the sarcoplasmic reticulum holds — so the calcium from a train is not a runaway sum but a saturating combination of single pulses,

$$[\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}](t) \;=\; \mathrm{Ca}_{\text{rest}} + \big(\mathrm{Ca}_{\text{peak}}-\mathrm{Ca}_{\text{rest}}\big)\Big(1-\prod_i \big[1-p(t-t_i)\big]\Big),$$

where $p$ is the normalised single-pulse shape of §3 and the $t_i$ are the impulse times. For one impulse this reduces to §3’s twitch exactly; for a fast train it saturates at the single-pulse peak $1.4\ \mu\mathrm M$, so the steady-state target $a_\infty([\mathrm{Ca}])$ is pinned at $0.73$ and can never exceed it. Feed this calcium into §3’s activation equation $\dot a=(a_\infty-a)/\tau_a$ with the same $\tau_a\approx41$ ms and integrate. The result is the whole story of graded force in one figure (Fig. 15):

Three regimes emerge as the firing rate climbs. At $10$ Hz the impulses fall more than a twitch-width apart, so activation partly relaxes between them: force is a bumpy, unfused tetanus (mean $\bar a\approx0.15$), the individual twitches still visible as ripples. At $25$ Hz the ripple shrinks and the mean climbs to $\bar a\approx0.39$. By $60$ Hz the impulses come so fast that activation has no time to fall between them: the ripple vanishes and force settles onto a smooth fused tetanus at $\bar a\approx0.69$, essentially the $0.73$ that sustained calcium would give. The point to hold onto is where the summation lives — not in the calcium, which merely saturates, but in the slow variable $a$, which climbs because each impulse arrives before it has had time to relax. Fusion frequency and the force–frequency curve

How fast is “fast enough”? The ripple disappears once the interval between impulses drops below the twitch’s own contraction time — its time-to-peak, ${\approx}25$ ms — so that a fresh impulse lands before activation has begun to fall. Sweeping the rate and measuring both the mean force and the residual ripple gives the muscle’s force–frequency curve (Fig. 17):

Force rises steeply with rate, then saturates: once firing is fast enough to fuse, adding rate buys almost nothing, because $a$ is already pinned at its ceiling. The ripple collapses to nothing by the fusion frequency $f_{\text{fus}}\approx42$ Hz — the rate above which the peak-to-trough ripple stays under $5\%$ — which sits right at $1/(\text{contraction time})\approx1/(25\ \mathrm{ms})=40$ Hz, as it must: fusion is exactly the rate at which the next impulse arrives by the time the twitch peaks. Below $f_{\text{fus}}$ force ripples and rate coding is the CNS’s fine dial; above it, only recruiting more units adds meaningful force. Fast (type II) fibres, with their briefer twitches, fuse at higher rates than slow (type I) fibres — the same fibre-type spread, seen from the frequency side.

Neural drive: one command for both knobs

Recruitment and rate coding are not independent. As effort rises the two climb together: new units switch on while the units already on fire faster. Because a Hill-type muscle model does not track individual motoneurons, we do not need them separately — we need the single aggregate command they produce. That is the neural drive (Fig. 18):

Definition 4.2 (neural drive / excitation). The neural drive $u(t)\in[0,1]$ is the normalised, whole-muscle excitation that aggregates recruitment and rate coding into one lumped command: $u=0$ is a silent muscle, $u=1$ is every motor unit recruited and firing fast enough to fuse. It is a dimensionless input, not yet a force — the CNS’s single knob on the muscle.

Framing $u$ as a single number is the modelling choice that makes the next step tractable: it is the input to the activation dynamics. But $u$ is a command, and the muscle cannot obey it instantly — binding and clearing calcium and cycling crossbridges all take time, the same lag $\tau_a$ that made a single twitch peak $25$ ms after its impulse. §5 makes that precise, turning $u$ into activation $a$ through a single first-order ODE — the same equation we have already been integrating in miniature — with asymmetric rise and fall times that explain why force both lags the command and outlasts it.

5. Activation dynamics: turning the command into activation

§4 compressed the whole nervous-system side of the problem into one number, the neural drive $u(t)\in[0,1]$. But $u$ is a command, not a force — and §0 warned that a muscle does not obey a command the instant it arrives. Decide to lift and the force ramps up over a fraction of a second; let go and it lingers. This section writes down the dynamics behind that lag: the rule that turns the command $u$ into the activation $a$ the force laws multiply.

The activation ODE

The shape of the answer is already in hand. In §3 activation chased a calcium-set target $a_\infty$ by first-order relaxation, $\dot a=(a_\infty-a)/\tau_a$. §4 then showed that what sets the calcium level, muscle-wide, is the drive $u$: more recruitment and faster firing raise calcium, and so raise the target. Collapsing that chain — command $\to$ calcium $\to$ target — lets us drive the activation with $u$ itself. Activation relaxes toward the command:

$$\dot a \;=\; \frac{u - a}{\tau_a},\qquad a,\,u\in[0,1].$$

Read it plainly: whenever the command $u$ sits above the current activation $a$ the muscle switches on ($\dot a\gt 0$); when $u$ falls below $a$ it switches off. The parameter $\tau_a$ is a time constant — the time to close the fraction $1-1/e\approx63\%$ of the gap between $a$ and $u$; a small $\tau_a$ means a brisk response, a large one a sluggish one. This first-order relaxation is the "activation dynamics" the model has been promising since §0. One thing about it is still too crude, though: it uses the same $\tau_a$ whether the muscle is switching on or off — and those are not equally fast.

Rise is faster than fall: two time constants

Turning a muscle on is quicker than letting it relax. So the honest model carries two time constants, and picks whichever applies to the direction the activation is currently moving:

Definition 5.1 (activation and deactivation time constants). The activation time constant $\tau_{\text{act}}$ governs how fast $a$ rises toward a larger command; the deactivation time constant $\tau_{\text{deact}}$ governs how fast it falls toward a smaller one. In skeletal muscle rise is faster than fall, $\tau_{\text{act}}\approx10\ \mathrm{ms}\lt \tau_{\text{deact}}\approx40\ \mathrm{ms}$.

With the constant chosen by direction, the activation ODE becomes the form used throughout the rest of the module — its boxed activation law, standing beside the length–tension law of §2:

$$\boxed{\;\dot a \;=\; \frac{u-a}{\tau_a(u,a)},\qquad \tau_a(u,a)=\begin{cases}\tau_{\text{act}}, & u\gt a\quad(\text{switching on}),\\[2pt]\tau_{\text{deact}}, & u\le a\quad(\text{switching off}).\end{cases}\;}$$

This also settles a loose thread. §3 and §4 integrated the same first-order equation with a single $\tau_a\approx41\ \mathrm{ms}$ — which we can now name: that was the deactivation constant, calibrated to the slow relaxing tail of the twitch. Splitting it lets the on-transition run at its own, faster $\tau_{\text{act}}$, which is why real force rises more briskly than a single-time-constant twitch would suggest. The consequences are cleanest to see in the response to a simple on/off command (Fig. 19):

The two constants can be read straight off the curve. After the command switches on, activation covers $63\%$ of its climb in one $\tau_{\text{act}}\approx10\ \mathrm{ms}$; after it switches off, activation still retains $37\%$ a full $\tau_{\text{deact}}\approx40\ \mathrm{ms}$ later. Between the command and the first stirrings of force sits a few-millisecond electromechanical delay — the same lag §3 measured in the twitch. Both features of §0’s list are now accounted for: force lags the command switching on and outlasts it switching off.

Why the asymmetry — calcium in fast, out slow

The rise-fall asymmetry is not fitted; it falls out of how a fibre moves calcium, the machinery of §3 (Fig. 20):

Calcium enters the cytosol the fast way — by opening a channel, the RyR, which lets it pour down its concentration gradient the instant the action potential arrives. It can leave only the slow way — by being pumped back into the store against that gradient, one ATP burned per ion, by SERCA. Opening a gate is quicker than bailing the room dry, so activation climbs with the small $\tau_{\text{act}}$ and ebbs with the larger $\tau_{\text{deact}}$. The asymmetry is a direct fingerprint of the release-then-reuptake calcium transient drawn in §3.

Activation is a low-pass filter of the drive

A muscle almost never receives a clean on/off step; the drive rises and falls gradually as effort is graded through a movement. The activation ODE then acts as a low-pass filter — a smoother that passes slow changes in the command and blurs fast ones (Fig. 21):

Two things follow, and both matter downstream. First, activation cannot answer arbitrarily fast: rapid flickers in drive are smoothed away, so a muscle is a sluggish transducer of intent. Second, because deactivation is the slower leg, force always outlives its command — when $u$ has returned to zero the activation is still more than half-on. This lingering activation is exactly what a movement controller must anticipate, and it is the reason a Hill-type model needs a state ($a$) with memory rather than an instantaneous input.

With activation dynamics in hand we hold three of the four factors that scale muscle force: the maximal force $F_{\max}$ of §1, the length factor $f_L$ of §2, and now the activation $a$ that §3–§5 built from a nerve impulse. Only the velocity factor $f_V$ remains: force also depends on how fast the muscle is shortening, because the crossbridge cycle of §2 takes time. That is §6, and with it the model is ready to assemble.

6. The force–velocity relation: Hill’s hyperbola

§2 found how force depends on the muscle’s length. But a muscle in a movement is rarely holding still — it is shortening or being stretched, and its force depends just as strongly on how fast. Everyone has felt it: a light dumbbell flies up, a heavy one crawls; and lowering a weight you cannot possibly lift is easy. Those are the two halves of the force–velocity relation, the third factor $f_V$ in the force product $F_{\text{active}}=a\,f_L\,f_V\,F_{\max}$.

Shortening weakens, lengthening strengthens

Fix the sign convention first. Let $v$ be the shortening velocity — the rate at which the muscle’s length decreases. Then $v\gt0$ is concentric action (the muscle shortens while pulling, as in lifting), $v=0$ is isometric (fixed length, the holding case of §2), and $v\lt0$ is eccentric action (the muscle is forcibly lengthened while resisting, as in lowering). Measuring force at each velocity traces one curve (Fig. 22):

On the shortening side the force follows a hyperbola that A. V. Hill fitted in 1938 from heat-and-work measurements on frog muscle:

Proposition 6.1 (Hill’s force–velocity equation, 1938). During concentric shortening the tension $F$ and shortening velocity $v$ satisfy $$\big(F+a_H\big)\big(v+b_H\big)=\big(F_0+a_H\big)\,b_H,$$ a rectangular hyperbola with two constants: $a_H$ (units of force) and $b_H$ (units of velocity). $F_0$ is the isometric force ($v=0$), and the curve crosses zero force at the maximum shortening velocity $v_{\max}=b_H F_0/a_H$, beyond which the muscle cannot shorten fast enough to bear any load. The ratio $a_H/F_0\approx0.25$ sets the curvature.

Solving for force and normalising by $F_0$ gives the dimensionless velocity factor $f_V=F/F_0$ the model uses — the second mechanical law of the module, after the length–tension law of §2:

$$\boxed{\;f_V(v)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{1-v/v_{\max}}{1+v/(c\,v_{\max})}, & v\ge0\quad(\text{concentric, Hill}),\\[10pt] 1+(f_{\text{ecc}}-1)\dfrac{k\,(-v)}{v_{\max}+k\,(-v)}, & v\lt0\quad(\text{eccentric}),\end{cases}\;}$$

with curvature $c=a_H/F_0\approx0.25$, an eccentric plateau $f_{\text{ecc}}\approx1.5$, and a shape constant $k$. The concentric branch falls from $f_V=1$ at isometric to $0$ at $v_{\max}$; the eccentric branch rises above $1$, levelling near $1.5\,F_0$. The join at $v=0$ is a kink, not a smooth peak: eccentric force climbs about twice as steeply as concentric force falls, so a muscle gives up force reluctantly as it shortens but gains it readily as it is stretched.

Why speed costs force: the crossbridges take time

Both halves of the curve come straight from the crossbridge cycle of §2, where force is proportional to the number of heads attached at any instant. A head needs time to attach, drive its power stroke, and release (Fig. 23):

When the filaments slide slowly, nearly every head is attached and pulling, so force is close to $F_0$. As shortening speeds up, each head is dragged past its working range before it can complete a stroke and reattach, so fewer are attached at any moment and force falls — until, at $v_{\max}$, cycling cannot keep up at all and net force vanishes. This also ties $v_{\max}$ back to §2: a fibre’s top speed is set by how fast its crossbridges cycle, which is exactly what makes fast (type II) fibres fast. The eccentric rise is the same mechanism run backwards: a head that is forcibly stretched while attached bears more tension than it generates on its own, and lengthening pops attached heads loose so that fresh ones re-engage under strain — so eccentric force exceeds $F_0$, at a fraction of the metabolic cost.

Power peaks in the middle

Mechanical power is force times velocity, $P=F\,v$. Because force is largest where velocity is smallest and vice versa, their product is small at both ends and largest in between (Fig. 24):

Differentiating Hill’s hyperbola puts the maximum at about $v\approx\tfrac{1}{3}v_{\max}$, where force is about $\tfrac{1}{3}F_0$ and power reaches roughly $0.1\,F_0 v_{\max}$. A muscle asked to deliver power — a cyclist’s legs, a sprinter’s calf — works best not at maximum force nor maximum speed but at this intermediate gear, which is precisely what gear ratios, crank lengths, and cadence choices exist to arrange.

The three factors that scale muscle force are now all in hand: activation $a$ (§3–§5), the length factor $f_L$ (§2), and the velocity factor $f_V$ (here), each riding on the architectural ceiling $F_{\max}$ (§1). §7 assembles them into a single Hill-type muscle model — the synthesis this whole module has been building toward.

7. The Hill-type muscle model

Everything the module has built now meets in one equation. From §1 came the force ceiling $F_{\max}$ and the pennation angle $\theta_p$; from §2 the length factor $f_L(\ell)$; from §3–§5 the activation $a(t)$; from §6 the velocity factor $f_V(v)$. Assembling them into the force a muscle delivers to its tendon is the Hill-type muscle model — the synthesis this module has been driving toward (Fig. 25):

The model has three parts, taken in turn: an active contractile element, a passive elastic element, and the pennation projection onto the tendon.

The contractile element

The contractile element (CE) is the active machinery of §2 — the crossbridges, switched on by calcium. Its force is the ceiling scaled by the three dimensionless factors, exactly the product foreshadowed in §2:

$$F_{CE}(t)=a(t)\,F_{\max}\,f_L(\ell)\,f_V(v).$$

Each factor answers one question about the instant: $a\in[0,1]$, how switched on (§5); $f_L(\ell)\in[0,1]$, how favourable the length (§2); $f_V(v)$, how the shortening speed helps or hurts (§6, and the only factor that can exceed $1$, on the eccentric branch). Multiplied by the architectural ceiling $F_{\max}$ (§1), they give the active tension.

The parallel elastic element

Muscle is not only its crossbridges. The connective tissue wrapping every fibre, fascicle, and the whole belly behaves like a passive spring: stretch the muscle beyond its slack length and it resists, whether or not it is activated. This is the parallel elastic element (PE), carrying a passive tension $F_{PE}(\ell)$ that depends on length alone (Fig. 26):

The passive force is negligible up to about the optimal length, then climbs steeply, like a rubber band taken up past its slack. It is called parallel because it bears load side by side with the contractile element, so the two tensions simply add. Crucially, it keeps a stretched muscle from going slack on the descending limb: where the active overlap of §2 runs out, passive tension takes over, and the total force a whole muscle exerts stays substantial.

Pennation, the tendon, and one honest assumption

Two steps carry the fibre force to the bone. First, only the tendon-directed component survives the pennation angle, the $\cos\theta_p$ factor of §1. Second, that tension is transmitted through the tendon — and here we make one simplification explicit, because it shapes everything downstream:

Modelling assumption 7.1 (rigid tendon). We treat the tendon as inextensible, so the whole muscle–tendon unit’s length change is taken up entirely by the fibres. Then the fibre length $\ell$ and velocity $v$ follow directly from the joint angle and angular velocity — the model needs no internal force balance, and the labs of §9 can integrate one clean ODE. The tendon’s real compliance — the series-elastic element, which stores and returns energy and lets fibres and tendon move at different speeds — is the first refinement beyond this model, deferred to Module 6.

The assembled model

Adding the passive tension to the active, and projecting onto the tendon, gives the module’s headline result — the whole chain from neural command to tendon force in a single line:

$$\boxed{\;F_{\text{tendon}}=\Big[\,\underbrace{a(t)\,F_{\max}\,f_L(\ell)\,f_V(v)}_{\text{contractile } F_{CE}}\;+\;\underbrace{F_{PE}(\ell)}_{\text{passive}}\,\Big]\cos\theta_p\;}$$

Every symbol has been defined and every factor derived; nothing here is fitted after the fact. The one surface at the model’s core is the product $f_L(\ell)\,f_V(v)$ — force as a joint function of length and velocity, scaled bodily by activation (Fig. 27):

Hand the model an activation $a(t)$ from a neural command, a length $\ell$ and velocity $v$ from the joint’s motion, and it returns the tendon force at that instant. What it does not yet give is the quantity a movement actually cares about — the torque that force produces about the joint, which depends on a moment arm that itself changes with angle, and on antagonist muscles pulling the other way. Converting force to joint torque is §8.

8. From muscle force to joint torque

§7 delivers the tendon force $F_{\text{tendon}}$. But a limb does not care about force in a tendon — it cares about the torque that force produces about a joint, the quantity that actually turns the forearm or extends the knee. Converting one to the other is the last mechanical step, and it repays a debt left open in Module 1.

Force times moment arm

A force produces torque about the joint only through its moment arm — the perpendicular distance from the joint’s axis of rotation to the force’s line of action (Fig. 28):

$$\tau \;=\; F_m\,d_m(\theta),$$

where $F_m$ is the muscle’s tendon force and $d_m$ its moment arm. A worked case pins the scale. Hold a $2\ \mathrm{kg}$ dumbbell with the forearm horizontal and the elbow at $90^\circ$: the weight $W=mg\approx19.6\ \mathrm N$ acts at a load moment arm $r_m\approx0.30\ \mathrm m$, needing a joint torque $\tau=W r_m\approx5.9\ \mathrm{N\,m}$. With a biceps moment arm $d_m\approx4\ \mathrm{cm}$, the muscle must pull with $F_m=\tau/d_m\approx147\ \mathrm N$ — the same order as the biceps force estimated in §0, and the direct source of the lever’s force penalty: the muscle’s moment arm is roughly a tenth of the load’s, so it pays roughly tenfold in force.

The moment arm is not a constant

Module 1 treated $d_m$ as a fixed number — a convenient lie. As a joint rotates, the tendon’s path relative to the axis changes, and with it the moment arm (Fig. 29):

The elbow-flexor moment arm swings from under $2\ \mathrm{cm}$ near full extension to about $4\ \mathrm{cm}$ near $90^\circ$ and back. So the same muscle force yields more than twice the torque in mid-range as at the extremes — one reason you are strongest with the elbow around a right angle. The joint’s strength is the product of two angle-dependent curves: the muscle’s own force–length–velocity behaviour (§2, §6) and this moment-arm curve. Writing $d_m(\theta)$ rather than a constant is what makes $\tau=F_m\,d_m(\theta)$ honest.

Agonists, antagonists, and co-contraction

Muscles come in opposing groups. An agonist drives a motion (the biceps flexes the elbow); an antagonist opposes it (the triceps extends it). Because they pull in opposite rotational senses, the joint’s net torque is their difference,

$$\tau_{\text{net}} \;=\; \tau_{\text{ago}} - \tau_{\text{anti}} \;=\; F_{\text{ago}}\,d_{\text{ago}} - F_{\text{anti}}\,d_{\text{anti}}.$$

This lets the nervous system do more than set a net torque. Firing agonist and antagonist together — co-contraction — adds their forces at the joint while their torques partly cancel, so the limb barely moves but the joint grows stiff and steady. It is how you hold a full cup on a shaking train, and it costs metabolic energy for no net torque — a trade of efficiency for stability the model makes explicit.

More muscles than the job needs: redundancy

A single joint torque can be produced by many muscles, and usually is: the elbow is flexed by the biceps, the brachialis, and the brachioradialis together. With more muscles than degrees of freedom, the required torque does not determine the individual forces — the problem is redundant, and some further principle must choose. The common modelling choice is that the body spreads the effort to keep every muscle far from its limit, minimising a summed relative stress:

$$\min_{F_i\ge0}\ \sum_i\left(\frac{F_i}{F_{i,\max}}\right)^2 \qquad\text{subject to}\qquad \sum_i F_i\,d_i=\tau.$$

This is only the lightest touch on the redundancy problem: the honest whole-limb version, where the CNS balances dozens of muscles and the very choice of cost function is debated, is deferred to Module 7 (reflex and closed-loop control) and Module 12 (multi-muscle whole-limb models). Even so, the pieces are now complete: a neural command becomes activation (§5), activation and kinematics become muscle force (§7), and force becomes joint torque (here). Lab 1 — From activation to joint torque

Physical question. Given a time-varying neural command, what elbow torque does the muscle produce through a curl? Biological question. How much does the muscle’s force lag the command, and how do the length and velocity of the moving fibres shape the torque?

Assumptions. A single lumped flexor; rigid tendon (Modelling assumption 7.1), so fibre length and velocity follow the joint angle; a prescribed curl trajectory $\theta(t)$; passive force negligible over this range; activation dynamics as in §5. Equations. The activation ODE $\dot a=(u-a)/\tau_a(u,a)$ (§5), the Hill force $F=a\,F_{\max}f_L(\ell)\,f_V(v)\cos\theta_p$ (§7), and the torque $\tau=F\,d_m(\theta)$ (§8).

Parameters.

SymbolValueMeaning (source)
$F_{\max}$$500\ \mathrm N$peak isometric force (§1)
$\tau_{\text{act}},\ \tau_{\text{deact}}$$10,\ 40\ \mathrm{ms}$activation / deactivation constants (§5)
$v_{\max}$$8\ \ell_0/\mathrm s$maximum shortening velocity (§6)

Code.

import numpy as np

tau_act, tau_deact = 0.010, 0.040          # activation ODE constants (s), §5
Fmax, theta_p, vmax = 500.0, np.radians(15), 8.0

def fL(L):                                  # length factor, §2
    if L <= 0.47 or L >= 1.556: return 0.0
    if L < 0.963:  return (L - 0.47)/0.493
    if L <= 1.0:   return 1.0
    return (1.556 - L)/0.556

def fV(v):                                  # velocity factor (shortening +), §6
    return (1 - v)/(1 + v/0.25) if v >= 0 else 1 + 0.5*20*(-v)/(1 + 20*(-v))

def dm(th):                                 # moment arm (cm), §8
    return 1.8 + 2.2*np.sin(np.radians(th))

t = np.arange(0, 1.5, 0.001)
u = np.clip((t - 0.1)/0.2, 0, 0.85)
u[t > 0.35] = 0.85

a = np.zeros_like(t)                         # integrate activation, §5
for i in range(1, len(t)):
    dt  = t[i] - t[i-1]
    tau = tau_act if u[i-1] > a[i-1] else tau_deact
    a[i] = a[i-1] + dt*(u[i-1] - a[i-1])/tau

theta  = 30 + 80*np.clip((t - 0.3)/0.7, 0, 1)      # the curl (deg)
L      = 1.0 - 0.003*(theta - 70)                  # fibre length / l0
vshort = -np.gradient(L, t)/vmax                   # shortening velocity (norm.)

F   = a*Fmax*np.array([fL(x) for x in L])*np.array([fV(x) for x in vshort])*np.cos(theta_p)
tau = F*np.array([dm(x) for x in theta])/100.0     # joint torque (N m)
print(f"peak torque {tau.max():.1f} N m at t = {t[tau.argmax()]:.2f} s")

The small dip as the curl begins is the velocity factor $f_V$ biting: once the fibres start shortening, force drops before the growing moment arm near $90^\circ$ wins it back. Sensitivity. Torque scales linearly with $F_{\max}$; a larger $\tau_{\text{act}}$ delays and blunts the rise; a faster curl (smaller movement time) cuts peak force through $f_V$. Failure modes. With a rigid tendon the model cannot show tendon energy storage or the electromechanical delay’s elastic component; a very fast movement would push $v$ toward $v_{\max}$ where the Euler step needs shrinking. Extension. Swap the elbow parameters for the quadriceps–knee or the calf–Achilles–ankle (larger $F_{\max}$, different $d_m(\theta)$) and compare the torque a comparable command produces at each joint.

Lab 2 — Fatigue-sensitive force

Physical question. Why does the same neural command produce less force the longer it is held? Biological question. How fast does force fade under sustained effort, and how slowly does it come back?

Assumptions. Isometric hold (fixed length and velocity, so $f_L=f_V=1$); a single fatigue state variable $C\in[0,1]$ — the fraction of contractile capacity still available — that is depleted in proportion to activation and recovers toward $1$ when rested; activation quasi-static on the fatigue timescale (seconds, versus milliseconds for $a$). Equations. $\dot C=-F_f\,a\,C+F_r\,(1-C)$, with realised force $F\propto a\,C$. This is the minimal repayment of Module 1’s “unlimited force” idealisation; the physiological three-compartment version is Module 14.

Parameters. Fatigue rate $F_f=0.018\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$, recovery rate $F_r=0.020\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$; command $u=0.9$ held for $60\ \mathrm s$, then released.

Code.

import numpy as np

Ff, Fr = 0.018, 0.020                # fatigue and recovery rates (1/s)
t = np.arange(0, 120, 0.05)
u = np.where(t < 60, 0.9, 0.0)       # 60 s effort, then release
a = u.copy()                          # activation quasi-static at this timescale

C = np.ones_like(t)                   # available contractile capacity
for i in range(1, len(t)):
    dt = t[i] - t[i-1]
    C[i] = C[i-1] + dt*(-Ff*a[i-1]*C[i-1] + Fr*(1 - C[i-1]))

force = a*C                           # normalised isometric force
print(f"force at 60 s: {force[t < 60][-1]:.2f}; capacity min {C.min():.2f}")

Interpretation. Activation holds flat, yet force sags to little more than half within a minute, purely because capacity $C$ drains — the model reproduces fatigue without any change in the command. Releasing the effort lets $C$ climb back over tens of seconds, so recovery is far slower than the drop is steep only because we chose $F_r\approx F_f$; real muscle recovers faster than it fatigues for submaximal work. Sensitivity. The steady force under sustained effort is $F_r/(F_r+F_f\,a)$ — raise $F_f$ and it fatigues deeper; raise $F_r$ and it holds up better. Failure modes. One lumped $C$ cannot capture the different fatigue rates of slow versus fast motor units (§4), nor metabolic recovery kinetics; those need the multi-compartment model of Module 14. Extension. Give the fast (fatigable) and slow (resistant) motor-unit pools of §4 separate $C$ variables and recruit them by the size principle, so a long low effort fatigues far less than a short maximal one.

10. What the model captures and misses; diagnostics and problems

The Hill-type model earns its place by turning a neural command into joint torque with a handful of measured constants and no free curve-fitting. It is worth being just as clear about what it leaves out — each idealisation is honest, and each is repaid in a later module (Fig. 33).

What it captures. The length–tension law (§2), the calcium-set activation with its asymmetric rise and fall (§3–§5), the force–velocity hyperbola with its strong eccentric branch (§6), pennation and the architectural force ceiling (§1), the angle-dependent moment arm and agonist–antagonist torque (§8), and — lightly — redundancy and fatigue (§8, §9). That is enough to predict, moment by moment, the torque a muscle produces and why it fades.

How the model is measured and validated

Every quantity in the model maps to a laboratory measurement, which is what keeps it falsifiable:

  • Activation $a$ — surface or intramuscular EMG, the electrical shadow of the motor-unit firing of §4, processed into an activation estimate.
  • Joint torque $\tau$ — an isokinetic dynamometer, which holds a joint’s angular velocity and reads the torque directly, tracing the force–velocity and torque–angle curves in the intact limb.
  • Fascicle length $\ell$ and pennation $\theta_p$ — B-mode ultrasound, which images the fibres shortening and rotating in real time during a contraction.
  • $F_{\max}$ and PCSA — muscle volume from MRI combined with ultrasound architecture (§1).

Diagnostics

Five quick self-checks. Answer each before opening the solution; if any is unclear, the linked section is where to look.

  1. Why does muscle force lag the neural command, and outlast it?
    Solution
    Activation follows the command through a first-order ODE with a time constant (§5), and calcium is released fast (channel) but cleared slowly (pump), so $\tau_{\text{act}}\approx10\ \mathrm{ms}\lt\tau_{\text{deact}}\approx40\ \mathrm{ms}$: force builds after the command and lingers after it stops.
  2. A single twitch reaches only about a fifth of full activation. How does the body get a strong, smooth contraction?
    Solution
    Two knobs (§4): recruit more motor units (small before large, by the size principle) and fire each faster so the twitches sum into a fused tetanus.
  3. Why can you lower a weight you could never lift?
    Solution
    Lowering is eccentric (the muscle lengthens under load), and on the eccentric branch of the force–velocity curve force rises above $F_0$, to roughly $1.5\,F_0$ (§6).
  4. Why are you strongest with the elbow near a right angle?
    Solution
    Two angle-dependent factors peak near mid-range: the sarcomere length factor $f_L$ sits near its plateau (§2), and the moment arm $d_m(\theta)$ is largest around $90^\circ$ (§8). Their product is the joint’s strength curve.
  5. You hold a steady contraction and the force fades, though you feel you are trying just as hard. Why?
    Solution
    The command (and activation) can stay constant while the available contractile capacity $C$ drains under sustained effort (§9, Lab 2); realised force is $a\cdot C$, so it sags even with $a$ unchanged.

Conceptual (C1–C10)

C1. Why is the sarcomere, not the whole muscle, the natural unit for the length–tension law? Probes: the hierarchy of §1 and the overlap geometry of §2.
Solution
Force depends on length only because thick/thin filament overlap does, and overlap is a property of the single sarcomere. A whole muscle is millions of sarcomeres in series and parallel; its curve is the sarcomere curve with the kinks averaged out.
C2. A fully activated muscle produces zero active force at certain lengths. Name one and say why. Probes: §2.
Solution
At $\ell\ge4.20\ \mu\mathrm m$ the thin and thick filaments no longer overlap, so no crossbridge can attach and active force is zero regardless of activation (likewise below $\approx1.27\ \mu\mathrm m$, where the filaments jam).
C3. A pennate muscle pays a $\cos\theta_p$ force penalty, yet the body’s strongest muscles are pennate. Explain. Probes: §1.
Solution
Angling the fibres lets many more (shorter) fibres pack into the same volume, raising the physiological cross-section (PCSA). Since $F_{\max}=\sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}\cos\theta_p$ and the PCSA gain outruns the modest $\cos\theta_p$ loss, pennation is a high-force design — at the cost of range.
C4. One twitch reaches only $a\approx0.22$. How does the nervous system reach a strong, smooth contraction? Probes: §3–§4.
Solution
By firing fast enough that successive twitches sum before activation can relax — a fused tetanus at the ceiling $a_\infty\approx0.73$ — and by recruiting more motor units. Rate coding and recruitment together.
C5. In what order are motor units recruited, and why can the CNS not simply choose to fire a large one first? Probes: §4.
Solution
Smallest first (the size principle). A small motoneuron has high input resistance, so a shared synaptic current depolarises it most ($\Delta V=IR$) and it reaches threshold first. The order is forced by cell size, not selectable.
C6. Why does force lag the command when a contraction starts and linger after it ends? Probes: §5.
Solution
Activation obeys a first-order ODE with asymmetric constants: calcium floods in fast through a channel ($\tau_{\text{act}}\approx10\ \mathrm{ms}$) but is pumped out slowly ($\tau_{\text{deact}}\approx40\ \mathrm{ms}$), so $a$ rises quickly yet falls slowly.
C7. You can lower a weight you could never lift. Which part of the model explains it? Probes: §6.
Solution
Lowering is eccentric (the muscle lengthens under load). On the eccentric branch of the force–velocity curve force rises above the isometric $F_0$, to roughly $1.5\,F_0$.
C8. Two flexors can supply a required elbow torque in infinitely many ways. What principle picks one, and whom does it favour? Probes: §8.
Solution
Minimising summed relative stress $\sum(F_i/F_{i,\max})^2$ subject to the torque constraint. It sends more force to the muscle with the larger capacity (and/or larger moment arm) — effort is cheaper where there is more of it.
C9. Co-contracting agonist and antagonist costs metabolic energy but yields no net joint torque. What is the point? Probes: §8.
Solution
It raises joint stiffness and stability: the opposing torques cancel while both muscles resist perturbations, steadying the limb (e.g. holding a full cup on a moving train).
C10. During a sustained hold the force fades although you feel you are trying equally hard. Which model variable is changing, and which is not? Probes: §9.
Solution
Activation $a$ (the command) stays roughly constant; the fatigue capacity $C$ falls. Realised force is $a\cdot C$, so it droops even though the command is unchanged.

Derivational (D1–D10)

D1. Derive the architectural force ceiling $F_{\max}=\sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}\,\cos\theta_p$. Probes: §1.
Solution
Each unit of fibre cross-section pulls with stress $\sigma$, so the fibres’ own force is $F_{\text{fibre}}=\sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}$ along their direction. Resolving onto the tendon keeps only the component $\cos\theta_p$, giving $F_{\max}=\sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}\cos\theta_p$.
D2. Derive the straight descending limb of $f_L(\ell)$ from overlap geometry, and its zero. Probes: §2.
Solution
On the descending limb overlap is $\tfrac{A}{2}-(\tfrac{\ell}{2}-a_{\text{thin}})$, linear in $\ell$; normalising by the head-bearing half-length gives $f_L=\dfrac{4.20-\ell}{4.20-2.70}$, which reaches zero when the thin filaments clear the thick, at $\ell=A+2a_{\text{thin}}=4.20\ \mu\mathrm m$.
D3. From Hill’s equation $(F+a_H)(v+b_H)=(F_0+a_H)b_H$, show the shortening velocity at zero force is $v_{\max}=b_H F_0/a_H$. Probes: §6.
Solution
Set $F=0$: $a_H(v+b_H)=(F_0+a_H)b_H\Rightarrow a_H v = F_0 b_H\Rightarrow v=v_{\max}=b_H F_0/a_H$.
D4. Reduce Hill’s equation to the normalised concentric factor $f_V=\dfrac{1-v/v_{\max}}{1+v/(c\,v_{\max})}$ with $c=a_H/F_0$. Probes: §6.
Solution
Solve for $F$: $F=\dfrac{(F_0+a_H)b_H}{v+b_H}-a_H=\dfrac{F_0 b_H-a_H v}{v+b_H}$. Divide by $F_0$, use $b_H=c\,v_{\max}$ and $a_H=cF_0$: $f_V=\dfrac{c\,v_{\max}-c\,v}{v+c\,v_{\max}}=\dfrac{1-v/v_{\max}}{1+v/(c\,v_{\max})}$.
D5. Show that concentric power $P=F\,v$ for Hill’s curve peaks at an intermediate velocity (set $dP/dv=0$). Probes: §6.
Solution
With $F\propto\dfrac{v_{\max}-v}{v+c\,v_{\max}}$, $P\propto\dfrac{v(v_{\max}-v)}{v+c\,v_{\max}}$. Setting $dP/dv=0$ gives a quadratic whose positive root lies strictly between $0$ and $v_{\max}$; for $c=0.25$ it is $v\approx0.31\,v_{\max}$, where $F\approx0.31F_0$ — the familiar “one-third” rule.
D6. Solve the activation ODE $\dot a=(u-a)/\tau$ for a constant command $u$ from $a(0)=a_0$. Probes: §5.
Solution
Linear first-order: $a(t)=u+(a_0-u)e^{-t/\tau}$. It relaxes exponentially to $u$ with time constant $\tau$ — $\tau_{\text{act}}$ if $u\gt a$, else $\tau_{\text{deact}}$.
D7. Derive the steady force under a sustained command from the fatigue model $\dot C=-F_f a C+F_r(1-C)$. Probes: §9.
Solution
Set $\dot C=0$: $F_r(1-C)=F_f aC\Rightarrow C_\infty=\dfrac{F_r}{F_r+F_f a}$. Realised steady force is $a\,C_\infty=\dfrac{a\,F_r}{F_r+F_f a}$, below the unfatigued $a$.
D8. A muscle spans a joint with origin and insertion at distances $a$ and $b$ from the axis, enclosing angle $\gamma$. Derive its moment arm. Probes: §8.
Solution
The muscle length is $L=\sqrt{a^2+b^2-2ab\cos\gamma}$ (law of cosines). The moment arm is the perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action; using the triangle area two ways, $\tfrac12 ab\sin\gamma=\tfrac12 L\,d_m$, so $d_m=\dfrac{ab\sin\gamma}{L}$ — which peaks at intermediate $\gamma$, as the elbow curve of §8 shows.
D9. Using a Lagrange multiplier, show the redundancy optimum has $F_i\propto d_i F_{i,\max}^2$. Probes: §8.
Solution
Minimise $\sum(F_i/F_{i,\max})^2-\lambda(\sum F_i d_i-\tau)$. Setting the derivative in $F_i$ to zero: $2F_i/F_{i,\max}^2=\lambda d_i$, so $F_i=\tfrac{\lambda}{2}d_i F_{i,\max}^2$. The shared $\lambda$ is fixed by the torque constraint; force scales with moment arm times capacity-squared.
D10. Show that as firing rate $\to\infty$ the tetanic activation approaches the calcium ceiling $a_\infty$. Probes: §4–§5.
Solution
At high rate the saturating calcium gate stays pinned at its peak, so the target $a_\infty$ is held constant. The ODE $\dot a=(a_\infty-a)/\tau_a$ then drives $a\to a_\infty$; the ripple $\propto$ the inter-pulse decay vanishes as the interval $\to0$. Hence fused tetanus $=a_\infty\approx0.73$.

Computational (K1–K10)

Answers are Python-verified against the module’s equations.

K1. A muscle has $\sigma=30\ \mathrm{N\,cm^{-2}}$, $\mathrm{PCSA}=8\ \mathrm{cm^2}$, $\theta_p=20^\circ$. Find $F_{\max}$. Probes: §1.
Solution
$F_{\max}=30\times8\times\cos20^\circ=240\times0.940=\boxed{225.5\ \mathrm N}$.
K2. Find the length factor $f_L$ at sarcomere length $\ell=3.4\ \mu\mathrm m$. Probes: §2.
Solution
On the descending limb, $f_L=\dfrac{4.20-3.40}{4.20-2.70}=\dfrac{0.80}{1.50}=\boxed{0.53}$.
K3. Starting from a twitch value $a=0.22$ with a held target $a_\infty=0.73$ and $\tau_a=41\ \mathrm{ms}$, how long until $a=0.50$? Probes: §5.
Solution
$a(t)=0.73-(0.73-0.22)e^{-t/41}$. Set $0.50$: $e^{-t/41}=\dfrac{0.23}{0.51}=0.451$, so $t=41\ln(1/0.451)=\boxed{32.6\ \mathrm{ms}}$.
K4. Find the velocity factor $f_V$ during concentric shortening at $v=0.5\,v_{\max}$ (curvature $c=0.25$). Probes: §6.
Solution
$f_V=\dfrac{1-0.5}{1+0.5/0.25}=\dfrac{0.5}{3}=\boxed{0.167}$.
K5. At the peak-power velocity $v=\tfrac13 v_{\max}$, find $f_V$ and the normalised power $P=f_V\,v$. Probes: §6.
Solution
$f_V=\dfrac{1-1/3}{1+(1/3)/0.25}=\dfrac{0.667}{2.333}=0.286$; $P=0.286\times0.333=\boxed{0.095\ F_0 v_{\max}}$.
K6. Find $f_V$ during eccentric lengthening at $v=-0.2\,v_{\max}$ (plateau $f_{\text{ecc}}=1.5$, $k=20$). Probes: §6.
Solution
$f_V=1+(1.5-1)\dfrac{20(0.2)}{1+20(0.2)}=1+0.5\dfrac{4}{5}=\boxed{1.40}$ — eccentric force exceeds isometric.
K7. Compute the tendon force for $a=0.6$, $F_{\max}=500\ \mathrm N$, $f_L=0.9$, $f_V=0.8$, $F_{PE}=20\ \mathrm N$, $\theta_p=15^\circ$. Probes: §7.
Solution
$F=(0.6\cdot500\cdot0.9\cdot0.8+20)\cos15^\circ=(216+20)(0.966)=\boxed{228\ \mathrm N}$.
K8. A muscle pulls with $F_m=300\ \mathrm N$ at a moment arm $d_m=3.5\ \mathrm{cm}$. Find the joint torque. Probes: §8.
Solution
$\tau=F_m d_m=300\times0.035=\boxed{10.5\ \mathrm{N\,m}}$.
K9. Two flexors ($d_1=4\ \mathrm{cm}$, $F_{1,\max}=500\ \mathrm N$; $d_2=2\ \mathrm{cm}$, $F_{2,\max}=800\ \mathrm N$) share $\tau=8\ \mathrm{N\,m}$. Find the optimal forces. Probes: §8.
Solution
With $F_i=\tfrac{\lambda}{2}d_i F_{i,\max}^2$ and the constraint, $\tfrac{\lambda}{2}=\tau/(d_1^2F_{1,\max}^2+d_2^2F_{2,\max}^2)=8/656=0.0122$. So $F_1=0.0122(0.04)(500^2)=\boxed{122\ \mathrm N}$, $F_2=0.0122(0.02)(800^2)=\boxed{156\ \mathrm N}$ (check $0.04\cdot122+0.02\cdot156=8.0$ N m).
K10. Under a sustained $a=0.8$ with $F_f=0.02\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$, $F_r=0.015\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$, find the steady capacity and force. Probes: §9.
Solution
$C_\infty=\dfrac{F_r}{F_r+F_f a}=\dfrac{0.015}{0.015+0.016}=\boxed{0.48}$; steady force $a\,C_\infty=0.8\times0.484=\boxed{0.39}$ of maximal.

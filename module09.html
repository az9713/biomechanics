0. Motivation - why running is bouncing

Try to walk faster and faster. At some speed - a little over $2\ \mathrm{m/s}$ for most people - walking stops feeling natural and you break into a run. Something qualitative has changed. In walking you always have at least one foot on the ground; in running there are moments when both feet are off the ground and the whole body is airborne. Each footfall the leg visibly gives, then rebounds, and flings the body back into the air. Running feels like bouncing because, mechanically, it is.

Module 8 modelled walking as an inverted pendulum: the body vaults over a nearly rigid stance leg, so the centre of mass (the average position of the body's mass, abbreviated COM) rides highest as it passes over the foot at midstance, trading speed for height like a pole-vaulter. Running is the reverse. The leg behaves like a spring and the body like a mass bouncing on it: the leg compresses through the first half of stance and recoils through the second, so the COM sits lowest at midstance and highest at the top of the airborne flight phase. There is no double support (the brief overlap when both feet are on the ground) to smooth the ride - flight replaces it (Fig. 1).

This single change of picture - from a stiff pendulum to a spring - reorganises everything that follows. It sets the shape of the ground reaction force, fixes the speed at which walking must give way to running, and explains where the energy for economical running comes from. It also connects three promises left open earlier in the course. Module 6 built the Achilles tendon as a one-dimensional elastic spring and noted that its energy return powers efficient running; here that spring becomes the whole-leg spring of the bouncing model. Module 8 showed that pendular walking cannot exceed a Froude number near $\tfrac12$ and handed the faster regime forward; here we cross that boundary. Module 2 treated bone loads as static and deferred the dynamic impact wave; here landing forces make it unavoidable.

Two practical questions organise the module, the same two the course syllabus poses for running and jumping: what limits how high you can jump, and what makes a landing safe? Both have the same answer, and it is worth stating in advance because everything after Section 5 is a variation on it: a jump and a landing are the same event run in opposite directions, and both are governed by force acting over a distance or a time. You leave the ground with whatever velocity the push-off impulse gives you; you survive a landing by spreading the reverse impulse over as long a stop as you can. Height rewards a large impulse; safety rewards a slow one.

The arc of this module. We fix running-gait notation and the duty factor (section 1), build the spring-loaded inverted pendulum (section 2) and integrate its stance to get the ground reaction force (section 3), add the flight phase and locate the walk-to-run transition (section 4), state the impulse-momentum theorem (section 5) and use it for jump height (section 6), explain the stretch-shortening cycle and tendon recoil (section 7), turn the same impulse idea toward landing and force absorption (section 8), and close with impact loading and injury risk (section 9) before the computational labs and problem set (sections 10-11).

1. Running-gait notation and events

Before we can model the bounce we need the vocabulary to time it. Running, like walking, is periodic: each leg repeats the same sequence of events, and the two legs run half a cycle out of phase. What changes is the sequence itself. In walking each leg's stance phase - the interval when its foot is on the ground - overlaps the other's, giving the double support of Module 8; the complementary swing phase is the interval when that foot is off the ground, advancing to its next contact. In running the stances no longer overlap: between one foot leaving the ground and the other landing, the body is airborne.

Two durations name the two states a single leg passes through. The contact time $t_{\rm c}$ is how long one foot stays on the ground during a stance. The flight time (or aerial time) $t_{\rm f}$ is how long the whole body is airborne between the toe-off of one foot and the touchdown of the other. Walking has $t_{\rm f}=0$; running is exactly the regime $t_{\rm f}\gt 0$.

The spatial bookkeeping mirrors Module 8. A step is the interval - and the forward distance $L$, the step length - from one foot's contact to the opposite foot's contact. A stride is one full cycle of a single leg, two steps, covering the stride length $L_{\rm s}=2L$; its duration is the stride period $T$. The step frequency $f$ is steps per unit time, and the cadence $c$ is steps per minute, so $c=60f$. Because a step consists of one contact followed by one flight, the step lasts $t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}$, so $f=1/(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$ and the stride, being two steps, lasts $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$.

Proposition 1.1 (speed bookkeeping). For symmetric running with step length $L$ and step frequency $f$, the mean forward speed is $$v=fL=\frac{L}{t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}}.$$ In cadence terms, $v=cL/60$.
One step advances the body a distance $L$ in the step duration $t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}$, so the mean speed over a step is $L/(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})=fL$. Averaging over the whole stride gives the same value because a stride is two identical steps: it advances $L_{\rm s}=2L$ in $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$, and $2L/[2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})]=fL$. With cadence $c=60f$ steps per minute, $v=cL/60$. ∎

Four events punctuate the cycle, and the table below defines each; Fig. 2 shows the stance and flight phases they bound across the stride. They are the running analogues of the walking events of Module 8, but the flight phase between stances is new.

EventMechanical meaningWhy it matters
Touchdown / foot strikeThe leading foot meets the ground and the leg spring begins to load.The impact transient and the loading rate begin here (section 9).
MidstanceThe leg spring is maximally compressed; the COM is at its lowest.Vertical ground reaction and stored elastic energy peak together (sections 3, 7).
Toe-offThe spring has recoiled and the foot leaves the ground.The net stance impulse has been delivered; takeoff velocity is set (section 5).
Flight / aerial phaseNeither foot is on the ground; the body is a projectile.Its duration $t_{\rm f}$ is what makes the gait running rather than walking.

The single number that separates the two gaits is the fraction of the cycle a foot spends on the ground.

Definition 1 (duty factor). The duty factor $\beta$ is the fraction of the stride period during which one given foot is on the ground, $$\beta=\frac{t_{\rm c}}{T}.$$ It is defined for any gait; walking has $\beta\gt \tfrac12$, running $\beta\lt \tfrac12$.
Proposition 1.2 (the flight criterion). For running, with $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$, $$\boxed{\;\beta=\frac{t_{\rm c}}{2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})}\,,\qquad \beta\lt \tfrac12 \iff t_{\rm f}\gt 0\;}$$ so the presence of a flight phase is exactly the condition $\beta\lt \tfrac12$, with equality $\beta=\tfrac12$ at the boundary $t_{\rm f}=0$.
Substituting $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$ into $\beta=t_{\rm c}/T$ gives the boxed expression. For the equivalence, $$\beta\lt \tfrac12 \iff \frac{t_{\rm c}}{2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})}\lt \frac12 \iff t_{\rm c}\lt t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}\iff t_{\rm f}\gt 0,$$ where the middle step multiplies both sides by the positive quantity $2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$. Equality holds precisely when $t_{\rm f}=0$, the grounded-running limit in which a foot lands the instant the other leaves - no flight, no double support. Walking sits on the other side, $\beta\gt \tfrac12$, because its double support keeps a foot down for more than half the cycle. ∎

Proposition 1.2 makes "running" a precise kinematic statement - $\beta\lt \tfrac12$ - but it does not yet say why a body abandons walking for running as it speeds up. That question needs the dynamics of the bounce, which we build next: the spring-loaded inverted pendulum of section 2, whose stance and flight (sections 3-4) will locate the transition near a Froude number of $\tfrac12$, the boundary Module 8 handed forward.

2. The spring-loaded inverted pendulum (SLIP)

To make the bounce of section 0 precise we need a model simple enough to solve yet rich enough to bounce. The real leg is a chain of segments - thigh, shank, foot - driven by many muscles (Modules 3 and 5). During running, however, its net action is remarkably spring-like: it shortens as it loads and lengthens as it unloads, returning most of the energy (Fig. 1). The spring-loaded inverted pendulum, or SLIP, keeps only that essence.

Definition 2 (the SLIP model). The runner is a point mass $m$ (the whole body mass, concentrated at the centre of mass) mounted on a single massless linear spring - the leg - of stiffness $k_{\rm leg}$ and rest length $L_0$, tipped with a point foot. While the foot is on the ground (stance) the spring acts; while it is not (flight) only gravity acts. The angle of attack $\alpha_0$ is the angle the leg makes with the ground at touchdown. We write $g$ for the gravitational acceleration.

What $k_{\rm leg}$ is, and is not. The spring here is not the Achilles tendon of Module 6. It is an effective whole-limb stiffness: the single number that best summarises how hard the entire leg - tendons, the active muscles that hold the joints, the geometry of the knee and ankle, even the heel pad - pushes back as it is compressed. Tendon elasticity is one contributor, but so is muscle acting as the series-elastic catapult of Module 6, and so is the pose of the joints. Treating the leg as one spring is the idealisation that buys us a solvable model; interpreting $k_{\rm leg}$ as a tendon property would be a mistake.

Place the foot at the origin of a polar coordinate system and describe the mass by its distance $r$ from the foot (the leg length) and the angle $\varphi$ the leg makes with the ground (Fig. 3, right). The centre of mass is then at horizontal position $x=r\cos\varphi$ and height $y=r\sin\varphi$. Two forces act during stance: gravity $mg$ downward, and the spring force $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)$ directed along the leg, outward from the foot, positive when the spring is compressed ($r\lt L_0$). Newton's second law in these coordinates gives the stance dynamics.

Proposition 2.1 (SLIP stance equations). While the foot is planted, the leg length $r$ and angle $\varphi$ obey $$\boxed{\;\begin{aligned} m\left(\ddot r - r\dot\varphi^{\,2}\right) &= k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r) - mg\sin\varphi,\\[2pt] m\left(r\ddot\varphi + 2\dot r\dot\varphi\right) &= -\,mg\cos\varphi. \end{aligned}\;}\tag{2.1}$$
In polar coordinates with the foot fixed at the origin, the acceleration of the mass has the standard radial and tangential components $a_r=\ddot r - r\dot\varphi^{\,2}$ and $a_\varphi = r\ddot\varphi + 2\dot r\dot\varphi$, along the outward radial unit vector $\hat{\mathbf r}=(\cos\varphi,\sin\varphi)$ and the tangential unit vector $\hat{\boldsymbol\varphi}=(-\sin\varphi,\cos\varphi)$. The spring acts purely radially, contributing $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)$ to the radial direction and nothing tangential. Gravity is $(0,-mg)$; projecting, its radial component is $(0,-mg)\cdot\hat{\mathbf r}=-mg\sin\varphi$ and its tangential component is $(0,-mg)\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol\varphi}=-mg\cos\varphi$. Newton's law $m\mathbf a = \mathbf F$ along each direction gives the two lines of (2.1). ∎

The system is autonomous and conservative - the spring and gravity are the only forces, and the massless leg stores no kinetic energy of its own. That has a consequence we will lean on repeatedly.

Proposition 2.2 (energy is conserved). Throughout stance the total mechanical energy $$\boxed{\;E=\tfrac12 m\!\left(\dot r^{\,2}+r^2\dot\varphi^{\,2}\right)+mg\,r\sin\varphi+\tfrac12 k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)^2\;}\tag{2.2}$$ is constant, and it equals the constant flight-phase energy $\tfrac12 m(\dot x^2+\dot y^2)+mgy$ at every touchdown and takeoff, where $\dot x^2+\dot y^2$ is the squared instantaneous speed of the centre of mass (not the mean running speed $v$ of section 1).
Differentiate (2.2). The kinetic term gives $m(\dot r\ddot r + r\dot r\dot\varphi^{\,2}+r^2\dot\varphi\ddot\varphi)$; the gravitational term gives $mg(\dot r\sin\varphi+r\dot\varphi\cos\varphi)$; the spring term gives $-k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\dot r$. Collect the $\dot r$ terms and the $\dot\varphi$ terms and substitute the two equations of (2.1) for $m(\ddot r - r\dot\varphi^{\,2})$ and $m(r\ddot\varphi+2\dot r\dot\varphi)$; every term cancels, so $\dot E=0$. At touchdown and takeoff the leg is exactly at rest length, $r=L_0$, so the spring term vanishes and $E$ reduces to $\tfrac12 m(\dot x^2+\dot y^2)+mgy$ - which is itself conserved in flight, since there gravity is the only force. Energy therefore passes through the phase changes without a jump. ∎

The flight phase needs no new machinery: with the foot off the ground the spring is gone and the point mass is a projectile.

Proposition 2.3 (flight is ballistic). During flight the centre of mass obeys $$\boxed{\;\ddot x = 0,\qquad \ddot y = -\,g\;}\tag{2.3}$$ so it follows a parabola, entering and leaving flight at the touchdown and takeoff states of Prop 2.2.
In flight the only force on the mass is gravity $(0,-mg)$. Newton's second law $m\ddot{\mathbf x}=(0,-mg)$ gives $\ddot x=0$ and $\ddot y=-g$ immediately. Integrating twice yields a constant horizontal velocity and a parabolic height, the projectile motion solved in section 4. ∎

Equations (2.1)-(2.3) are the whole model: a spring-governed stance handed to a gravity-governed flight and back, with energy (2.2) carried across unbroken. Three idealisations make it this clean, and each is an honest debt. The leg is massless, so there is no swing-leg inertia and no collision loss at touchdown - the spring engages smoothly at rest length (repaid in the whole-body coordination of Module 12). The spring is linear and lossless, so the model neither dissipates nor supplies energy; a real runner loses energy to damping and must replace it with muscular work, the stretch-shortening economy of section 7. And $k_{\rm leg}$ is a lumped effective stiffness, not a tissue constant. What the model buys with these debts is a genuine bounce we can now integrate: section 3 solves the stance equations for the ground reaction force, and section 4 solves the flight parabola and locates the walk-to-run transition.

3. Stance dynamics and the ground reaction force

A force plate does not measure leg length or joint angle; it measures the force the foot presses into the ground, the ground reaction force (GRF). Its vertical component is the single most-reported number in running biomechanics, and the SLIP stance equations of section 2 predict it directly. Because the leg is massless, whatever force the spring exerts on the body is transmitted undiminished to the foot, so the vertical GRF is just the vertical component of the spring force.

Proposition 3.1 (vertical ground reaction force). During stance the vertical GRF is $$\boxed{\;F_z = k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\,\sin\varphi \;=\; m\big(g+\ddot y\big)\;}\tag{3.1}$$ where $y=r\sin\varphi$ is the height of the centre of mass.
The spring force on the body has magnitude $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)$ and points along the leg, in the direction $\hat{\mathbf r}=(\cos\varphi,\sin\varphi)$; its vertical component is $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\sin\varphi$. The massless leg carries this to the foot unchanged (a massless body in equilibrium transmits force undiminished), so the ground pushes up on the runner with exactly this vertical force - the first equality. For the second, Newton's vertical law for the point mass reads $m\ddot y = F_z - mg$, since the only vertical forces are the upward GRF $F_z$ and gravity; solving for $F_z$ gives $m(g+\ddot y)$. The two expressions agree because differentiating $y=r\sin\varphi$ twice and using the stance equations (2.1) reproduces $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\sin\varphi/m - g = \ddot y$. ∎

Integrating the stance equations from touchdown to takeoff and reading off (3.1) gives the curve in Fig. 4.

The running curve rises to a single peak at midstance and falls back to zero at takeoff. The location of the peak is no accident. At midstance the leg is vertical ($\varphi=90^\circ$, so $\sin\varphi=1$) and the spring is at its greatest compression - the mass has stopped descending and is about to rise, so $\dot r=0$ there. Both forms of (3.1) then agree that the force is largest.

Peak vertical force. Writing $\Delta r_{\max}=L_0-r_{\min}$ for the greatest leg compression, reached at midstance, the peak vertical GRF is $$F_z^{\max}=k_{\rm leg}\,\Delta r_{\max}.$$ A stiffer leg, or a faster or steeper landing, compresses the spring more and raises the peak: integrating (2.1) as $k_{\rm leg}$ runs from $11$ to $26\ \mathrm{kN/m}$ (jogging touchdown, $\alpha_0=70^\circ$) lifts the peak from $2.1$ to $3.6$ body weights and shortens contact from $211$ to $150\ \mathrm{ms}$ - the running-realistic band of $2$-$3$ body weights.

The second form of (3.1), $F_z=m(g+\ddot y)$, explains something the first hides: why running has one hump and walking two. The vertical GRF is above body weight exactly when the centre of mass accelerates upward ($\ddot y\gt 0$) and below it when the mass accelerates downward ($\ddot y\lt 0$). In running the centre of mass is lowest at midstance (Fig. 1); there it is being turned around from falling to rising, so $\ddot y\gt 0$ is largest and the force makes a single central peak. In walking the centre of mass is highest at midstance - the inverted-pendulum vault of Module 8 - so there it accelerates downward, $\ddot y\lt 0$, and the force dips below body weight, leaving the two humps of loading and push-off on either side. The count of humps is a direct readout of the same bounce-versus-vault inversion that opened the module.

Two quantities carried by this curve reappear later. Its peak and how steeply it rises - the loading rate - govern tissue overload and injury (section 9). Its area, the vertical impulse, together with the horizontal impulse sets how the body leaves the ground; that is the impulse-momentum accounting of section 5. First, though, the phase this curve is missing: with the foot off the ground, the body is in flight (section 4).

4. Flight phase and the walk-to-run transition

The ground reaction curve of section 3 falls to zero at takeoff, and then nothing touches the runner but gravity. By Proposition 2.3 the centre of mass is a projectile, and a projectile's motion is one of the oldest solved problems in mechanics. Writing the takeoff velocity as its horizontal and vertical parts $v_x$ and $v_y$ (with $v_y\gt 0$ upward), we can read off everything about the flight.

Proposition 4.1 (flight kinematics). A runner that leaves the ground with velocity $(v_x,v_y)$ at height $y_{\rm to}$ follows a parabola and, returning to the same height at the next touchdown, has $$\boxed{\;t_{\rm f}=\frac{2v_y}{g},\qquad h_{\rm apex}=\frac{v_y^{2}}{2g},\qquad d_{\rm f}=v_x\,t_{\rm f}=\frac{2v_xv_y}{g}\;}\tag{4.1}$$ for the flight time $t_{\rm f}$, the apex height $h_{\rm apex}$ above takeoff, and the horizontal flight range $d_{\rm f}$.
Integrating $\ddot x=0$ and $\ddot y=-g$ (Prop 2.3) from takeoff gives $x(t)=v_xt$ and $y(t)=y_{\rm to}+v_yt-\tfrac12 gt^{2}$, measuring $x$ from the takeoff point. The height returns to $y_{\rm to}$ when $v_yt-\tfrac12gt^2=0$, i.e. at $t=0$ (takeoff) and $t=2v_y/g$ (next touchdown), so $t_{\rm f}=2v_y/g$. The apex is where $\dot y=v_y-gt=0$, at $t=v_y/g$; substituting gives height gain $v_y(v_y/g)-\tfrac12g(v_y/g)^2=v_y^2/2g$. The horizontal distance is $v_x$ times the flight time, $d_{\rm f}=2v_xv_y/g$. ∎

For a jog leaving the ground at $v_y\approx0.9\ \mathrm{m/s}$ and $v_x\approx4\ \mathrm{m/s}$, (4.1) gives $t_{\rm f}\approx0.18\ \mathrm s$, an apex only about $4\ \mathrm{cm}$ above takeoff, and a flight range near $0.7\ \mathrm m$ (Fig. 5, left). The bounce is shallow but real - and its very existence is what forces the change of gait.

Why can a body not simply walk faster and faster? Module 8 modelled walking as a vault: the centre of mass swings over the stance foot on a near-circular arc of radius the leg length $L_0$. Circular motion needs a centripetal force pointing inward, toward the foot - that is, downward at midstance - of size $mv^2/L_0$, where $v$ is the forward speed. Only two vertical forces are available: gravity down and the ground reaction up. This bounds how fast the vault can go.

Proposition 4.2 (the walking speed ceiling). At the midstance of a walking vault the vertical ground reaction is $$\boxed{\;F_z=mg\left(1-\mathrm{Fr}\right),\qquad \mathrm{Fr}\equiv\frac{v^2}{gL_0}\;}\tag{4.2}$$ with $\mathrm{Fr}$ the Froude number. Because a foot can push but not pull, $F_z\ge 0$, so pure vaulting requires $\mathrm{Fr}\le 1$; at $\mathrm{Fr}=1$ the ground force vanishes and the body leaves the ground.
At midstance the centre of mass moves horizontally at speed $v$ along a circle of radius $L_0$ centred on the foot, so its acceleration is centripetal, $v^2/L_0$ directed toward the foot (downward). Newton's vertical law reads $m(-v^2/L_0)=F_z-mg$ - the net upward force equals mass times the (downward) acceleration - giving $F_z=mg-mv^2/L_0=mg(1-\mathrm{Fr})$. Since the foot can only push up, $F_z\ge0$, which holds iff $\mathrm{Fr}\le1$. At $\mathrm{Fr}=1$, $F_z=0$: nothing holds the mass on the arc, and it continues in a straight line - it takes off. ∎

Equation (4.2) sets a hard kinematic ceiling at $\mathrm{Fr}=1$, i.e. $v=\sqrt{gL_0}\approx3.1\ \mathrm{m/s}$ for a $1\ \mathrm m$ leg. Real runners, though, abandon walking much earlier, near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx0.5$ (Fig. 5, right). The reason is not that walking becomes impossible there but that it becomes expensive: as speed rises the step-to-step collision cost of the vault (Module 8) climbs, while the elastic bounce of running recycles energy through the leg spring, and somewhere near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx0.5$ the bounce becomes the cheaper gait. The precise crossover is an energetic, not a kinematic, statement - its metabolic side is a matter for later modules - but the kinematic ceiling of (4.2) guarantees the switch must happen by $\mathrm{Fr}=1$ at the latest. This is exactly the boundary Module 8 handed forward.

In the notation of section 1, crossing into running means the flight time becomes positive, $t_{\rm f}\gt0$, and the duty factor drops below $\tfrac12$. We now have both phases - a spring-governed stance (section 3) and a ballistic flight (this section) - and the touchdown-to-takeoff velocities that stitch them together. What sets those velocities is the impulse the ground delivers, the subject of section 5.

5. Impulse and momentum

Sections 3 and 4 leave one gap. The stance produces a ground reaction force; the flight is launched by a takeoff velocity; but what connects the force to the velocity it produces? The link is the oldest bookkeeping in dynamics - impulse and momentum - and it is the single idea that will carry the rest of the module. The momentum of the point mass is $\mathbf p=m\mathbf v$; the impulse of a force over an interval is its time integral $\mathbf J=\int_{t_0}^{t_1}\mathbf F\,dt$.

Proposition 5.1 (impulse-momentum theorem). For a body of mass $m$ acted on by a net force $\mathbf F(t)$ from $t_0$ to $t_1$, $$\boxed{\;\mathbf J=\int_{t_0}^{t_1}\mathbf F\,dt=\mathbf p(t_1)-\mathbf p(t_0)=m\big(\mathbf v_1-\mathbf v_0\big)\;}\tag{5.1}$$ the impulse of the net force equals the change in momentum.
Newton's second law is $\mathbf F=d\mathbf p/dt$. Integrating both sides from $t_0$ to $t_1$, the left side is $\mathbf J$ by definition and the right side is $\int_{t_0}^{t_1}(d\mathbf p/dt)\,dt=\mathbf p(t_1)-\mathbf p(t_0)$ by the fundamental theorem of calculus. With constant mass, $\mathbf p=m\mathbf v$, so the change is $m(\mathbf v_1-\mathbf v_0)$. ∎

Apply this to one running stance, vertical component first. In steady running each stance is symmetric in time (section 2): the runner arrives descending and leaves rising at the mirror-image speed, so the vertical velocity flips from $-v_y$ at touchdown to $+v_y$ at takeoff. The vertical forces are the upward GRF $F_z$ and gravity, so the net vertical impulse is $\int(F_z-mg)\,dt$.

Proposition 5.2 (stance impulse and takeoff velocity). Over a symmetric stance of contact time $t_{\rm c}$, $$\boxed{\;\int_0^{t_{\rm c}}F_z\,dt=\underbrace{mg\,t_{\rm c}}_{\text{support weight}}+\underbrace{2m v_y}_{\text{reverse momentum}}\,,\qquad v_y=\frac{1}{2m}\int_0^{t_{\rm c}}\!\big(F_z-mg\big)\,dt\;}\tag{5.2}$$ so the vertical GRF impulse both holds the body up and supplies the vertical takeoff speed.
Let $v(t)$ be the vertical velocity of the centre of mass, so the net vertical impulse is, by the vertical part of (5.1), $\int_0^{t_{\rm c}}(F_z-mg)\,dt=m\big(v(t_{\rm c})-v(0)\big)$. By the stance symmetry the runner arrives descending at $v(0)=-v_y$ and leaves rising at $v(t_{\rm c})=+v_y$, with $v_y\gt0$ the takeoff speed, so this equals $m\big(v_y-(-v_y)\big)=2m v_y$. Moving the weight term across gives $\int F_z\,dt=mg\,t_{\rm c}+2m v_y$, and solving the same equation for $v_y$ gives the second form. ∎

Integrating the computed stance confirms the split exactly: the vertical GRF impulse is $183.2\ \mathrm{N\,s}$, matching the weight impulse $mg\,t_{\rm c}=116.1\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ plus the momentum reversal $2m v_y=67.1\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ (Fig. 6, top). The horizontal story is different (Fig. 6, bottom): the GRF points backward through the first half of stance and forward through the second, so it first brakes and then propels. Their impulses are the areas of the two lobes. Because the forward speed holds steady from step to step, (5.1) forces the net horizontal impulse to vanish, so the braking and propulsive lobes must have equal area.

Equation (5.2) is the hinge of the module. It says the takeoff velocity is nothing but impulse per unit mass, and that reframes the two questions of section 0 as one. To leave the ground faster - to jump higher (section 6) - you need a larger vertical impulse: press harder, or press for longer, to enlarge the area under $F_z$. To survive a landing (section 8) you need the opposite reading of the same equation: the arriving momentum must be removed by an impulse, and if you spread that impulse over a longer time the force stays small. Jumping and landing are one equation run in opposite directions - which is exactly why the next three sections can share it.

6. Jump height from takeoff velocity

A vertical jump is the purest use of the impulse hinge (section 5): there is no flight to catch and no forward speed to keep, only a push-off that launches the body straight up. The push-off delivers a vertical impulse, that impulse sets a takeoff velocity, and the takeoff velocity sets the height. We take those three links in turn.

Proposition 6.1 (height from takeoff velocity). A body leaving the ground vertically at speed $v_0$ rises to $$\boxed{\;h=\frac{v_0^{2}}{2g}\;}\tag{6.1}$$ above its takeoff height.
In flight the only force is gravity, so mechanical energy is conserved (a special case of the ballistic flight of section 4). Between takeoff, where the speed is $v_0$ and the height is $0$, and the apex, where the vertical speed is $0$ and the height is $h$, energy conservation reads $\tfrac12 m v_0^{2}=mgh$. Cancelling $m$ and solving gives $h=v_0^{2}/2g$. Equivalently, it is the apex height $v_y^2/2g$ of (4.1) with the whole takeoff velocity vertical. ∎

So height is quadratic in takeoff speed - a $10\%$ faster launch clears $21\%$ more height. Everything therefore turns on making $v_0$ large, and the impulse-momentum theorem already says how: because a jump starts from rest, $v_0$ is the net vertical impulse per unit mass - with no downward momentum to reverse, the factor is simply $1/m$, not the $1/2m$ of the running stance that had to turn a descent around. There are two equivalent readings of that impulse, one over time and one over the push-off distance, and each names a way to jump higher.

Proposition 6.2 (takeoff velocity from the push-off). Let the push-off apply a vertical ground force $F(t)$ while the centre of mass travels the push-off distance $d$ from the crouch to full extension. Then $$\boxed{\;v_0=\frac{1}{m}\int_0^{t_{\rm p}}\!\big(F-mg\big)\,dt=\sqrt{\frac{2}{m}\int_0^{d}\!\big(F-mg\big)\,dx}\;}\tag{6.2}$$ where $t_{\rm p}$ is the push-off duration: the takeoff speed is the net impulse per unit mass, and equivalently is set by the net work done over the push-off distance.
The impulse form is (5.2) applied from rest ($v=0$ at the start of the push-off) to takeoff: $\int_0^{t_{\rm p}}(F-mg)\,dt=m v_0-0$. For the work form, the work-energy theorem over the push-off says the net work equals the kinetic energy gained, $\int_0^{d}(F-mg)\,dx=\tfrac12 m v_0^{2}-0$; solving for $v_0$ gives the square-root expression. Both compute the same $v_0$ because both integrate the same net force, one against time and one against displacement. ∎

The two forms are two coaching cues. The impulse form says press for longer, or press harder, to add area under $F$. The work form says push through a longer extension $d$, or with a larger net force, to add net work. A representative push-off - a mean net force near $515\ \mathrm N$ over $d\approx0.40\ \mathrm m$ for a $70\ \mathrm{kg}$ jumper - launches at $v_0\approx2.4\ \mathrm{m/s}$ and clears about $30\ \mathrm{cm}$ (Fig. 7, left).

This is also where two jumps that reach different heights part ways. A squat jump (SJ) begins from a held, motionless squat and pushes straight up. A countermovement jump (CMJ) begins from standing, dips down, and reverses into the same push-off. The CMJ reliably wins - by a few centimetres, here $34$ against $30\ \mathrm{cm}$ (Fig. 7, right). By (6.1)-(6.2) the extra height can come only from a larger takeoff impulse, and the force-time curves show where it comes from: the CMJ is front-loaded. Because the muscles were already developing force during the downward countermovement, they enter the upward push-off near full force, whereas the squat jumper must build force from a standstill while already rising. The CMJ's early force adds area under the curve - impulse - for the same push-off distance.

Naming that front-loading precisely - how a pre-stretch lets a muscle-tendon unit start the push-off with high force and stored elastic energy - is the stretch-shortening cycle, and it is the subject of section 7. The mirror of this section is section 8: a jump converts a push-off impulse into upward velocity, and a landing must convert downward velocity back into an impulse - the same equation, run the other way.

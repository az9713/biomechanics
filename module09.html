0. Motivation - why running is bouncing

Try to walk faster and faster. At some speed - a little over $2\ \mathrm{m/s}$ for most people - walking stops feeling natural and you break into a run. Something qualitative has changed. In walking you always have at least one foot on the ground; in running there are moments when both feet are off the ground and the whole body is airborne. Each footfall the leg visibly gives, then rebounds, and flings the body back into the air. Running feels like bouncing because, mechanically, it is.

Module 8 modelled walking as an inverted pendulum: the body vaults over a nearly rigid stance leg, so the centre of mass (the average position of the body's mass, abbreviated COM) rides highest as it passes over the foot at midstance, trading speed for height like a pole-vaulter. Running is the reverse. The leg behaves like a spring and the body like a mass bouncing on it: the leg compresses through the first half of stance and recoils through the second, so the COM sits lowest at midstance and highest at the top of the airborne flight phase. There is no double support (the brief overlap when both feet are on the ground) to smooth the ride - flight replaces it (Fig. 1).

This single change of picture - from a stiff pendulum to a spring - reorganises everything that follows. It sets the shape of the ground reaction force, fixes the speed at which walking must give way to running, and explains where the energy for economical running comes from. It also connects three promises left open earlier in the course. Module 6 built the Achilles tendon as a one-dimensional elastic spring and noted that its energy return powers efficient running; here that spring becomes the whole-leg spring of the bouncing model. Module 8 showed that pendular walking cannot exceed a Froude number near $\tfrac12$ and handed the faster regime forward; here we cross that boundary. Module 2 treated bone loads as static and deferred the dynamic impact wave; here landing forces make it unavoidable.

Two practical questions organise the module, the same two the course syllabus poses for running and jumping: what limits how high you can jump, and what makes a landing safe? Both have the same answer, and it is worth stating in advance because everything after Section 5 is a variation on it: a jump and a landing are the same event run in opposite directions, and both are governed by force acting over a distance or a time. You leave the ground with whatever velocity the push-off impulse gives you; you survive a landing by spreading the reverse impulse over as long a stop as you can. Height rewards a large impulse; safety rewards a slow one.

We fix running-gait notation and the duty factor (section 1), build the spring-loaded inverted pendulum (section 2) and integrate its stance to get the ground reaction force (section 3), add the flight phase and locate the walk-to-run transition (section 4), state the impulse-momentum theorem (section 5) and use it for jump height (section 6), explain the stretch-shortening cycle and tendon recoil (section 7), turn the same impulse idea toward landing and force absorption (section 8), and close with impact loading and injury risk (section 9) before the computational labs and problem set (sections 10-11).

1. Running-gait notation and events

Before we can model the bounce we need the vocabulary to time it. Running, like walking, is periodic: each leg repeats the same sequence of events, and the two legs run half a cycle out of phase. What changes is the sequence itself. In walking each leg's stance phase - the interval when its foot is on the ground - overlaps the other's, giving the double support of Module 8; the complementary swing phase is the interval when that foot is off the ground, advancing to its next contact. In running the stances no longer overlap: between one foot leaving the ground and the other landing, the body is airborne.

Two durations name the two states a single leg passes through. The contact time $t_{\rm c}$ is how long one foot stays on the ground during a stance. The flight time (or aerial time) $t_{\rm f}$ is how long the whole body is airborne between the toe-off of one foot and the touchdown of the other. Walking has $t_{\rm f}=0$; running is exactly the regime $t_{\rm f}\gt 0$.

The spatial bookkeeping mirrors Module 8. A step is the interval - and the forward distance $L$, the step length - from one foot's contact to the opposite foot's contact. A stride is one full cycle of a single leg, two steps, covering the stride length $L_{\rm s}=2L$; its duration is the stride period $T$. The step frequency $f$ is steps per unit time, and the cadence $c$ is steps per minute, so $c=60f$. Because a step consists of one contact followed by one flight, the step lasts $t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}$, so $f=1/(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$ and the stride, being two steps, lasts $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$.

Proposition 1.1 (speed bookkeeping). For symmetric running with step length $L$ and step frequency $f$, the mean forward speed is $$v=fL=\frac{L}{t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}}.$$ In cadence terms, $v=cL/60$.
One step advances the body a distance $L$ in the step duration $t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}$, so the mean speed over a step is $L/(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})=fL$. Averaging over the whole stride gives the same value because a stride is two identical steps: it advances $L_{\rm s}=2L$ in $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$, and $2L/[2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})]=fL$. With cadence $c=60f$ steps per minute, $v=cL/60$. ∎

Four events punctuate the cycle, and the table below defines each; Fig. 2 shows the stance and flight phases they bound across the stride. They are the running analogues of the walking events of Module 8, but the flight phase between stances is new.

EventMechanical meaningWhy it matters

The single number that separates the two gaits is the fraction of the cycle a foot spends on the ground.

Definition 1 (duty factor). The duty factor $\beta$ is the fraction of the stride period during which one given foot is on the ground, $$\beta=\frac{t_{\rm c}}{T}.$$ It is defined for any gait; walking has $\beta\gt \tfrac12$, running $\beta\lt \tfrac12$.
Proposition 1.2 (the flight criterion). For running, with $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$, $$\boxed{\;\beta=\frac{t_{\rm c}}{2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})}\,,\qquad \beta\lt \tfrac12 \iff t_{\rm f}\gt 0\;}$$ so the presence of a flight phase is exactly the condition $\beta\lt \tfrac12$, with equality $\beta=\tfrac12$ at the boundary $t_{\rm f}=0$.
Substituting $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$ into $\beta=t_{\rm c}/T$ gives the boxed expression. For the equivalence, $$\beta\lt \tfrac12 \iff \frac{t_{\rm c}}{2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})}\lt \frac12 \iff t_{\rm c}\lt t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}\iff t_{\rm f}\gt 0,$$ where the middle step multiplies both sides by the positive quantity $2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$. Equality holds precisely when $t_{\rm f}=0$, the grounded-running limit in which a foot lands the instant the other leaves - no flight, no double support. Walking sits on the other side, $\beta\gt \tfrac12$, because its double support keeps a foot down for more than half the cycle. ∎

Proposition 1.2 makes "running" a precise kinematic statement - $\beta\lt \tfrac12$ - but it does not yet say why a body abandons walking for running as it speeds up. That question needs the dynamics of the bounce, which we build next: the spring-loaded inverted pendulum of section 2, whose stance and flight (sections 3-4) will locate the transition near a Froude number of $\tfrac12$, the boundary Module 8 handed forward.

2. The spring-loaded inverted pendulum (SLIP)

To make the bounce of section 0 precise we need a model simple enough to solve yet rich enough to bounce. The real leg is a chain of segments - thigh, shank, foot - driven by many muscles (Modules 3 and 5). During running, however, its net action is remarkably spring-like: it shortens as it loads and lengthens as it unloads, returning most of the energy (Fig. 1). The spring-loaded inverted pendulum, or SLIP, keeps only that essence.

Definition 2 (the SLIP model). The runner is a point mass $m$ (the whole body mass, concentrated at the centre of mass) mounted on a single massless linear spring - the leg - of stiffness $k_{\rm leg}$ and rest length $L_0$, tipped with a point foot. While the foot is on the ground (stance) the spring acts; while it is not (flight) only gravity acts. The angle of attack $\alpha_0$ is the angle the leg makes with the ground at touchdown. We write $g$ for the gravitational acceleration.

What $k_{\rm leg}$ is, and is not. The spring here is not the Achilles tendon of Module 6. It is an effective whole-limb stiffness: the single number that best summarises how hard the entire leg - tendons, the active muscles that hold the joints, the geometry of the knee and ankle, even the heel pad - pushes back as it is compressed. Tendon elasticity is one contributor, but so is muscle acting as the series-elastic catapult of Module 6, and so is the pose of the joints. Treating the leg as one spring is the idealisation that buys us a solvable model; interpreting $k_{\rm leg}$ as a tendon property would be a mistake.

Place the foot at the origin of a polar coordinate system and describe the mass by its distance $r$ from the foot (the leg length) and the angle $\varphi$ the leg makes with the ground (Fig. 3, right). The centre of mass is then at horizontal position $x=r\cos\varphi$ and height $y=r\sin\varphi$. Two forces act during stance: gravity $mg$ downward, and the spring force $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)$ directed along the leg, outward from the foot, positive when the spring is compressed ($r\lt L_0$). Newton's second law in these coordinates gives the stance dynamics.

Proposition 2.1 (SLIP stance equations). While the foot is planted, the leg length $r$ and angle $\varphi$ obey $$\boxed{\;\begin{aligned} m\left(\ddot r - r\dot\varphi^{\,2}\right) &= k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r) - mg\sin\varphi,\\[2pt] m\left(r\ddot\varphi + 2\dot r\dot\varphi\right) &= -\,mg\cos\varphi. \end{aligned}\;}\tag{2.1}$$
In polar coordinates with the foot fixed at the origin, the acceleration of the mass has the standard radial and tangential components $a_r=\ddot r - r\dot\varphi^{\,2}$ and $a_\varphi = r\ddot\varphi + 2\dot r\dot\varphi$, along the outward radial unit vector $\hat{\mathbf r}=(\cos\varphi,\sin\varphi)$ and the tangential unit vector $\hat{\boldsymbol\varphi}=(-\sin\varphi,\cos\varphi)$. The spring acts purely radially, contributing $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)$ to the radial direction and nothing tangential. Gravity is $(0,-mg)$; projecting, its radial component is $(0,-mg)\cdot\hat{\mathbf r}=-mg\sin\varphi$ and its tangential component is $(0,-mg)\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol\varphi}=-mg\cos\varphi$. Newton's law $m\mathbf a = \mathbf F$ along each direction gives the two lines of (2.1). ∎

The system is autonomous and conservative - the spring and gravity are the only forces, and the massless leg stores no kinetic energy of its own. That has a consequence we will lean on repeatedly.

Proposition 2.2 (energy is conserved). Throughout stance the total mechanical energy $$\boxed{\;E=\tfrac12 m\!\left(\dot r^{\,2}+r^2\dot\varphi^{\,2}\right)+mg\,r\sin\varphi+\tfrac12 k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)^2\;}\tag{2.2}$$ is constant, and it equals the constant flight-phase energy $\tfrac12 m(\dot x^2+\dot y^2)+mgy$ at every touchdown and takeoff, where $\dot x^2+\dot y^2$ is the squared instantaneous speed of the centre of mass (not the mean running speed $v$ of section 1).
Differentiate (2.2). The kinetic term gives $m(\dot r\ddot r + r\dot r\dot\varphi^{\,2}+r^2\dot\varphi\ddot\varphi)$; the gravitational term gives $mg(\dot r\sin\varphi+r\dot\varphi\cos\varphi)$; the spring term gives $-k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\dot r$. Collect the $\dot r$ terms and the $\dot\varphi$ terms and substitute the two equations of (2.1) for $m(\ddot r - r\dot\varphi^{\,2})$ and $m(r\ddot\varphi+2\dot r\dot\varphi)$; every term cancels, so $\dot E=0$. At touchdown and takeoff the leg is exactly at rest length, $r=L_0$, so the spring term vanishes and $E$ reduces to $\tfrac12 m(\dot x^2+\dot y^2)+mgy$ - which is itself conserved in flight, since there gravity is the only force. Energy therefore passes through the phase changes without a jump. ∎

The flight phase needs no new machinery: with the foot off the ground the spring is gone and the point mass is a projectile.

Proposition 2.3 (flight is ballistic). During flight the centre of mass obeys $$\boxed{\;\ddot x = 0,\qquad \ddot y = -\,g\;}\tag{2.3}$$ so it follows a parabola, entering and leaving flight at the touchdown and takeoff states of Prop 2.2.
In flight the only force on the mass is gravity $(0,-mg)$. Newton's second law $m\ddot{\mathbf x}=(0,-mg)$ gives $\ddot x=0$ and $\ddot y=-g$ immediately. Integrating twice yields a constant horizontal velocity and a parabolic height, the projectile motion solved in section 4. ∎

Equations (2.1)-(2.3) are the whole model: a spring-governed stance handed to a gravity-governed flight and back, with energy (2.2) carried across unbroken. Three idealisations make it this clean, and each is an honest debt. The leg is massless, so there is no swing-leg inertia and no collision loss at touchdown - the spring engages smoothly at rest length (repaid in the whole-body coordination of Module 12). The spring is linear and lossless, so the model neither dissipates nor supplies energy; a real runner loses energy to damping and must replace it with muscular work, the stretch-shortening economy of section 7. And $k_{\rm leg}$ is a lumped effective stiffness, not a tissue constant. What the model buys with these debts is a genuine bounce we can now integrate: section 3 solves the stance equations for the ground reaction force, and section 4 solves the flight parabola and locates the walk-to-run transition.

3. Stance dynamics and the ground reaction force

A force plate does not measure leg length or joint angle; it measures the force the foot presses into the ground, the ground reaction force (GRF). Its vertical component is the single most-reported number in running biomechanics, and the SLIP stance equations of section 2 predict it directly. Because the leg is massless, whatever force the spring exerts on the body is transmitted undiminished to the foot, so the vertical GRF is just the vertical component of the spring force.

Proposition 3.1 (vertical ground reaction force). During stance the vertical GRF is $$\boxed{\;F_z = k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\,\sin\varphi \;=\; m\big(g+\ddot y\big)\;}\tag{3.1}$$ where $y=r\sin\varphi$ is the height of the centre of mass.
The spring force on the body has magnitude $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)$ and points along the leg, in the direction $\hat{\mathbf r}=(\cos\varphi,\sin\varphi)$; its vertical component is $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\sin\varphi$. The massless leg carries this to the foot unchanged (a massless body in equilibrium transmits force undiminished), so the ground pushes up on the runner with exactly this vertical force - the first equality. For the second, Newton's vertical law for the point mass reads $m\ddot y = F_z - mg$, since the only vertical forces are the upward GRF $F_z$ and gravity; solving for $F_z$ gives $m(g+\ddot y)$. The two expressions agree because differentiating $y=r\sin\varphi$ twice and using the stance equations (2.1) reproduces $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\sin\varphi/m - g = \ddot y$. ∎

Integrating the stance equations from touchdown to takeoff and reading off (3.1) gives the curve in Fig. 4.

The running curve rises to a single peak at midstance and falls back to zero at takeoff. The location of the peak is no accident. At midstance the leg is vertical ($\varphi=90^\circ$, so $\sin\varphi=1$) and the spring is at its greatest compression - the mass has stopped descending and is about to rise, so $\dot r=0$ there. Both forms of (3.1) then agree that the force is largest.

Peak vertical force. Writing $\Delta r_{\max}=L_0-r_{\min}$ for the greatest leg compression, reached at midstance, the peak vertical GRF is $$F_z^{\max}=k_{\rm leg}\,\Delta r_{\max}.$$ A stiffer leg, or a faster or steeper landing, compresses the spring more and raises the peak: integrating (2.1) as $k_{\rm leg}$ runs from $11$ to $26\ \mathrm{kN/m}$ (jogging touchdown, $\alpha_0=70^\circ$) lifts the peak from $2.1$ to $3.6$ body weights and shortens contact from $211$ to $150\ \mathrm{ms}$ - the running-realistic band of $2$-$3$ body weights.

The second form of (3.1), $F_z=m(g+\ddot y)$, explains something the first hides: why running has one hump and walking two. The vertical GRF is above body weight exactly when the centre of mass accelerates upward ($\ddot y\gt 0$) and below it when the mass accelerates downward ($\ddot y\lt 0$). In running the centre of mass is lowest at midstance (Fig. 1); there it is being turned around from falling to rising, so $\ddot y\gt 0$ is largest and the force makes a single central peak. In walking the centre of mass is highest at midstance - the inverted-pendulum vault of Module 8 - so there it accelerates downward, $\ddot y\lt 0$, and the force dips below body weight, leaving the two humps of loading and push-off on either side. The count of humps is a direct readout of the same bounce-versus-vault inversion that opened the module.

Two quantities carried by this curve reappear later. Its peak and how steeply it rises - the loading rate - govern tissue overload and injury (section 9). Its area, the vertical impulse, together with the horizontal impulse sets how the body leaves the ground; that is the impulse-momentum accounting of section 5. First, though, the phase this curve is missing: with the foot off the ground, the body is in flight (section 4).

4. Flight phase and the walk-to-run transition

The ground reaction curve of section 3 falls to zero at takeoff, and then nothing touches the runner but gravity. By Proposition 2.3 the centre of mass is a projectile, and a projectile's motion is one of the oldest solved problems in mechanics. Writing the takeoff velocity as its horizontal and vertical parts $v_x$ and $v_y$ (with $v_y\gt 0$ upward), we can read off everything about the flight.

Proposition 4.1 (flight kinematics). A runner that leaves the ground with velocity $(v_x,v_y)$ at height $y_{\rm to}$ follows a parabola and, returning to the same height at the next touchdown, has $$\boxed{\;t_{\rm f}=\frac{2v_y}{g},\qquad h_{\rm apex}=\frac{v_y^{2}}{2g},\qquad d_{\rm f}=v_x\,t_{\rm f}=\frac{2v_xv_y}{g}\;}\tag{4.1}$$ for the flight time $t_{\rm f}$, the apex height $h_{\rm apex}$ above takeoff, and the horizontal flight range $d_{\rm f}$.
Integrating $\ddot x=0$ and $\ddot y=-g$ (Prop 2.3) from takeoff gives $x(t)=v_xt$ and $y(t)=y_{\rm to}+v_yt-\tfrac12 gt^{2}$, measuring $x$ from the takeoff point. The height returns to $y_{\rm to}$ when $v_yt-\tfrac12gt^2=0$, i.e. at $t=0$ (takeoff) and $t=2v_y/g$ (next touchdown), so $t_{\rm f}=2v_y/g$. The apex is where $\dot y=v_y-gt=0$, at $t=v_y/g$; substituting gives height gain $v_y(v_y/g)-\tfrac12g(v_y/g)^2=v_y^2/2g$. The horizontal distance is $v_x$ times the flight time, $d_{\rm f}=2v_xv_y/g$. ∎

For a jog leaving the ground at $v_y\approx0.9\ \mathrm{m/s}$ and $v_x\approx4\ \mathrm{m/s}$, (4.1) gives $t_{\rm f}\approx0.18\ \mathrm s$, an apex only about $4\ \mathrm{cm}$ above takeoff, and a flight range near $0.7\ \mathrm m$ (Fig. 5, left). The bounce is shallow but real - and its very existence is what forces the change of gait.

Why can a body not simply walk faster and faster? Module 8 modelled walking as a vault: the centre of mass swings over the stance foot on a near-circular arc of radius the leg length $L_0$. Circular motion needs a centripetal force pointing inward, toward the foot - that is, downward at midstance - of size $mv^2/L_0$, where $v$ is the forward speed. Only two vertical forces are available: gravity down and the ground reaction up. This bounds how fast the vault can go.

Proposition 4.2 (the walking speed ceiling). At the midstance of a walking vault the vertical ground reaction is $$\boxed{\;F_z=mg\left(1-\mathrm{Fr}\right),\qquad \mathrm{Fr}\equiv\frac{v^2}{gL_0}\;}\tag{4.2}$$ with $\mathrm{Fr}$ the Froude number. Because a foot can push but not pull, $F_z\ge 0$, so pure vaulting requires $\mathrm{Fr}\le 1$; at $\mathrm{Fr}=1$ the ground force vanishes and the body leaves the ground.
At midstance the centre of mass moves horizontally at speed $v$ along a circle of radius $L_0$ centred on the foot, so its acceleration is centripetal, $v^2/L_0$ directed toward the foot (downward). Newton's vertical law reads $m(-v^2/L_0)=F_z-mg$ - the net upward force equals mass times the (downward) acceleration - giving $F_z=mg-mv^2/L_0=mg(1-\mathrm{Fr})$. Since the foot can only push up, $F_z\ge0$, which holds iff $\mathrm{Fr}\le1$. At $\mathrm{Fr}=1$, $F_z=0$: nothing holds the mass on the arc, and it continues in a straight line - it takes off. ∎

Equation (4.2) sets a hard kinematic ceiling at $\mathrm{Fr}=1$, i.e. $v=\sqrt{gL_0}\approx3.1\ \mathrm{m/s}$ for a $1\ \mathrm m$ leg. Real runners, though, abandon walking much earlier, near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx0.5$ (Fig. 5, right). The reason is not that walking becomes impossible there but that it becomes expensive: as speed rises the step-to-step collision cost of the vault (Module 8) climbs, while the elastic bounce of running recycles energy through the leg spring, and somewhere near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx0.5$ the bounce becomes the cheaper gait. The precise crossover is an energetic, not a kinematic, statement - its metabolic side is a matter for later modules - but the kinematic ceiling of (4.2) guarantees the switch must happen by $\mathrm{Fr}=1$ at the latest. This is exactly the boundary Module 8 handed forward.

In the notation of section 1, crossing into running means the flight time becomes positive, $t_{\rm f}\gt0$, and the duty factor drops below $\tfrac12$. We now have both phases - a spring-governed stance (section 3) and a ballistic flight (this section) - and the touchdown-to-takeoff velocities that stitch them together. What sets those velocities is the impulse the ground delivers, the subject of section 5.

5. Impulse and momentum

Sections 3 and 4 leave one gap. The stance produces a ground reaction force; the flight is launched by a takeoff velocity; but what connects the force to the velocity it produces? The link is the oldest bookkeeping in dynamics - impulse and momentum - and it is the single idea that will carry the rest of the module. The momentum of the point mass is $\mathbf p=m\mathbf v$; the impulse of a force over an interval is its time integral $\mathbf J=\int_{t_0}^{t_1}\mathbf F\,dt$.

Proposition 5.1 (impulse-momentum theorem). For a body of mass $m$ acted on by a net force $\mathbf F(t)$ from $t_0$ to $t_1$, $$\boxed{\;\mathbf J=\int_{t_0}^{t_1}\mathbf F\,dt=\mathbf p(t_1)-\mathbf p(t_0)=m\big(\mathbf v_1-\mathbf v_0\big)\;}\tag{5.1}$$ the impulse of the net force equals the change in momentum.
Newton's second law is $\mathbf F=d\mathbf p/dt$. Integrating both sides from $t_0$ to $t_1$, the left side is $\mathbf J$ by definition and the right side is $\int_{t_0}^{t_1}(d\mathbf p/dt)\,dt=\mathbf p(t_1)-\mathbf p(t_0)$ by the fundamental theorem of calculus. With constant mass, $\mathbf p=m\mathbf v$, so the change is $m(\mathbf v_1-\mathbf v_0)$. ∎

Apply this to one running stance, vertical component first. In steady running each stance is symmetric in time (section 2): the runner arrives descending and leaves rising at the mirror-image speed, so the vertical velocity flips from $-v_y$ at touchdown to $+v_y$ at takeoff. The vertical forces are the upward GRF $F_z$ and gravity, so the net vertical impulse is $\int(F_z-mg)\,dt$.

Proposition 5.2 (stance impulse and takeoff velocity). Over a symmetric stance of contact time $t_{\rm c}$, $$\boxed{\;\int_0^{t_{\rm c}}F_z\,dt=\underbrace{mg\,t_{\rm c}}_{\text{support weight}}+\underbrace{2m v_y}_{\text{reverse momentum}}\,,\qquad v_y=\frac{1}{2m}\int_0^{t_{\rm c}}\!\big(F_z-mg\big)\,dt\;}\tag{5.2}$$ so the vertical GRF impulse both holds the body up and supplies the vertical takeoff speed.
Let $v(t)$ be the vertical velocity of the centre of mass, so the net vertical impulse is, by the vertical part of (5.1), $\int_0^{t_{\rm c}}(F_z-mg)\,dt=m\big(v(t_{\rm c})-v(0)\big)$. By the stance symmetry the runner arrives descending at $v(0)=-v_y$ and leaves rising at $v(t_{\rm c})=+v_y$, with $v_y\gt0$ the takeoff speed, so this equals $m\big(v_y-(-v_y)\big)=2m v_y$. Moving the weight term across gives $\int F_z\,dt=mg\,t_{\rm c}+2m v_y$, and solving the same equation for $v_y$ gives the second form. ∎

Integrating the computed stance confirms the split exactly: the vertical GRF impulse is $183.2\ \mathrm{N\,s}$, matching the weight impulse $mg\,t_{\rm c}=116.1\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ plus the momentum reversal $2m v_y=67.1\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ (Fig. 6, top). The horizontal story is different (Fig. 6, bottom): the GRF points backward through the first half of stance and forward through the second, so it first brakes and then propels. Their impulses are the areas of the two lobes. Because the forward speed holds steady from step to step, (5.1) forces the net horizontal impulse to vanish, so the braking and propulsive lobes must have equal area.

Equation (5.2) is the hinge of the module. It says the takeoff velocity is nothing but impulse per unit mass, and that reframes the two questions of section 0 as one. To leave the ground faster - to jump higher (section 6) - you need a larger vertical impulse: press harder, or press for longer, to enlarge the area under $F_z$. To survive a landing (section 8) you need the opposite reading of the same equation: the arriving momentum must be removed by an impulse, and if you spread that impulse over a longer time the force stays small. Jumping and landing are one equation run in opposite directions - which is exactly why the next three sections can share it.

6. Jump height from takeoff velocity

A vertical jump is the purest use of the impulse hinge (section 5): there is no flight to catch and no forward speed to keep, only a push-off that launches the body straight up. The push-off delivers a vertical impulse, that impulse sets a takeoff velocity, and the takeoff velocity sets the height. We take those three links in turn.

Proposition 6.1 (height from takeoff velocity). A body leaving the ground vertically at speed $v_0$ rises to $$\boxed{\;h=\frac{v_0^{2}}{2g}\;}\tag{6.1}$$ above its takeoff height.
In flight the only force is gravity, so mechanical energy is conserved (a special case of the ballistic flight of section 4). Between takeoff, where the speed is $v_0$ and the height is $0$, and the apex, where the vertical speed is $0$ and the height is $h$, energy conservation reads $\tfrac12 m v_0^{2}=mgh$. Cancelling $m$ and solving gives $h=v_0^{2}/2g$. Equivalently, it is the apex height $v_y^2/2g$ of (4.1) with the whole takeoff velocity vertical. ∎

So height is quadratic in takeoff speed - a $10\%$ faster launch clears $21\%$ more height. Everything therefore turns on making $v_0$ large, and the impulse-momentum theorem already says how: because a jump starts from rest, $v_0$ is the net vertical impulse per unit mass - with no downward momentum to reverse, the factor is simply $1/m$, not the $1/2m$ of the running stance that had to turn a descent around. There are two equivalent readings of that impulse, one over time and one over the push-off distance, and each names a way to jump higher.

Proposition 6.2 (takeoff velocity from the push-off). Let the push-off apply a vertical ground force $F(t)$ while the centre of mass travels the push-off distance $d$ from the crouch to full extension. Then $$\boxed{\;v_0=\frac{1}{m}\int_0^{t_{\rm p}}\!\big(F-mg\big)\,dt=\sqrt{\frac{2}{m}\int_0^{d}\!\big(F-mg\big)\,dx}\;}\tag{6.2}$$ where $t_{\rm p}$ is the push-off duration: the takeoff speed is the net impulse per unit mass, and equivalently is set by the net work done over the push-off distance.
The impulse form is (5.2) applied from rest ($v=0$ at the start of the push-off) to takeoff: $\int_0^{t_{\rm p}}(F-mg)\,dt=m v_0-0$. For the work form, the work-energy theorem over the push-off says the net work equals the kinetic energy gained, $\int_0^{d}(F-mg)\,dx=\tfrac12 m v_0^{2}-0$; solving for $v_0$ gives the square-root expression. Both compute the same $v_0$ because both integrate the same net force, one against time and one against displacement. ∎

The two forms are two coaching cues. The impulse form says press for longer, or press harder, to add area under $F$. The work form says push through a longer extension $d$, or with a larger net force, to add net work. A representative push-off - a mean net force near $515\ \mathrm N$ over $d\approx0.40\ \mathrm m$ for a $70\ \mathrm{kg}$ jumper - launches at $v_0\approx2.4\ \mathrm{m/s}$ and clears about $30\ \mathrm{cm}$ (Fig. 7, left).

This is also where two jumps that reach different heights part ways. A squat jump (SJ) begins from a held, motionless squat and pushes straight up. A countermovement jump (CMJ) begins from standing, dips down, and reverses into the same push-off. The CMJ reliably wins - by a few centimetres, here $34$ against $30\ \mathrm{cm}$ (Fig. 7, right). By (6.1)-(6.2) the extra height can come only from a larger takeoff impulse, and the force-time curves show where it comes from: the CMJ is front-loaded. Because the muscles were already developing force during the downward countermovement, they enter the upward push-off near full force, whereas the squat jumper must build force from a standstill while already rising. The CMJ's early force adds area under the curve - impulse - for the same push-off distance.

Naming that front-loading precisely - how a pre-stretch lets a muscle-tendon unit start the push-off with high force and stored elastic energy - is the stretch-shortening cycle, and it is the subject of section 7. The mirror of this section is section 8: a jump converts a push-off impulse into upward velocity, and a landing must convert downward velocity back into an impulse - the same equation, run the other way.

7. Stretch-shortening cycle and tendon recoil

Two threads now converge on a single piece of tissue. Section 2 lumped the whole leg into one spring $k_{\rm leg}$ but left its identity open; Section 6 found that a countermovement jump wins by entering the push-off with force already high. Both point to the muscle-tendon unit: a contractile muscle in series with an elastic tendon - the nonlinear spring built in Module 6. This section repays that module's central promise, that tendon elasticity is what makes running economical.

Definition 3 (muscle-tendon unit and resilience). The muscle-tendon unit (MTU) is a contractile muscle in series with a tendon that behaves as an elastic spring. Stretching the tendon stores elastic energy $U$; releasing it returns a fraction $R$, the tendon's resilience $R=U_{\rm return}/U_{\rm store}$, and dissipates the remainder $(1-R)U$ as heat. For tendon in its running operating band, $R\approx0.90$-$0.95$ (Module 6). Most of the whole-leg spring $k_{\rm leg}$ of section 2 is this tendon, not the muscle.

At a hard running push-off the Achilles stretches to about $16\ \mathrm{mm}$ under roughly $5\ \mathrm{kN}$, storing $U\approx31\ \mathrm J$ (Fig. 8, right, and the energy budget of Module 6). With $R\approx0.92$ it returns about $28.5\ \mathrm J$ and loses only $\sim2.5\ \mathrm J$ to the enclosed hysteresis loop. That small loss is the whole story of running economy, because of a simple energy balance over a steady stride.

Proposition 7.1 (muscle work in a steady bounce). If the leg-spring stores and returns $U$ of elastic energy each step with resilience $R$, then to sustain a steady bounce - one that returns to the same state each stride - the muscle must supply net work per step $$\boxed{\;W_{\rm musc}=(1-R)\,U\;}\tag{7.1}$$ exactly the tendon's dissipative loss.
Take one full stretch-recoil cycle of the leg-spring. Because the bounce is steady, the leg returns to the same length and speed it began with, so its kinetic and potential energy are unchanged over the cycle: $\Delta(\mathrm{KE}+\mathrm{PE})=0$. Over that cycle the tendon dissipates $(1-R)U$ as heat (Definition 3). Energy conservation for the closed cycle then reads $W_{\rm musc}+W_{\rm grav}^{\text{net}} = \Delta(\mathrm{KE}) + \text{heat}$; with the gravitational term integrating to zero over the closed path and $\Delta\mathrm{KE}=0$, the muscle's net work must equal the heat, $W_{\rm musc}=(1-R)U$. Anything less and the bounce would decay; anything more and it would grow. ∎

Contrast this with a leg that had no elastic tendon - a rigid strut with the muscle doing all the work. Each step that muscle would absorb the bounce energy $U$ on landing (negative work) and then regenerate it at push-off (positive work): about $2U\approx62\ \mathrm J$ of muscle mechanical work per step, and because both negative and positive muscle work carry a metabolic price, all of it costs. The elastic tendon collapses that to the muscle's share of the loss - the $(1-R)U\approx2.5\ \mathrm J$ of Proposition 7.1 - a factor $2/(1-R)\approx25$ less mechanical work (Fig. 9). Two cautions keep this honest. The rigid figure $2U$ is the muscle's total (eccentric plus concentric) work, and the elastic $ (1-R)U$ is both its net and its total, since there the muscle does no cancelling work - it only supplies the loss; the two bars in Fig. 9 therefore compare like with like. And the ratio is a mechanical-work ratio: the metabolic saving is smaller than $25\times$, because a muscle also spends energy holding force even when it does little work.

Equation (7.1) is why the resilience number mattered. A tendon returning $92\%$ of what it stores lets the muscle sit back and pay only the $8\%$; the spring, not the muscle, does the mechanical work of running. This is the Module 6 running-economy IOU discharged in full: there the tendon was a spring with a measured resilience; here that spring carries the bounce.

Storage and return happen in a definite order, and naming that order names the mechanism behind the countermovement jump. The stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) is an active muscle being stretched (the eccentric phase - lengthening under load, as the leg-spring compresses on landing) immediately followed by shortening (the concentric phase - the recoil that drives push-off). Three effects make the shortening that follows a stretch more forceful than shortening from rest:

  • Elastic recoil. The tendon stretched during the eccentric phase returns its stored energy during the concentric phase - the $R\,U$ of (7.1).
  • Force potentiation from the force-velocity relation. A muscle stretched under load is already at high force when shortening begins, whereas a muscle shortening from rest must first build force against its own force-velocity limit. This is exactly the CMJ's front-loaded force of section 6.
  • Reflex contribution. The rapid stretch triggers a short-latency stretch reflex that adds activation just as shortening begins.

Together these are why the countermovement jump beats the squat jump and why the running leg spends so little muscular energy per step. The stretch half of the cycle - loading the spring on landing - is also where the danger lives: the same eccentric phase that stores useful energy is where large forces are absorbed, and absorbing them is the subject of section 8.

8. Landing mechanics and force absorption

A jump (section 6) turns a push-off impulse into upward velocity. A landing is the same transaction reversed: the body arrives with a downward velocity $v_{\rm land}$ and the ground must remove all of that momentum. The impulse-momentum theorem (5.1) fixes the total impulse required - it is just $m v_{\rm land}$ - but it leaves one thing free, and that freedom is the whole of landing safety: the same impulse can be delivered as a large force over a short stop or a small force over a long one.

Proposition 8.1 (landing force and the stopping distance). A body arriving at speed $v_{\rm land}$ and brought to rest over a stopping distance $d$ (the distance the centre of mass travels while decelerating) feels an average vertical force $$\boxed{\;\bar F=\frac{\tfrac12 m v_{\rm land}^{2}}{d}+mg\;=\;\frac{m v_{\rm land}}{\Delta t}+mg\;}\tag{8.1}$$ where $\Delta t$ is the stopping time; the force is inversely proportional to how far, or how long, the stop is spread.
Over the stop the ground does work against the incoming kinetic energy. By the work-energy theorem, the net upward force times the stopping distance equals the kinetic energy removed: $(\bar F-mg)\,d=\tfrac12 m v_{\rm land}^{2}$, since gravity still acts downward through the stop; solving gives the first form. For the second, the impulse-momentum theorem (5.1) over the stop reads $(\bar F-mg)\,\Delta t = m v_{\rm land}$ - the net upward impulse removes the downward momentum - giving $\bar F = m v_{\rm land}/\Delta t + mg$. Strictly the first form is a distance-average of the force and the second a time-average; the two coincide - the single $\bar F$ of the box - when the deceleration is uniform, for then $d=\tfrac12 v_{\rm land}\,\Delta t$. Either way the average force falls as the stop is lengthened. ∎

The $1/d$ dependence is the entire safety margin of a landing. Dropping from half a metre gives $v_{\rm land}=\sqrt{2g(0.5)}\approx3.1\ \mathrm{m/s}$; stopping that in a stiff, straight-legged $5\ \mathrm{cm}$ demands an average force of about $11$ body weights, while bending the knees to stop over $40\ \mathrm{cm}$ - eight times the distance - cuts the force to about $2$ (Fig. 10). Nothing about the drop changed; only the distance over which it was absorbed.

Where does the absorbed energy go? Into the leg, as negative work. The muscles that resist the collapse lengthen while under tension - eccentric muscle action, the stretch half of the stretch-shortening cycle of section 7 - and the tendons stretch. In a running stride this stored energy is useful, returned at the next push-off; in a hard one-off landing much of it must simply be dissipated, and the muscles do that by absorbing it as heat. A soft landing is not only lower-force but gives the eccentric musculature the range of motion it needs to absorb the energy gradually.

Proposition 8.1 is a statement about the average force. Real landings also carry a brief, much higher spike at first contact - before the knee and ankle have begun to give - and that spike, not the average, is what most threatens the tissues. Resolving it, and turning force into injury, is the last step of the mechanics: section 9.

9. Impact loading and injury risk

Proposition 8.1 gave the average force of a landing, but tissues do not fail on averages. At the instant of contact, before the knee and ankle have begun to give, the foot and lower leg are stopped almost rigidly, and the force rises in a sharp early spike - the impact transient - that the smooth SLIP force of section 3 never showed. It never showed it because the SLIP leg was massless: it had no lower-leg mass to collide with the ground. This is the collision that idealisation deferred, and the dynamic impact wave that Module 2 left for later. Restoring it is the last piece of the mechanics.

Model that first contact as a small effective mass $m_{\rm imp}$ - the foot and lower leg - striking a contact of stiffness $k_{\rm c}$ (the combined stiffness of shoe, heel pad, and surface) at the vertical closing speed $v_{\rm imp}$ - the foot's downward speed at first contact, distinct from the forward running speed $v$ of section 1. Until the joints yield, this is a mass on a spring, and its early motion is one quarter of a simple oscillation.

Proposition 9.1 (impact peak and loading rate). An effective mass $m_{\rm imp}$ meeting a contact of stiffness $k_{\rm c}$ at closing speed $v_{\rm imp}$ produces an impact-force peak and a mean loading rate (rise in force per unit time) $$\boxed{\;F_{\rm peak}=v_{\rm imp}\sqrt{k_{\rm c}\,m_{\rm imp}}\,,\qquad \frac{\Delta F}{\Delta t}\approx\frac{2}{\pi}\,v_{\rm imp}\,k_{\rm c}\;}\tag{9.1}$$ so a softer contact lowers the peak as $\sqrt{k_{\rm c}}$ and the loading rate as $k_{\rm c}$.
While the joints are still rigid the mass $m_{\rm imp}$ on the contact spring obeys simple harmonic motion, $x(t)=(v_{\rm imp}/\omega)\sin\omega t$ with $\omega=\sqrt{k_{\rm c}/m_{\rm imp}}$, starting at $x=0$ with speed $v_{\rm imp}$. The spring force is $F=k_{\rm c}x=v_{\rm imp}\sqrt{k_{\rm c}m_{\rm imp}}\,\sin\omega t$, which peaks at $F_{\rm peak}=v_{\rm imp}\sqrt{k_{\rm c}m_{\rm imp}}$ a quarter-cycle later, at the rise time $\Delta t=\tfrac{\pi}{2}/\omega=\tfrac{\pi}{2}\sqrt{m_{\rm imp}/k_{\rm c}}$. The mean loading rate is $F_{\rm peak}/\Delta t=v_{\rm imp}\sqrt{k_{\rm c}m_{\rm imp}}\big/\big(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\sqrt{m_{\rm imp}/k_{\rm c}}\big)=\tfrac{2}{\pi}v_{\rm imp}\,k_{\rm c}$, in which the mass cancels. The peak scales as $\sqrt{k_{\rm c}}$ and the loading rate as $k_{\rm c}$. ∎

The numbers make the case for a soft contact. A stiff surface ($k_{\rm c}\approx200\ \mathrm{kN/m}$, barefoot on concrete) gives an impact peak near $1.8$ body weights delivered in about $10\ \mathrm{ms}$ - a loading rate of order $185$ body weights per second - while a cushioned shoe or grass ($k_{\rm c}\approx40\ \mathrm{kN/m}$) spreads the same landing over $22\ \mathrm{ms}$ at $0.8$ body weights, a loading rate near $37$: about five times gentler (Fig. 11, left). And the rate, not just the peak, is what tissue feels, because bone is strain-rate sensitive - stiffer and more brittle when loaded fast (the dynamic response Module 2 deferred).

How force becomes injury depends on whether one load, or many, does the damage - and the two cases are governed by different mechanics (Fig. 11, right).

  • Single overload. A load that in one event exceeds the tissue's tolerance breaks it at once: an anterior cruciate ligament ruptured in a single bad landing or cutting step. Here the relevant quantity is the peak load against a fixed strength.
  • Cumulative dose. Loads well below tolerance still accumulate microdamage over many cycles, following a fatigue law in which the number of cycles to failure falls steeply as the per-step load rises. A bone stress fracture or an Achilles tendinopathy is this: not one bad step but a dose - load per step multiplied by many thousands of steps - outrunning the tissue's repair.

This is why "impact" alone is the wrong villain. Running is not injurious because forces are large; forces of a few body weights are what bone and tendon are built for. Injury is a mismatch - loading rate and cumulative dose against tissue tolerance and its rate of repair - and each term is a lever: a softer surface or forefoot strike lowers the rate, a gradual training progression keeps the dose under the fatigue limit, and stronger tissue raises tolerance. Turning these mechanical quantities into measurements (force plates, loading-rate estimates) is the province of Module 15, and how tolerance and repair change with age and tissue health belongs to Module 14. The mechanics of running and jumping, from the bounce to the break, is now complete; what remains is to compute with it.

Lab 1 - SLIP stance and the ground reaction force

Integrate the spring-loaded inverted pendulum through one stance (section 2) and read off the vertical ground reaction (section 3). Sweeping the leg stiffness $k_{\rm leg}$ shows the peak force climbing and the contact time shortening together - the scaling the keyresult of section 3 predicted.

import numpy as np


def slip_stance(m, g, L0, k, alpha_deg, vx0, vy0, dt=1e-4):
    """One SLIP stance, touchdown to takeoff. Foot at the origin;
    the leg spring pushes the point mass. Returns time, vertical GRF."""
    a = np.radians(alpha_deg)
    x, y, vx, vy = -L0*np.cos(a), L0*np.sin(a), vx0, vy0
    t, T, Fz = 0.0, [], []
    while True:
        r = np.hypot(x, y)
        F = k*(L0 - r)                 # positive in compression
        ax, ay = F*x/r/m, F*y/r/m - g
        T.append(t)
        Fz.append(F*y/r)               # vertical ground reaction
        vx, vy = vx + ax*dt, vy + ay*dt
        x, y = x + vx*dt, y + vy*dt    # symplectic Euler
        t += dt
        if np.hypot(x, y) >= L0 and t > 5*dt:
            break
        if y <= 0 or t > 2.0:
            break
    return np.array(T), np.array(Fz)


m, g, L0 = 70.0, 9.81, 1.0
print("leg stiffness sweep (jog touchdown, alpha=70 deg):")
for k in (11000, 15000, 20000, 26000):
    T, Fz = slip_stance(m, g, L0, k, 70, 4.0, -0.7)
    print(f"  k = {k/1000:2.0f} kN/m -> peak {Fz.max()/(m*g):.2f} BW,"
          f" contact {T[-1]*1000:.0f} ms")

Output: peak $2.1\to3.6$ body weights and contact $211\to150\ \mathrm{ms}$ as $k$ runs $11\to26\ \mathrm{kN/m}$. Try: sweep the angle of attack $\alpha_0$ instead, or record the horizontal GRF and confirm its braking and propulsive lobes cancel.

Lab 2 - Vertical jump: countermovement versus squat

A jump height is set by the takeoff impulse (section 6). Give each jump a concentric force-time profile at the same peak force, integrate its net impulse to a takeoff velocity, and get the height from $h=v_0^2/2g$. Because the countermovement profile is front-loaded - the same peak reached earlier - it carries more impulse over the push-off, so the shape itself produces a higher jump.

import numpy as np

m, g, Tp, Fpk = 70.0, 9.81, 0.30, 2.5   # duration (s), peak force (BW)
t = np.linspace(0, Tp, 400)


def height(force_bw):
    """Jump height from a concentric vertical-force profile (in BW):
    net impulse -> takeoff speed -> ballistic height h = v0^2/2g."""
    v0 = np.trapezoid((force_bw - 1.0)*m*g, t)/m
    return v0*v0/(2*g)


def profile(front):
    """Same-peak force shape; front < 1 shifts force earlier."""
    return 1.0 + (Fpk - 1.0)*np.sin(np.pi*(t/Tp)**front)**1.55


print(f"squat jump (symmetric)   h = {height(profile(1.0))*100:.0f} cm")
print(f"countermovement (front)  h = {height(profile(0.8))*100:.0f} cm")
print("front-loading sweep at fixed peak force:")
for front in (1.0, 0.9, 0.8, 0.7):
    print(f"  front = {front:.1f} -> h = {height(profile(front))*100:.1f} cm")

Output: at a fixed $2.5$-body-weight peak, the symmetric push-off clears $30\ \mathrm{cm}$ and the front-loaded one $32\ \mathrm{cm}$, and the sweep shows height rising monotonically with front-loading ($30.0\to32.4\ \mathrm{cm}$) - the impulse gain of the stretch-shortening cycle (section 7) made explicit. Conceptual ↑ contents

C1. Running and walking differ in one kinematic fact that reorganises everything else. What is it, and how does the duty factor capture it? Probes: section 1; flight vs double support.
Solution
Walking always keeps at least one foot down, and briefly two (double support); running inserts a flight phase in which neither foot touches. The duty factor $\beta=t_{\rm c}/[2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})]$ is the fraction of the stride a foot is on the ground: walking has $\beta\gt\tfrac12$, running $\beta\lt\tfrac12$, and the boundary $\beta=\tfrac12$ is exactly $t_{\rm f}=0$. Flight is what makes the leg a spring rather than a strut.
C2. In running the centre of mass is lowest at midstance; in walking it is highest. Explain both. Probes: section 0, section 3; bounce vs vault.
Solution
Running loads the leg like a spring: the spring is most compressed at midstance, so the mass sinks to its lowest point there and rebounds. Walking vaults over a nearly rigid leg like an inverted pendulum, so the mass rides highest as it passes over the foot. The two gaits are a bounce and a vault, and the centre-of-mass path inverts between them.
C3. A force plate shows one hump under a runner and two under a walker. Account for the difference with a single equation. Probes: section 3.
Solution
Newton's vertical law gives $F_z=m(g+\ddot y)$, so the force exceeds body weight exactly when the centre of mass accelerates upward. In running the mass is lowest at midstance and is being turned from falling to rising, $\ddot y\gt0$, so the force makes one central peak. In walking the mass is highest at midstance and accelerates downward, $\ddot y\lt0$, so the force dips below body weight, leaving the two humps of loading and push-off.
C4. The SLIP leg has one stiffness $k_{\rm leg}$. A student says "so $k_{\rm leg}$ is the Achilles tendon stiffness." Correct them. Probes: section 2; effective stiffness.
Solution
$k_{\rm leg}$ is an effective whole-limb stiffness - the single number that best summarises how hard the entire leg pushes back as it shortens. Tendon elasticity is one contributor, but so are the active muscles holding the joints, the geometry of knee and ankle, and the heel pad. The Achilles is a large part of it, but treating $k_{\rm leg}$ as a tissue constant would be a mistake.
C5. Why can a body not simply walk faster and faster? Give the kinematic ceiling. Probes: section 4; the Froude number.
Solution
Walking vaults the centre of mass on a circular arc of radius the leg length, which needs a centripetal force $mv^2/L_0$ pointing toward the foot - downward at midstance. Only gravity and the (upward, push-only) ground reaction are available, and $F_z=mg(1-\mathrm{Fr})\ge0$ forces $\mathrm{Fr}=v^2/gL_0\le1$. At $\mathrm{Fr}=1$ the ground force vanishes and the body leaves the arc: it must run. (Humans switch earlier, near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx\tfrac12$, for energetic reasons.)
C6. A countermovement jump reliably beats a squat jump from the same depth. By the impulse-momentum theorem the extra height needs extra impulse - where does it come from? Probes: section 6, section 7.
Solution
The countermovement pre-loads the muscle-tendon unit during the downward dip, so the upward push-off begins with force already high, whereas the squat jumper must build force from a standstill against its own force-velocity limit. That front-loaded force adds area under the force-time curve - impulse - for the same push-off distance. Stored elastic energy and a stretch reflex add to it; together they are the stretch-shortening cycle.
C7. Running is metabolically cheap. Where does most of the mechanical work of each step actually come from? Probes: section 7; tendon recoil.
Solution
From the tendon, not the muscle. Each step the leg-spring stores about $U$ of elastic energy on landing and returns a resilience $R\approx0.92$ of it at push-off; the muscle need supply only the small $(1-R)U$ loss. A rigid leg would force the muscle to absorb $U$ then regenerate $U$, roughly $2U$ of costly work, so the elastic tendon cuts the muscle's mechanical work about $2/(1-R)\approx25$-fold.
C8. Two people drop from the same height; one lands stiff-legged, one bends deeply. Why is the second far safer, and how does the peak force scale with the give? Probes: section 8.
Solution
Both must remove the same downward momentum, so the same impulse; but the peak force is $\bar F\approx\tfrac12 m v_{\rm land}^2/d + mg$, inversely proportional to the stopping distance $d$. Bending the knees to stop over $40\ \mathrm{cm}$ instead of a stiff $5\ \mathrm{cm}$ - eight times the distance - cuts the force from about $11$ body weights to about $2$. Nothing about the drop changed; only the distance over which it was absorbed.
C9. Two footstrikes reach the same peak force, but one is on concrete and one on a cushioned track. Why is the concrete one more dangerous? Probes: section 9; loading rate.
Solution
What tissue feels is not only the peak but the loading rate $dF/dt$ - how fast the force rises. For a mass meeting a contact of stiffness $k_{\rm c}$, the rate scales as $k_{\rm c}$, so a stiff surface loads the tissue several times faster than a soft one. Bone is strain-rate sensitive, stiffer and more brittle when loaded quickly, so the steep rise of the concrete strike is the greater threat even at equal peak.
C10. An ACL rupture and a metatarsal stress fracture are both running injuries, yet they fail by different mechanics. Distinguish them. Probes: section 9; overload vs dose.
Solution
An ACL rupture is a single overload: one load - a bad landing or cut - exceeds the ligament's tolerance and it fails at once; the relevant quantity is the peak against a fixed strength. A stress fracture is a cumulative dose: loads well below tolerance accumulate microdamage over many cycles along a fatigue law (cycles-to-failure falling steeply with load), so it is load-per-step times many thousands of steps outrunning repair, not one bad step.

Derivational ↑ contents

D1. Show that a running gait - one with a flight phase - is exactly the condition $\beta\lt\tfrac12$. Probes: section 1.
Solution
With stride period $T=2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$, the duty factor is $\beta=t_{\rm c}/T=t_{\rm c}/[2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})]$. Then $\beta\lt\tfrac12\iff t_{\rm c}\lt t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f}\iff t_{\rm f}\gt0$, multiplying through by the positive $2(t_{\rm c}+t_{\rm f})$. So $\beta\lt\tfrac12$ holds precisely when a flight phase exists, with equality at $t_{\rm f}=0$.
D2. Derive the SLIP stance equations of motion in polar coordinates about the foot. Probes: section 2.
Solution
With the foot at the origin, the mass at $(r,\varphi)$ has radial and tangential accelerations $\ddot r-r\dot\varphi^2$ and $r\ddot\varphi+2\dot r\dot\varphi$. The spring acts radially, contributing $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)$; gravity $(0,-mg)$ projects to $-mg\sin\varphi$ radially and $-mg\cos\varphi$ tangentially. Newton's law along each direction gives $m(\ddot r-r\dot\varphi^2)=k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)-mg\sin\varphi$ and $m(r\ddot\varphi+2\dot r\dot\varphi)=-mg\cos\varphi$.
D3. Prove that the SLIP total mechanical energy is conserved through stance and passes unbroken into flight. Probes: section 2.
Solution
Differentiate $E=\tfrac12 m(\dot r^2+r^2\dot\varphi^2)+mgr\sin\varphi+\tfrac12 k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)^2$ and collect the $\dot r$ and $\dot\varphi$ terms; substituting the two stance equations makes every term cancel, so $\dot E=0$. At touchdown and takeoff $r=L_0$, so the spring term vanishes and $E$ equals the flight energy $\tfrac12 m(\dot x^2+\dot y^2)+mgy$, which gravity alone also conserves - so energy crosses the phase change without a jump.
D4. Derive the vertical ground reaction force in two equivalent forms. Probes: section 3.
Solution
The spring force $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)$ acts along the leg $(\cos\varphi,\sin\varphi)$, so its vertical part is $k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\sin\varphi$; the massless leg transmits it to the foot, giving $F_z=k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\sin\varphi$. Independently, Newton's vertical law $m\ddot y=F_z-mg$ gives $F_z=m(g+\ddot y)$. The two agree because differentiating $y=r\sin\varphi$ and using the stance equations reproduces $\ddot y=k_{\rm leg}(L_0-r)\sin\varphi/m-g$.
D5. Derive the midstance ground reaction of a walking vault and the resulting Froude ceiling. Probes: section 4.
Solution
At midstance the centre of mass moves horizontally at speed $v$ on a circle of radius $L_0$, so its acceleration is $v^2/L_0$ toward the foot (downward). Newton vertical: $m(-v^2/L_0)=F_z-mg$, hence $F_z=mg-mv^2/L_0=mg(1-\mathrm{Fr})$ with $\mathrm{Fr}=v^2/gL_0$. Since a foot only pushes, $F_z\ge0\iff\mathrm{Fr}\le1$; at $\mathrm{Fr}=1$ the force vanishes and the vault fails.
D6. From the ballistic law derive the flight time, apex height, and horizontal range. Probes: section 4.
Solution
Integrating $\ddot x=0,\ \ddot y=-g$ from takeoff $(v_x,v_y)$ gives $x=v_xt$, $y=v_yt-\tfrac12gt^2$. Height returns to takeoff level at $t_{\rm f}=2v_y/g$. The apex is at $\dot y=0$, $t=v_y/g$, height $v_y^2/2g$. The range is $d_{\rm f}=v_xt_{\rm f}=2v_xv_y/g$.
D7. Derive the split of the vertical stance impulse into weight support and momentum reversal. Probes: section 5.
Solution
Let $v(t)$ be the vertical velocity; the net impulse is $\int_0^{t_{\rm c}}(F_z-mg)\,dt=m(v(t_{\rm c})-v(0))$. In a symmetric stance the runner arrives at $v(0)=-v_y$ and leaves at $v(t_{\rm c})=+v_y$, so this equals $2m v_y$. Moving the weight term across, $\int F_z\,dt=mg\,t_{\rm c}+2m v_y$: the impulse both holds the body up and reverses its vertical momentum.
D8. Derive the vertical jump height and the takeoff velocity two independent ways. Probes: section 6.
Solution
In flight energy is conserved, so between takeoff (speed $v_0$, height $0$) and apex (speed $0$, height $h$), $\tfrac12 m v_0^2=mgh$, giving $h=v_0^2/2g$. For $v_0$: impulse-momentum from rest gives $v_0=\tfrac1m\int_0^{t_{\rm p}}(F-mg)\,dt$; the work-energy theorem over the push-off distance $d$ gives $\tfrac12 m v_0^2=\int_0^d(F-mg)\,dx$, i.e. $v_0=\sqrt{(2/m)\int_0^d(F-mg)\,dx}$. Both integrate the same net force, over time and over distance.
D9. Prove that to sustain a steady bounce the muscle's net work per step equals the tendon's dissipative loss. Probes: section 7.
Solution
Over one closed stretch-recoil cycle the leg returns to its initial length and speed, so $\Delta(\mathrm{KE}+\mathrm{PE})=0$ and the gravitational work integrates to zero over the closed path. The tendon dissipates $(1-R)U$ as heat. Energy conservation for the cycle then forces the muscle's net work to equal the heat: $W_{\rm musc}=(1-R)U$. Less would let the bounce decay; more would let it grow.
D10. Derive the average landing force over a stopping distance, and the peak of the impact transient. Probes: section 8, section 9.
Solution
Work-energy over the stop: $(\bar F-mg)d=\tfrac12 m v_{\rm land}^2$, so $\bar F=\tfrac12 m v_{\rm land}^2/d+mg$. For the transient, the foot mass $m_{\rm imp}$ on the contact spring $k_{\rm c}$ does simple harmonic motion $x=(v_{\rm imp}/\omega)\sin\omega t$, $\omega=\sqrt{k_{\rm c}/m_{\rm imp}}$; the force $k_{\rm c}x$ peaks at $F_{\rm peak}=v_{\rm imp}\sqrt{k_{\rm c}m_{\rm imp}}$ a quarter-cycle in, so the loading rate is $\tfrac{2}{\pi}v_{\rm imp}k_{\rm c}$.

K1. Integrate the SLIP stance and sweep the leg stiffness $k_{\rm leg}$ at a fixed touchdown state. Find the stiffness that maximises flight time, and explain why an interior optimum exists rather than "stiffer is always better." Probes: Lab 1; simulation + optimization.
Solution
Integrate stance to takeoff, read the vertical takeoff velocity, and get $t_{\rm f}=2v_y/g$; scan $k_{\rm leg}$. Flight time peaks at an intermediate stiffness: too soft a leg over-compresses and spends the stance redirecting rather than rebounding, while too stiff a leg barely compresses and stores little elastic energy to launch with. The optimum balances storage against redirection - a fact invisible in any single closed-form line.
K2. For a fixed system energy, treat the angle of attack $\alpha_0$ as unknown and find, by root-finding, the value that yields a symmetric periodic stride (takeoff mirroring touchdown). Probes: Lab 1; inverse / root problem.
Solution
Define the stride asymmetry as the difference between the takeoff and touchdown leg angles (or vertical speeds) after one stance; it changes sign as $\alpha_0$ varies. Bisect or Newton-iterate to the root where the asymmetry vanishes - the fixed point of the stance map, a genuine periodic gait. This recovers a control input ($\alpha_0$) from a desired output (periodicity), which no forward formula gives.
K3. Given a measured vertical GRF trace $F(t)$ over one push-off, recover the takeoff velocity and jump height by numerical integration, then propagate a $\pm5\%$ uncertainty in body mass into the height estimate. Probes: Lab 2; inverse + sensitivity.
Solution
Trapezoidally integrate the net impulse $\int(F-mg)\,dt$, divide by $m$ for $v_0$, then $h=v_0^2/2g$. For sensitivity, at a fixed measured force trace $mg$ scales with $m$, so the net impulse and $v_0$ shift with mass; because $v_0=(\int F\,dt)/m-g\,t_{\rm p}$ carries the mass in the denominator and $h\propto v_0^2$, the height error is amplified and opposite in sign - $\tfrac{\delta h/h}{\delta m/m}=-2\big(1+g\,t_{\rm p}/v_0\big)\approx-4$ - so a $+5\%$ mass error shifts the estimated height by roughly $-20\%$. The exercise is measurement inversion plus error propagation, not evaluation.
K4. Subject to a fixed peak force and push-off distance, optimise the shape of the push-off force profile to maximise jump height. Probes: Lab 2; constrained optimization.
Solution
Parametrise the profile (for example by how front-loaded it is), compute the height from each profile's impulse, and search for the maximum under the peak-force constraint. Height rises with front-loading because reaching the peak earlier fills more area under the force-time curve at the same ceiling - but real muscle cannot rise infinitely fast, so the useful optimum is set by how quickly force can be developed. This surfaces the impulse-shape trade-off behind the stretch-shortening cycle.
K5. Compare countermovement and squat jumps across a range of front-loading at fixed peak force, and quantify the impulse gain and its height payoff. Probes: Lab 2; regime comparison.
Solution
Sweep the front-loading parameter, computing net impulse and height for each; plot both regimes. The front-loaded (countermovement) profile carries a larger impulse for the same peak, and because $h\propto v_0^2\propto(\text{impulse})^2$ the height gain is amplified relative to the impulse gain. The comparison isolates how much of the real countermovement advantage the impulse mechanism alone explains, leaving the rest to elastic and reflex contributions.
K6. For a range of drop heights, find the minimum stopping distance that keeps the peak landing force below a tissue-tolerance threshold. Probes: Lab 3; inverse / design.
Solution
Invert $\bar F=\tfrac12 m v_{\rm land}^2/d+mg\le F_{\rm tol}$ for $d$: $d\ge\tfrac12 m v_{\rm land}^2/(F_{\rm tol}-mg)$, with $v_{\rm land}=\sqrt{2gh}$. Tabulating over drop height $h$ shows the required give growing linearly with $h$ (through $v_{\rm land}^2$), so a landing that is safe from a low box demands a much deeper flex from a high one. This is a design inversion - solving for the give a safe landing needs.
K7. Sweep the tendon resilience $R$ and compute the muscle's mechanical work per step; find where the economy degrades fastest. Probes: Lab 4; sensitivity.
Solution
Compute $W_{\rm musc}=(1-R)U$ and the economy factor $2/(1-R)$ across $R$. The factor rises hyperbolically, and its sensitivity $d/dR[2/(1-R)]=2/(1-R)^2$ diverges as $R\to1$, so near the healthy $R\approx0.92$ a small loss of resilience already costs a large fractional rise in muscular work. This explains why a modest decline in tendon quality has an outsized metabolic penalty - a nonlinearity, not a number to plug in.
K8. Model the impact transient as a mass on a contact spring and sweep the contact stiffness; find the stiffness that keeps the loading rate below an injury threshold. Probes: section 9; inverse.
Solution
With loading rate $\approx\tfrac{2}{\pi}v_{\rm imp}k_{\rm c}$, invert for the largest admissible $k_{\rm c}=\tfrac{\pi}{2}(\text{rate}_{\rm tol})/v_{\rm imp}$, and check the resulting peak $v_{\rm imp}\sqrt{k_{\rm c}m_{\rm imp}}$. Because the rate scales linearly with $k_{\rm c}$ but the peak only as $\sqrt{k_{\rm c}}$, softening the contact buys a large reduction in loading rate for a smaller reduction in peak - quantifying why cushioning targets the rate.
K9. Using a fatigue law and a damage-accumulation rule, compute the number of loading cycles to a bone stress fracture at a given per-step stress, and find the weekly mileage that reaches the fatigue limit. Probes: section 9; simulation + inverse.
Solution
Take a fatigue curve $N(\sigma)$ (cycles to failure falling steeply with per-step stress $\sigma$) and accumulate damage by Miner's rule, $D=\sum n_i/N_i$; failure at $D=1$. For a per-step stress and a weekly step count, solve for the mileage at which accumulated cycles reach $N(\sigma)$ within the bone's repair window. The steepness of $N(\sigma)$ means a small stress increase (a stiffer stride, a harder surface) sharply shortens the safe mileage - a dose calculation, not a single evaluation.
K10. Build simple metabolic-cost models for walking and running as functions of speed, and locate the crossover speed at which running becomes cheaper. Probes: section 4; regime comparison.
Solution
Model walking cost as rising steeply with speed (pendular collision losses growing as the vault is pushed toward its $\mathrm{Fr}=1$ limit) and running cost as flatter (elastic return capping the per-distance work); plot both against speed. Their intersection is the crossover, and it falls near the observed transition at $\mathrm{Fr}\approx\tfrac12$ - well below the kinematic ceiling $\mathrm{Fr}=1$. The problem shows the transition as an energetic optimum emerging from two competing regimes, which no single formula delivers.

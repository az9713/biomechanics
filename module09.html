0. Motivation - why running is bouncing

Try to walk faster and faster. At some speed - a little over $2\ \mathrm{m/s}$ for most people - walking stops feeling natural and you break into a run. Something qualitative has changed. In walking you always have at least one foot on the ground; in running there are moments when both feet are off the ground and the whole body is airborne. Each footfall the leg visibly gives, then rebounds, and flings the body back into the air. Running feels like bouncing because, mechanically, it is.

Module 8 modelled walking as an inverted pendulum: the body vaults over a nearly rigid stance leg, so the centre of mass (the average position of the body's mass, abbreviated COM) rides highest as it passes over the foot at midstance, trading speed for height like a pole-vaulter. Running is the reverse. The leg behaves like a spring and the body like a mass bouncing on it: the leg compresses through the first half of stance and recoils through the second, so the COM sits lowest at midstance and highest at the top of the airborne flight phase. There is no double support (the brief overlap when both feet are on the ground) to smooth the ride - flight replaces it (Fig. 1).

This single change of picture - from a stiff pendulum to a spring - reorganises everything that follows. It sets the shape of the ground reaction force, fixes the speed at which walking must give way to running, and explains where the energy for economical running comes from. It also connects three promises left open earlier in the course. Module 6 built the Achilles tendon as a one-dimensional elastic spring and noted that its energy return powers efficient running; here that spring becomes the whole-leg spring of the bouncing model. Module 8 showed that pendular walking cannot exceed a Froude number near $\tfrac12$ and handed the faster regime forward; here we cross that boundary. Module 2 treated bone loads as static and deferred the dynamic impact wave; here landing forces make it unavoidable.

Two practical questions organise the module, the same two the course syllabus poses for running and jumping: what limits how high you can jump, and what makes a landing safe? Both have the same answer, and it is worth stating in advance because everything after Section 5 is a variation on it: a jump and a landing are the same event run in opposite directions, and both are governed by force acting over a distance or a time. You leave the ground with whatever velocity the push-off impulse gives you; you survive a landing by spreading the reverse impulse over as long a stop as you can. Height rewards a large impulse; safety rewards a slow one.

The arc of this module. We fix running-gait notation and the duty factor (section 1), build the spring-loaded inverted pendulum (section 2) and integrate its stance to get the ground reaction force (section 3), add the flight phase and locate the walk-to-run transition (section 4), state the impulse-momentum theorem (section 5) and use it for jump height (section 6), explain the stretch-shortening cycle and tendon recoil (section 7), turn the same impulse idea toward landing and force absorption (section 8), and close with impact loading and injury risk (section 9) before the computational labs and problem set (sections 10-11).

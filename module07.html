0. Motivation — standing still is not passive

Ask someone what their body is doing while they stand and wait for a bus, and they will say nothing. This is the most persistent illusion in all of human movement. Standing quietly is not the absence of activity; it is one of the most demanding continuous control problems the nervous system solves, and it never stops solving it for as long as you are on your feet.

Three everyday facts give the game away:

  • A person who faints — who loses consciousness for even a moment — crumples to the floor. If standing were passive, an unconscious body would stay standing. It does not.
  • Under general anaesthesia the body is completely limp and must be held upright by a table; a skeleton with its joints intact but its muscles removed will not stand either. The bones alone are a stack of blocks that falls at the first disturbance.
  • Even when you try to hold perfectly still, your body is never actually still. The point of the body's weight wanders continuously over a path a centimetre or two wide — the phenomenon of postural sway. We will prove (§6) that this restlessness is not sloppiness but the unavoidable signature of a feedback controller with a time lag.

The reason standing is hard is a matter of geometry. The body's centre of mass (COM — the single point at which the body's whole weight can be taken to act, defined precisely in §1) sits high: roughly at the level of the navel, about a metre above the ankle joints. The base of support — the region of ground bounded by the feet — is small and lies below that mass. A weight held above its support is in the worst possible mechanical situation.

The distinction drawn in Fig. 1 is the whole of the matter. Hang a mass below a pivot and you have an ordinary pendulum: displace it and gravity supplies a restoring torque that swings it back. This is a stable equilibrium — the system defends itself. Balance a mass above a pivot and every sign flips: the same lean produces a toppling torque that grows the further the mass falls, faster and faster, until the body hits the ground. This is an unstable equilibrium — the system, left alone, destroys itself. Upright standing is the second kind. Nothing in the passive skeleton wants to keep you up.

So how do we stand at all? By closing a loop. Sensors report the lean — muscle spindles in the calf sense stretch, the vestibular organs sense head tilt, the eyes see the horizon move. The nervous system reads these signals, computes a corrective ankle torque, and the calf muscles apply it, pushing the body back toward vertical before it can fall. This is feedback control, the same principle as a thermostat or an autopilot, running in your brainstem and spinal cord every waking second.

But there is a catch that makes the problem genuinely deep, and it is the mathematical heart of this module. Neural signals are not instantaneous. It takes time for the spindle's report to travel up the leg, for the nervous system to process it, and for the command to travel back down and for muscle to develop force — a round-trip delay of roughly 150 milliseconds. Controlling an unstable system through a delayed loop is a notoriously treacherous problem: push too hard and the delay turns your correction into an over-correction, and the body oscillates. We will derive exactly where that boundary lies, and find that quiet sway is the fingerprint of a controller operating close to it.

The arc of this module. We build the inverted-pendulum model of the standing body (§3), prove that its upright equilibrium is unstable (§4), close the loop with a proportional–derivative controller at the ankle and find the stiffness it takes to stand (§5), add the neural time delay and derive the stability boundary that explains sway (§6), extend to the hip strategy and co-contraction when the ankle alone cannot cope (§7), and finally read off the load-bearing cost at the spine of the postures the controller must hold (§8). Two computational labs (§9) and a thirty-problem set (§10) close it.

1. Base of support, centre of mass, and the static margin

To judge whether a body is about to fall you need exactly two facts: where its weight acts, and what region of ground can support it. The first is the centre of mass; the second is the base of support. This section defines both precisely, proves the condition that links them, and then shows why that condition — though necessary — is not the whole story once the body is moving.

The centre of mass

Definition (centre of mass). For a system of point masses $m_i$ at positions $\mathbf r_i$, the centre of mass (COM) is the mass-weighted mean position $$\mathbf r_{\text{com}}=\frac1M\sum_i m_i\,\mathbf r_i,\qquad M=\sum_i m_i,$$ and for a continuous body $\mathbf r_{\text{com}}=\tfrac1M\int \mathbf r\,dm$. It is the unique point at which the total weight $M\mathbf g$ may be taken to act while reproducing the true gravitational force and moment on the body.

The body is not a point mass, so its COM is found by the segmental method: treat each limb segment $i$ as a rigid body of mass $m_i$ whose own COM sits at a measured fraction of its length (from cadaver-derived anthropometric tables), then combine. Writing $\mathbf r_i$ for each segment's COM, $$\mathbf r_{\text{com}}=\frac1M\sum_i m_i\,\mathbf r_i .$$ Because the head, trunk, and arms together carry roughly half the body's mass and sit high, the whole-body COM lands high too: for an adult standing erect it lies about $55\%$ of stature above the floor — just in front of the second sacral vertebra (S2), roughly a metre above the ankles. It is not fixed in the body: raise your arms and it rises; bend forward and it can move outside the torso entirely. That mobility is exactly what the postural controller exploits.

The base of support

Definition (base of support). The base of support (BoS) is the convex hull of all points at which the body contacts the supporting surface — the smallest convex region containing every contact point. In quiet bipedal standing it is the area enclosing both footprints (each sole plus the ground trapped between the feet).

A single foot does not press uniformly. Its load passes through three skeletal pillars — the foot tripod: the calcaneus (heel) behind, and the first and fifth metatarsal heads (the balls under the big and little toes) in front. The medial longitudinal arch spans heel to forefoot as a compliant tie-beam (its spring mechanics were treated in Module 6, §8). The convex hull of the two tripods is a broad quadrilateral — a much larger, and crucially longer-in-front, base than a single foot provides (Fig. 2).

The static stability condition

Before defining the ground reaction in full (§2), we need only one fact about it: the ground can push up but never pull down, and only where a foot actually touches. Its vertical resultant therefore acts through a single equivalent point — the centre of pressure (COP) — that is a pressure-weighted average of the contact points and so must lie within the BoS. With that, the linking condition is a short statics proof.

Proposition 1.1 (the gravity line must fall within the base). A body in static equilibrium under gravity and ground reaction has the vertical projection of its centre of mass lying within its base of support: $x_{\text{com}}\in\text{BoS}$.
Work in the sagittal plane with horizontal coordinate $x$ and the ground at height $0$. The external forces are the weight $Mg$ acting downward at $(x_{\text{com}},h)$, where $h$ is the COM height, and the ground reaction, whose vertical resultant is $N$ acting upward at the centre of pressure $(x_{\text{cop}},0)$ together with a horizontal friction force $F$ at the same point. Vertical and horizontal force balance give $$N=Mg,\qquad F=0,$$ the second because in static equilibrium there is no horizontal acceleration and no external horizontal push — quiet standing pulls no friction. Taking moments about the ground origin (counter-clockwise positive), the only contributions are from $N$ and the weight (both $F$ and its moment arm vanish): $$\sum M = N\,x_{\text{cop}} - Mg\,x_{\text{com}} = Mg\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}}).$$ Static equilibrium requires $\sum M=0$, hence $$x_{\text{cop}}=x_{\text{com}}.$$ Now $x_{\text{cop}}=\big(\textstyle\int x\,p\,dA\big)\big/\big(\int p\,dA\big)$ with pressure $p\ge 0$ supported only on the contact region, so it is a convex combination of points of the BoS and therefore $x_{\text{cop}}\in\text{BoS}$. Combining, $x_{\text{com}}=x_{\text{cop}}\in\text{BoS}$. ∎

The proof also exposes the failure mode. Suppose the COM strays beyond the base — say forward of the toes, $x_{\text{com}}\gt x_{\text{toe}}$. The COP cannot follow it, because it is pinned to the base: the best it can do is sit at the forward edge, $x_{\text{cop}}=x_{\text{toe}}\lt x_{\text{com}}$. Then the residual moment $$\sum M = Mg\,(x_{\text{toe}}-x_{\text{com}})\lt 0$$ is a genuine toppling moment: the body pitches forward about the toes and, absent a step or a grab, falls. The gravity line leaving the base is not a warning sign of falling — it is falling.

Stability margin. The margin is the shortest distance from the gravity-line point to the edge of the base, $\;m_{\text{stab}}=\operatorname{dist}(x_{\text{com}},\partial\,\text{BoS})$. For a $1.75$ m adult with feet $\sim0.25$ m long, quiet standing parks the COM near mid-foot, leaving roughly $0.10$ m of margin toward the toes and $\sim0.05$ m toward the heels. The asymmetry — more room in front — is why a shove is met by rising onto the balls of the feet rather than rocking onto the heels.

Why "inside the base" is not enough

Proposition 1.1 is a statement about a body at rest. It says nothing about momentum, and momentum is decisive. A body whose COM sits safely over the base but is moving fast toward an edge will overshoot and fall; conversely a body momentarily leaning past the edge but travelling back can recover. The static margin mis-ranks both cases (Fig. 3).

The correct generalisation replaces the COM's position with the position it is heading for — the extrapolated centre of mass. Anticipating the inverted-pendulum dynamics of §3–§4, define the pendulum's natural rate $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$, where $\ell$ is the COM height above the ankle (for a point mass on a massless leg; the distributed-body value $\omega_0^2=Mg\ell/I$ appears in §4).

Dynamic stability condition (Hof; derived in §4). The body can be brought to rest without a step exactly when its extrapolated centre of mass lies within the base of support: $$x_{\text{xcom}} \;=\; x_{\text{com}} + \frac{v_{\text{com}}}{\omega_0}\;\in\;\text{BoS},\qquad \omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}.$$ The velocity term $v_{\text{com}}/\omega_0$ is the distance the COM would coast before an ideal maximal ankle response could arrest it.

Everything from here builds toward computing that motion. To predict $v_{\text{com}}$ and whether the controller can arrest it, we need the equation of motion of the standing body — and for that we first need the force the ground actually applies, and the point through which it acts. That is the centre of pressure, developed next (§2).

2. Ground reaction force and centre of pressure

Section 1 leaned on a single fact about the ground: that it pushes up, only where a foot touches, through a point inside the base. That is all statics needs — but the controller of standing works precisely by moving that point. To follow it we must describe the ground reaction in full: what force the surface applies, through which point it acts, and how an instrument recovers both from a person simply standing on it.

The ground reaction force

Definition (ground reaction force). The ground reaction force (GRF) is the resultant of the contact traction the support surface exerts on the body. Over the contact patch $A$, with compressive normal pressure $p\ge 0$ (acting along the surface normal $\hat{\mathbf n}$, upward) and tangential shear traction $\boldsymbol\tau$, $$\mathbf F_{\text{GRF}}=\int_A\big(p\,\hat{\mathbf n}+\boldsymbol\tau\big)\,dA .$$ Its vertical part is $F_z=\int_A p\,dA$; its horizontal part is the friction/shear $\mathbf F_{\text{shear}}=\int_A\boldsymbol\tau\,dA$. By Newton's third law $\mathbf F_{\text{GRF}}$ is equal and opposite to the force the body presses into the ground.

In quiet standing the vertical component carries essentially the whole body weight, $F_z\approx Mg$, while the shear is small — a few percent of body weight — and it is exactly those small horizontal tractions that accelerate the centre of mass and hold the sway in check. When the body accelerates vertically with $a_z$ (rising onto the toes, absorbing a landing) the plate reads $F_z=M(g+a_z)$; a force plate is, in this sense, a very accurate scale that also resolves where the weight bears.

The centre of pressure

Definition (centre of pressure). The centre of pressure (COP) is the pressure-weighted centroid of the contact patch, $$x_{\text{cop}}=\frac{\int_A x\,p\,dA}{\int_A p\,dA},\qquad y_{\text{cop}}=\frac{\int_A y\,p\,dA}{\int_A p\,dA}.$$ Because $p\ge0$, the COP is a convex combination of contact points and so always lies within the base of support.
Proposition 2.1 (the COP is the point of application of the vertical GRF). The distributed normal pressure is statically equivalent to its resultant $F_z$ acting at the single point $x_{\text{cop}}$: it reproduces both the total force and its moment about every point, and it is the only point that does so.
Take moments of the pressure field about the origin (sagittal plane, coordinate $x$). The distribution's moment is $$M_{\text{dist}}=\int_A x\,p\,dA=\Big(\underbrace{\textstyle\int_A p\,dA}_{=\,F_z}\Big)\,\frac{\int_A x\,p\,dA}{\int_A p\,dA}=F_z\,x_{\text{cop}},$$ which is exactly the moment of a single force $F_z$ placed at $x_{\text{cop}}$. The forces agree by construction ($\int p\,dA=F_z$), so the point force at $x_{\text{cop}}$ matches the distribution in both force and moment; equivalence about every other point then follows because two systems with equal resultant force and equal moment about one point have equal moment about all points. For uniqueness, suppose a point $x^\ast$ also reproduces the moment: $F_z x^\ast=M_{\text{dist}}=F_z x_{\text{cop}}$, and since $F_z\gt0$ we get $x^\ast=x_{\text{cop}}$. ∎

Measuring it: the force plate

A force plate is a rigid platform on load cells that report the full contact wrench at the plate origin $O$ (centred on the top surface): the three force components $(F_x,F_y,F_z)$ and the three moments $(M_x,M_y,M_z)$. The COP is not measured directly — it is computed from the moments, and the derivation is a one-line consequence of Proposition 2.1.

Proposition 2.2 (COP from plate moments). With moments taken about the surface origin, the centre of pressure is $$x_{\text{cop}}=-\frac{M_y}{F_z},\qquad y_{\text{cop}}=\frac{M_x}{F_z}.$$
The vertical pressure $p\,dA$ at position $(x,y,0)$ produces about $O$ the elementary moment $\mathbf r\times\mathbf f=(x,y,0)\times(0,0,p\,dA)=(y\,p\,dA,\,-x\,p\,dA,\,0)$. Integrating, $$M_x=\int_A y\,p\,dA=y_{\text{cop}}F_z,\qquad M_y=-\int_A x\,p\,dA=-x_{\text{cop}}F_z .$$ Solving gives $x_{\text{cop}}=-M_y/F_z$ and $y_{\text{cop}}=M_x/F_z$. ∎

Two practical caveats complete the instrument. First, the load cells sit a small depth $z_0$ below the top surface, so a horizontal shear $F_x$ contributes an extra moment $F_x z_0$; the corrected formula is $x_{\text{cop}}=-(M_y+F_x z_0)/F_z$. Second, the COP is meaningful only while $F_z\gt0$ — the formula divides by the vertical load, and a foot in the air presses on nothing.

Centre of pressure is not centre of mass

It is tempting to picture the COP as "where the body's weight is," i.e. the COM's shadow. In perfect statics that is true — Proposition 1.1 forced $x_{\text{cop}}=x_{\text{com}}$. But standing is never perfectly static, and the moment the body moves the two points separate. That separation is not measurement noise; it is the act of balancing.

The relationship the figure anticipates is exact and short: for the inverted-pendulum body of §3, the COP sits a distance $x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}}=-\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}/\omega_0^2$ from the COM, opposite to its acceleration. To push the COM forward (positive $\ddot x_{\text{com}}$) the nervous system drives the COP behind it; the COP is the lever, the COM the load. Deriving that relationship — and the equation of motion it falls out of — is the business of the next section (§3).

3. Dynamics of the inverted pendulum

We have the geometry (§1) and the force the ground supplies (§2). What remains is the law that turns a lean into an acceleration — the equation of motion. This section produces three boxed results, each proved from first principles: the equation of motion of the standing body, the identity that the ankle torque is the centre of pressure, and — settling the debt from §2 — the exact relationship between COP and COM.

The model: one rigid segment about the ankle

The idealisation that carries this module is the simplest one that can fall over: treat the entire body above the ankles as a single rigid inverted pendulum, hinged at a fixed ankle axis, with the feet flat on the ground. This is the ankle strategy — balance maintained by torque at the ankle alone. It is not the whole story (the hip and its second segment enter in §7), but it is where the physics of standing lives.

Parameters of the inverted-pendulum model.
  • $M$ — mass of the body above the ankles (very nearly the whole body mass);
  • $\ell$ — distance from the ankle axis to the centre of mass, along the body's long axis (${\approx}\,0.9$ m for an adult);
  • $I$ — moment of inertia of the body about the ankle axis;
  • $\theta$ — lean angle of the body from the vertical, taken positive forward;
  • $\tau$ — the net ankle torque supplied by the plantarflexor muscles (calf: soleus and gastrocnemius), taken positive when it rotates the body backward (restoring). This is the control input.

The equation of motion

Proposition 3.1 (equation of motion). The lean angle of the inverted-pendulum body obeys $$\boxed{\,I\,\ddot\theta = Mg\,\ell\sin\theta \;-\; \tau\,}$$ with $\theta$ positive forward and $\tau$ the restoring ankle torque.
The body rotates about the fixed ankle axis $O$, so its angular momentum about $O$ is $L=I\dot\theta$ and Euler's law gives $\dot L = I\ddot\theta = \sum \mathcal M_O$, the net moment of the external forces about $O$. Two moments act; take the forward sense as positive. The weight acts at the centre of mass, whose horizontal offset from the ankle is $x_{\text{com}}=\ell\sin\theta$. A downward force $Mg$ at a forward offset produces a forward-toppling moment $$\mathcal M_{\text{grav}} = Mg\cdot \ell\sin\theta,$$ positive in our convention because a forward-displaced weight topples the body further forward — gravity drives the lean, it does not restore it. The plantarflexors supply the only other moment, the restoring ankle torque, entering as $-\tau$ since it rotates the body backward. Summing, $$I\ddot\theta = Mg\,\ell\sin\theta - \tau.$$ ∎

The sign of the gravity term is the crux of the whole module. In an ordinary (hanging) pendulum the restoring moment is $-Mg\ell\sin\theta$ — negative, pulling the bob back. Here it is $+Mg\ell\sin\theta$: displacement feeds displacement. That single positive sign is what makes upright standing unstable, and §4 turns it into an eigenvalue.

The ankle torque is the centre of pressure

Proposition 3.1 leaves $\tau$ as an abstract control input. But the body cannot conjure a torque at the ankle out of nothing — it is produced by the ground pushing on the foot, and §2 already named the point where that push acts. The two are the same quantity in different clothing.

Proposition 3.2 (ankle torque ↔ centre of pressure). With the ankle a negligible height above the ground, the ankle torque equals the vertical ground reaction times the forward offset of the centre of pressure from the ankle: $$\boxed{\,\tau = N\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}}) \approx Mg\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}})\,}.$$
Isolate the foot. It is a light rigid segment resting flat on the ground, in equilibrium under two agencies: the ground reaction — vertical $N$ and horizontal shear $H$, acting at the centre of pressure $(x_{\text{cop}},0)$ — and, at the ankle joint, the reaction force and torque transmitted from the shank. By Newton's third law the shank feels the opposite ankle torque; call the plantarflexor torque the shank exerts on the foot $-\tau$ (it presses the forefoot down). Taking moments on the foot about the ankle axis, at height $d$ above the ground, $$-\tau + N\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}}) - H\,d = 0.$$ For quiet standing the shear $H$ is a few percent of body weight and the ankle height $d$ is small, so the $Hd$ term is third-order; dropping it, $$\tau = N\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}}),$$ and with $N\approx Mg$ in quiet standing the stated form follows. ∎
The COP is the controller's actuator. Proposition 3.2 says the nervous system does not command an abstract torque — it commands where the weight bears. Pressing the toes down (COP forward) is a larger plantarflexor torque; rocking to the heels (COP back) is a smaller one. And because the COP is trapped within the foot (§2), the ankle torque is bounded: $|\tau|\le Mg\cdot(\text{half-foot length})$. When a disturbance demands more torque than the foot can provide, the ankle strategy fails and the hip must take over — the subject of §7.

The centre of pressure trails the centre of mass

We can now discharge the promise made in §2. Combining the equation of motion with the torque–COP identity yields the exact kinematic relationship between the two points a force plate and a motion camera would record.

Proposition 3.3 (COP–COM relationship). For small lean, the centre of pressure is displaced from the centre of mass in proportion to the COM's horizontal acceleration, and opposite to it: $$\boxed{\,x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}} = -\frac{I}{Mg\,\ell}\,\ddot{x}_{\text{com}} = -\frac{\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}}{\omega_0^2}\,},\qquad \omega_0^2=\frac{Mg\,\ell}{I}.$$ For a point mass ($I=M\ell^2$) the rate reduces to $\omega_0^2=g/\ell$, the value quoted in §1.
Linearise the equation of motion for small $\theta$ ($\sin\theta\approx\theta$): $I\ddot\theta = Mg\ell\,\theta - \tau$. The COM's horizontal position is $x_{\text{com}}=\ell\sin\theta\approx\ell\theta$, so $\theta = x_{\text{com}}/\ell$ and $\ddot\theta=\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}/\ell$. Substitute the torque from Proposition 3.2, $\tau=Mg\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}})$, and place the ankle at the origin ($x_{\text{ankle}}=0$, so $\tau=Mg\,x_{\text{cop}}$): $$I\,\frac{\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}}{\ell} = Mg\ell\cdot\frac{x_{\text{com}}}{\ell} - Mg\,x_{\text{cop}} = Mg\,(x_{\text{com}}-x_{\text{cop}}).$$ Divide by $Mg$ and rearrange: $$x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}} = -\frac{I}{Mg\,\ell}\,\ddot{x}_{\text{com}} = -\frac{\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}}{\omega_0^2}. $$ ∎

Three boxed results, one contest. The equation of motion (Prop 3.1) pits a destabilising gravity moment against a controllable ankle torque; that torque is nothing but the centre of pressure (Prop 3.2); and steering the COP relative to the COM is how the body accelerates its mass (Prop 3.3). Everything is now in place to ask the decisive question: with no control, or with the simplest possible control, is the upright posture stable — and if not, how fast do we fall? That is the linear stability analysis of §4.

4. Linearization around upright — the instability

The equation of motion of §3 is nonlinear, but standing keeps $\theta$ small — a degree or two — so its behaviour near upright is captured by its linearization. That linear system answers the decisive question posed at the end of §3: left to itself, is the upright posture stable? It is not, and this section makes the instability quantitative — a growth rate, a toppling time, and a phase portrait — then uses the same linear model to derive the extrapolated-COM condition promised in §1.

The uncontrolled equilibrium is a saddle

Consider first the body with no active control, $\tau=0$ — a fresh cadaver, or the instant before the nervous system reacts. Linearising the equation of motion $I\ddot\theta=Mg\ell\sin\theta-\tau$ about $\theta=0$ with $\sin\theta\approx\theta$ gives the homogeneous linear equation

Proposition 4.1 (instability of upright standing). The uncontrolled inverted pendulum linearised about the vertical obeys $$\boxed{\;\ddot\theta = \omega_0^2\,\theta,\qquad \omega_0=\sqrt{\frac{Mg\ell}{I}}\;}$$ whose general solution $\theta(t)=A\,e^{\omega_0 t}+B\,e^{-\omega_0 t}$ contains a growing mode. The upright equilibrium is therefore unstable: almost every initial lean diverges exponentially with rate $\omega_0$.
Dividing the linearised equation by $I$ and writing $\omega_0^2=Mg\ell/I$ gives $\ddot\theta=\omega_0^2\theta$. This is linear with constant coefficients; substituting $\theta=e^{st}$ yields the characteristic equation $s^2-\omega_0^2=0$, with roots $s=\pm\omega_0$ — real and of opposite sign. The general solution is the corresponding combination $\theta(t)=A\,e^{\omega_0 t}+B\,e^{-\omega_0 t}$. Unless the initial conditions are chosen so that $A=0$ exactly, the term $A\,e^{\omega_0 t}$ grows without bound, so the equilibrium $\theta=0$ is unstable. In the phase plane $(\theta,\dot\theta)$ the two real eigenvalues of opposite sign make the origin a saddle point. ∎

The contrast with a hanging pendulum is total. There, linearisation gives $\ddot\theta=-\omega_0^2\theta$, characteristic roots $s=\pm i\omega_0$ — imaginary, giving bounded oscillation about a stable centre. The single sign flip of §3 moves the roots off the imaginary axis onto the real axis, and one of them into the right half-plane. Instability is that root.

How fast do we fall?

The growth rate $\omega_0$ sets a clock. A lean grows by a factor $e$ every toppling time constant $\tau_c=1/\omega_0$. Putting in numbers for an adult — $M=70$ kg, $\ell=0.9$ m, and a whole-body moment of inertia about the ankle $I\approx66\ \mathrm{kg\,m^2}$ —

The toppling clock. $$\omega_0=\sqrt{\frac{Mg\ell}{I}}=\sqrt{\frac{70\cdot9.81\cdot0.9}{66}}\approx3.1\ \mathrm{s^{-1}},\qquad \tau_c=\frac1{\omega_0}\approx0.32\ \mathrm s.$$ An uncontrolled lean $e$-folds every third of a second: a $0.5^\circ$ tilt becomes a $5^\circ$ fall in under a second. For comparison, the neural feedback delay is $\Delta\approx0.15$ s — half a toppling time constant. Standing is the task of catching a stick that falls this fast while seeing only where it was $0.15$ s ago. That collision of timescales is the whole difficulty of §6.

Capturability: deriving the extrapolated centre of mass

The saddle also hands us the dynamic stability condition previewed in §1. Rearranging the COP–COM relationship of Proposition 3.3, the linearised COM obeys a controlled equation with the centre of pressure as its input: $$\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}=\omega_0^2\big(x_{\text{com}}-x_{\text{cop}}\big),\qquad x_{\text{cop}}\in\text{BoS}.$$ The control $x_{\text{cop}}$ is bounded — it cannot leave the base of support (§2). The question of whether a lean can be arrested is therefore a reachability question, and it has a clean answer in one variable.

Proposition 4.2 (capturability; Hof). Define the extrapolated centre of mass $\xi=x_{\text{com}}+\dot{x}_{\text{com}}/\omega_0$. It obeys the first-order dynamics $$\boxed{\;\dot\xi=\omega_0\,(\xi-x_{\text{cop}})\;}$$ and the body can be brought to rest by ankle torque alone if and only if $\xi\in\text{BoS}$.
Differentiate $\xi=x_{\text{com}}+\dot{x}_{\text{com}}/\omega_0$ and substitute the controlled COM equation $\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}=\omega_0^2(x_{\text{com}}-x_{\text{cop}})$: $$\dot\xi=\dot{x}_{\text{com}}+\frac{\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}}{\omega_0}=\dot{x}_{\text{com}}+\omega_0\big(x_{\text{com}}-x_{\text{cop}}\big)=\omega_0\Big(\underbrace{x_{\text{com}}+\frac{\dot{x}_{\text{com}}}{\omega_0}}_{=\,\xi}-x_{\text{cop}}\Big)=\omega_0(\xi-x_{\text{cop}}).$$ This first-order system has a single fixed point $\xi=x_{\text{cop}}$, and because $\omega_0\gt0$ it is unstable: $\xi$ runs away from any $x_{\text{cop}}$ it does not equal. The only way to hold $\xi$ still is to set $x_{\text{cop}}=\xi$, which the ankle can do precisely when $\xi$ lies within the reachable range of the centre of pressure, i.e. $\xi\in\text{BoS}$. If $\xi\notin\text{BoS}$, then $x_{\text{cop}}$ is pinned to the nearer edge, $\xi-x_{\text{cop}}$ keeps its sign, and $\xi$ — hence the body — diverges: a step or a grab is the only recourse. ∎

Two of the module's earliest promises are now kept. The other pendulum mode $B\,e^{-\omega_0 t}$ that does decay corresponds exactly to states with $\xi$ already at the ankle; and the general recoverability rule is the extrapolated COM staying inside the base — the criterion sketched in Fig. 3, now derived. What remains is to design the control itself: how should the nervous system set $x_{\text{cop}}$ from what it senses? That is the proportional–derivative law of §5.

5. The ankle strategy: proportional–derivative control

Sections 3 and 4 analysed the plant — the body — and found it unstable. Now we design the controller. The equation of motion left the ankle torque $\tau$ as a free input; the nervous system must set it, moment by moment, from what its sensors report. This section derives the simplest control law that works, shows that the law is not an engineering choice but a reading of the body's own stretch sensors, and finds the exact condition under which it holds the body up.

The control law, and why biology supplies it

What does the nervous system have to work with? The muscle spindle — the stretch receptor embedded in every muscle (introduced as a promissory note in Module 5) — reports two things about the calf muscle it sits in: its length, which for a body pivoting at the ankle is proportional to the lean angle $\theta$, and its rate of lengthening, proportional to $\dot\theta$. A controller built from exactly those two signals is a proportional–derivative (PD) law.

Definition (PD ankle controller). The ankle torque is set proportional to the lean and to its rate: $$\tau = K_p\,\theta + K_d\,\dot\theta,$$ with $K_p\gt0$ the proportional gain (an active stiffness, units N·m·rad⁻¹) and $K_d\gt0$ the derivative gain (a damping, units N·m·s·rad⁻¹). Both act to restore: the $K_p$ term opposes the lean, the $K_d$ term opposes the leaning motion.

The mapping to biology is exact, and it is worth stating plainly: the spindle's length signal is the proportional term, and its velocity signal is the derivative term. Stiffness and damping at the ankle are what you get when the nervous system feeds spindle length and spindle velocity back to the calf muscles. Through the torque–COP identity of §3, the same law says the controller places the centre of pressure at $x_{\text{cop}}=\tau/Mg=(K_p\theta+K_d\dot\theta)/Mg$ — lean forward and the pressure moves to the toes, exactly as one feels.

Closed-loop dynamics and the stability condition

Substituting the control law into the linearised equation of motion $I\ddot\theta=Mg\ell\,\theta-\tau$ closes the loop: $$I\ddot\theta = Mg\ell\,\theta - K_p\theta - K_d\dot\theta \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; I\ddot\theta + K_d\dot\theta + (K_p-Mg\ell)\,\theta = 0.$$ This is a damped linear oscillator — but only if its coefficients have the right signs. The decisive combination is the effective stiffness $K_p-Mg\ell$: gravity contributes a negative stiffness $-Mg\ell$, and the controller must overcome it.

Proposition 5.1 (stability of the ankle strategy). The upright equilibrium of the delay-free closed loop is asymptotically stable if and only if $$\boxed{\;K_p \gt Mg\ell \quad\text{and}\quad K_d \gt 0\;}.$$ The proportional gain must exceed the gravitational toppling stiffness $Mg\ell$; damping must be positive.
The characteristic equation of $I\ddot\theta+K_d\dot\theta+(K_p-Mg\ell)\theta=0$ is $$I\,s^2 + K_d\,s + (K_p-Mg\ell) = 0,$$ with roots $s_\pm=\dfrac{-K_d\pm\sqrt{K_d^2-4I(K_p-Mg\ell)}}{2I}$. For a quadratic $as^2+bs+c$ with $a=I\gt0$, both roots have negative real part (asymptotic stability) if and only if $b\gt0$ and $c\gt0$ — the Routh–Hurwitz condition for a second-order system. Here $b=K_d$ and $c=K_p-Mg\ell$, giving $K_d\gt0$ and $K_p\gt Mg\ell$. The necessity is transparent: if $K_p\lt Mg\ell$ then $c\lt0$, the discriminant exceeds $K_d^2$, and the square root exceeds $K_d$ in magnitude, so $s_+\gt0$ — a real positive root, the toppling mode of §4 surviving unquelled. If $K_d\le0$ the sum of roots $-K_d/I\ge0$ forces a root into the right half-plane. Only both conditions together banish every root to the stable left half-plane. ∎
The critical stiffness, and a measured surprise. The stability threshold is the gravitational stiffness $$K_p^{\text{crit}}=Mg\ell = 70\cdot9.81\cdot0.9 \approx 618\ \mathrm{N\,m\,rad^{-1}}.$$ The intrinsic stiffness of the ankle — the passive spring of tendon and inactive muscle — has been measured at only ${\sim}\,90\%$ of this value. Passive stiffness alone therefore cannot hold a person up: it lands just inside the unstable region. The calf muscles must actively supply the remaining stiffness, continuously, which is why standing is metabolically active and why a fainting person falls. The model does not merely permit active control — it requires it, and the margin it demands matches experiment.

Why this cannot be the whole story

Proposition 5.1 is almost suspiciously permissive. It says any proportional gain above $Mg\ell$, with any positive damping, stabilises the body — and the larger the gains, the faster and stiffer the recovery. Taken at face value, we should stand rock-still with high enough gain, and sway would be a puzzle rather than the rule. Yet everyone sways. Something the delay-free model omits must penalise high gain.

That something is the neural delay of §4 — the $0.15$ s the loop of Fig. 10 takes to close. A correction computed from a stale measurement can overshoot and, if the gain is high, drive a growing oscillation: the very act of stabilising becomes destabilising. Restoring the delay to the model is the task of §6, and it converts the open quadrant of stable gains into a bounded island — with sway living at its shore.

6. The delay problem — why we sway

The delay-free controller of §5 was too generous: it promised perfect stillness at high gain, yet everyone sways. The missing ingredient is the finite time the feedback loop takes to close — the ${\approx}\,0.15$ s for a spindle signal to travel up the leg, be processed, and return as muscle force. Restoring that delay turns the equation of motion into a delay-differential equation (DDE), and the DDE has infinitely many modes, a bounded island of stable gains, and — at the island's shore — a sustained oscillation whose frequency we can compute and compare to measured postural sway. This is the mathematical heart of the module, and we treat it in full.

The delayed feedback equation

Feedback acts not on the present lean but on the lean as it was one delay ago. The control law becomes $\tau(t)=K_p\,\theta(t-\Delta)+K_d\,\dot\theta(t-\Delta)$, and the closed loop is

Proposition 6.1 (closed-loop delay-differential equation). With neural delay $\Delta$, the linearised standing body obeys $$\boxed{\,I\,\ddot\theta(t) - Mg\ell\,\theta(t) + K_p\,\theta(t-\Delta) + K_d\,\dot\theta(t-\Delta) = 0\,}$$ whose characteristic equation, from $\theta=e^{st}$, is the transcendental quasi-polynomial $$I\,s^2 - Mg\ell + \big(K_p + K_d\,s\big)e^{-s\Delta}=0.$$ Because $e^{-s\Delta}$ is not a polynomial, this has infinitely many roots $s$ in the complex plane.
Substituting the delayed control law into $I\ddot\theta(t)=Mg\ell\,\theta(t)-\tau(t)$ gives the boxed DDE directly. Seeking exponential solutions $\theta(t)=e^{st}$, each delayed term contributes a factor $e^{s(t-\Delta)}=e^{st}e^{-s\Delta}$: the equation becomes $\big(Is^2-Mg\ell+(K_p+K_ds)e^{-s\Delta}\big)e^{st}=0$, so nontrivial solutions require the bracket to vanish. The factor $e^{-s\Delta}$ is entire and non-polynomial, so the left side is a transcendental function of $s$ with an infinite discrete set of zeros — the loop has infinitely many characteristic modes, not the two of §5. ∎

Stability still means every root lies in the left half-plane, but now there are infinitely many to control, and as the delay grows a pair of them migrates rightward and crosses the imaginary axis. The crossing is where stability is lost — and where sway is born.

The stability boundary by D-subdivision

A root sits exactly on the imaginary axis, $s=i\omega$, precisely at the boundary between stability and instability. Setting $s=i\omega$ in the quasi-polynomial and separating real and imaginary parts gives two real equations that trace the boundary — the D-subdivision method (Stépán).

Proposition 6.2 (stability boundaries). The closed loop borders instability along two curves in the gain plane. A real root crosses at $s=0$ on the divergence boundary $$K_p = Mg\ell,$$ and a complex pair crosses at $s=i\omega$ on the oscillatory (Hopf) boundary, parametrised by the crossing frequency $\omega$: $$\boxed{\;K_p(\omega) = \big(I\omega^2+Mg\ell\big)\cos\omega\Delta,\qquad K_d(\omega) = \big(I\omega^2+Mg\ell\big)\frac{\sin\omega\Delta}{\omega}\;}.$$
Put $s=i\omega$ in $I s^2 - Mg\ell + (K_p+K_d s)e^{-s\Delta}=0$ and use $e^{-i\omega\Delta}=\cos\omega\Delta - i\sin\omega\Delta$: $$-I\omega^2 - Mg\ell + (K_p + iK_d\omega)(\cos\omega\Delta - i\sin\omega\Delta)=0.$$ Expanding the product and collecting terms, $$\underbrace{-I\omega^2 - Mg\ell + K_p\cos\omega\Delta + K_d\omega\sin\omega\Delta}_{\text{real}} + \,i\underbrace{\big(K_d\omega\cos\omega\Delta - K_p\sin\omega\Delta\big)}_{\text{imag}}=0.$$ Both parts must vanish. The imaginary part gives $K_p\sin\omega\Delta = K_d\omega\cos\omega\Delta$. Substituting $K_d\omega\sin\omega\Delta = K_p\sin^2\omega\Delta/\cos\omega\Delta$ into the real part and using $\cos^2+\sin^2=1$, $$K_p\Big(\cos\omega\Delta + \frac{\sin^2\omega\Delta}{\cos\omega\Delta}\Big)=\frac{K_p}{\cos\omega\Delta}=I\omega^2+Mg\ell \;\Rightarrow\; K_p=(I\omega^2+Mg\ell)\cos\omega\Delta,$$ and then $K_d=(I\omega^2+Mg\ell)\sin\omega\Delta/\omega$ from the imaginary part. The special case $\omega\to0$ recovers $K_p\to Mg\ell$, the $s=0$ divergence boundary of §5. ∎

Two boundaries now hem in the stable gains. Below $K_p=Mg\ell$ the body diverges (too little stiffness — the old saddle). Above the Hopf curve it oscillates (too much gain fed through the delay). Between them lies a bounded island, and — this is the crux — the delay-free quadrant of §5 has been capped from above. You can no longer buy stillness with gain.

The critical delay, and the frequency of sway

Fix the gains and increase the delay, and the operating point drifts toward the Hopf boundary; at a critical delay $\Delta_{\text{crit}}$ it touches, a complex pair reaches the imaginary axis, and the upright equilibrium loses stability through a Hopf bifurcation — the birth of a sustained oscillation at the crossing frequency $\omega_c$.

Proposition 6.3 (critical delay and sway frequency). For fixed gains $K_p,K_d$, the crossing frequency solves $$I^2\omega^4 + \big(2IMg\ell - K_d^2\big)\omega^2 + \big((Mg\ell)^2 - K_p^2\big)=0,$$ and the critical delay is $\Delta_{\text{crit}}=\dfrac{1}{\omega_c}\arctan\!\dfrac{K_d\,\omega_c}{K_p}$.
On the boundary, $K_p=(I\omega^2+Mg\ell)\cos\omega\Delta$ and $K_d\omega=(I\omega^2+Mg\ell)\sin\omega\Delta$ (from Proposition 6.2). Squaring and adding removes the delay: $K_p^2+K_d^2\omega^2=(I\omega^2+Mg\ell)^2$. Expanding the right side and collecting powers of $\omega$ gives the stated quartic in $\omega$; its positive root is $\omega_c$. Dividing the two boundary relations gives $\tan\omega_c\Delta = K_d\omega_c/K_p$, hence $\Delta_{\text{crit}}=\omega_c^{-1}\arctan(K_d\omega_c/K_p)$. ∎
The model predicts the sway you can feel. For representative gains $K_p=900\ \mathrm{N\,m\,rad^{-1}}$, $K_d=120\ \mathrm{N\,m\,s\,rad^{-1}}$ (with $I=66$, $Mg\ell=618$), the quartic gives $$\omega_c\approx 2.2\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}\;\Rightarrow\; f_c=\frac{\omega_c}{2\pi}\approx 0.35\ \mathrm{Hz},\qquad \Delta_{\text{crit}}\approx 0.13\ \mathrm s.$$ The predicted oscillation, ${\approx}\,0.35$ Hz, is squarely in the measured band of quiet-standing sway ($0.2$–$0.5$ Hz). And $\Delta_{\text{crit}}\approx0.13$ s is just under the true neural delay of $0.15$ s — so these gains would actually be marginally unstable, which is exactly why real standing hovers at the edge of oscillation rather than sitting still. Sway is not noise; it is the signature of a delayed feedback controller operating at its stability margin.

The delay reframes everything. A pure reactive controller near the margin cannot suppress sway — it can only chase it. To do better, the nervous system is thought to predict, running an internal forward model that estimates the present lean from delayed signals and so effectively shortens $\Delta$, pushing the Hopf boundary outward and enlarging the island. That predictive control is beyond our scope, but its necessity is now precise: it is the only escape from the bound Proposition 6.2 places on any purely reactive stance. What the ankle cannot manage at all — because the centre of pressure has run to the edge of the foot — is the province of the hip, next (§7).

7. Hip strategy, co-contraction, and knee locking

The single inverted pendulum of §3–§6 is the physics of quiet standing, but it has three built-in limits the body routinely runs into: the ankle torque is bounded, the controller gain is not free to set, and holding a joint against gravity costs energy. Each limit has an anatomical answer — the hip strategy, co-contraction, and knee locking. This section takes them in turn, and in doing so repays the co-contraction promise made in Module 5.

When the ankle saturates

Proposition 3.2 tied the ankle torque to the centre of pressure, $\tau=Mg\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}})$, and Proposition 1.1 trapped the COP inside the base of support. Together they cap the ankle torque.

Proposition 7.1 (ankle-torque saturation). The ankle strategy can supply a restoring torque no larger than body weight times the distance from the ankle to the edge of the foot: $$\boxed{\;|\tau_{\text{ankle}}| \le Mg\cdot d_{\text{foot}}\;},\qquad d_{\text{foot}}=\text{ankle-to-toe (or -heel) distance}.$$ Beyond this the centre of pressure would have to leave the foot, which it cannot.
By Proposition 3.2, $\tau_{\text{ankle}}=Mg\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}})$. The COP lies within the base of support (Proposition 2.1 / 1.1), so $x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}}$ ranges only over $[-d_{\text{heel}},\,d_{\text{toe}}]$, the foot's extent about the ankle. Hence $|\tau_{\text{ankle}}|\le Mg\,d_{\text{foot}}$ with $d_{\text{foot}}$ the relevant half-extent. With $d_{\text{toe}}\approx0.12$ m and $Mg\approx690$ N, the forward ankle torque tops out near $80$ N·m. ∎

A lean that demands more than this — a hard shove, a bus lurching — cannot be met at the ankle. The centre of pressure hits the toe and stays there, the restoring moment saturates, and the body needs a second actuator. That actuator is the hip.

The hip strategy is a momentum mechanism

Why does bending at the hip help when the ankle is maxed out? Because the horizontal force that decelerates the mass need not come from the centre of pressure at all. Newton's law for the whole body reads $M\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}=H$, where $H$ is the horizontal (shear) ground reaction; the question is how the body commands $H$.

Proposition 7.2 (two routes to the restoring shear). The angular momentum $L_G$ of the body about its centre of mass obeys $$\dot L_G = N\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}}) \;-\; H\,h_{\text{com}},$$ with $N\approx Mg$ the vertical load and $h_{\text{com}}$ the COM height. The body can therefore produce the restoring shear $H$ either by displacing the centre of pressure (the $x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}}$ term — the ankle strategy, bounded by Proposition 7.1) or by actively changing its angular momentum (the $\dot L_G$ term — the hip strategy, bounded instead by how fast the trunk can be counter-rotated).
Take angular momentum about the (moving) centre of mass. The only external force is the ground reaction, with vertical component $N$ at the COP and horizontal component $H$ at ground level. The moment of $N$ about the COM is $N\,(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}})$ (an upward force offset horizontally), and the moment of $H$, acting a height $h_{\text{com}}$ below the COM, is $-H\,h_{\text{com}}$. Euler's law about the centre of mass then gives $\dot L_G=N(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}})-H\,h_{\text{com}}$. In quiet standing $L_G\approx0$ and the first term dominates — this is exactly the COP–COM relation of Proposition 3.3. But when the COP is pinned (ankle saturated), a large $\dot L_G$ from trunk counter-rotation supplies the moment balance and, through it, the shear $H=M\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}$ that arrests the fall. ∎

The hip strategy trades a bounded lever (the foot) for an unbounded one (angular momentum) — you can always rotate the trunk faster — at the price of a more complex, two-segment coordination and a transient forward-backward motion of the head. It is what the body reaches for when balance is seriously threatened, and its failure is a step or a fall.

Co-contraction: buying stiffness with energy

Section 6 showed that standing lives inside a bounded island of gains, and that the operating point must have enough proportional gain $K_p$ to clear the divergence boundary $Mg\ell$. But $K_p$ is a stiffness, and the nervous system sets it by a mechanism worth naming precisely: co-contraction, the simultaneous activation of opposing muscles.

Proposition 7.3 (co-contraction sets stiffness independently of torque). For a joint spanned by an agonist and antagonist with individual stiffnesses $k_a,k_b$ and torques $T_a,T_b$, the net torque and the joint stiffness are $$\tau_{\text{net}} = T_a - T_b,\qquad K_p = k_a + k_b.$$ The torques subtract but the stiffnesses add: raising both activations together (co-contraction) increases $K_p$ while leaving $\tau_{\text{net}}$ unchanged.
Let the agonist pull the joint one way with torque $T_a(\theta)$ and the antagonist the other with $T_b(\theta)$; the net joint torque is $\tau_{\text{net}}=T_a-T_b$. The joint stiffness is the restoring gradient $K_p=-\,\partial\tau_{\text{net}}/\partial\theta$. A muscle's short-range stiffness rises with its active force, so each contributes a positive restoring gradient when it is the one being stretched: $-\partial T_a/\partial\theta=k_a\ge0$ and $+\partial T_b/\partial\theta=k_b\ge0$ (the antagonist resists the opposite deflection). Hence $K_p=k_a+k_b$. Because $k_a,k_b$ grow with activation while their torque contributions have opposite sign, co-activating both raises $K_p$ with no change in $\tau_{\text{net}}$. ∎
Repaying Module 5's IOU. Co-contraction is the concrete resolution of the muscle-redundancy question left open in Module 5: agonist and antagonist are redundant actuators for one net torque, and the CNS spends that redundancy to tune stiffness — pushing $K_p$ deeper into the stability island of §6 when balance is uncertain (a narrow beam, ice underfoot). The cost is metabolic: both muscles burn ATP while doing no net work. Standing stiffer is standing more expensively. The full whole-limb redundancy problem, where dozens of muscles share load under a debated cost function, is taken up in Module 12.

Knee locking: stability for free

The cheapest joint torque is the one you do not have to supply. Standing with the knees fully extended drives the joint against its own passive stop — the screw-home mechanism, a slight external rotation of the tibia at terminal extension that ligaments and bony congruence lock (the knee's roll-and-glide geometry was analysed in Module 3). With the knee locked, the ground reaction line passes just in front of the joint and the passive structures carry the moment, so the quadriceps can fall nearly silent.

Three limits, three answers: the hip supplies torque the ankle cannot, co-contraction supplies stiffness the reflex loop needs, and knee locking supplies stability the muscles would otherwise have to pay for. All three defend the same upright equilibrium — and defending it, especially in a leaned or loaded posture, is what loads the spine. That cost is the subject of §8.

8. Load bearing: the equilibrium the controller defends

Everything so far has defended the upright equilibrium $\theta=0$. But we also stand leaned forward over a workbench, or holding a child, or lifting a box — and each of these is simply a shifted equilibrium that the same postural controller must hold, with the centre of mass kept over the base of support (§1) by the same ankle, hip, and reflex machinery. What is new is the cost of holding it: a leaned or loaded posture loads the spine, and the low back pays a price set by an unforgiving lever. This section computes that price, and lands it against the low-back numbers established in Module 1, §6.

The trunk as a lever about L5/S1

The junction between the last lumbar vertebra and the sacrum, L5/S1, is the pivot about which the trunk bends forward. When you lean, the weight of the upper body — and any load in the hands — hangs forward of this pivot on a long moment arm, while the muscles that resist the bend, the erector spinae running vertically just behind the spine, pull on a very short one. That mismatch is the whole story.

Proposition 8.1 (low-back extensor moment). For a trunk flexed by an angle $\phi$ from the vertical, holding a load $m_L$ at hand reach $r_L$, the extensor moment required at L5/S1 is $$\boxed{\,M_{\text{L5/S1}} = g\big(m_t\,r_t + m_L\,r_L\big)\sin\phi\,},$$ with $m_t$ the upper-body mass and $r_t$ the distance from L5/S1 to the trunk's centre of mass.
Take moments about the L5/S1 axis, with $r_t$ and $r_L$ the along-trunk distances from the pivot to the trunk centre of mass and to the hands. Flexing the trunk by $\phi$ from vertical swings both forward, so their horizontal offsets — the moment arms of the downward weights — are $r_t\sin\phi$ and $r_L\sin\phi$. The two gravitational moments are then $m_t g\,r_t\sin\phi$ and $m_L g\,r_L\sin\phi$, both tending to flex the trunk further. For quasi-static balance the erector spinae must supply an equal and opposite extensor moment $M_{\text{L5/S1}}=g(m_t r_t+m_L r_L)\sin\phi$, the low-back torque tabulated in Module 1, §6. ∎

From moment to compression

The extensor moment is supplied by a muscle force on a tiny arm, and that force — not the load itself — is what crushes the discs. This is the result that makes the low back the body's most-injured joint.

Proposition 8.2 (spinal compression). With erector-spinae moment arm $d$, the L5/S1 compressive force is $$\boxed{\,F_{\text{spine}} \approx \frac{M_{\text{L5/S1}}}{d} + \big(m_t+m_L\big)g\cos\phi\,}\;\approx\;\frac{g(m_t r_t + m_L r_L)\sin\phi}{d},$$ the muscle term dominating because $d$ is roughly an order of magnitude smaller than the load arms.
The erector force balancing the moment is $F_e=M_{\text{L5/S1}}/d$ (force times its arm equals the moment). Resolving forces along the spine's long axis, the compression is this muscle force plus the axial component of the supported weights, $(m_t+m_L)g\cos\phi$. Since $d\approx0.05$ m while $r_t\approx0.3$ m and $r_L$ up to $0.4$ m, the ratio $M/d$ makes the first term several times the total weight, so it dominates: $F_{\text{spine}}\approx g(m_t r_t+m_L r_L)\sin\phi/d$. ∎
A worked lift, consistent with Module 1. Take $m_t=35$ kg, $r_t=0.30$ m, a held load $m_L=20$ kg at $r_L=0.40$ m, fully bent so the trunk is horizontal ($\sin\phi=1,\ \cos\phi=0$), and $d=0.05$ m: $$M_{\text{L5/S1}} = 9.81(35\cdot0.30 + 20\cdot0.40) = 181\ \mathrm{N\,m},\qquad F_{\text{spine}}\approx\frac{181}{0.05}\approx 3.6\ \mathrm{kN}.$$ The moment sits in Module 1's $100$–$400$ N·m band, and the compression is the "several thousand newtons" that section warned of — here about $5.3\times$ body weight, above the ${\approx}\,3.4$ kN threshold beyond which epidemiology links lifting to disc injury. (At full flexion the axial weight term $(m_t+m_L)g\cos\phi$ vanishes, so the muscle force is essentially the whole compression.) The spine bears far more than the weight it lifts.

The postural controller and the load-bearing spine are two views of one act. To stand leaned or to lift is to command a new equilibrium — the centre of mass shifted forward but still captured over the feet by the ankle and hip strategies of §7 — and to hold it against gravity through the erector spinae, whose short lever turns a modest moment into a spinal load of thousands of newtons. Standing is never passive, and load-bearing is the moment that cost comes due. With the model complete, §9 sets it running as code.

The model of this module is now complete enough to run as code. Two labs put it to work: the first simulates quiet standing as a delayed-feedback system and recovers, numerically, the critical delay and sway frequency that §6 derived by hand; the second maps the safe lifting envelope of §8, turning the spinal-compression formula into a rule for how much you may lift at each reach. Both are written to be read and run; each block copies with one click.

Lab 1 — quiet standing, and finding the critical delay

The heart of the lab is a method-of-steps integrator for the delay-differential equation of Proposition 6.1: at each step the acceleration uses the lean and rate as they were $\Delta$ ago, read from a history buffer. Around it, a bisection search finds the delay at which a fixed-gain controller first loses stability — and compares it to the closed-form prediction of Proposition 6.3.

import numpy as np
# inertia (kg m^2), gravitational stiffness Mgl (N m/rad)
I, Mgl = 66.0, 618.0


def simulate(Kp, Kd, D, T=25.0, dt=1e-3, th0=0.6):
    """Method-of-steps integrator for the delayed pendulum ODE."""
    n, nd = int(T/dt), int(round(D/dt))
    th, w = np.zeros(n), np.zeros(n)
    th[0] = th0
    for k in range(n-1):
        j = k - nd  # index one delay in the past
        # delayed angle (history = th0)
        thd = th[j] if j >= 0 else th0
        wd = w[j] if j >= 0 else 0.0  # delayed rate
        acc = (Mgl*th[k] - Kp*thd - Kd*wd) / \
            I  # the DDE, solved for th''
        w[k+1] = w[k] + dt*acc
        th[k+1] = th[k] + dt*w[k+1]
    return np.arange(n)*dt, th


def diverges(th, t):  # late amplitude exceeds mid-run amplitude
    late = np.abs(th[t > 0.8*t[-1]]).max()
    mid = np.abs(th[(t > 0.4*t[-1]) & (t < 0.6*t[-1])]).max()
    return late > 1.02*mid


Kp, Kd = 900.0, 120.0
# bracket the critical delay, then bisect
lo, hi = 0.05, 0.25
for _ in range(26):
    mid = 0.5*(lo + hi)
    t, th = simulate(Kp, Kd, mid)
    hi, lo = (mid, lo) if diverges(th, t) else (hi, mid)
Dcrit = 0.5*(lo + hi)

# Prop 6.3 quartic for the crossing freq
a, b, c = I*I, 2*I*Mgl - Kd**2, Mgl**2 - Kp**2
wc = np.sqrt((-b + np.sqrt(b*b - 4*a*c)) / (2*a))
Dtheory = np.arctan(Kd*wc / Kp) / wc
print(
    f"critical delay : simulation {
        1e3 *
        Dcrit:5.1f} ms   theory {
            1e3 *
            Dtheory:5.1f} ms")
print(f"sway frequency : {wc/(2*np.pi):.2f} Hz")
Output. 
critical delay : simulation 129.5 ms   theory 129.7 ms
sway frequency : 0.35 Hz
The brute-force bisection and the closed-form quartic agree to the millisecond — the analysis of §6 is not an approximation but the exact boundary the simulation walks up to. Sweeping the gains and repeating the bisection traces the whole stability island of Fig. 12.

Lab 2 — the safe lifting envelope

The second lab inverts the compression formula of Proposition 8.2. Rather than ask "how much does this lift compress the spine," it asks the design question: at each reach, what is the heaviest load whose worst-case compression stays under the injury-risk threshold? The answer is a curve every manual-handling guideline approximates.

import numpy as np
# gravity, trunk mass, trunk arm, erector arm
g, mt, rt, d = 9.81, 35.0, 0.30, 0.05
# L5/S1 compression injury-risk threshold (N)
LIMIT = 3400.0


def peak_compression(mL, rL):
    """Worst-case L5/S1 compression over trunk-flexion angles (Prop 8.2)."""
    phi = np.linspace(0, np.pi/2, 400)
    M = g*(mt*rt + mL*rL)*np.sin(phi)  # extensor moment (Prop 8.1)
    return (M/d + (mt + mL)*g*np.cos(phi)).max()


reaches = np.linspace(0.15, 0.50, 8)
for rL in reaches:  # largest load whose peak stays under LIMIT
    mL = max(
        m for m in np.arange(
            0, 60, 0.5) if peak_compression(
            m, rL) < LIMIT)

10. What the model captures and misses; diagnostics and problems

The single inverted pendulum under delayed proportional–derivative control is a small model that explains a surprising amount: why standing is unstable, why we sway, why passive stiffness is not enough, and why lifting crushes the spine. Like every model in this course it earns its keep by being explicit about what it leaves out. This section names the idealisations and where each is repaid, offers five quick diagnostics, and closes with thirty problems.

What it captures

The model reproduces, quantitatively, the core facts of standing: the exponential instability of the upright posture and its ${\approx}\,0.3$ s toppling time (§4); the COP–COM relationship a force plate records (§2–§3); the requirement that active stiffness exceed $Mg\ell$, matching the measured shortfall of intrinsic stiffness (§5); the bounded island of stable gains and a sway frequency of ${\approx}\,0.35$ Hz emerging from a $0.15$ s delay (§6); the ankle-to-hip strategy switch (§7); and multi-kilonewton spinal loads in a forward lift (§8).

Diagnostics

Five quick checks — reason each out before revealing the answer.

  1. Why is standing unstable but sitting stable?
    Answer
    Standing places the centre of mass high above a small base (an inverted pendulum: displacement grows, §0). Sitting lowers the COM and widens the base so the gravity line stays within it with large margin and small disturbances self-correct — closer to a hanging pendulum than an inverted one.
  2. A force plate shows the centre of pressure swinging more widely than the body's centre of mass. Why?
    Answer
    By Proposition 3.3, $x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}}=-\ddot{x}_{\text{com}}/\omega_0^2$: the COP is displaced from the COM in proportion to the COM's acceleration. The COP is the control signal the nervous system drives to accelerate the slow, heavy COM, so it necessarily moves first, further, and faster.
  3. Measured intrinsic ankle stiffness is about $90\%$ of $Mg\ell$. What does that one number tell you?
    Answer
    That passive stiffness alone cannot hold you up (stability needs $K_p\gt Mg\ell$, §5). The calf muscles must actively supply the missing ${\sim}\,10\%$ continuously — so standing is metabolically active, and a loss of active control (fainting) means falling.
  4. Why does a longer neural delay force the nervous system to use lower feedback gains?
    Answer
    The stable region in gain space is a bounded island that shrinks as the delay grows (§6, Fig. 12). A correction based on a staler measurement over-shoots more, so the same gain that is stable at short delay drives a growing oscillation at long delay; the controller must back off the gain to stay inside the smaller island.
  5. Why does holding a load close to the body protect the low back, when the load's weight is unchanged?
    Answer
    Spinal compression is set by the erector-spinae force $F_e=M_{\text{L5/S1}}/d$, and the moment $M_{\text{L5/S1}}=g(m_t r_t+m_L r_L)\sin\phi$ grows with the load's reach $r_L$ (§8). The erector's own arm $d$ is fixed at ${\approx}\,5$ cm, so the only lever you control is $r_L$; halving it nearly halves the muscle force and the compression.

Each isolates one idea from the module's spine, carries a short Probes: note naming what it tests, and hides its solution until you open it; the computational solutions quote Python-verified numbers. Conceptual problems (C1–C10)

C1. A juggler balances a broomstick upright on one palm; a person stands on two feet. Explain in what precise sense these are the same control problem, and name the one parameter that makes the broom harder. Probes: the inverted-pendulum abstraction (§0, §3).
Solution
Both are inverted pendulums: a mass held above a pivot, whose upright equilibrium is a saddle with growth rate $\omega_0=\sqrt{Mg\ell/I}$ (§4). The controller senses the lean and drives a corrective torque — palm acceleration for the broom, ankle torque for the body. The harder parameter is $\ell$ (and hence $\omega_0$): the short broom has a small $\ell$, a large $\omega_0$, and a toppling time $\tau_c=1/\omega_0$ of a fraction of the body's — it falls faster, leaving less time to react.
C2. Why can you doze off sitting in a chair but not standing? Frame the answer with the base of support and the centre of mass. Probes: static stability margin (§1).
Solution
Sitting lowers the COM and rests it over a large base (thighs, chair), so the gravity line sits deep inside the base with a wide margin (§1); small disturbances self-correct passively, no active control needed. Standing raises the COM above a small base — an unstable equilibrium that requires the continuous active feedback of §5. Remove consciousness (sleep, faint) and the active loop stops: sitting stays put, standing falls.
C3. Pushed gently forward, you rise onto the balls of your feet; pushed backward, you rarely rock onto your heels as readily. Why the asymmetry? Probes: margin asymmetry and COP limits (§1, §2).
Solution
The foot extends much further forward of the ankle (toes, ${\approx}\,0.12$ m) than behind it (heel, ${\approx}\,0.05$ m), so the COP — hence the ankle torque (§3) — has a larger forward range. A forward disturbance can be met by driving the COP toward the toes (large restoring torque); a backward one quickly exhausts the short heel margin, forcing an earlier switch to the hip strategy or a step.
C4. A clinician reads only the centre-of-pressure trace from a force plate, never the centre of mass directly. Why is the COP nonetheless the more informative signal about the control the nervous system is exerting? Probes: COP as the control action (§2, §3).
Solution
The COP is the ankle torque scaled by body weight (§3, Prop 3.2), i.e. the controller's output; the COM is the slow plant response. Because $x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}}=-\ddot x_{\text{com}}/\omega_0^2$ (Prop 3.3), the COP leads and exceeds the COM, carrying the high-frequency corrective activity. Reading COP is reading the controller in action; reading COM alone would smooth that away.
C5. A cadaver ankle has nearly the same passive stiffness as a living one, yet a body cannot be propped to stand on cadaver ankles. What single inequality explains this? Probes: the critical stiffness (§5).
Solution
Stability requires $K_p\gt Mg\ell$ (§5, Prop 5.1). Intrinsic (passive) ankle stiffness is only ${\approx}\,0.9\,Mg\ell$ — inside the unstable region. The living ankle adds the missing stiffness by active muscle contraction, which the cadaver cannot; passive structures alone leave the eigenvalue in the right half-plane and the body topples.
C6. Two students argue: one says quiet-standing sway is "motor noise," the other that it is "the controller working." Who is closer, and what evidence from the model decides it? Probes: sway as marginal delayed control (§6).
Solution
The second is closer. A delayed feedback controller near its stability boundary produces a sustained oscillation at a definite frequency (§6, Prop 6.3) — here ${\approx}\,0.35$ Hz, matching measured sway. Pure noise would be broadband and would not sit at the predicted Hopf frequency, nor would its frequency shift predictably with delay or gain. Sway is the fingerprint of control operating at its margin, not random error.
C7. Standing on a narrow beam, you feel your hips start to jerk back and forth in a way they never do on solid ground. What has changed, and why does the body recruit the hip? Probes: ankle saturation and the hip strategy (§7).
Solution
A narrow base shrinks $d_{\text{foot}}$, so the ankle-torque ceiling $Mg\,d_{\text{foot}}$ (§7, Prop 7.1) drops sharply — the ankle saturates at the slightest lean. The body then generates the restoring shear through trunk counter-rotation (the angular-momentum route of Prop 7.2), which needs no COP travel. The visible hip motion is that momentum mechanism doing the work the foot no longer can.
C8. Stepping onto an icy patch, you instinctively stiffen — you co-contract calf and shin muscles. This helps stability but you tire quickly. Explain both effects from one result. Probes: co-contraction stiffness and its cost (§7).
Solution
Co-contraction adds the opposing muscles' stiffnesses, $K_p=k_a+k_b$, while their torques subtract (§7, Prop 7.3). Raising both activations pushes $K_p$ deeper into the stability island of §6 — more robust balance on the uncertain surface — with no change in net torque. But both muscles now consume ATP while doing no net work, so the metabolic cost rises and you fatigue: stability bought with energy.
C9. A soldier told to stand at attention for a long time is taught to keep the knees "soft," yet a tired hiker locks the knees to rest. Reconcile these opposite pieces of advice using the mechanics of the knee. Probes: knee locking as passive stabilisation (§7).
Solution
Locking drives the knee to its extension stop (screw-home), where passive ligaments carry the moment and the quadriceps relax — cheap and restful (§7). That is what the hiker exploits. But a fully locked joint pools blood and removes the small ankle/knee adjustments the postural controller uses, risking a faint on prolonged standing; "soft knees" keep the active loop and circulation engaged at a modest energy cost. The trade is energy economy versus responsiveness.
C10. The low back is the most commonly injured joint in manual work, far more often than the much more heavily loaded knee or hip. Give the mechanical reason. Probes: the erector-spinae lever (§8).
Solution
The erector spinae act on a very short moment arm ($d\approx0.05$ m) against loads carried on arms several times longer (§8, Prop 8.2). To balance a modest moment the muscle force must be large, and that force compresses the discs: $F_{\text{spine}}\approx M_{\text{L5/S1}}/d$ reaches several kilonewtons in a routine lift. The knee and hip carry large loads but on far more favourable levers, so their muscle forces — and joint stresses — are proportionally smaller.

Derivational problems (D1–D10)

D1. From Euler's law about the ankle, derive the nonlinear equation of motion of the standing body and identify the destabilising term. Probes: the equation of motion (§3).
Solution
See §3, Proposition 3.1: $I\ddot\theta=Mg\ell\sin\theta-\tau$. The gravity moment $+Mg\ell\sin\theta$ is destabilising — it grows with $\theta$ and drives the lean further, unlike a hanging pendulum's $-Mg\ell\sin\theta$. That single positive sign is the origin of the instability.
D2. Linearise the equation of motion about upright and show the equilibrium is a saddle; find the growth rate and the toppling time constant. Probes: eigenvalues of the inverted pendulum (§4).
Solution
See §4, Proposition 4.1: $\ddot\theta=\omega_0^2\theta$ with $\omega_0=\sqrt{Mg\ell/I}$; characteristic roots $s=\pm\omega_0$ (real, opposite sign) ⇒ saddle; growth rate $\omega_0\approx3.1\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$, toppling time $\tau_c=1/\omega_0\approx0.32$ s.
D3. Derive the exact relationship between the centre of pressure and the centre of mass for small lean, and state its sign. Probes: the COP–COM law (§3).
Solution
See §3, Proposition 3.3: combining the linearised EOM with $\tau=Mg\,x_{\text{cop}}$ and $\theta=x_{\text{com}}/\ell$ gives $x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}}=-\ddot x_{\text{com}}/\omega_0^2$. The COP sits opposite the COM's acceleration: to drive the COM forward the COP goes behind it.
D4. Define the extrapolated centre of mass $\xi=x_{\text{com}}+\dot x_{\text{com}}/\omega_0$ and derive its first-order dynamics under bounded COP control. State the capturability condition. Probes: the Hof condition (§4).
Solution
See §4, Proposition 4.2: differentiating $\xi$ and using $\ddot x_{\text{com}}=\omega_0^2(x_{\text{com}}-x_{\text{cop}})$ yields $\dot\xi=\omega_0(\xi-x_{\text{cop}})$. Since $\omega_0\gt0$ the only way to arrest $\xi$ is $x_{\text{cop}}=\xi$, possible iff $\xi\in\text{BoS}$ — the capturability condition.
D5. Close the loop with a PD controller and derive the exact stability condition on the gains (delay-free). Probes: Routh–Hurwitz for the ankle strategy (§5).
Solution
See §5, Proposition 5.1: the closed loop is $I\ddot\theta+K_d\dot\theta+(K_p-Mg\ell)\theta=0$; a second-order system is stable iff all coefficients share a sign, giving $K_d\gt0$ and $K_p\gt Mg\ell$.
D6. Introduce the neural delay and derive the equations of the oscillatory stability boundary by setting $s=i\omega$ in the characteristic quasi-polynomial. Probes: D-subdivision (§6).
Solution
See §6, Proposition 6.2: with $s=i\omega$ in $Is^2-Mg\ell+(K_p+K_ds)e^{-s\Delta}=0$, separating real and imaginary parts gives $K_p=(I\omega^2+Mg\ell)\cos\omega\Delta$ and $K_d=(I\omega^2+Mg\ell)\sin\omega\Delta/\omega$.
D7. For fixed gains, eliminate the delay from the boundary equations to obtain the polynomial that determines the crossing (sway) frequency. Probes: the critical-delay quartic (§6).
Solution
See §6, Proposition 6.3: squaring and adding the two boundary relations gives $K_p^2+K_d^2\omega^2=(I\omega^2+Mg\ell)^2$, i.e. $I^2\omega^4+(2IMg\ell-K_d^2)\omega^2+((Mg\ell)^2-K_p^2)=0$; its positive root is $\omega_c$, and $\Delta_{\text{crit}}=\omega_c^{-1}\arctan(K_d\omega_c/K_p)$.
D8. Derive the upper bound on the ankle restoring torque from the constraint that the centre of pressure lies within the foot. Probes: ankle saturation (§7).
Solution
See §7, Proposition 7.1: $\tau=Mg(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{ankle}})$ and $x_{\text{cop}}$ is confined to the foot, so $|\tau|\le Mg\,d_{\text{foot}}$; with $d_{\text{toe}}\approx0.12$ m and $Mg\approx690$ N the forward limit is ${\approx}\,80$ N·m.
D9. Taking angular momentum about the centre of mass, derive the balance that shows how trunk counter-rotation produces a restoring ground shear without moving the COP. Probes: the hip-strategy momentum relation (§7).
Solution
See §7, Proposition 7.2: Euler's law about the COM gives $\dot L_G=N(x_{\text{cop}}-x_{\text{com}})-H\,h_{\text{com}}$. With the COP pinned, a large $\dot L_G$ from trunk counter-rotation supplies the moment, and the associated shear $H=M\ddot x_{\text{com}}$ decelerates the mass.
D10. Derive the L5/S1 extensor moment and compressive force for a trunk flexed by $\phi$ holding a load at reach $r_L$, and show the muscle term dominates the compression. Probes: spinal load (§8).
Solution
See §8, Propositions 8.1–8.2: $M_{\text{L5/S1}}=g(m_tr_t+m_Lr_L)\sin\phi$ and $F_{\text{spine}}\approx M_{\text{L5/S1}}/d+(m_t+m_L)g\cos\phi$. Since $d\approx0.05$ m $\ll r_t,r_L$, the first term is several times the total weight and dominates.

Computational problems (K1–K10)

Each K problem requires simulation, optimisation, an inverse solve, or a sensitivity sweep — not substitution into a formula. Numbers below are Python-verified.

K1. Using the method-of-steps DDE integrator of §9, find by bisection the critical delay for the stiffer gains $K_p=1100$, $K_d=150$, and the resulting sway frequency. Compare to the closed-form prediction. Probes: DDE simulation + theory check.
Solution
Bisection gives $\Delta_{\text{crit}}\approx129$ ms, matching the quartic's $130$ ms; the crossing frequency is $\omega_c\approx3.0\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$, i.e. $f_c\approx0.47$ Hz — higher than the $0.35$ Hz of the softer gains, since stiffer control sways faster. Simulation and Proposition 6.3 agree to the millisecond.
K2. Map the stability island in $(K_p,K_d)$ by grid simulation for $\Delta=0.12$ s and $\Delta=0.18$ s, and compute the ratio of their areas. Probes: how strongly delay shrinks the island.
Solution
Grid-simulating stability over $(K_p,K_d)$ and summing the stable cells gives areas in the ratio ${\approx}\,4.6$ ($\Delta=0.12$ island is about $4.6\times$ the $\Delta=0.18$ island). A $50\%$ longer delay costs roughly four-fifths of the usable gain space — the island shrinks far faster than the delay grows.
K3. A patient's quiet-standing sway is measured at $0.40$ Hz. Assuming the sway is a marginal Hopf oscillation with derivative gain $K_d=100$, infer the proportional gain $K_p$ and the implied effective delay. Probes: inverse problem from an observable.
Solution
Invert the boundary relations: from $K_p^2=(Mg\ell)^2+I^2\omega^4+(2IMg\ell-K_d^2)\omega^2$ with $\omega=2\pi(0.40)$ one gets $K_p\approx1004\ \mathrm{N\,m\,rad^{-1}}$, and $\Delta=\omega^{-1}\arctan(K_d\omega/K_p)\approx98$ ms. The observed frequency alone, plus one gain, pins down both the other gain and the loop delay.
K4. By how much does the tolerable neural delay change between a $1.6$ m and a $1.9$ m person? Model the body as a point mass with $\ell=0.55\,h$ and sweep. Probes: sensitivity to body scale.
Solution
With $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$ and $\ell=0.55h$: $\omega_0$ falls from $3.34\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ at $1.6$ m to $3.06\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ at $1.9$ m — about $9\%$ slower. Since the toppling clock and the tolerable delay scale as $1/\omega_0\propto\sqrt{h}$, the taller person tolerates ${\approx}\,9\%$ more delay: a bigger inverted pendulum falls more slowly. (This is why toddlers, short and fast-toppling, wobble more than adults.)
K5. For a fixed delay $\Delta=0.15$ s, search the gain plane for the pair $(K_p,K_d)$ that gives the fastest stable recovery (largest decay rate) from a perturbation. Probes: optimisation over the stable island.
Solution
Simulating the DDE across the island and fitting the envelope decay rate, the optimum sits near $K_p\approx940$, $K_d\approx260\ \mathrm{N\,m\,s\,rad^{-1}}$, with a decay rate of ${\approx}\,1.5\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ (recovery time constant ${\approx}\,0.7$ s). The best gains lie well inside the island, not at its edge — pushing $K_p$ higher trades decay rate for proximity to the oscillatory boundary.
K6. A forward shove imparts a horizontal impulse to the standing body. Using the capturability condition, compute the largest impulse that can be arrested without a step. Probes: XcoM applied to a disturbance.
Solution
An impulse $J$ gives $\dot x_{\text{com}}=J/M$ at $x_{\text{com}}\approx0$, so $\xi=\dot x_{\text{com}}/\omega_0=J/(M\omega_0)$. Recovery needs $\xi\le d_{\text{toe}}\approx0.10$ m, i.e. $J\le M\omega_0 d_{\text{toe}}$. With $M=70$ kg, $\omega_0=3.06\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$: the COM velocity limit is ${\approx}\,0.31\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ and the maximum recoverable impulse ${\approx}\,21$ N·s. Beyond it a step is unavoidable.
K7. Simulate a quiet-standing sway signal and compute the RMS excursion of the COP and of the COM. Which is larger, and by how much? Probes: COP-vs-COM statistics.
Solution
Building the COM as band-limited sway and the COP via $x_{\text{cop}}=x_{\text{com}}-\ddot x_{\text{com}}/\omega_0^2$ (Prop 3.3), a representative run gives COP RMS ${\approx}\,6.6$ mm against COM RMS ${\approx}\,4.5$ mm — the COP swings about $1.5\times$ wider, because differentiation amplifies the higher-frequency content the COP must supply.
K8. To stiffen from $K_p=Mg\ell$ to $K_p=1.5\,Mg\ell$ by co-contraction, estimate the relative metabolic cost, taking cost proportional to total muscle activation. Probes: regime comparison, stiffness vs. economy.
Solution
Joint stiffness adds as $K_p=k_a+k_b$ with each $k_i$ proportional to its muscle's activation (Prop 7.3), and metabolic cost scales with total activation $\propto k_a+k_b=K_p$. Raising $K_p$ by $50\%$ therefore raises the co-contraction cost by ${\approx}\,50\%$ for no change in net torque or work — pure overhead paid for robustness. This is why we destiffen as soon as a surface feels secure.
K9. Compute the heaviest load that may be lifted at a reach of $0.35$ m without the worst-case L5/S1 compression exceeding $3.4$ kN. Probes: inverse of the compression model.
Solution
Bisecting on load with the peak-over-flexion compression of Proposition 8.2 gives a maximum safe load of ${\approx}\,18.9$ kg at $r_L=0.35$ m — down from ${\approx}\,42$ kg at $0.15$ m. The safe load falls steeply with reach, tracing the envelope of Fig. 20.
K10. For the reference lift ($m_L=20$ kg, $r_L=0.40$ m, $m_t=35$ kg), compute the elasticity of spinal compression with respect to load mass, reach, and trunk mass. Which factor dominates, and is that intuitive? Probes: sensitivity ranking.
Solution
The elasticities (fractional change in $F_{\text{spine}}$ per fractional change in each input) come out to ${\approx}\,0.43$ for both load mass and reach, and ${\approx}\,0.57$ for trunk mass. The trunk's own weight dominates — counter-intuitively — because its moment $m_t r_t=10.5$ exceeds the load's $m_L r_L=8.0$: at this posture your upper body loads the spine more than the box does. Reducing your trunk lever (bending less, using the legs) beats shaving the load.

The Human Body as a Controlled Mechanical–Biological System

A quantitative, mathematically rigorous course on the human musculoskeletal system — modeling, governing equations, derivations, and Python-computed simulations.

  1. Module 1Mechanical Foundations of Human Biomechanics Forces, moments, free-body diagrams, static equilibrium; the torque to hold a load and the muscle force behind it.
  2. Module 2Bones as Hierarchical Load-Bearing Structures Stress and strain, Euler–Bernoulli bending, fracture in a fall, and the remodeling feedback that tunes bone to its loads.
  3. Module 3Joints as Constrained Mechanical Interfaces Degrees of freedom, constraint forces and Lagrange multipliers, joint reaction forces.
  4. Module 4Cartilage, Synovial Fluid, and Joint Contact Biophysics Charged porous composite, Donnan swelling pressure, the biphasic consolidation equation, interstitial fluid load support, stress relaxation and creep, contact pressure, lubrication and friction, and osteoarthritis as loss of fluid support.
  5. Module 5Muscles as Chemo-Electro-Mechanical Actuators From the calcium switch in a sarcomere to a Hill-type muscle model: architecture and pennation, the length–tension and force–velocity laws, excitation–contraction coupling, motor-unit recruitment and rate coding, activation dynamics, and joint torque from neural drive.
  6. Module 6Tendons, Ligaments, Fascia, and Elastic Energy Storage Passive collagen springs: crimp and the J-shaped law, the tendon as a nonlinear spring and its stored energy, the series-elastic element that lets muscle act as a catapult, viscoelasticity (Maxwell / Kelvin–Voigt / standard linear solid) and hysteresis, and ligaments, fascia, and the foot arch.
  7. Module 7Standing, Posture, and Load Bearing Why standing is an unstable equilibrium held by an active, delayed feedback loop: the inverted-pendulum body, centre of pressure, the instability and its toppling clock, proportional–derivative ankle control, the neural-delay stability boundary that explains sway, the hip strategy and co-contraction, and the spinal cost of bearing load.
  8. Module 8Walking Biomechanics Walking as controlled falling: the gait cycle, the inverted-pendulum COM vault and the Froude speed limit, ground reaction and the rolling centre of pressure, the compass-gait impact map, the step-to-step transition cost and pre-emptive push-off, inverse-dynamics joint torques and power, preferred-speed energetics, arm swing, passive dynamic walking, and older-adult falls as a physics–chemistry–biology stack.
  9. Module 9Running and Jumping Running as a spring-mass bounce: the spring-loaded inverted pendulum, stance and flight, the ground reaction force, the walk-to-run transition, impulse and momentum, jump height from takeoff velocity, the stretch-shortening cycle and tendon recoil, landing force absorption, and impact loading and injury risk.
  10. Module 10Balance, Stability, and Sensorimotor Control Balance as active feedback control under delay and noise: the margin of stability and extrapolated centre of mass, the state-space inverted pendulum, the sensory channels as noisy delayed measurements, delayed-feedback instability and its stability island, stochastic sway, the Kalman-filter estimate of body state, the recovery ladder from ankle to hip to step, slips versus trips, and aging as the drift of four control parameters.
  11. Module 11Reaching, Waving, Holding, Gripping & Manipulation The arm and hand as a redundant manipulator: forward kinematics of the two-link chain, the Jacobian and its transpose (τ = Jᵀ F), inverse kinematics with two elbow solutions and edge-of-reach singularities, the velocity and force ellipsoids, kinematic redundancy and the null space, the inverse dynamics of a wave, the shoulder as mobility bought with stability, the friction cone and force closure that decide a grip, and the tactile grip-load loop closed by a slip reflex.
  12. Module 12Whole-Body Coordination and Motor Control How the nervous system chooses one movement from infinitely many: Bernstein's degrees-of-freedom problem and the null space, movement as optimal control, the minimum-jerk trajectory, the linear-quadratic regulator, impedance control and the arm as a programmable spring, internal models and feedforward prediction, optimal feedback control and the minimal-intervention principle, muscle synergies and central pattern generators, and human versus robot control.
  13. Module 13Daily-Life Movement Case Studies The mechanics of Modules 1-12 applied to an ordinary day: rising from a chair (knee torque and the momentum strategy), climbing and descending stairs (work, power, eccentric control), lifting and carrying (spinal compression and the safe-lift envelope), turning and catching a stumble (impulse and the recovery step), opening a jar and pushing a door (grip and hinge torque), walking a slope, and how aging shrinks each margin.
  14. Module 14Aging, Injury, Degeneration, and Adaptation Aging as the drift of measurable mechanical parameters, each shrinking a reserve some task already spends: the capacity-over-demand margin, sarcopenia and the strength reserve, osteoporosis and fragility fracture (strength ∝ density², impact vs bone strength), the osteoarthritis cartilage-stress cycle, sensorimotor slowing and the fall-risk model, the mechanostat by which load reverses the drift, and the coupled cascade of frailty.
  15. Module 15Measurement, Estimation, and Inverse Dynamics Recovering the joint torques the course assumed from noisy data: the measurement chain (motion capture, phone video, force plates, IMUs, EMG), the planar marker model and body-segment parameters, why differentiation amplifies noise and the optimal filter cutoff, the centre of mass and its force-plate validation, the Newton-Euler inverse-dynamics recursion, the assembled measurement-to-torque pipeline, error and uncertainty propagation, and parameter identification and model validation.
  16. Module 16Continuum and Finite-Element-Style Tissue Models The three-dimensional mechanics beneath the beams, springs, and biphasic gels: strain and the deformation gradient, the Cauchy stress tensor and equilibrium, linear elasticity and strain energy, the bar and beam finite elements and their assembly, anisotropy and fibre reinforcement, hyperelasticity at finite strain, viscoelasticity and poroelasticity, and bone, tendon, and cartilage as continua.
  17. Module 17Capstone modeling projects (to come)

0. Motivation — a femur that carries tonnes yet snaps in a fall

The same femur routinely survives gait forces of several times body weight, millions of cycles per year, with a large margin to spare — yet in an elderly person a sideways fall from standing height can break it. Two puzzles sit here. First: how does a tube of bone carry such loads without yielding? Second: why does the safe everyday bone become fragile, and why does the fracture appear in bending/impact rather than in the much larger compression of standing?

The answers are the content of this module. We take the muscle and joint forces derived in Module 1 as the loads, run them through beam theory to get internal stress, compare against bone's strength to get a safety factor, and then let the bone respond: the remodeling feedback that builds bone where strain is high and removes it where strain is low. That feedback, run forward in time, explains both training-induced strengthening and the bone loss of disuse and aging that sets up the fall-fracture in the first place.

1. Stress, strain, and the elastic law

SI units throughout: stress in pascals (Pa $=\mathrm{N\,m^{-2}}$; bone strengths run to $10^8\ \mathrm{Pa}=100\ \mathrm{MPa}$), strain dimensionless (often quoted in microstrain, $1\ \mu\varepsilon=10^{-6}$).

Definition 1.1 (stress). Stress $\sigma$ is internal force per unit area on a surface inside the material, $\sigma=F/A$ for a force $F$ normal to area $A$. Normal stress is tensile (pulling apart, $\sigma\gt 0$) or compressive ($\sigma\lt 0$); a force parallel to the surface gives shear stress $\tau$.
Definition 1.2 (strain). Strain $\varepsilon$ is fractional deformation: for a bar of length $L$ stretched by $\Delta L$, $\varepsilon=\Delta L/L$. It is dimensionless.
Definition 1.3 (Young's modulus, Hooke's law). In the small-strain elastic regime, stress is proportional to strain: $$\sigma = E\,\varepsilon,\tag{1.1}$$ where $E$ (Pa) is Young's modulus, the material's axial stiffness. For cortical (dense, outer) bone $E\approx 17\ \mathrm{GPa}$ along the long axis; trabecular (spongy, internal) bone is far softer, $E\approx 0.1$–$2\ \mathrm{GPa}$. Bone is anisotropic ($E$ depends on direction) and heterogeneous ($E$ varies with position); we use an effective longitudinal value and revisit its origin in §5.

With (1.1) as the constitutive law, the rest of the module is bookkeeping of where in a loaded bone $\sigma$ is largest, and how that compares with the stress bone can bear.

2. Axial loading: why compression is almost free

Model the femoral midshaft as a hollow circular tube (the medullary canal is the hole), outer radius $R$, inner radius $r$, cross-sectional area $A=\pi(R^2-r^2)$. Under a purely axial compressive force $F$ (Definition 1.1): $$\sigma_\text{axial}=\frac{F}{A},\qquad \varepsilon=\frac{\sigma}{E}.\tag{2.1}$$

Worked number. Single-leg stance loads the femur to roughly $2.5\times$ body weight including hip-muscle force (from Module 1, §6): $F\approx 2.5\times70\times9.81\approx 1715\ \mathrm N$. With $R=14\ \mathrm{mm}$, $r=7\ \mathrm{mm}$, $A=4.62\times10^{-4}\ \mathrm m^2$, so $\sigma_\text{axial}=1715/4.62\times10^{-4}\approx 3.7\ \mathrm{MPa}$. Against a compressive strength $\sim170\ \mathrm{MPa}$ that is a safety factor of $\sim45$. Pure compression is not what breaks bones.

3. Bending: the dominant danger, and why bone is hollow

Body weight rarely acts exactly along the bone axis; offsets create a bending moment $M$ (the joint torques of Module 1, now applied to the shaft). Bending, not compression, produces the largest stresses. We derive the Euler–Bernoulli result.

Definition 3.1 (neutral axis, second moment of area). Under pure bending the cross-section rotates about a line through its centroid — the neutral axis — where strain is zero. The second moment of area about that axis is $I=\int_A y^2\,dA$, with $y$ the distance from the neutral axis. $I$ (units $\mathrm m^4$) measures how far material sits from the axis. The section modulus is $Z=I/c$, where $c$ is the distance to the outermost fibre.
Theorem 3.2 (Euler–Bernoulli bending stress). For a beam in pure bending with moment $M$, the axial stress varies linearly across the section: $$\boxed{\;\sigma(y)=\frac{M\,y}{I},\qquad \sigma_\max=\frac{M\,c}{I}=\frac{M}{Z}.\;}\tag{3.1}$$
Assume plane cross-sections remain plane and rotate by curvature $\kappa$. Measure $y$ from an as-yet-unknown zero-strain line; a fibre at $y$ then has strain $\varepsilon(y)=\kappa y$ (geometry of rotation), and by Hooke's law (1.1), $\sigma(y)=E\kappa y$ — linear in $y$. The neutral axis is the centroidal axis. Pure bending applies a moment but no net axial force, so the stresses must sum to zero force: $$\int_A \sigma\,dA = E\kappa\int_A y\,dA = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \int_A y\,dA = 0.$$ Since $\kappa\ne0$, the first moment of area about the zero-strain line vanishes — which is precisely the definition of the centroid. So the neutral axis passes through the section's centroid; for the symmetric hollow circle that is its centre. With $y$ now measured from the centroid, the internal stresses must reproduce the applied moment about that axis: $$M=\int_A y\,\sigma(y)\,dA = E\kappa\int_A y^2\,dA = E\kappa\,I,$$ using Definition 3.1. Hence $E\kappa=M/I$, and substituting back into $\sigma=E\kappa y$ gives $\sigma(y)=My/I$. The extreme fibre at $y=c$ carries $\sigma_\max=Mc/I=M/Z$. ∎

For the hollow circular section, $I=\tfrac{\pi}{4}(R^4-r^4)$ and $c=R$. Because $I$ weights area by $y^2$, moving material outward (hollowing the centre, which sits near the neutral axis where it does little) buys bending strength per unit mass.

Proposition 3.3 (hollow beats solid). A hollow tube and a solid rod of the same cross-sectional area (hence same mass per length) have section moduli in ratio $Z_\text{hollow}/Z_\text{solid}\approx 1.44$ for the femoral proportions $R/r=2$. The hollow bone is $\sim44\%$ stronger in bending at no extra mass — the engineering reason long bones are tubes.
Let the tube have outer radius $R$, inner radius $r$, and let the solid rod have radius $a$. Equal cross-sectional area (equal mass per length) requires $\pi a^2=\pi(R^2-r^2)$, i.e. $$a^2=R^2-r^2.$$ The section moduli $Z=I/c$ are, using $I=\tfrac{\pi}{4}(R^4-r^4)$, $c=R$ for the tube and $I=\tfrac{\pi}{4}a^4$, $c=a$ for the rod: $$Z_\text{hollow}=\frac{\pi}{4}\,\frac{R^4-r^4}{R},\qquad Z_\text{solid}=\frac{\pi}{4}\,a^3=\frac{\pi}{4}\,(R^2-r^2)^{3/2}.$$ Introduce the bore ratio $\beta=r/R$. Then $R^4-r^4=R^4(1-\beta^4)$ and $(R^2-r^2)^{3/2}=R^3(1-\beta^2)^{3/2}$, so $$\frac{Z_\text{hollow}}{Z_\text{solid}}=\frac{R^3(1-\beta^4)}{R^3(1-\beta^2)^{3/2}}=\frac{1-\beta^4}{(1-\beta^2)^{3/2}}=(1+\beta^2)\,(1-\beta^2)^{-1/2},$$ where the last step factors $1-\beta^4=(1+\beta^2)(1-\beta^2)$. For the femoral proportions $R/r=2$, $\beta=\tfrac12$: the ratio is $(1+\tfrac14)(1-\tfrac14)^{-1/2}=\tfrac{5}{4}\big/\sqrt{\tfrac34}=1.443$. The factor $(1+\beta^2)(1-\beta^2)^{-1/2}$ exceeds $1$ for every $0\lt\beta\lt1$ and grows without bound as $\beta\to1$: the thinner the wall (larger bore) at fixed area, the greater the bending advantage. ∎

Which strength sets the limit? Bending loads one face in tension and the opposite face in equal-magnitude compression (Fig. 1). But cortical bone is not equally strong in the two senses: it bears $\sim170\ \mathrm{MPa}$ in compression (§2) yet only $\sim120$–$150\ \mathrm{MPa}$ in tension — a strength anisotropy layered on top of the stiffness anisotropy of §5. The two faces reach $\sigma_\max$ together, so the tensile face is the one that yields first. Bending failure is governed by the tensile strength. That is why the yield line drawn below sits at the tensile value $\sim130\ \mathrm{MPa}$, not the larger compressive $170\ \mathrm{MPa}$, and why the safety factor of $\sim1.3$ — not $\sim1.8$ — is the honest one. This same tension-first rule reappears in torsion (§4), where the crack opens on the surface of maximum tensile stress.

The animation shows the mechanism behind (3.1): as the beam curves, fibres far from the neutral axis stretch or compress most, so they carry the most stress.

4. Torsion: shear, the angle of twist, and the spiral fracture

A planted foot while the trunk rotates — a ski catching an edge, a tackle, a stumble with a fixed shoe — applies a torque $T$ about the bone's long axis. Bending stretched fibres along the axis; torsion instead shears them around it. The analysis runs perfectly parallel to bending, with shear strain replacing axial strain and the polar second moment of area replacing $I$.

Definition 4.1 (rate of twist, shear modulus). Let the shaft twist so each cross-section rotates rigidly about the axis, the rotation angle $\varphi(z)$ increasing along the axis $z$. The rate of twist is $\theta=d\varphi/dz$ (rad m$^{-1}$). A material fibre at radius $\rho$ from the axis is then sheared through angle $\gamma(\rho)=\rho\,\theta$ (the arc $\rho\,d\varphi$ over the length $dz$). Shear stress follows the elastic shear law $\tau=G\,\gamma$, where $G$ (Pa) is the shear modulus — the stiffness against shape change, the shear analogue of Young's modulus $E$. For cortical bone $G\approx 3.3\ \mathrm{GPa}$ (roughly $E/5$).
Proposition 4.1 (torsion formula and angle of twist). For a circular shaft under torque $T$, the shear stress grows linearly with radius, and the twist accumulates linearly with length: $$\boxed{\;\tau(\rho)=\frac{T\rho}{J},\qquad \tau_\max=\frac{T R}{J},\qquad \varphi=\frac{T L}{G\,J},\qquad J=\frac{\pi}{2}(R^4-r^4).\;}\tag{4.1}$$ $J=\int_A\rho^2\,dA$ is the polar second moment of area; for a circle $J=2I$ (it sums $I$ about two perpendicular diameters).
By Definition 4.1, $\tau(\rho)=G\gamma=G\rho\theta$ — linear in $\rho$, exactly as bending stress was linear in $y$. The internal shear stresses must reproduce the applied torque about the axis (each element contributes force $\tau\,dA$ at moment arm $\rho$): $$T=\int_A \rho\,\tau(\rho)\,dA = G\theta\int_A \rho^2\,dA = G\theta\,J,$$ using $J=\int_A\rho^2\,dA$. For the hollow circle, $\int_A\rho^2\,dA=\int_r^R \rho^2\,(2\pi\rho\,d\rho)=\tfrac{\pi}{2}(R^4-r^4)$. Hence the rate of twist is $\theta=T/(GJ)$; substituting into $\tau=G\rho\theta$ gives $\tau(\rho)=T\rho/J$, maximal at the surface $\rho=R$. Integrating $\theta$ over a shaft of length $L$ (constant $T$, $J$) gives the total angle of twist $\varphi=\theta L=TL/(GJ)$. ∎

Why the fracture spirals at 45°. The torsion formula gives a state of pure shear at the bone surface: on the axial and circumferential planes there is shear $\tau$ but no normal stress. Rotate that surface element by $45^\circ$, however, and the shear resolves into pure normal stresses — a principal tension $\sigma_1=+\tau$ along one $45^\circ$ diagonal and an equal principal compression $\sigma_2=-\tau$ along the other (the Mohr's-circle result for pure shear; derived in §10, D7). Because bone is brittle and — as §3 established — weakest in tension, the crack opens on the plane perpendicular to $\sigma_1$. That plane wraps the shaft as a $45^\circ$ helix: the classic spiral fracture (Fig. 4). A ductile metal, failing instead on the plane of maximum shear, breaks flat across the axis — bone does not.

Worked numbers. For the femoral tube ($R=14$ mm, $r=7$ mm), $J=\tfrac{\pi}{2}(R^4-r^4)=5.66\times10^{-8}\ \mathrm m^4$. An everyday pivoting torque $T\approx 30\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ gives $\tau_\max=TR/J\approx 7.4\ \mathrm{MPa}$ and, over a $0.40\ \mathrm m$ shaft, an angle of twist $\varphi=TL/(GJ)\approx 3.7^\circ$ — small and safe. A violent torsional load $T\approx 150\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ raises $\tau_\max$ to $\approx 37\ \mathrm{MPa}$. Setting $\tau_\max$ to bone's torsional (shear) strength $\sim 65\ \mathrm{MPa}$ gives a failure torque $T_f=\tau_f J/R\approx 260\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ for this idealized stout tube — real femora, with thinner cortices, fail nearer $140$–$180\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, so a hard rotational fall lands within a safety factor of $\sim 2$ of a spiral fracture.

5. Where $E$ comes from: the mineral–collagen composite

The modulus $E\approx17\ \mathrm{GPa}$ used above is not fundamental — it emerges from bone's two-phase nanostructure. Type-I collagen fibrils (compliant, $E\sim1$ GPa, but tough) are reinforced by plate-shaped crystals of hydroxyapatite, $\mathrm{Ca_{10}(PO_4)_6(OH)_2}$ (stiff, $E\sim100$ GPa, but brittle). The result is a classic stiff-filler/ductile-matrix composite: its modulus lies between the two, near a volume-fraction average, while its toughness exceeds either phase because cracks are deflected and bridged at interfaces.

PhaseRoleMechanical contribution
Hydroxyapatite mineral ($\sim$50% vol)Stiff reinforcementRaises $E$ and compressive strength; brittle alone
Type-I collagen ($\sim$40% vol)Ductile matrixTensile toughness, energy absorption, crack bridging
Water / non-collagenous proteinsInterface, sacrificial bondsViscoelasticity, damage tolerance

Validating the 17 GPa. Two elementary bounds check that a mineral–collagen mix can plausibly give $E\approx17\ \mathrm{GPa}$. The Voigt (isostrain, phases in parallel) bound is the volume-weighted average $E_V=v_m E_m+(1-v_m)E_c$; the Reuss (isostress, phases in series) bound is the harmonic average $E_R=\big(v_m/E_m+(1-v_m)/E_c\big)^{-1}$. With mineral $E_m\approx100\ \mathrm{GPa}$, collagen $E_c\approx1\ \mathrm{GPa}$, and $v_m\approx0.5$, these bracket $E_R\approx2.0\ \mathrm{GPa}\le E\le E_V\approx50\ \mathrm{GPa}$. The measured $17\ \mathrm{GPa}$ sits comfortably inside — nearer the Reuss floor, because the stiff mineral is discontinuous platelets, not continuous fibres, so it cannot fully share load in parallel. (The bounds are derived in §10, D9; the volume fraction is swept in K8.)

Mineralization is the controlled deposition of hydroxyapatite within the collagen scaffold; too little (osteomalacia, rickets) lowers $E$ and strength, too much (some treatments) raises stiffness but embrittles. Anisotropy ($E$ largest along the loaded axis) is itself an adaptation — the structural echo of the feedback loop in §7.

6. Failure: yield, fatigue, fracture, and the falling body

Three failure routes matter for daily life.

(i) Monotonic overload. If $\sigma_\max$ from (3.1) exceeds the yield/ultimate strength $\sigma_Y\ (\sim120$–$150$ MPa in tension), the bone yields then breaks. The safety factor is $n=\sigma_Y/\sigma_\max$; §3 showed $n\sim1.3$ in vigorous bending — bone runs a deliberately lean margin, trading mass for mobility.

(ii) Stress concentration & fatigue. A geometric notch (a screw hole, a cortical defect) raises local stress by a factor $K_t$, $\sigma_\text{local}=K_t\,\sigma_\text{nominal}$. Repeated loading drives fatigue: microcracks nucleate and grow, captured empirically by an S–N (stress vs. cycles-to-failure) curve $N_f\propto(\Delta\sigma)^{-m}$ with $m\approx5$–$8$ for bone. Living bone normally repairs this microdamage (§7); a stress fracture is fatigue outpacing repair.

(iii) Fracture mechanics. A crack of length $a$ fails when the stress intensity $K=Y\sigma\sqrt{\pi a}$ reaches the toughness $K_{Ic}\ (\sim2$–$6\ \mathrm{MPa\sqrt m}$ for bone), so the tolerable flaw size shrinks as load rises.

A fall-impact fracture model

Why a fall, not standing, breaks the hip: a sideways fall delivers a large force over a few centimetres of soft-tissue compression. Model the impact as energy capture by an effective spring (soft tissue + pelvis) of stiffness $k_s$ (the subscript $s$ keeps it distinct from the remodeling gain $k$ of §7; the symbol $E$ stays reserved for Young's modulus). An effective mass $m_\text{eff}$ falling a height $h$ (with $g$ the gravitational acceleration) arrives with kinetic energy $m_\text{eff}\,g\,h$; equating this to the spring's stored energy $\tfrac12 k_s x^2$ and using $F=k_s x$ gives the peak force $$\boxed{\;F_\text{peak}=\sqrt{2\,k_s\,m_\text{eff}\,g\,h}\;}\tag{6.1}$$

Worked number. $m_\text{eff}=28\ \mathrm{kg}$ (effective fraction of body in a sideways fall), $h=0.5\ \mathrm m$, $k_s=5\times10^{4}\ \mathrm{N\,m^{-1}}$: the impact energy is $\approx137\ \mathrm J$ and $F_\text{peak}\approx3.7\ \mathrm{kN}$. A healthy proximal femur tolerates $\sim4\ \mathrm{kN}$ (survives); an osteoporotic one $\sim2\ \mathrm{kN}$ (fractures). The same fall is safe or catastrophic depending on a strength the body itself sets over years (§7).

7. Adaptation: Wolff's law as a strain feedback loop

"Wolff's law" — bone adapts to the loads it carries — becomes quantitative as the mechanostat (Frost): bone tissue senses its own peak strain and adds or removes material to keep that strain in a target band. The mechanism is cellular:

  • Osteocytes — the sensor network buried in the matrix — detect strain-driven flow of interstitial fluid through the lacunar–canalicular system (the microscopic fluid channels housing the osteocytes and their processes) and convert it to chemical signals (mechanotransduction).
  • High strain suppresses sclerostin, releasing the brake on osteoblasts $\to$ bone formation. Low strain (disuse) raises the RANKL/OPG ratio, activating osteoclasts $\to$ bone resorption.
  • Between the two thresholds lies a "lazy zone" where formation and resorption balance and net mass is steady.
Model 7.1 (remodeling ODE). Let the section modulus $Z$ stand for "how much bone, well placed" (it sets bending strength via (3.1)). Peak strain under habitual moment $M$ is $\varepsilon(Z)=M/(Z\,E)$. The mechanostat is modelled (a stated constitutive law, not a derived theorem) as $$\frac{dZ}{dt}= \begin{cases} k\,Z\,\bigl(\varepsilon-\varepsilon_\text{hi}\bigr), & \varepsilon\gt \varepsilon_\text{hi}\ \text{(formation)}\\[2pt] 0, & \varepsilon_\text{lo}\le\varepsilon\le\varepsilon_\text{hi}\ \text{(lazy zone)}\\[2pt] k\,Z\,\bigl(\varepsilon-\varepsilon_\text{lo}\bigr), & \varepsilon\lt \varepsilon_\text{lo}\ \text{(resorption)} \end{cases}\tag{7.1}$$ At equilibrium the bone grows or shrinks until strain re-enters the band: $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\,\varepsilon_\text{hi})$ after a load increase, $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\,\varepsilon_\text{lo})$ after disuse. The bone chases a target strain, not a target size.

8. Computational lab: the remodeling ODE

Physical question: how does a bone's strength evolve when habitual load changes? Biological question: can one feedback law produce both training gains and disuse loss? Model: integrate (7.1) from the healthy $Z$ for three activity levels (habitual, $+60\%$ load, $-60\%$ disuse). Parameters in the Appendix.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

R, r = 0.014, 0.007
I_h = np.pi*(R**4 - r**4)/4
Z0 = I_h/R                          # initial section modulus
E = 17e9
eps_lo, eps_hi = 1000e-6, 1500e-6   # lazy-zone strain band
k = 80.0                            # remodeling gain (1/strain/day)
M_ref = 1250e-6 * Z0 * E            # habitual moment -> mid-band strain


def rate(Z, M):
    e = M/(Z*E)
    if e > eps_hi:
        return k*Z*(e - eps_hi)     # formation
    if e < eps_lo:
        return k*Z*(e - eps_lo)     # resorption (negative)
    return 0.0


def simulate(scale, days=400, dt=1.0):
    Z = Z0
    out = []
    for _ in range(days+1):
        out.append(Z)
        Z = max(Z + rate(Z, scale*M_ref)*dt, 1e-8)
    return np.array(out)


t = np.arange(401)
cases = [(1.6, "+60% load"), (1.0, "habitual"), (0.4, "disuse -60%")]
for scale, lab in cases:
    plt.plot(t, 100*simulate(scale)/Z0, label=lab)
plt.xlabel("time (days)")
plt.ylabel("section modulus (% of initial)")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(alpha=0.3)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("m2_remodel.png")

Output (computed): increased load drives a $+33\%$ gain, disuse a $-50\%$ loss, habitual load is a stable equilibrium — all from the single law (7.1).

Sensitivity analysis

The equilibrium $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\varepsilon^*)$ is inversely proportional to the strain setpoint $\varepsilon^*$ and proportional to load $M$: a $10\%$ rise in habitual load yields a $10\%$ stronger bone at steady state, while a drugs/aging-driven rise in the setpoint $\varepsilon^*$ (the bone "tolerating" more strain before building) lowers $Z_\text{eq}$ — a mechanism for age-related fragility independent of load. The gain $k$ sets only the speed of approach, not the destination: doubling $k$ halves the adaptation time but leaves $Z_\text{eq}$ unchanged.

Extension challenge. Couple this module to §6: drive the bone with a daily load and a slow age-related rise in $\varepsilon^*$, track $Z(t)$ to age 80, then at each age compute the fall force (6.1) against the strength implied by $Z$. At what age does the safety factor cross 1? Which intervention — more load (raising $M$) or lowering $\varepsilon^*$ — buys more years?

9. What the model captures and misses

Captures: correct stress magnitudes and the compression-vs-bending asymmetry; the structural logic of hollow bones; a lean safety factor; one feedback law reproducing both training gains and disuse loss; the qualitative fall-fracture crossover with age.

10. Diagnostics and problem set

Thirty problems — ten conceptual (C), ten derivational (D), ten computational (K) — preceded by five diagnostics. Each problem carries a figure, a "Probes" note naming the idea it exercises, and a collapsible solution; every computational solution reports Python-verified numbers with runnable code. Diagnostics have short model answers; problems have full solutions.

Five diagnostic questions

  1. Why is the safety factor against compression ($\sim45$) so much larger than against bending ($\sim1.3$), and what does that imply about which loads bones are "designed" for?
    Answer

    Compression spreads a force over the whole area $A$ ($\sigma=F/A$), while bending concentrates it at the outer fibre through the much smaller section modulus $Z$ ($\sigma=M/Z$); a modest offset turns a small force into a large moment. Evolution tunes bone to the load that actually threatens it — bending and impact — so it runs a lean $\sim1.3$ margin there and a wastefully large one in pure compression, which it never approaches.

  2. Two bones have equal mass per length; one is a solid rod, one a tube. Explain via $I=\int y^2\,dA$ why the tube is stronger in bending but identical in pure axial loading.
    Answer

    Axial stress is $F/A$, and the two share the same $A$ (same mass), so they are identical in compression. Bending weights area by $y^2$: moving material outward to form the tube wall places it at larger $y$, raising $I$ and hence $Z=I/c$. Equal mass, more $I$ — the tube wins in bending only (Prop 3.3, factor $1.44$ at $R/r=2$).

  3. In the mechanostat, why does a bone reach a steady size even though the feedback is on strain? What sets the final size?
    Answer

    Adding bone lowers strain ($\varepsilon=M/(ZE)$ falls as $Z$ grows), so growth is self-limiting: it stops when strain re-enters the lazy zone. The steady size is $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\varepsilon^*)$ — set by the load $M$ and the strain setpoint $\varepsilon^*$, not by any target size. The bone chases a target strain.

  4. Why does a fall fracture the hip, when standing — a far longer-lasting load — does not? Use (6.1) and the energy/force distinction.
    Answer

    Standing applies a static force ($\sim2.5\times$ body weight, well inside the margin). A fall converts gravitational energy $m_\text{eff}gh$ into force over a few centimetres of soft-tissue compression; by (6.1) the peak force $\sqrt{2k_sm_\text{eff}gh}\approx3.7$ kN is dynamic and momentary but exceeds the osteoporotic strength. Duration is irrelevant; the impulsive force is what breaks bone.

  5. Astronauts lose bone in microgravity despite exercising. Which model parameter changes, and why does exercise only partly compensate?
    Answer

    Microgravity removes the gravitational component of habitual load $M$, dropping peak strain below $\varepsilon_\text{lo}$ into the resorption branch, so $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\varepsilon_\text{lo})$ falls. Resistive exercise restores load only during brief sessions and along limited directions, so the peak daily strain that drives the mechanostat is only partly recovered — net loss persists.

Conceptual (C1–C10) ↑ contents

C1. Two femoral cross-sections carry the same mass per length: the real tube ($R/r=2$) and a solid rod of equal area. Rank them for (a) axial stiffness, (b) bending strength, and (c) resistance to local buckling of the wall.

Probes: the $A$-vs-$I$ split — why the same mass gives equal axial but unequal bending performance, and the cost of going hollow.

Show solution

(a) Axial: identical. Axial stiffness is $EA$ and both share the same area $A$, so $\sigma=F/A$ and $\varepsilon$ are the same. (b) Bending: the tube wins. Bending weights area by $y^2$ (Def 3.1), and the tube's material sits at larger $y$, so $I$ and $Z=I/c$ are larger — by the factor $1.44$ of Prop 3.3. (c) Buckling: the solid rod wins. The tube's advantage comes from a thin wall, and thin walls buckle locally (a mode the solid rod lacks). Hollowing therefore trades a little buckling margin for bending efficiency; real cortices keep a wall thick enough that material yielding, not wall buckling, governs.

C2. On the bent femoral section, where is the bending stress largest and where is it zero? Explain why drilling out the central medullary bore costs almost none of the bending strength.

Probes: the linear stress field $\sigma=My/I$ and the $y^2$ weighting that makes central material nearly free to remove.

Show solution

Stress varies linearly with distance from the neutral (centroidal) axis, $\sigma(y)=My/I$: zero at the axis, maximal $\pm Mc/I$ at the outer fibres. Material near the centre sits at small $y$ and contributes to $I=\int y^2\,dA$ only in proportion to $y^2\to0$. Removing the central bore therefore subtracts almost nothing from $I$ (hence from $Z$) while removing real mass — and creating room for marrow. That is precisely why long bones are tubes.

C3. A sideways fall bends the femoral neck, loading one cortex in tension and the opposite in compression to equal magnitude $\sigma_\max$. Given compressive strength $\sim170$ MPa and tensile $\sim130$ MPa, on which face does the crack begin, and why?

Probes: strength anisotropy — why "tension governs bending" even though the two faces see the same stress magnitude.

Show solution

The crack starts on the tensile face. Both faces reach the same $\sigma_\max$ simultaneously, but they are compared against different limits: the tensile face against $\sim130$ MPa, the compressive against $\sim170$ MPa. The tensile face reaches its (smaller) limit first, so failure initiates there and the crack runs across the section. This is why §3 draws the yield line at the tensile value and why the honest bending safety factor uses $\sim130$ MPa, not $170$.

C4. A planted ski twists the tibia to failure. Explain why the fracture spirals along a $45^\circ$ helix, whereas a steel rod under the same torsion would shear flat across its axis.

Probes: pure shear $\to$ principal stresses at $45^\circ$, and the brittle-vs-ductile failure-plane selection.

Show solution

Torsion puts the surface in pure shear $\tau$ (Prop 4.1). On planes at $45^\circ$ this shear resolves into a principal tension $\sigma_1=+\tau$ and compression $\sigma_2=-\tau$ (Mohr, D7). Bone is brittle and weakest in tension, so it cracks on the plane perpendicular to $\sigma_1$ — a $45^\circ$ helix, the spiral fracture. Ductile steel instead yields on the plane of maximum shear (the transverse plane), giving a flat break. The material's failure mode, not the loading, chooses the plane.

C5. A healed surgical screw hole leaves a cortex whose nominal stress is only $40$ MPa (safety factor $\sim3$), yet it can still initiate a fracture. Explain, defining the stress-concentration factor $K_t$, and give the fatigue consequence.

Probes: geometric stress concentration $\sigma_\text{local}=K_t\sigma_\text{nom}$ and its role in fatigue crack nucleation.

Show solution

A hole forces the stress trajectories to crowd around it, raising the local stress to $\sigma_\text{local}=K_t\,\sigma_\text{nom}$ with $K_t\approx2$–$3$ for a circular hole. At $\sigma_\text{nom}=40$ MPa the wall sees $\sim100$ MPa locally — a safety factor that collapses from $\sim3$ to $\sim1.3$. Under cyclic load, this hot-spot is where fatigue microcracks nucleate first (the $\Delta\sigma^{-m}$ law of §6ii), so activity must stay limited until remodeling refills the defect.

C6. A runner who doubles weekly mileage abruptly develops a tibial stress fracture; the same total mileage reached gradually instead strengthens the bone. Explain why the rate of load increase, not the total, governs the outcome.

Probes: the competition of two timescales — per-cycle fatigue damage vs. weeks-slow remodeling repair/adaptation.

Show solution

Two processes race. Microdamage accumulates every loading cycle (fast, load-proportional); remodeling both repairs damage and adds bone, but over weeks to months (slow). An abrupt jump makes the damage rate exceed the repair rate, so cracks outgrow repair and coalesce into a stress fracture. A gradual ramp keeps peak strain inside the adaptive window, letting remodeling raise $Z$ (and lower strain) ahead of the damage — the same total load now strengthens. Rate is the control variable; the threshold rate is computed in K4.

C7. Two people load their femora to the same habitual peak strain, but one has twice the body mass (twice the bending moment $M$). Predict their steady section moduli. Then predict the effect of a drug that raises the strain setpoint $\varepsilon^*$.

Probes: $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\varepsilon^*)$ — the bone chases a target strain, so size tracks load and inversely tracks the setpoint.

Show solution

At equilibrium $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\varepsilon^*)$. Same $\varepsilon^*$, double $M$ $\Rightarrow$ double $Z$: the heavier person carries twice the bone at the same strain. A drug that raises $\varepsilon^*$ (bone tolerates more strain before building) lowers $Z_\text{eq}$ at fixed load — thinner, more fragile bone independent of activity level. Conversely an anti-resorptive that lowers $\varepsilon^*$ raises $Z_\text{eq}$; K2 compares this against simply loading more.

C8. Hydroxyapatite alone is stiff but brittle; collagen alone is compliant and weak. Bone is both stiff and tough. Explain how the two-phase nanostructure achieves a toughness exceeding either phase — and why stiffness, unlike toughness, cannot exceed a simple mixture bound.

Probes: composite mechanics — stiffness is bounded by rule-of-mixtures (Voigt/Reuss) but toughness is an interface/architecture property.

Show solution

Stiff mineral platelets carry load and set $E$; the compliant collagen matrix and the mineral–collagen interfaces deflect, bridge, and blunt advancing cracks, dissipating energy over a large process zone. Toughness thus arises from architecture (crack-tip shielding), so it can beat both phases. Stiffness cannot: it is a weighted average of the phase moduli and is rigorously bracketed by the Voigt (parallel) and Reuss (series) bounds (derived in D9), which $17$ GPa obeys.

C9. Explain why an elephant cannot have leg bones that are simply a gazelle's scaled up geometrically. Identify which stress grows with body size and by what power.

Probes: Galileo's square–cube scaling — bending stress rises as $\propto L$ under geometric similarity, forcing disproportionate stoutness.

Show solution

Under geometric similarity (every length $\times\lambda$): body weight $\propto$ volume $\propto\lambda^3$; the bending moment that weight applies over a limb $\propto\lambda^3\cdot\lambda=\lambda^4$; the section modulus $Z\propto d^3\propto\lambda^3$. Hence peak bending stress $\sigma\propto M/Z\propto\lambda^4/\lambda^3=\lambda$ — it grows linearly with size. A gazelle scaled to elephant size would exceed bone strength, so large animals adopt elastic similarity (diameter $\propto L^{3/2}$), i.e. disproportionately stout bones. Quantified in K6.

C10. Bone's modulus and strength are largest along its habitual loading axis, and the trabeculae inside the femoral head fan out along the joint-load lines. Explain why such directional material is efficient, and how it is a product of the §7 feedback.

Probes: anisotropy as optimized material placement — the trabecular architecture as a frozen map of principal-stress directions.

Show solution

Aligning stiff material with the dominant stress direction maximizes strength and stiffness per unit mass: material placed off-axis carries little of the load. The mechanostat senses strain and deposits (and orients) tissue where strain is high, so over time the trabeculae grow along the trajectories of principal stress. The fan of struts in the femoral head is therefore the frozen record of the habitual joint-load directions — Wolff's law made visible.

Derivational (D1–D10) ↑ contents

D1. For a rectangular cross-section of width $b$ and height $h$, derive the second moment of area $I$ about the centroidal axis by integrating $I=\int y^2\,dA$, and give the peak bending stress $\sigma_\max$ in terms of $M,b,h$.

Probes: the definition $I=\int y^2\,dA$ made concrete, and the section modulus $Z=I/c$.

Show solution

Take a horizontal strip at height $y$, thickness $dy$: $dA=b\,dy$. Then $$I=\int_{-h/2}^{h/2} y^2\,b\,dy = b\Big[\tfrac{y^3}{3}\Big]_{-h/2}^{h/2} = b\cdot\frac{2}{3}\Big(\frac{h}{2}\Big)^3=\frac{bh^3}{12}.$$ The outer fibre is at $c=h/2$, so $Z=I/c=\dfrac{bh^3/12}{h/2}=\dfrac{bh^2}{6}$ and $\sigma_\max=\dfrac{M}{Z}=\dfrac{6M}{bh^2}$. The $h^2$ in $Z$ is why beam depth buys strength faster than width.

D2. Prove that under pure bending (a moment with no net axial force) the neutral axis passes through the centroid of the cross-section.

Probes: the zero-axial-force condition $\int\sigma\,dA=0$ and its identification with the first moment of area.

Show solution

Plane sections rotate, so measured from the zero-strain line the stress is $\sigma(y)=E\kappa y$ (linear). Pure bending has no resultant axial force: $$\int_A \sigma\,dA = E\kappa\int_A y\,dA = 0.$$ Since $\kappa\ne0$, $\int_A y\,dA=0$. But $\int_A y\,dA=0$ is exactly the statement that the reference line passes through the centroid (the first moment of area vanishes there). Hence the neutral axis is the centroidal axis. $\;\blacksquare$

D3. Derive the polar second moment of area $J=\int_A\rho^2\,dA$ for a hollow circular section (outer $R$, inner $r$), and hence $I$, using a ring element.

Probes: integrating with the annular element $dA=2\pi\rho\,d\rho$, and the identity $J=2I$ for a circle.

Show solution

Use a thin ring of radius $\rho$, width $d\rho$: $dA=2\pi\rho\,d\rho$. Then $$J=\int_r^R \rho^2\,(2\pi\rho\,d\rho)=2\pi\Big[\tfrac{\rho^4}{4}\Big]_r^R=\frac{\pi}{2}(R^4-r^4).$$ By symmetry $I_x=I_y$ for a circle, and $J=I_x+I_y=2I$ (since $\rho^2=x^2+y^2$), so $I=J/2=\dfrac{\pi}{4}(R^4-r^4)$ — the value used throughout §3–§4.

D4. A hollow tube (outer $R$, inner $r$, bore ratio $\beta=r/R$) and a solid rod of equal cross-sectional area are compared in bending. Derive the section-modulus ratio and evaluate it at $\beta=\tfrac12$.

Probes: the equal-mass constraint $a^2=R^2-r^2$ and the closed form $(1+\beta^2)/\sqrt{1-\beta^2}$ behind Prop 3.3.

Show solution

Equal area gives $\pi a^2=\pi(R^2-r^2)$, so $a^2=R^2-r^2$. With $Z_\text{hollow}=\tfrac{\pi}{4}(R^4-r^4)/R$ and $Z_\text{solid}=\tfrac{\pi}{4}a^3=\tfrac{\pi}{4}(R^2-r^2)^{3/2}$, $$\frac{Z_\text{hollow}}{Z_\text{solid}}=\frac{R^4-r^4}{R\,(R^2-r^2)^{3/2}}=\frac{1-\beta^4}{(1-\beta^2)^{3/2}}=\frac{(1+\beta^2)(1-\beta^2)}{(1-\beta^2)^{3/2}}=\frac{1+\beta^2}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}.$$ At $\beta=\tfrac12$: $\dfrac{1+\tfrac14}{\sqrt{1-\tfrac14}}=\dfrac{5/4}{\sqrt{3}/2}=1.443$. It exceeds $1$ for all $0\lt\beta\lt1$ and diverges as $\beta\to1$ — thinner walls, more advantage (until buckling intervenes, C1).

D5. From $\sigma=My/I$ and Hooke's law derive the curvature–moment relation $\kappa=M/(EI)$. Then, for two geometrically similar bones scaled by $\lambda$, show how $EI$, the self-weight bending moment, the curvature, and the peak stress each scale — and interpret.

Probes: bending stiffness $EI$ and dimensional scaling — the bridge from beam theory to allometry (C9, K6).

Show solution

Plane-section geometry gives $\sigma=E\kappa y$; equating to $\sigma=My/I$ yields $\boxed{\kappa=M/(EI)}$, with $EI$ the bending stiffness. Scaling every length by $\lambda$: $I\propto d^4\propto\lambda^4$ so $EI\propto\lambda^4$; self-weight $\propto$ volume $\propto\lambda^3$ acting over a moment arm $\propto\lambda$ gives $M\propto\lambda^4$. Hence curvature $\kappa=M/EI\propto\lambda^4/\lambda^4=\lambda^0$ (similar bones bow to the same shape), but peak stress $\sigma_\max=Mc/I\propto\lambda^4\cdot\lambda/\lambda^4=\lambda$ grows with size. Stress, not deflection, is the scaling problem — the reason big animals need stouter bones.

D6. For a circular shaft, use $J=2I$ to compare torsion and bending. Show that under an applied moment of equal magnitude the surface shear stress is half the bending stress, while the angular compliance (twist per unit moment vs. curvature per unit moment) is larger by $E/2G$. Interpret for bone ($E/G\approx5$).

Probes: the parallel structure of $\sigma=My/I$ and $\tau=T\rho/J$, and the role of $G$ vs $E$.

Show solution

Surface stresses at equal moment magnitude $M=T$: $\sigma_\max=MR/I$, $\tau_\max=TR/J=TR/(2I)=\tfrac12\sigma_\max$ — torsion generates half the surface stress of bending, because a circle's material is twice as effective about the polar axis. Compliances: curvature $\kappa=M/(EI)$, rate of twist $\theta=T/(GJ)=T/(2GI)$; at $M=T$ their ratio is $\theta/\kappa=EI/(2GI)=E/(2G)$. For bone $E/G\approx5$, so $\theta/\kappa\approx2.5$: a bone twists far more compliantly than it bends, which is why torsional loads produce large angular deflections and the tension-driven spiral failure of §4.

D7. Starting from the plane-stress transformation $\sigma_n(\theta)=\tfrac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2}+\tfrac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\cos2\theta+\tau_{xy}\sin2\theta$, prove that a state of pure shear ($\sigma_x=\sigma_y=0,\ \tau_{xy}=\tau$) has principal stresses $\pm\tau$ oriented at $\pm45^\circ$.

Probes: Mohr's-circle result underlying the $45^\circ$ spiral fracture (§4, C4).

Show solution

With $\sigma_x=\sigma_y=0$ and $\tau_{xy}=\tau$, the transformation reduces to $\sigma_n(\theta)=\tau\sin2\theta$. This is extremal where $\dfrac{d\sigma_n}{d\theta}=2\tau\cos2\theta=0$, i.e. $2\theta=\pm90^\circ\Rightarrow\theta=\pm45^\circ$. There $\sigma_n(45^\circ)=+\tau$ (principal tension $\sigma_1$) and $\sigma_n(-45^\circ)=-\tau$ (principal compression $\sigma_2$). The principal directions are the $45^\circ$ diagonals — the tension diagonal is where a brittle, tension-weak material cracks, giving the helix. $\;\blacksquare$

D8. For the formation branch $dZ/dt=kZ(\varepsilon-\varepsilon_\text{hi})$ with $\varepsilon=M/(ZE)$, find the equilibrium $Z_\text{eq}$ and prove it is stable by linearizing. What sets the adaptation time constant?

Probes: fixed-point analysis of the remodeling ODE — why the bone converges to a strain-set size (§7).

Show solution

Substitute $\varepsilon=M/(ZE)$: $\dfrac{dZ}{dt}=kZ\Big(\dfrac{M}{ZE}-\varepsilon_\text{hi}\Big)=k\Big(\dfrac{M}{E}-\varepsilon_\text{hi}Z\Big)$ — linear in $Z$. Setting it to zero gives $Z_\text{eq}=\dfrac{M}{E\varepsilon_\text{hi}}$. Linearizing, $\dfrac{d}{dZ}\Big[k\big(\tfrac{M}{E}-\varepsilon_\text{hi}Z\big)\Big]=-k\varepsilon_\text{hi}\lt0$, so a perturbation $\delta=Z-Z_\text{eq}$ obeys $\dot\delta=-k\varepsilon_\text{hi}\,\delta$ and decays: the equilibrium is stable, with time constant $\tau=1/(k\varepsilon_\text{hi})$. The gain $k$ sets only the speed; $M$ and $\varepsilon_\text{hi}$ set the destination.

D9. Derive the Voigt (isostrain) and Reuss (isostress) bounds for the modulus of a two-phase composite with mineral fraction $v_m$, and evaluate the bracket for bone ($E_m=100$, $E_c=1$ GPa, $v_m=0.5$).

Probes: the parallel/series models that bracket $E$ and validate the $17$ GPa of §5.

Show solution

Voigt (parallel, equal strain $\varepsilon$): each phase carries $\sigma_i=E_i\varepsilon$; the area-averaged stress is $\sigma=v_mE_m\varepsilon+(1-v_m)E_c\varepsilon$, so $E_V=v_mE_m+(1-v_m)E_c$. Reuss (series, equal stress $\sigma$): strains add in proportion, $\varepsilon=v_m\sigma/E_m+(1-v_m)\sigma/E_c$, so $\dfrac1{E_R}=\dfrac{v_m}{E_m}+\dfrac{1-v_m}{E_c}$. Any real microstructure lies between: $E_R\le E\le E_V$. At $v_m=0.5$: $E_V=50.5$ GPa, $E_R=2.0$ GPa. The measured $17$ GPa sits inside, near the Reuss floor — consistent with discontinuous mineral platelets rather than continuous fibres. (Swept in K8.)

D10. Model a fall as an effective mass $m_\text{eff}$ dropping height $h$ onto a linear soft-tissue spring of stiffness $k_s$. Derive the peak impact force $F_\text{peak}=\sqrt{2k_sm_\text{eff}gh}$, and show it dwarfs the static weight.

Probes: energy $\to$ force conversion and why an impulsive load, not a large mass, breaks the hip (§6, K7).

Show solution

Gravitational energy released in the drop equals the spring's stored energy at peak compression $x$: $m_\text{eff}gh=\tfrac12k_sx^2$, so $x=\sqrt{2m_\text{eff}gh/k_s}$. The peak force is $F_\text{peak}=k_sx=\boxed{\sqrt{2k_sm_\text{eff}gh}}$. Its ratio to the static weight is $\dfrac{F_\text{peak}}{m_\text{eff}g}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2k_sh}{m_\text{eff}g}}$; with $k_s=5\times10^4$, $h=0.5$, $m_\text{eff}=28$ this is $\approx13$ — the fall delivers $\sim13\times$ the body's static weight. Note $F_\text{peak}\propto\sqrt{k_s}$: a softer landing (hip pad, flexed muscles) lowers the force, the design lever exploited in K7.

K1 (regime comparison). Integrate the remodeling ODE (7.1) from the healthy bone under a $+60\%$ load applied two ways: as an abrupt step, and as a linear ramp over $50$ days. Compare the peak transient strain in each and relate it to stress-fracture risk.

Probes: transient overshoot — why the same final load is safe when ramped but injurious when stepped (the quantitative core of C6).

Show solution 
import numpy as np
Z0, E, k = 2.02e-6, 17e9, 80.0
eps_lo, eps_hi = 1000e-6, 1500e-6
M_ref = 1250e-6*Z0*E


def rate(Z, M):
    e = M/(Z*E)
    if e > eps_hi:
        return k*Z*(e - eps_hi)
    if e < eps_lo:
        return k*Z*(e - eps_lo)
    return 0.0


def peak_strain(load, ramp_days, days=200, dt=0.5):
    Z, emax = Z0, 0.0
    for i in range(int(days/dt) + 1):
        frac = min(i*dt/ramp_days, 1.0) if ramp_days else 1.0
        M = M_ref + (load - 1)*M_ref*frac
        emax = max(emax, M/(Z*E))
        Z = max(Z + rate(Z, M)*dt, 1e-9)
    return round(emax*1e6)


print(peak_strain(1.6, 0), peak_strain(1.6, 50))
# -> 2000 1602   (microstrain)

Result: the step overshoots to $\varepsilon_\text{peak}=2000\ \mu\varepsilon$ — $33\%$ past the $1500\ \mu\varepsilon$ formation setpoint, deep in the microdamage regime — while the ramp peaks at $1602\ \mu\varepsilon$, barely above setpoint (ratio $1.25$). The bone cannot thicken faster than $\sim$weeks, so an abrupt load spends days at injurious strain before $Z$ catches up; the ramp keeps strain in the adaptive window throughout. Rate, not magnitude, is the risk variable.

K2 (threshold + intervention optimization). Starting from peak bone mass at age $30$, let the strain setpoint drift up at $0.2\%$/yr (aging). Using $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\varepsilon^*)$ and the fall force (6.1), find the age at which the fall safety factor crosses $1$. Then compare two interventions of equal size — $+10\%$ habitual load vs. $-10\%$ setpoint — for which buys more years.

Probes: $Z_\text{eq}\propto M/\varepsilon^*$ — a fractional change in the setpoint beats the same fractional change in load, a non-obvious therapeutic ranking.

Show solution 
import numpy as np
E, Z0 = 17e9, 2.02e-6
M_ref = 1250e-6*Z0*E
meff, h, g = 28.0, 0.5, 9.81
Fpeak = np.sqrt(2*5e4*meff*g*h)      # 3706 N
S0 = 4000.0                           # healthy strength at Z0


def cross_age(load=1.0, lower=0.0, drift=0.002):
    eps0 = 1250e-6*(1 - lower)
    M = load*M_ref
    for yr in np.arange(0, 130, 0.25):
        Z = M/(E*eps0*(1 + drift*yr))
        if S0*(Z/Z0)/Fpeak < 1:
            return 30 + yr
    return None


print(cross_age(), cross_age(load=1.1), cross_age(lower=0.1))
# -> 69.75 123.75 129.75

Result: the baseline safety factor (healthy $\approx1.08$) crosses $1$ near age 70. Both interventions push the crossover far out, but they are not equal: $Z_\text{eq}=M/(E\varepsilon^*)$, so $+10\%$ load raises strength $10.0\%$ while $-10\%$ setpoint raises it $1/0.9-1=11.1\%$. Lowering the setpoint (an anti-resorptive that makes bone build at lower strain) buys $\sim6$ more years than the equal-percentage loading. The lever is the ratio $M/\varepsilon^*$, and $\varepsilon^*$ enters inversely.

K4 (critical-rate threshold). Model fatigue with $N_f=C\,\sigma^{-m}$ ($m=6$) and Miner's rule, against a remodeling repair rate. As weekly running mileage rises at rate $\rho$ (%/week), find the critical $\rho$ at which per-day microdamage first outpaces repair.

Em, Ec = 100e9, 1e9
vm = np.linspace(0, 1, 1001)
Ev = vm*Em + (1 - vm)*Ec
Er = 1/(vm/Em + (1 - vm)/Ec)
print(round(vm[np.argmin(abs(Ev - 17e9))], 2),
      round(vm[np.argmin(abs(Er - 17e9))], 2))
# -> 0.16 0.95

Result: the parallel (Voigt) model reaches $17$ GPa with only $16\%$ mineral; the series (Reuss) model needs $95\%$. Real bone is $\sim\!50\%$ mineral, and its measured modulus falls between the bounds, much closer to the Reuss floor. That position is diagnostic: it says the stiff mineral is present as discontinuous platelets that cannot share load fully in parallel, so the composite behaves nearer the isostress limit. The inverse read of the bounds recovers microstructure, not just a modulus.

K9 (parameter identification). Given noisy serial DXA measurements of section modulus during bed rest, recover the two mechanostat parameters — remodeling gain $k$ and disuse load fraction — by least-squares fitting the ODE (7.1) trajectory to the data.

Probes: the inverse problem — turning a monitoring time-series into the underlying feedback parameters.

Show solution 
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize
E, Z0 = 17e9, 2.02e-6
M_ref = 1250e-6*Z0*E
eps_lo, eps_hi = 1000e-6, 1500e-6


def traj(k, sc, days=300):
    Z, out = Z0, []
    for _ in range(days + 1):
        out.append(Z)
        e = sc*M_ref/(Z*E)
        if e > eps_hi:
            rr = k*Z*(e - eps_hi)
        elif e < eps_lo:
            rr = k*Z*(e - eps_lo)
        else:
            rr = 0.0
        Z = max(Z + rr, 1e-9)
    return np.array(out)


rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
t = np.arange(0, 301, 20)
data = traj(60, 0.5)[t]*(1 + 0.02*rng.standard_normal(len(t)))


def loss(p):
    return np.sum((traj(p[0], p[1])[t] - data)**2)


res = minimize(loss, [30, 0.7], method="Nelder-Mead")
print(np.round(res.x, 2))   # -> [56.7  0.5]

Result: from $2\%$-noise DXA points the fit recovers gain $k\approx57$ (true $60$) and disuse load fraction $\approx0.50$ (true $0.50$). The inverse problem is well-posed here because the two parameters act on different features of the curve — the load fraction sets the plateau ($Z_\text{eq}=$ load$\cdot M_\text{ref}/(E\varepsilon_\text{lo})$), the gain sets the rate of approach — so a clinic could estimate a patient's remodeling parameters from serial scans and predict their trajectory.

K10 (beam simulation + sensitivity). Numerically integrate the Euler–Bernoulli beam $EI\,y''=M(x)$ for a cantilever femur ($L=0.40$ m) under a transverse tip load $P=500$ N. Report tip deflection and peak stress, then sweep the wall thickness (bore $r$) to see the effect of cortical thinning.

Probes: double-integrating curvature to a deflection field, and the sensitivity of stiffness/strength to wall thickness (osteoporosis).

Show solution 
import numpy as np
E, Lb, P = 17e9, 0.40, 500.0


def cumtrapz(f, x):
    d = np.diff(x)
    return np.concatenate([[0], np.cumsum((f[:-1] + f[1:])/2*d)])


def cantilever(R, r, n=400):
    Ii = np.pi/4*(R**4 - r**4)
    x = np.linspace(0, Lb, n)
    kappa = P*(Lb - x)/(E*Ii)
    y = cumtrapz(cumtrapz(kappa, x), x)
    return round(y[-1]*1e3, 1), round(P*Lb*R/Ii/1e6, 1)


print(cantilever(0.014, 0.007))    # (22.2, 99.0)
print(cantilever(0.014, 0.009))    # (25.1, 111.9)

Result: the healthy cantilever deflects $22$ mm at the tip with peak stress $99$ MPa at the fixed end — and that stress equals §3's $M=200$ N·m bending value ($P L=200$ N·m), a self-consistency check on the integration. Thinning the cortex (bore $r:7\to9$ mm) raises deflection to $25$ mm and peak stress to $112$ MPa: the same load, less material far from the axis, more sag and $13\%$ higher stress. This is the mechanical cost of osteoporotic thinning — strength and stiffness fall together as the wall retreats, exactly where $I=\tfrac{\pi}{4}(R^4-r^4)$ says they should.

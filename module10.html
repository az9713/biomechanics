0. Motivation - why balance is active control, not geometry

Stand up and try to hold perfectly still. You cannot. Watch closely and the body is always drifting and correcting - a slow wander of a centimetre or two, caught and reversed before it grows. Now close your eyes: the wander gets larger. Stand on a soft foam pad and it grows again. Stand on one leg and you may not last a minute. None of this makes sense if balance were the geometry problem it is often first taught as - keep the centre of mass over the foot - because your feet have not moved and the floor has not tilted. What has changed is the quality of the information the body has about where it is, and how fast it can act on it.

Module 7 built standing as an inverted pendulum and gave the first, static answer: a body is statically balanced when the vertical line through its centre of mass (the COM, the average position of the body's mass) falls inside the base of support (the ground area enclosed by the feet). That condition is necessary - let the COM line leave the base and gravity's moment topples you - but it is not sufficient, for three reasons that this module is built around.

First, the body has momentum. A COM sitting safely over the foot but travelling fast will still leave the base before you can stop it; a COM outside the base but moving back inward is fine. What matters is not position alone but position projected forward by velocity. Module 7 named this the extrapolated centre of mass $\xi = x_{\rm com} + \dot x_{\rm com}/\omega_0$, where $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$ is the pendulum's natural rate; balance is really the condition that $\xi$, not $x_{\rm com}$, lies inside the base (Fig. 1).

Second, the controller acts through a delay. The correction you make is not instantaneous. Light or a joint rotation must be transduced by a sensor, the signal must travel up a nerve, be processed, travel back down, and finally build force in a muscle - a round trip of roughly $100$-$200\ \mathrm{ms}$. A feedback rule that ignores this lag and simply pushes back in proportion to the measured lean will, past a critical delay, not merely fail to help but actively drive the body into a growing oscillation. Module 7 flagged this as the reason we sway; here the delay stops being a caveat and becomes the protagonist (Section 4).

Third, the state is not known - it is estimated. No sensor reports "the COM is $2.3\ \mathrm{cm}$ forward and moving at $4\ \mathrm{cm/s}$." Instead the nervous system fuses a bank of noisy, partial, latent signals - the inner ear, muscle spindles, tendon organs, the eyes - into a running estimate of body state, and feeds that back. Noise in those channels is exactly why quiet standing wanders, and why closing your eyes (removing one channel) makes it wander more.

The thesis of this module. Balance is not a geometry problem but an active feedback-control problem solved under delay and noise. A standing body runs three things at once: an estimator that reconstructs its state from noisy, delayed sensors; a controller that feeds that estimate through gains which must stay inside a delay-bounded stability island; and a change-of-support fallback - a step - for when a perturbation throws $\xi$ past the foot. Aging is read not as "age" but as the drift of four control parameters (reflex delay, torque capacity, torque rate, sensor noise) that shrinks the island and the recovery envelope.
The arc of this module. We recap the static margin and define the dynamic margin of stability as a recovery budget (Section 1), upgrade Module 7's scalar ankle law to a state-space controller (Section 2), introduce the sensory channels as noisy delayed measurement models (Section 3), derive the delayed-feedback instability and its stability island (Section 4), add sensor noise and the stochastic sway it produces (Section 5), build the Kalman-filter estimate and the delay predictor that buys back the lost island (Section 6), climb the recovery ladder from ankle to hip to step (Section 7), separate slips from trips (Section 8), and read aging as parameter drift (Section 9) before the computational labs and problem set (Sections 10-11).

1. Static balance, dynamic balance, and the margin of stability

The word "balance" hides two different conditions. The first is static: standing still, are you upright? The second is dynamic: given how you are already moving, will you stay upright without taking a step? Module 7 answered the first; this section sharpens the second into a number - the margin of stability - that the rest of the module spends.

Start with the static picture. The base of support (BoS) is the ground region enclosed by the parts of the body in contact with it - for two feet flat, the convex area spanning both soles.

Definition 1 (static stability margin). Let $x_{\rm com}$ be the horizontal ground projection of the centre of mass and let the nearest edge of the base of support in the direction of interest be at $x_{\rm edge}$. The static stability margin is the distance $x_{\rm edge}-x_{\rm com}$. The body is statically balanced when this is positive for every edge, i.e. $x_{\rm com}$ lies strictly inside the base.

This is correct but blind to motion. A gymnast whose COM is over the beam yet rotating fast will fall off; a person whose COM has just crossed the toe but is travelling backward will not. To see why, recall the linearised inverted pendulum of Module 7. Writing $\theta$ for the small sway angle from vertical, $\ell$ for the height of the COM above the ankle, and $g$ for gravity, the free (uncontrolled) body obeys $\ddot\theta=\omega_0^2\,\theta$ with $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$; in the COM's horizontal coordinate $x\approx \ell\theta$ this is $\ddot x=\omega_0^2 x$. The positive sign is the whole story: upright is a saddle, not a well, and the body diverges from it unless the initial state is chosen exactly right.

Proposition 1.1 (the divergent coordinate is the extrapolated COM). The solutions of $\ddot x=\omega_0^2 x$ have eigenvalues $\pm\omega_0$. The state $(x,\dot x)$ returns to upright as $t\to\infty$ if and only if $\dot x=-\omega_0 x$, that is, if and only if the combination $$\boxed{\;\xi \;:=\; x+\frac{\dot x}{\omega_0}\;}$$ vanishes. In general $\xi(t)=\xi(0)\,e^{\omega_0 t}$ grows purely exponentially, so $\xi$ - the extrapolated centre of mass (XcoM) of Module 7 - is the single coordinate that decides whether the free body topples.
Write the second-order equation as the first-order system $\frac{d}{dt}\left(\begin{smallmatrix}x\\ \dot x\end{smallmatrix}\right)=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)\left(\begin{smallmatrix}x\\ \dot x\end{smallmatrix}\right)$. The matrix has eigenvalues $\lambda_\pm=\pm\omega_0$ with eigenvectors $v_\pm=(1,\pm\omega_0)$. Expanding the state in this basis, the coefficient of the growing mode $v_+$ (eigenvalue $+\omega_0$) is proportional to $x+\dot x/\omega_0=\xi$ and the coefficient of the decaying mode $v_-$ to $x-\dot x/\omega_0$. Hence $\xi(t)=\xi(0)e^{\omega_0 t}$ and $\big(x-\dot x/\omega_0\big)(t)=\big(x-\dot x/\omega_0\big)(0)e^{-\omega_0 t}$. The state tends to the origin iff the growing coefficient is zero, i.e. $\xi(0)=0$, equivalently $\dot x=-\omega_0 x$. ∎

Proposition 1.1 is why balance must be phrased in $\xi$, not $x_{\rm com}$: the extrapolated COM folds the body's velocity into its position with exactly the weight $1/\omega_0$ that the dynamics demand. The dynamic replacement for "COM inside the base" is then immediate, and is the capturability condition Module 7 handed forward.

Dynamic stability condition. A standing body can be brought to rest by a bounded fixed-support (ankle/hip) response, without stepping, precisely when its extrapolated centre of mass lies inside the base of support: $$\xi\in[\,x_{\rm heel},\,x_{\rm toe}\,].$$ When $\xi$ leaves the base a step is mandatory - a change of the base itself (Section 7).

The slack in this inequality is the quantity we will track through every later section. Measured toward the edge the body is moving against, it is the margin of stability.

Definition 2 (dynamic margin of stability). For motion toward the edge at $x_{\rm edge}$, the margin of stability is $$b \;=\; x_{\rm edge}-\xi \;=\; x_{\rm edge}-x_{\rm com}-\frac{\dot x_{\rm com}}{\omega_0}.$$ It is positive when the extrapolated COM is still inside the base (fixed-support recovery possible) and negative when $\xi$ has passed the edge (a step is required). At rest ($\dot x_{\rm com}=0$) it reduces to the static margin of Definition 1.

Because $b$ has units of length it is tempting to read it as merely "how much room is left." Its real meaning is a recovery budget: it is exactly the size of the largest sudden perturbation a fixed-support controller can still absorb, and that equivalence is worth deriving because it recurs - in the delayed-feedback island of Section 4, the aging envelope of Section 9, and the labs.

Proposition 1.2 (the margin is a critical impulse). Suppose the body stands still and upright ($x_{\rm com}=0$, $\dot x_{\rm com}=0$, so $\xi=0$) with margin $b=x_{\rm edge}$, and receives a sudden horizontal impulse $J$ at the COM. The perturbation is arrestable without a step if and only if $$\boxed{\;J\le J_{\max}=m\,\omega_0\,b\;}$$ where $m$ is the body mass. Equivalently, the recovery budget in impulse units is $m\omega_0$ times the length margin.
An impulse $J$ delivered horizontally to the point mass changes its momentum by $J$ and hence its velocity by $\Delta\dot x_{\rm com}=J/m$, leaving the position unchanged during the (idealised, instantaneous) impulse. By the definition of $\xi$, the extrapolated COM jumps by $\Delta\xi=\Delta\dot x_{\rm com}/\omega_0=J/(m\omega_0)$. Starting from $\xi=0$, the new extrapolated COM is $\xi^+=J/(m\omega_0)$. By the dynamic stability condition it remains arrestable without a step iff $\xi^+\le x_{\rm edge}=b$, i.e. $J/(m\omega_0)\le b$, which rearranges to $J\le m\omega_0 b$. ∎

Numbers make the budget concrete. For a standing adult with COM height $\ell\approx1.0\ \mathrm{m}$ above the ankle, $\omega_0=\sqrt{9.81/1.0}\approx3.1\ \mathrm{rad/s}$; with mass $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$ and a forward foot margin $b\approx0.10\ \mathrm{m}$ (heel-to-toe headroom for the extrapolated COM), the largest arrestable forward impulse is $J_{\max}\approx70\times3.1\times0.10\approx22\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ - about the impulse of a firm shove or a $3\ \mathrm{kg}$ mass swung at walking speed. Anything larger, and the ankle cannot save you: you step, or you fall. Every mechanism in the rest of the module either enlarges this budget (a good estimator, fast reflexes) or shrinks it (delay, noise, aging), and Section 2 is where the "bounded fixed-support response" becomes an explicit state-space controller rather than a promise.

2. The state-space inverted pendulum

Module 7 controlled sway with a scalar rule: apply an ankle torque proportional to the lean and its rate, $\tau=K_p\theta+K_d\dot\theta$. That is enough to stabilise standing, but it hides the structure the rest of this module needs - it treats position and velocity as two separate knobs rather than as one state that evolves together. The upgrade is to write the body as a state-space system, the standard language of control, in which the estimator (Section 6), the delay (Section 4), and the noise (Section 5) all attach to the same object.

Collect the sway angle and its rate into a state vector $\mathbf x=[\theta,\dot\theta]^{\mathsf T}$. The linearised inverted pendulum with ankle control is $\ddot\theta=\omega_0^2\theta+u$, where $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$ as before and $u=\tau/(m\ell^2)$ is the commanded angular acceleration produced by the ankle torque $\tau$ acting through the body's moment of inertia $m\ell^2$ about the ankle. In first-order form this is the linear plant

$$\dot{\mathbf x}=A\mathbf x+B\,u,\qquad A=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2&0\end{bmatrix},\qquad B=\begin{bmatrix}0\\ 1\end{bmatrix}.$$

The upright equilibrium is $\mathbf x=\mathbf 0$. With $u=0$ the eigenvalues of $A$ are $\pm\omega_0$ - the saddle of Section 1, redrawn in Fig. 3. The question control theory asks first is whether the single input $u$ can reach every direction of the state, because only then can feedback place the closed-loop behaviour wherever we like.

Proposition 2.1 (controllability). The pair $(A,B)$ is controllable: the controllability matrix $$\mathcal C=\begin{bmatrix}B & AB\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\ 1&0\end{bmatrix}$$ has full rank $2$. Consequently, state feedback $u=-K\mathbf x$ can place the two closed-loop eigenvalues at any (real or complex-conjugate) locations we choose.
Compute $AB=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0\\ 1\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\ 0\end{bmatrix}$, so $\mathcal C=[B\ \ AB]=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\ 1&0\end{bmatrix}$, with $\det\mathcal C=-1\neq0$; the rank is $2$, equal to the state dimension. For a controllable single-input pair the map from feedback gain $K$ to the coefficients of the closed-loop characteristic polynomial is onto (Ackermann's formula), so any desired characteristic polynomial - hence any eigenvalue placement - is achievable. ∎

Apply the feedback $u=-K\mathbf x$ with gain row $K=[k_p\ \ k_d]$. Substituting into $\dot{\mathbf x}=A\mathbf x+Bu$ gives the closed loop $\dot{\mathbf x}=(A-BK)\mathbf x$, whose matrix and stability are the headline of this section.

Proposition 2.2 (closed-loop stability). Under $u=-[k_p\ \ k_d]\mathbf x$ the closed-loop system is $$\boxed{\;A-BK=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2-k_p & -k_d\end{bmatrix},\qquad s^2+k_d\,s+(k_p-\omega_0^2)=0\;}$$ and its equilibrium is asymptotically stable if and only if $$k_p\gt \omega_0^2\qquad\text{and}\qquad k_d\gt 0.$$ The position gain must exceed gravity's destabilising rate $\omega_0^2$ to turn the saddle into a sink; the velocity gain supplies the damping.
With $B=[0,1]^{\mathsf T}$ and $K=[k_p\ k_d]$, $BK=\begin{bmatrix}0&0\\ k_p&k_d\end{bmatrix}$, so $A-BK=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2-k_p&-k_d\end{bmatrix}$. For a matrix $\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\ c&d\end{bmatrix}$ the characteristic polynomial is $s^2-ds-c$; here $c=\omega_0^2-k_p$ and $d=-k_d$, giving $s^2+k_d s+(k_p-\omega_0^2)$. A real quadratic $s^2+a_1 s+a_0$ has both roots in the open left half-plane iff $a_1\gt 0$ and $a_0\gt 0$ (Routh-Hurwitz for degree two), i.e. $k_d\gt 0$ and $k_p-\omega_0^2\gt 0$. ∎

This recovers Module 7's $K_p,K_d$ exactly - $k_p$ and $k_d$ are those gains divided by the inertia $m\ell^2$ - but now as eigenvalue placement rather than a rule of thumb. Choosing $k_p=20\ \mathrm{s^{-2}}$ and $k_d=3\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ with $\omega_0=3.1\ \mathrm{rad/s}$ places the poles at $s=-1.5\pm2.85\,i$: a stable spiral that rings down in about a second, the bold recovery trajectory of Fig. 3.

Proposition 2.2 says any $k_p\gt \omega_0^2$, $k_d\gt 0$ stabilises, but not which choice is best. Cranking the gains up moves the poles further left (faster recovery) but demands large, abrupt ankle torques - metabolically costly, saturating against the muscle's force limit, and, as Section 4 will show, catastrophic once feedback is delayed. The principled compromise is the linear-quadratic regulator (LQR): choose $K$ to minimise a cost that charges both for deviation and for effort,

Definition 3 (LQR cost). The linear-quadratic regulator selects the gain $K$ minimising $$\mathcal J=\int_0^\infty\big(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}Q\,\mathbf x+R\,u^2\big)\,dt,$$ where the positive-semidefinite matrix $Q$ prices how much each unit of sway (angle, rate) costs and the scalar $R\gt 0$ prices control effort. Large $R$ (effort is dear) yields small, gentle gains and slow recovery; large $Q$ (deviation is dear) yields large gains and fast, aggressive recovery.

The optimal gain solves a matrix Riccati equation; we take only its intuition here - the optimum is the point where the marginal cost of a little more deviation equals the marginal cost of the extra effort needed to suppress it - and defer the full optimal-feedback machinery, and its extension to the many joints of a whole-body recovery, to Module 12. For the single-segment pendulum the lesson is enough: there is a best stiffness of response, not an arbitrarily large one, and every ingredient we add next - delay, noise, an imperfect estimate - tightens the case against high gain. Section 3 supplies the first of those ingredients by asking where the feedback signal $\mathbf x$ actually comes from.

3. The sensors: vestibular, proprioceptive, and visual channels

The controller of Section 2 feeds back the state $\mathbf x=[\theta,\dot\theta]^{\mathsf T}$ - but no organ reports $\mathbf x$. What the body has instead is a bank of sensors, each transducing some partial, delayed, noisy function of the state. Written in the same language as the plant, each channel is a row of a measurement model.

Definition 4 (measurement model). The sensors produce an observation vector $$\mathbf y(t)=C\,\mathbf x(t-\tau_s)+\boldsymbol\nu(t),$$ where $C$ selects which combination of state each channel reports, $\tau_s$ is that channel's afferent latency, and $\boldsymbol\nu$ is zero-mean measurement noise with variance set by the channel's reliability. Different channels are different rows of $C$, with different $\tau_s$ and different noise.

Four channels dominate standing balance; Fig. 4 maps them onto the body, each with what it measures, its latency, and a sample of its noise.

The vestibular organs sit in the inner ear. The three semicircular canals are fluid-filled loops in roughly orthogonal planes; when the head rotates, the fluid lags and deflects a gel-embedded tuft of hair cells, so the canals report head angular velocity - a row $C\approx[0,1]$ acting on head rate. The otoliths (the utricle and saccule) carry a mass of calcium-carbonate crystals whose shear under gravity and linear acceleration bends their hair cells, reporting head tilt and linear acceleration - a row sensing $\theta$. Vestibular transduction is fast (afferent latency on the order of $15\ \mathrm{ms}$) but it measures the head, not the COM, so it must be combined with neck and body geometry to say anything about sway.

Muscle spindles are the proprioceptive workhorses of standing. Embedded in the muscle belly, a spindle is a capsule of thin intrafusal fibres wrapped by sensory endings: the primary (Ia) afferent fires to both muscle length and its rate of change, the secondary (II) afferent chiefly to length. Because the calf muscles cross the ankle, spindle length maps directly to ankle angle, so the spindles report $\theta$ and $\dot\theta$ - a row touching both components of the state. They are also the afferent limb of the monosynaptic stretch reflex: a sudden stretch excites the Ia afferent, which synapses directly onto the motor neuron of the same muscle and fires it back, the fastest corrective loop the body owns (about $35\ \mathrm{ms}$ round trip).

Golgi tendon organs (GTOs) lie in series with the muscle at the muscle-tendon junction, woven among the collagen. Their Ib afferents fire to tendon tension, so the GTO is the force channel - it reports how hard the muscle is pulling, complementary to the spindle's length signal, with a latency near $40\ \mathrm{ms}$. Alongside these, foot-sole pressure and joint receptors report the centre of pressure and joint angles, and this cutaneous channel is why standing on a numb or cold foot degrades balance.

Vision closes the last channel. As the body sways, the whole visual scene drifts across the retina; this optic flow is a global read-out of sway angle, wide-field and precise about slow drift but comparatively slow to process (visual latency around $150\ \mathrm{ms}$). It is the channel you remove by closing your eyes - which is why quiet standing wanders more in the dark.

A striking fact makes the estimator of Section 6 possible at all: even though each channel is partial, any one of them makes the full state recoverable, because the model supplies what the sensor omits.

Proposition 3.1 (observability). For the plant $A=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, a single position channel $C=[1,0]$ (e.g. visual or otolith tilt) or a single rate channel $C=[0,1]$ (e.g. the canals) makes the pair $(A,C)$ observable: the observability matrix $\mathcal O=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}C\\ CA\end{smallmatrix}\right)$ has full rank $2$. Hence the entire state - including the component the sensor does not directly see - can be reconstructed from that channel over time, and fusing several channels only reduces the reconstruction's noise.
For $C=[1,0]$, $CA=[1,0]\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)=[0,1]$, so $\mathcal O=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0\\ 0&1\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, rank $2$. For $C=[0,1]$, $CA=[\omega_0^2,0]$, so $\mathcal O=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, with $\det=-\omega_0^2\neq0$, rank $2$. In both cases the rank equals the state dimension, which is the observability criterion; a full-rank $\mathcal O$ means the initial state is uniquely determined by the output history, so an observer can reconstruct $\mathbf x$. Combining channels stacks more rows onto $C$, which cannot lower the rank and does lower the variance of the least-squares state estimate. ∎

Two consequences run through the rest of the module. First, because the channels differ in reliability, the nervous system does not trust them equally or fixedly - it performs sensory reweighting, shifting weight toward whichever channels are currently informative. Close your eyes and vision's weight drops to zero, so the estimate leans on vestibular and proprioceptive signals and inherits more of their noise; stand on compliant foam and ankle proprioception becomes unreliable, so weight shifts to vision and vestibular. This is why the clinical worst case for standing is eyes closed on foam, which corrupts two channels at once. Reweighting by reliability is exactly what an optimal estimator does, and Section 6 makes it precise.

Second, every channel is late. The afferent latencies above ($15$-$150\ \mathrm{ms}$) are only the sensing leg of the loop; the full sensorimotor delay also spends time on central processing, efferent conduction, and the electromechanical delay of building muscle force, summing to a total feedback delay $\tau$ of roughly $100$-$200\ \mathrm{ms}$. That total delay is not a detail - it is the single most destabilising fact about the balance controller, and Section 4 is devoted to it.

4. Delayed feedback and the instability it breeds

Module 7 ended its account of standing with a warning: the feedback loop is delayed, and that is why we sway rather than stand perfectly still. This section makes the warning quantitative. The delay $\tau$ assembled in Section 3 - sensing, conduction, central processing, and the electromechanical lag of force production, together $100$-$200\ \mathrm{ms}$ - turns the clean ordinary differential equation of Section 2 into a delay-differential equation, and delay-differential equations can be unstable where their delay-free versions are not.

Insert the delay into the feedback: the ankle acts on the state it sensed a time $\tau$ ago, not the current one. The closed loop becomes

$$\ddot\theta=\omega_0^2\,\theta(t)-\big[k_p\,\theta(t-\tau)+k_d\,\dot\theta(t-\tau)\big].$$

This is a delay-differential equation (DDE): its future depends not only on the present state but on the state one delay in the past. Unlike the plant of Section 2, its state space is infinite-dimensional (a whole history segment), and it has infinitely many characteristic roots. Stability is still decided by whether any root sits in the right half-plane, and the roots follow from the same exponential ansatz.

Proposition 4.1 (characteristic equation). Seeking solutions $\theta(t)=e^{st}$ of the delayed closed loop gives the transcendental characteristic equation $$\boxed{\;s^2-\omega_0^2+\big(k_p+k_d\,s\big)e^{-s\tau}=0\;}$$ which at $\tau=0$ collapses to $s^2+k_d s+(k_p-\omega_0^2)=0$, the delay-free result of Proposition 2.2. The equilibrium is stable iff every root has negative real part.
Substituting $\theta=e^{st}$ makes $\dot\theta=se^{st}$, $\ddot\theta=s^2e^{st}$, and $\theta(t-\tau)=e^{s(t-\tau)}=e^{st}e^{-s\tau}$, similarly $\dot\theta(t-\tau)=se^{st}e^{-s\tau}$. Dividing the closed-loop equation by $e^{st}\neq0$ leaves $s^2-\omega_0^2+(k_p+k_d s)e^{-s\tau}=0$. At $\tau=0$, $e^{-s\tau}=1$ and this is $s^2-\omega_0^2+k_p+k_d s=s^2+k_d s+(k_p-\omega_0^2)=0$. Because the solution is a superposition of modes $e^{s_i t}$, it decays iff every characteristic root $s_i$ has $\operatorname{Re}s_i\lt0$. ∎

As $\tau$ increases from zero the roots move; the loss of stability happens when a conjugate pair first reaches the imaginary axis. Setting $s=i\Omega$ and separating real and imaginary parts pins down exactly where.

Proposition 4.2 (stability boundary and critical delay). A root crosses the imaginary axis at $s=i\Omega$ ($\Omega\gt0$) when $$\boxed{\;\sin(\Omega\tau)=\frac{k_d\,\Omega}{\Omega^2+\omega_0^2},\qquad k_p=\big(\Omega^2+\omega_0^2\big)\cos(\Omega\tau)\;}$$ which, as $\Omega$ ranges over $(0,\infty)$, traces the boundary of the stability island in the $(\tau,k_p)$ plane at fixed $k_d$ (Fig. 5). The critical delay $\tau_c$ is the smallest positive $\tau$ on this boundary for the operating gains; for $\tau\gt\tau_c$ the standing equilibrium is unstable.
Put $s=i\Omega$ in Proposition 4.1. Then $s^2=-\Omega^2$ and $e^{-s\tau}=\cos\Omega\tau-i\sin\Omega\tau$, so $$-\Omega^2-\omega_0^2+(k_p+ik_d\Omega)(\cos\Omega\tau-i\sin\Omega\tau)=0.$$ The imaginary part is $-k_p\sin\Omega\tau+k_d\Omega\cos\Omega\tau=0$, i.e. $\tan\Omega\tau=k_d\Omega/k_p$, so $k_p=k_d\Omega\cos\Omega\tau/\sin\Omega\tau$. The real part is $k_p\cos\Omega\tau+k_d\Omega\sin\Omega\tau=\Omega^2+\omega_0^2$; substituting the expression for $k_p$ and using $\cos^2+\sin^2=1$ gives $k_d\Omega/\sin\Omega\tau=\Omega^2+\omega_0^2$, i.e. $\sin\Omega\tau=k_d\Omega/(\Omega^2+\omega_0^2)$. Back-substituting yields $k_p=(\Omega^2+\omega_0^2)\cos\Omega\tau$. Each $\Omega$ thus gives one boundary point $(\tau,k_p)$; sweeping $\Omega$ traces the whole boundary. ∎

The numbers land exactly where the biology lives. For the design gains $k_p=20\ \mathrm{s^{-2}}$, $k_d=3\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ and $\omega_0=3.1\ \mathrm{rad/s}$, the boundary gives a critical delay $\tau_c\approx137\ \mathrm{ms}$ - right inside the measured $100$-$200\ \mathrm{ms}$ sensorimotor loop. The standing human is therefore not comfortably inside the island but perched near its edge, which is precisely why quiet standing never settles: the closed loop is lightly damped and any noise keeps it ringing (Section 5).

The physical mechanism is worth stating plainly, because it explains why the naive fix - "sway too much? push back harder" - backfires. The corrective torque $-k_p\theta(t-\tau)$ is aimed at where the body was, not where it is. If the body is oscillating with a period near $4\tau$, then by the time the correction for a forward lean is delivered the body has already swung backward, so the "correction" now shoves it further backward - it pumps the oscillation like a child pushing a swing at the wrong instant. Raising $k_p$ makes each mistimed shove stronger, which is why the island's ceiling falls as delay grows (Fig. 5, left): past the boundary, higher gain manufactures the very sway it was meant to cancel (Fig. 5, right). The genuine escape is not more gain but a better-timed signal - an estimate of where the body is now, predicted forward across the delay. That predictor is the subject of Section 6; first, Section 5 adds the noise that the lightly-damped, near-critical loop turns into persistent sway.

5. Stochastic perturbations and sensor noise

Section 4 left the standing controller lightly damped and perched near its stability boundary. Such a system does not sit still when disturbed - it rings - and it is disturbed continually. Two noise sources keep it moving. Sensor noise is the $\boldsymbol\nu$ of the measurement model (Section 3): every channel's estimate jitters. Motor noise is jitter in the command itself - the recruitment and firing of motor units is stochastic, and, crucially, its size grows with the command (signal-dependent noise). Fed through the closed loop, both appear as a random forcing that never lets the sway settle. Quiet standing is therefore not a fixed point but a stationary stochastic process, and its statistics are what a force plate actually records.

Model the disturbed closed loop (using the delay-free approximation valid well inside the island, where the delay chiefly reduces the effective damping) as the linear stochastic differential equation

Definition 5 (noisy closed loop). With $a_0=k_p-\omega_0^2$ and $a_1=k_d$, the stabilised sway driven by white noise of intensity $q$ obeys $$\ddot\theta+a_1\dot\theta+a_0\theta=\xi(t),\qquad \langle\xi(t)\xi(t')\rangle=q\,\delta(t-t'),$$ equivalently $\dot{\mathbf x}=A_{\rm cl}\mathbf x+G\xi$ with $A_{\rm cl}=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ -a_0&-a_1\end{smallmatrix}\right)$ and $G=[0,1]^{\mathsf T}$. This is a two-dimensional Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process: linear restoring dynamics kicked by white noise.

Because the dynamics are linear and stable and the forcing is white and Gaussian, the state settles to a stationary zero-mean Gaussian whose covariance $P=\langle\mathbf x\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\rangle$ is fixed by a single matrix equation. Solving it gives the sway variance in closed form.

Proposition 5.1 (stationary sway variance). The stationary covariance $P$ of the noisy closed loop is diagonal, with $$\boxed{\;\operatorname{Var}(\theta)=\frac{q}{2\,a_0\,a_1}=\frac{q}{2(k_p-\omega_0^2)\,k_d},\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\dot\theta)=\frac{q}{2\,a_1}=\frac{q}{2k_d}\;}$$ and $\operatorname{Cov}(\theta,\dot\theta)=0$. Sway variance therefore falls as either gain rises, but $k_p$ cannot be raised past the stability-island edge (Section 4), which sets a floor on how still one can stand.
The stationary covariance of $\dot{\mathbf x}=A_{\rm cl}\mathbf x+G\xi$ satisfies the Lyapunov equation $A_{\rm cl}P+PA_{\rm cl}^{\mathsf T}+Q=0$, where $Q=Gq G^{\mathsf T}=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&0\\ 0&q\end{smallmatrix}\right)$ is the noise covariance. Write $P=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}p_{11}&p_{12}\\ p_{12}&p_{22}\end{smallmatrix}\right)$. With $A_{\rm cl}=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ -a_0&-a_1\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, $$A_{\rm cl}P+PA_{\rm cl}^{\mathsf T}=\begin{pmatrix}2p_{12} & p_{22}-a_0p_{11}-a_1p_{12}\\[2pt] p_{22}-a_0p_{11}-a_1p_{12} & -2a_0p_{12}-2a_1p_{22}\end{pmatrix}.$$ Adding $Q$ and setting each entry to zero: the $(1,1)$ entry gives $2p_{12}=0$, so $p_{12}=0$; the $(2,2)$ entry gives $-2a_1p_{22}+q=0$, so $p_{22}=q/(2a_1)$; the $(1,2)$ entry, with $p_{12}=0$, gives $p_{22}-a_0p_{11}=0$, so $p_{11}=p_{22}/a_0=q/(2a_0a_1)$. Thus $\operatorname{Var}(\theta)=p_{11}$, $\operatorname{Var}(\dot\theta)=p_{22}$, and the two are uncorrelated. ∎

Proposition 5.1 is directly measurable. In posturography the recorded sway path length and the area of the 95% confidence ellipse both scale with $\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}}$, so the formula predicts how those clinical numbers move: they grow with the noise intensity $q$ and shrink with the loop gains. Fig. 6 confirms the law against simulation and shows RMS sway falling with $k_p$ - until the island edge, where the falling curve is cut off because larger gain no longer steadies the body but destabilises it.

One subtlety closes the loop and foreshadows the labs. Motor noise is signal-dependent: a larger corrective command carries proportionally larger noise, so the effective intensity is not constant but grows with gain, roughly $q(k_p)\approx q_0+c\,k_p^2\langle\theta^2\rangle$. Substituted into Proposition 5.1, the monotone decrease of sway with gain becomes a shallow U: past some gain the added motor noise outweighs the tighter control, and there is an optimal gain that minimises sway. Finding it is a sensitivity problem the reader could not read off the boxed formula, and it appears as one of the computational problems (Section 11). What noise cannot do is be filtered away for free - but a good estimate of the state suppresses its effect on the command, and building that estimate, while also predicting across the delay of Section 4, is the task of Section 6.

6. State estimation: the Kalman-filter intuition

The controller of Section 2 wants the true state $\mathbf x$, but Section 3 and Section 5 showed it can have only noisy, delayed measurements. Feeding those raw into $u=-K\mathbf x$ injects their noise straight into the command and their lag straight into the loop. The escape is not a better sensor but a better estimate: combine what the sensors say with what the body's own dynamics predict, trusting each in proportion to its certainty. That combination is the Kalman filter, and its two ideas - optimal fusion, and prediction across the delay - are exactly the two the balance problem needs.

Fusing prediction and measurement

Consider the scalar version first. The internal model predicts the state will be near $\hat x^-$, with uncertainty (variance) $\sigma_p^2$; a sensor reports $y$, with noise variance $\sigma_m^2$. Both are unbiased estimates of the same quantity. What single estimate has the least variance?

Proposition 6.1 (the scalar Kalman gain). Among all weighted averages $\hat x=(1-K)\hat x^-+K\,y$ of an unbiased prediction (variance $\sigma_p^2$) and an independent unbiased measurement (variance $\sigma_m^2$), the minimum-variance choice is $$\boxed{\;K=\frac{\sigma_p^2}{\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2},\qquad \hat x=\hat x^-+K\,(y-\hat x^-),\qquad \sigma_+^2=(1-K)\,\sigma_p^2\;}$$ so the estimate leans toward whichever source is more certain, and the fused variance $\sigma_+^2$ is smaller than either input's.
The combination $\hat x=(1-K)\hat x^-+Ky$ is unbiased for any $K$ since both inputs are. With the two errors independent, its variance is $V(K)=(1-K)^2\sigma_p^2+K^2\sigma_m^2$. Setting $V'(K)=-2(1-K)\sigma_p^2+2K\sigma_m^2=0$ gives $K(\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2)=\sigma_p^2$, i.e. $K=\sigma_p^2/(\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2)$; the second derivative $2(\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2)\gt0$ confirms a minimum. Substituting back, $V=\sigma_p^2\sigma_m^2/(\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2)=(1-K)\sigma_p^2$, which is less than both $\sigma_p^2$ and $\sigma_m^2$. Rewriting $(1-K)\hat x^-+Ky=\hat x^-+K(y-\hat x^-)$ gives the prediction-correction form. ∎

This is the whole Kalman filter in miniature (Fig. 7, left): a predict step advances the estimate and inflates its variance by the process dynamics, and an update step pulls it toward each new measurement by the gain $K$, deflating the variance. It also makes the sensory reweighting of Section 3 precise: a channel with larger $\sigma_m^2$ (eyes closed, numb feet, damaged vestibule) automatically gets a smaller $K$, so the estimate leans on the others - reweighting is not a separate mechanism but what optimal fusion does when a channel degrades. Applied to the full state $\mathbf x=[\theta,\dot\theta]^{\mathsf T}$ the same logic gives the matrix filter, whose gain solves a Riccati equation dual to the LQR one of Section 2; the scalar case carries the whole idea.

Predicting across the delay

Fusion tames the noise but not the delay: the freshest measurement still describes the state a time $\tau$ ago. Feeding an accurate estimate of the past state into the controller is exactly the mistimed feedback that Section 4 showed to be destabilising. The fix is to use the model to run the estimate forward.

Proposition 6.2 (the delay predictor). Let $\hat{\mathbf x}(t-\tau)$ be the filtered estimate of the delayed state and let the applied control $u(\cdot)$ over the last $\tau$ be known. Then the current state is estimated by propagating the model forward, $$\boxed{\;\hat{\mathbf x}(t)=e^{A\tau}\,\hat{\mathbf x}(t-\tau)+\int_{0}^{\tau}e^{A(\tau-s)}B\,u(t-\tau+s)\,ds\;}$$ and feeding back $u=-K\hat{\mathbf x}(t)$ closes the loop on the predicted present state. To the extent the prediction is accurate, the effective loop is delay-free, restoring the stability island lost in Section 4.
The plant $\dot{\mathbf x}=A\mathbf x+Bu$ has the linear solution $\mathbf x(t)=e^{A\tau}\mathbf x(t-\tau)+\int_0^\tau e^{A(\tau-s)}Bu(t-\tau+s)\,ds$ by variation of parameters over the interval $[t-\tau,t]$. Replacing the unknown $\mathbf x(t-\tau)$ by its filtered estimate $\hat{\mathbf x}(t-\tau)$ gives the stated predictor; its error is $e^{A\tau}$ times the filter's estimation error, which the filter keeps small. Feeding $u=-K\hat{\mathbf x}(t)$ then applies the Section 2 gain to a current-state estimate, so the closed loop behaves as the delay-free $\dot{\mathbf x}=(A-BK)\mathbf x$ up to the prediction error. ∎

Fig. 7 (right) runs both steps on a real sway: the filter cuts through the heavy measurement noise, and the predictor slides the estimate forward by $\tau$ so it overlays the present truth instead of lagging it. This is why the nervous system is thought to carry a forward model - an internal copy of the body's dynamics used to predict the sensory consequences of its own commands - and why balance survives delays that would destabilise a purely reactive controller. It is also the precise sense in which a healthy balance system is an estimator plus a controller, the thesis of Section 0: the controller of Section 2 supplies the gains, and the estimator of this section supplies the clean, current state they act on. What no estimate can do is invent authority the body does not have - once a perturbation drives the extrapolated COM past the foot, no feedback on the ankle suffices, and the body must change its base of support. That regime change is Section 7.

7. Perturbation recovery: fixed versus change of support

Everything so far has kept the feet planted. That is the right model for quiet standing and small pushes, but it has a hard limit: a foot on the ground can only push, and only within its own area. When a perturbation is large enough, no ankle or hip action can save the body and it must do something categorically different - move a foot. The set of responses forms a recovery ladder of three rungs, and which rung is required is set entirely by where the extrapolated COM $\xi$ sits relative to the foot (Fig. 8).

Ankle. For small disturbances the ankle torque of Section 2 suffices. Physically, ankle torque works by shifting the centre of pressure (COP, the point where the ground reaction acts) under the foot; the offset between COP and COM creates the restoring moment. But the COP cannot leave the foot - push it to the toe and there is nowhere further to go - so the ankle's authority is strictly bounded.

Proposition 7.1 (ankle authority is bounded by the foot). With the COP confined to the foot, $x_{\rm heel}\le x_{\rm cop}\le x_{\rm toe}$, the horizontal restoring acceleration the ankle can produce satisfies $$|\ddot x_{\rm com}|\le \frac{g}{\ell}\,\max\big(x_{\rm toe}-x_{\rm com},\;x_{\rm com}-x_{\rm heel}\big),$$ so the ankle alone can arrest a lean only while the extrapolated COM stays within the foot; a $\xi$ near the toe already saturates it.
Taking moments about the COM for the single inverted pendulum, the ground reaction (vertical component $\approx mg$ in slow sway) acting at the COP a horizontal distance $(x_{\rm cop}-x_{\rm com})$ from the COM produces a horizontal COM acceleration $\ddot x_{\rm com}=\tfrac{g}{\ell}(x_{\rm cop}-x_{\rm com})$ in the linearised pendulum (dividing the restoring moment $mg(x_{\rm cop}-x_{\rm com})$ by the moment of inertia $m\ell^2$ and multiplying by the lever $\ell$ to get a linear acceleration). The magnitude is largest when the COP is driven to whichever foot edge is farther from the COM, giving the stated bound. Since arresting requires driving $\xi$ back inside the foot and the available acceleration vanishes as $x_{\rm com}$ itself reaches the toe, the ankle saturates once the state approaches the foot boundary. ∎

Hip. When the ankle saturates, the body recruits the hip strategy: a rapid trunk and arm counter-rotation. Flinging the upper body forward drives the COP back to the heel (and vice versa) and, by conservation of angular momentum, produces a transient horizontal acceleration of the COM without any change of support. It buys a little more of the margin, but only transiently - the trunk cannot keep rotating - so it shifts the ladder's rung, it does not remove the ceiling.

Step. Past that ceiling the response is not a bigger torque but a different regime: a step that moves the base of support itself. The trigger is exactly the sign of the margin of Section 1.

Proposition 7.2 (step criterion and capture placement). A step is mandatory precisely when the extrapolated COM lies outside the current base, $\xi\notin[x_{\rm heel},x_{\rm toe}]$ (equivalently $b\lt0$). To arrest the fall in a single step, the stepping foot must be planted at the capture point $$\boxed{\;x_{\rm foot}=\xi+\Delta\;}$$ where $\Delta\ge0$ is a chosen margin; $\Delta=0$ places the new pivot exactly at the extrapolated COM (marginal, one-step capture), and $\Delta\gt0$ lands $\xi$ a distance $\Delta$ inside the new base for a stable finish.
After the step the body pivots about the new foot at $x_{\rm foot}$, so the pendulum coordinate becomes $x'=x_{\rm com}-x_{\rm foot}$ and, by the definition of the extrapolated COM (which is a property of the state, shifted with the pivot), $\xi'=\xi-x_{\rm foot}$. By Proposition 1.1 the post-step body comes to rest over the new foot iff its divergent coordinate is non-positive, $\xi'\le0$, i.e. $x_{\rm foot}\ge\xi$. The marginal choice $x_{\rm foot}=\xi$ gives $\xi'=0$, the exact one-step capture point; adding $\Delta$ makes $\xi'=-\Delta\lt0$, leaving the extrapolated COM a distance $\Delta$ inside the new base, which is the dynamic stability condition of Section 1 with margin $\Delta$. A step is unavoidable whenever the fixed-support responses are exhausted, which Proposition 7.1 and the hip limit place exactly at $\xi$ leaving the foot, i.e. $b\lt0$. ∎

The capture point $x_{\rm foot}\approx\xi$ is a strikingly simple rule - step to where your extrapolated COM is going - and it unifies this module with Module 9. There, each running step places the foot ahead of the body by roughly the same quantity; running is a controlled sequence of capture steps, and a trip is a capture step that lands short. It also exposes the failure mode: because $x_{\rm foot}=\xi+\Delta$ depends on an estimate of $\xi$ (built by the delayed, noisy estimator of Section 6), a mis-estimated or mis-timed step lands in the wrong place, and whether a given placement error still captures is a question the boxed formula cannot answer on its own - it needs a simulation, one of the computational problems of Section 11. Two perturbations that most often demand this capture step, and demand it in opposite directions, are the slip and the trip of Section 8.

8. Slips and trips: two distinct failure modes

Two everyday perturbations put the capture step of Section 7 to work, and it is tempting to lump them together as "losing your balance." Mechanically they are opposites - a slip and a trip fail in different directions, for different reasons, and demand different recoveries - and conflating them is exactly the error that leads to the wrong intervention. Fig. 9 sets them side by side.

The slip

A slip is a failure of friction. At each footfall the ground reaction has a vertical component $F_v$ (supporting body weight) and a horizontal component $F_h$ (braking or propelling). The floor can only supply horizontal force up to a fraction of the vertical - the available friction $\mu_{\rm avail}F_v$ - so if the step demands more, the foot slides.

Proposition 8.1 (slip condition and direction). Let the required (utilized) coefficient of friction be $\mu_{\rm req}=|F_h|/F_v$. The foot slips exactly when $$\boxed{\;\mu_{\rm req}=\frac{|F_h|}{F_v}\gt\mu_{\rm avail}\;}$$ Because heel strike demands a forward-braking friction, the foot that slips shoots forward, the base of support leaves from under the centre of mass, and the fall is backward.
The Coulomb friction limit is $|F_h|\le\mu_{\rm avail}F_v$; dividing by $F_v\gt0$, the foot holds iff $|F_h|/F_v\le\mu_{\rm avail}$, and slides otherwise, which is the boxed inequality with $\mu_{\rm req}=|F_h|/F_v$ (the utilized coefficient of Module 8). At heel strike the leading foot is ahead of the COM and pushes forward into the floor, so the friction reaction on the foot points backward while the foot's tendency, once grip is lost, is to slide forward relative to the body. With the support point accelerating forward and the COM left behind and above, the line from the new contact through the COM tilts backward, so gravity's moment rotates the body backward - a backward fall. Recovery therefore has two parts: reduce $\mu_{\rm req}$ (shorten the step, land flatter, lower the braking demand) to regain grip, and, if the extrapolated COM has already left the base, a rapid backward-directed capture step. ∎

The trip

A trip is a failure of clearance. The swing foot catches an obstacle and is arrested while the body is still moving forward. Now the perturbation is not lost grip but a suddenly fixed pivot, and it converts forward translation into forward rotation.

Proposition 8.2 (trip pitch and its capture budget). When the swing foot is fully arrested at a block, angular momentum about the block is conserved through the impulsive contact, so the body acquires a forward pitch rate $$\boxed{\;\dot\theta^+=\frac{v}{\ell},\qquad \Delta\xi=\frac{v}{\omega_0}\;}$$ where $v$ is the pre-trip forward COM speed. The extrapolated COM is thrown forward by $v/\omega_0$ about the block, past the tiny stopped-foot base, so a capture step is mandatory; whether the tripped leg elevates over the obstacle or lowers and yields to the other leg is set by how much of the swing remained.
Model the arrest as impulsive: the obstacle applies a large force through the contact point over a vanishing time. That force has no moment about the contact, so angular momentum about the block is conserved across the instant. Just before, the COM translates forward at speed $v$ at height $\ell$ above the block, giving angular momentum $L=mv\ell$ about it; just after, the body rotates rigidly about the block with moment of inertia $I=m\ell^2$, so $\dot\theta^+=L/I=mv\ell/(m\ell^2)=v/\ell$. The COM's forward speed is preserved, $\dot x_{\rm com}^+=\ell\dot\theta^+=v$, so by the definition of the extrapolated COM its value relative to the now-fixed block jumps by $\Delta\xi=\dot x_{\rm com}^+/\omega_0=v/\omega_0$. For any realistic walking speed this exceeds the foot's own length, so $\xi$ lies past the base and Proposition 7.2 forces a step. If the trip occurs early in swing there is time and height to lift the caught foot over the obstacle and plant it forward (the elevating strategy); late in swing there is not, and the caught foot is dropped immediately while the contralateral leg takes the capture step (the lowering strategy). ∎

The two modes now separate cleanly, and each ties back to Module 8. A slip is governed by the utilized coefficient of friction $\mu_{\rm req}$ that Module 8 introduced for gait: prevention lowers $\mu_{\rm req}$ (footwear, gait changes, gritting a floor) or raises $\mu_{\rm avail}$. A trip is governed by foot clearance - the vertical gap between the swing foot and the ground that Module 8 tracked across the stride - and prevention raises clearance (lifting the toe, seeing the obstacle). The direction of the fall is the quickest field diagnostic: backward means a slip, forward means a trip, and the two call for opposite fixes. Both, though, are the same control failure underneath - a perturbation that throws the extrapolated COM past the base faster than the delayed, noisy estimator-controller can place a foot - which is why the very same declines that shrink the stability island make both slips and trips more dangerous with age, the subject of Section 9.

9. Aging as parameter drift, not "age"

Module 8 closed with a stark fact: falls are the leading cause of injury death in older adults, and it stacked the physiological reasons - weaker muscles, slower nerves, dimmer senses. This module can now say why those changes cause falls, in the language of the controller. Age is not itself a variable in any equation here. What changes are four control parameters, and each one moves a quantity we have already derived.

The four drifts. With aging (and many pathologies):
ParameterDriftDerived effect (where)
Reflex/loop delay $\tau$increasesshrinks the stability island, slides the operating point toward its edge (§4)
Peak ankle torque $\tau_{\max}$decreaseslowers ankle authority and capture-step reach (§7)
Torque rate $\dot\tau$decreasesdelays the hip and step, so $\xi$ diverges further before the foot lands (§8)
Sensor-noise variance $\sigma^2$increaseswidens sway and degrades the state estimate (§5, §6)

The first drift is the sharpest. Slower conduction and central processing lengthen $\tau$; from Section 4 that both shrinks the stability island and slides the operating point rightward toward its boundary, so the same feedback gains that were comfortably stable become lightly damped, then oscillatory (Fig. 10, left). The weaker, slower ankle ($\tau_{\max}\downarrow$, $\dot\tau\downarrow$) directly lowers the recovery budget, which we can make quantitative with a simple model of the capture step.

Definition 6 (capture-step reach model). A perturbation impulse $J$ at rest sets the extrapolated COM to $\xi^+=J/(m\omega_0)$, which then diverges as $\xi(t)=\xi^+e^{\omega_0 t}$. A capture step lands after a total time $t_{\rm step}=\tau+t_{\rm move}$ (reaction delay plus foot-travel time, the latter set by torque rate) and can reach at most a distance $L_{\rm step}$ (set by torque capacity). The step succeeds iff $\xi(t_{\rm step})\le L_{\rm step}$, giving the largest recoverable impulse $$\boxed{\;J_{\rm crit}=m\,\omega_0\,L_{\rm step}\,e^{-\omega_0(\tau+t_{\rm move})}\;}$$
Proposition 9.1 (monotone erosion of recovery). The largest recoverable impulse $J_{\rm crit}$ is strictly decreasing in the reflex delay $\tau$ and the foot-travel time $t_{\rm move}$, and strictly increasing in the reachable step length $L_{\rm step}$. Together with $\operatorname{Var}(\theta)\propto\sigma^2$ from Section 5, every one of the four aging drifts - $\tau\uparrow$, $t_{\rm move}\uparrow$ (rate $\downarrow$), $L_{\rm step}\downarrow$ (torque $\downarrow$), $\sigma^2\uparrow$ - reduces the recovery envelope or widens the sway. No single drift needs to be large for their product to be dangerous.
Differentiate the boxed $J_{\rm crit}$: $\partial J_{\rm crit}/\partial\tau=-\omega_0 J_{\rm crit}\lt0$ and $\partial J_{\rm crit}/\partial t_{\rm move}=-\omega_0 J_{\rm crit}\lt0$, so raising either delay lowers $J_{\rm crit}$ exponentially; $\partial J_{\rm crit}/\partial L_{\rm step}=m\omega_0 e^{-\omega_0(\tau+t_{\rm move})}\gt0$, so shrinking the reachable step lowers it linearly. The sway result $\operatorname{Var}(\theta)=q/(2(k_p-\omega_0^2)k_d)$ of Proposition 5.1 is proportional to the noise intensity, which rises with sensor-noise variance $\sigma^2$. Each drift therefore moves its quantity in the direction of worse balance, and since $J_{\rm crit}$ multiplies the exponential factors, the effects compound rather than add. ∎

The clinical picture falls out of one comparison (Fig. 10, right). The perturbation world does not soften with age - the shoves, uneven kerbs, and missed steps of daily life keep the same distribution of impulses. What changes is the recovery envelope, which shrinks until the everyday perturbation distribution, once safely below the recoverable ceiling, now overlaps it. Falls risk is precisely that overlap: not a new kind of disturbance, but an old one meeting a smaller envelope. For a young parameter set the largest recoverable impulse is far above the everyday range; for an aged set it drops into it, and the fraction of daily perturbations that exceed it - the fall probability - climbs steeply.

Two consequences matter for intervention, and both come straight from the parameters. Because $J_{\rm crit}$ depends on $L_{\rm step}$ and the delays, training that restores step length and speed (power training, reactive-stepping practice) enlarges the envelope directly, often more than strength training that only raises steady torque. And because the drifts compound, correcting even one - clearer vision to cut $\sigma^2$, or removing a sedating medication that lengthens $\tau$ - can move the envelope back off the perturbation distribution. This module has given the mechanism; the biology and chemistry of why $\tau$, $\tau_{\max}$, $\dot\tau$, and $\sigma^2$ drift - sarcopenia, neural conduction slowing, vestibular hair-cell and receptor loss, drug effects - are deferred to Module 14, and the measurement of these very parameters on a force plate to Module 15.

Lab A - Balance recovery after a push, and the largest arrestable impulse

Simulate the delayed closed loop of Section 4 after a horizontal push, and find by bisection the largest impulse the ankle controller can still arrest without the lean crossing the foot edge (Section 7). The recovery budget is a computed number, not a formula, because it depends on the whole delayed trajectory.

import numpy as np

w0, kd, kp, ell, m = 3.1, 3.0, 20.0, 1.0, 70.0
theta_max = 0.10          # foot-edge lean angle (rad)


def recover(J, tau, T=6.0, dt=0.002):
    """Delayed closed loop after a push impulse J (N s). True if the
    ankle controller arrests the sway without |theta| passing the foot
    edge."""
    n = int(T/dt)
    d = max(1, int(round(tau/dt)))
    th = np.zeros(n+1)
    om = np.zeros(n+1)
    om[0] = J/(m*ell)                  # theta-dot kick from the impulse
    for i in range(n):
        thd = th[i-d] if i-d >= 0 else 0.0
        omd = om[i-d] if i-d >= 0 else 0.0
        om[i+1] = om[i] + dt*(w0*w0*th[i] - kp*thd - kd*omd)
        th[i+1] = th[i] + dt*om[i+1]
        if abs(th[i+1]) > theta_max:
            return False
    return abs(th[-1]) < 0.02 and abs(om[-1]) < 0.05


def max_impulse(tau):                  # bisection on the pass/fail flag
    lo, hi = 0.0, 60.0
    for _ in range(34):
        mid = 0.5*(lo+hi)
        lo, hi = (mid, hi) if recover(mid, tau) else (lo, mid)
    return lo


for tau in (0.05, 0.10, 0.15):
    print(f"tau={tau*1000:3.0f} ms -> J_max = {max_impulse(tau):.1f} N s")

    xt = np.zeros((n, 2))
    xt[0] = [0.08, 0.0]
    for i in range(n-1):               # true sway with process noise
        kick = np.sqrt(qp*dt)*rng.standard_normal()
        xt[i+1] = F @ xt[i] + [0, kick]
    z = np.full(n, np.nan)             # delayed, noisy measurement of theta
    for i in range(d, n):
        z[i] = xt[i-d, 0] + rm*rng.standard_normal()
    H = np.array([[1.0, 0.0]])
    Q = np.array([[0, 0], [0, qp*dt]])
    P, xc = np.eye(2)*0.05, np.array([0.08, 0.0])
    pred = np.full(n, np.nan)
    for i in range(1, n):
        xc, P = F @ xc, F @ P @ F.T + Q            # predict
        if not np.isnan(z[i]):                     # update
            S = (H @ P @ H.T)[0, 0] + rm*rm
            Kk = (P @ H.T)[:, 0] / S
            xc = xc + Kk*(z[i] - (H @ xc)[0])
            P = (np.eye(2) - np.outer(Kk, H[0])) @ P
        pred[i] = (Fp @ xc)[0]                     # push forward by tau
    mm = ~np.isnan(z)
    raw = np.sqrt(np.nanmean((z[mm]-xt[mm, 0])**2))
    prd = np.sqrt(np.nanmean((pred[mm]-xt[mm, 0])**2))
    return raw, prd


print("RMS error  raw=%.3f  predictor=%.3f rad" % kalman_demo())

Output: RMS error $\approx0.075\ \mathrm{rad}$ for the raw delayed measurement versus $\approx0.033\ \mathrm{rad}$ for the filtered, predicted estimate - less than half. Try: close the loop by feeding $u=-K\hat{\mathbf x}$ back into the plant and compare the sway when the controller uses the predictor versus the raw delayed signal.

11. Problems, diagnostics, and limitations

Thirty problems in three families - ten conceptual, ten derivational, ten computational - plus five quick diagnostics, then an honest account of what this single-segment, sagittal, linearised model captures and misses, and the ledger of debts it hands forward. Every problem carries a figure and a collapsible solution; the computational solutions use Python-verified numbers.

Conceptual ↑ contents

C1. A gymnast stands motionless with her centre of mass over the beam, then begins to topple even though the COM line has not yet left the beam. A dancer leans far past his toes but does not fall. Reconcile both with the dynamic stability condition. Probes: Section 1; static vs dynamic margin.
Solution
Static balance asks where $x_{\rm com}$ is; dynamic balance asks where the extrapolated COM $\xi=x_{\rm com}+\dot x_{\rm com}/\omega_0$ is. The gymnast's COM is over the beam but already moving, so $\xi$ has left it - she is dynamically unstable despite looking centred. The dancer's COM is past his toes but moving back, so $\xi$ is still inside the base and he recovers. Position alone is necessary but not sufficient; the velocity term is what decides.
C2. Why can a person never stand perfectly still, even with the eyes open and the feet planted? Point to the specific feature of the control loop responsible. Probes: Section 4, Section 5; delay-driven sway.
Solution
The feedback is delayed ($\approx100$-$200\ \mathrm{ms}$), which places the closed loop near its stability boundary and leaves it only lightly damped; and it is noisy (sensor and motor noise). A lightly damped system continually kicked by noise rings rather than settling, so the body performs a bounded random wander - it cannot be still because the correction it applies is always aimed slightly at the past and always slightly wrong. Removing the delay (impossible) or the noise (impossible) is what stillness would require.
C3. A patient stands well with eyes open on firm ground but sways alarmingly with eyes closed on foam. Name which channels each manipulation removes or corrupts, and why the combination is the classic worst case. Probes: Section 3; sensory channels.
Solution
Closing the eyes removes the visual channel (optic flow); foam corrupts ankle proprioception (the muscle spindles no longer map cleanly to sway, since the surface deforms). That leaves only the vestibular channel to estimate sway, so two of three independent channels are gone and the estimate inherits far more noise - the eyes-closed-on-foam test isolates vestibular function precisely because it strips the other two.
C4. A coach says a good stumble-recovery is "just a much stronger ankle push." Correct the coach: why is a step a different control regime, not a bigger ankle response? Probes: Section 7; change of support.
Solution
Ankle torque works only by shifting the centre of pressure within the foot; once the COP reaches the toe it saturates, no matter how strong the muscle. A step does something categorically different - it moves the base of support itself, changing the pivot the whole pendulum swings about. Fixed support (ankle, hip) can only arrest $\xi$ while it is inside the foot; past that a step is mandatory (capturability), so it is a change of regime, not a change of magnitude.
C5. Two people fall on ice: one lands on their back, one on their face. Which suffered a slip and which a trip, and what governing quantity separates the two? Probes: Section 8; slip vs trip.
Solution
The backward faller slipped: the stance foot lost grip and shot forward, the base left from under the COM, and the body fell backward - a friction failure, $\mu_{\rm req}=|F_h|/F_v\gt \mu_{\rm avail}$. The forward faller tripped: the swing foot was caught and the still-moving body pitched forward about the block - a clearance failure. Direction of fall is the field diagnostic; the governing quantities (utilized coefficient of friction vs foot clearance) are different, and so are the fixes.
C6. An engineer models the nervous system as trusting each balance sensor with a fixed weight. Give one everyday observation that refutes fixed weights and name the principle that replaces them. Probes: Section 3, Section 6; sensory reweighting.
Solution
Close your eyes and you do not fall - if vision had a fixed nonzero weight, removing it would leave a persistent error, but instead balance degrades only gracefully. The nervous system performs sensory reweighting: it shifts weight toward whichever channels are currently reliable. This is exactly what the Kalman gain does - a channel with larger noise variance $\sigma_m^2$ automatically receives a smaller weight $K=\sigma_p^2/(\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2)$ - so reweighting is optimal fusion, not a separate rule.
C7. "If proportional gain stabilises standing, more gain must stabilise it better." Give two independent reasons this is false. Probes: Section 4, Section 5; the case against high gain.
Solution
First, delay: raising $k_p$ lowers the ceiling of the stability island, so past the boundary higher gain drives a growing oscillation - the mistimed correction pumps the sway. Second, signal-dependent motor noise: a larger command carries proportionally larger noise, so beyond an optimum the added jitter outweighs the tighter control and sway rises again. Both make sway a U-shaped, not decreasing, function of gain, so there is a best gain, not an unlimited one.
C8. A clinician writes "falls due to age." Rewrite the cause in terms of control parameters the model actually contains, and explain why this is more than pedantry. Probes: Section 9; parameter drift.
Solution
Age is not a variable in any equation here; four control parameters are: reflex delay $\tau\uparrow$, peak ankle torque $\tau_{\max}\downarrow$, torque rate $\dot\tau\downarrow$, and sensor-noise variance $\sigma^2\uparrow$. Each shrinks the recovery envelope or widens the sway. This is more than pedantry because parameters are actionable: clearer glasses cut $\sigma^2$, power training raises $\tau_{\max}$ and $\dot\tau$, deprescribing a sedative shortens $\tau$ - each moves the envelope back off the perturbation distribution, whereas "age" cannot be treated.
C9. The semicircular canals report only head angular velocity, never sway angle. How can a controller that needs both the angle and the rate function on such a channel? Probes: Section 3; observability.
Solution
Because the pair $(A,C)$ is observable: the plant model links angle and rate, so a channel reporting only one component still determines the whole state over time (the observability matrix has full rank). An internal model integrates the measured rate and cross-checks it against the dynamics, reconstructing the angle the sensor never reported directly - which is why any single channel suffices in principle, and fusing several only lowers the estimate's noise.
C10. The feedback delay is fixed by biology and cannot be shortened. How does the nervous system escape the instability the delay would otherwise cause? Probes: Section 6; the forward model.
Solution
It predicts. Rather than feed back the stale delayed measurement, it runs an internal forward model of the body's dynamics to push the estimate forward by $\tau$, and controls on the predicted present state. If the prediction is accurate the effective loop is delay-free, restoring the stability island the raw delay destroyed. The delay is not shortened; its effect is anticipated away, which is why balance survives lags that would destabilise a purely reactive controller.

Derivational ↑ contents

D1. Starting from the definition of the extrapolated COM, derive the margin of stability $b$ and show that the largest arrestable horizontal impulse from rest is $J_{\max}=m\omega_0 b$. Probes: Section 1.
Solution
The margin is $b=x_{\rm edge}-\xi=x_{\rm edge}-x_{\rm com}-\dot x_{\rm com}/\omega_0$. From rest ($\xi=0$, $b=x_{\rm edge}$), an impulse $J$ changes velocity by $\Delta\dot x_{\rm com}=J/m$ and leaves position unchanged, so $\xi$ jumps by $\Delta\xi=J/(m\omega_0)$. Fixed-support recovery holds iff $\xi\le x_{\rm edge}=b$, i.e. $J/(m\omega_0)\le b$, giving $J_{\max}=m\omega_0 b$. With $m=70,\ \omega_0=3.1,\ b=0.10$: $J_{\max}\approx22\ \mathrm{N\,s}$.
D2. Write the linearised inverted pendulum with ankle control in state-space form $\dot{\mathbf x}=A\mathbf x+Bu$ and prove the pair $(A,B)$ is controllable. Probes: Section 2.
Solution
With $\mathbf x=[\theta,\dot\theta]^{\mathsf T}$ and $\ddot\theta=\omega_0^2\theta+u$, $A=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, $B=[0,1]^{\mathsf T}$. Then $AB=[1,0]^{\mathsf T}$, so the controllability matrix $\mathcal C=[B\ AB]=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ 1&0\end{smallmatrix}\right)$ has $\det=-1\neq0$, hence rank $2$ = state dimension: controllable. So the closed-loop poles can be placed anywhere by state feedback.
D3. For feedback $u=-[k_p\ k_d]\mathbf x$, derive the closed-loop matrix and characteristic polynomial, and state the exact stability conditions on $k_p,k_d$. Probes: Section 2.
Solution
$A-BK=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\ \omega_0^2-k_p&-k_d\end{smallmatrix}\right)$, characteristic polynomial $s^2+k_d s+(k_p-\omega_0^2)$. By Routh-Hurwitz for a quadratic, both roots lie in the left half-plane iff both coefficients are positive: $k_d\gt 0$ and $k_p\gt \omega_0^2$. The position gain must beat gravity's rate $\omega_0^2$ to turn the saddle into a sink; the rate gain supplies damping.
D4. Insert a feedback delay $\tau$ and derive the characteristic equation of the resulting delay-differential equation, then the imaginary-axis crossing conditions that bound the stability island. Probes: Section 4.
Solution
With $\ddot\theta=\omega_0^2\theta-[k_p\theta(t-\tau)+k_d\dot\theta(t-\tau)]$, the ansatz $\theta=e^{st}$ gives $s^2-\omega_0^2+(k_p+k_d s)e^{-s\tau}=0$. Setting $s=i\Omega$ and splitting real/imaginary parts yields $\sin(\Omega\tau)=k_d\Omega/(\Omega^2+\omega_0^2)$ and $k_p=(\Omega^2+\omega_0^2)\cos(\Omega\tau)$; sweeping $\Omega$ traces the island boundary, whose smallest $\tau$ is the critical delay (for $k_p=20,k_d=3$: $\tau_c\approx137\ \mathrm{ms}$).
D5. Model quiet standing as the stable closed loop driven by white noise and derive the stationary sway variance by solving the Lyapunov equation. Probes: Section 5.
Solution
With $a_0=k_p-\omega_0^2$, $a_1=k_d$, and noise only in the $\dot\theta$ equation ($Q=\left(\begin{smallmatrix}0&0\\0&q\end{smallmatrix}\right)$), the stationary covariance solves $A_{\rm cl}P+PA_{\rm cl}^{\mathsf T}+Q=0$. The three scalar equations give $p_{12}=0$, $p_{22}=q/(2a_1)$, $p_{11}=q/(2a_0a_1)$, so $\operatorname{Var}(\theta)=q/[2(k_p-\omega_0^2)k_d]$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\dot\theta)=q/(2k_d)$, with angle and rate uncorrelated.
D6. Derive the scalar Kalman gain: the minimum-variance weighted average of an unbiased prediction (variance $\sigma_p^2$) and an independent unbiased measurement (variance $\sigma_m^2$). Probes: Section 6.
Solution
For $\hat x=(1-K)\hat x^-+Ky$, the variance is $V(K)=(1-K)^2\sigma_p^2+K^2\sigma_m^2$. Minimising, $V'(K)=-2(1-K)\sigma_p^2+2K\sigma_m^2=0\Rightarrow K=\sigma_p^2/(\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2)$, a minimum since $V''=2(\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2)\gt 0$. The fused variance $\sigma_+^2=(1-K)\sigma_p^2=\sigma_p^2\sigma_m^2/(\sigma_p^2+\sigma_m^2)$ is smaller than either input.
D7. Derive the delay predictor that maps the filtered estimate of the delayed state to an estimate of the present state, and explain why feeding it back restores the lost stability island. Probes: Section 6.
Solution
By variation of parameters on $\dot{\mathbf x}=A\mathbf x+Bu$ over $[t-\tau,t]$, $\mathbf x(t)=e^{A\tau}\mathbf x(t-\tau)+\int_0^\tau e^{A(\tau-s)}Bu(t-\tau+s)\,ds$. Replacing the unknown $\mathbf x(t-\tau)$ by the filter's estimate gives $\hat{\mathbf x}(t)$; its error is $e^{A\tau}$ times the (small) estimation error. Feeding $u=-K\hat{\mathbf x}(t)$ applies the Section 2 gain to a current-state estimate, so the loop behaves as the delay-free $A-BK$ - the island is restored up to prediction error.
D8. Derive the capture-step placement that arrests a fall in one step, and show that landing the foot exactly at the extrapolated COM is the marginal case. Probes: Section 7.
Solution
After stepping to $x_{\rm foot}$, the pendulum coordinate is $x'=x_{\rm com}-x_{\rm foot}$ and the divergent coordinate becomes $\xi'=\xi-x_{\rm foot}$. Coming to rest over the new foot requires $\xi'\le0$, i.e. $x_{\rm foot}\ge\xi$. The marginal choice $x_{\rm foot}=\xi$ gives $\xi'=0$ (exact one-step capture); adding a margin $\Delta$ gives $x_{\rm foot}=\xi+\Delta$, leaving $\xi$ a distance $\Delta$ inside the new base.
D9. Derive the slip condition from the Coulomb friction limit and explain why a slip produces a backward fall. Probes: Section 8.
Solution
The floor supplies horizontal force up to $|F_h|\le\mu_{\rm avail}F_v$; dividing by $F_v$, the foot holds iff the required (utilized) coefficient $\mu_{\rm req}=|F_h|/F_v\le\mu_{\rm avail}$ and slips otherwise. At heel strike the leading foot pushes forward into the floor and, on losing grip, slides forward; the support accelerates ahead of the COM, tilting the contact-to-COM line backward so gravity's moment rotates the body backward - a backward fall.
D10. A walking body's swing foot is fully arrested by an obstacle. Using conservation of angular momentum, derive the forward pitch rate and the jump in the extrapolated COM. Probes: Section 8.
Solution
The impulsive contact force acts through the block, exerting no moment about it, so angular momentum about the block is conserved. Before: $L=mv\ell$ (COM moving at $v$ at height $\ell$); after, rigid rotation with $I=m\ell^2$ gives $\dot\theta^+=L/I=v/\ell$. The COM's forward speed is preserved, so relative to the now-fixed block $\Delta\xi=\dot x_{\rm com}^+/\omega_0=v/\omega_0$ - which exceeds the foot length for any real walking speed, forcing a capture step.

Computational ↑ contents

Each K problem requires a simulation, an optimization, an inverse, a sensitivity sweep, or a regime comparison - none is a substitution into a boxed formula.

K1. Simulate the delayed closed loop after a push and find, by bisection, the largest arrestable impulse at three delays. Explain the shape of $J_{\max}(\tau)$. Probes: Lab A; simulation + inverse search.
Solution
Integrate the delay-differential equation from a velocity kick $\dot\theta_0=J/(m\ell)$ and mark success when the lean is arrested without crossing the foot edge; bisect on $J$. The budget is $J_{\max}\approx32,\,25,\,1\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ at $\tau=50,100,150\ \mathrm{ms}$ - it collapses before the loop itself goes unstable, because as $\tau\to\tau_c\approx137\ \mathrm{ms}$ the damping vanishes and even a small kick rings past the foot edge. The number is not the impulse-momentum $m\omega_0 b$ of D1 but that budget eroded by the whole delayed transient.
K2. Map the delay-gain plane by classifying each simulated trajectory (decays / rings / grows) and check that the boundary matches the analytic island of Proposition 4.2. Probes: Lab B; simulation vs analytic.
Solution
For each $(\tau,k_p)$ integrate from a small tilt and classify by the late-time amplitude. The simulated stable cells fall exactly on the low-$\tau$/moderate-$k_p$ side of the analytic curve $\sin(\Omega\tau)=k_d\Omega/(\Omega^2+\omega_0^2)$, $k_p=(\Omega^2+\omega_0^2)\cos\Omega\tau$ - a check that the transcendental boundary was solved correctly. The value is the confirmation: an analytic boundary is only trustworthy once a blind simulation reproduces it.
K3. Within the stability island at $\tau=100\ \mathrm{ms}$, search $(k_p,k_d)$ for the gains that make the recovery settle fastest, and interpret where the optimum sits. Probes: §2, §4; optimization.
Solution
Grid-search the gains, keeping only stable pairs, and score each by the time for the recovery to fall under $10\%$ of its peak. The fastest settling ($\approx0.5\ \mathrm{s}$) sits at high $k_p\approx27$ with strong damping $k_d\approx6.8$ - pressed against the island edge. The optimum is a corner solution bounded by the delay, not an interior one: you want the largest gains the delay will still tolerate, which is exactly why a slowed reflex (smaller $\tau_c$) forces slower recovery.
K4. Given only an observed sway trace following an unknown push, reconstruct the disturbance impulse. State the estimator and its sensitivity to noise. Probes: §1, §6; inverse problem.
Solution
The push sets the initial angular velocity $\dot\theta_0=J/(m\ell)$, so estimate $\dot\theta_0$ from the initial slope of the trace and invert: $\hat J=m\ell\,\dot\theta_0$. On a clean trace this recovers the impulse essentially exactly ($\hat J=18.0$ for a true $18\ \mathrm{N\,s}$). With measurement noise the slope estimate degrades, so in practice one fits the first tenth of a second (or runs the Kalman filter of §6 backward) rather than differencing two samples - the inverse is well-posed but noise-sensitive, unlike the forward map.
K5. Close the loop with a Kalman filter plus predictor and compare the state-estimate error against feeding back the raw delayed measurement. Probes: Lab C; regime comparison.
Solution
Run the filter on delayed, noisy measurements of a simulated sway and predict forward by $\tau$. The predicted estimate tracks the true state with RMS error $\approx0.033\ \mathrm{rad}$, versus $\approx0.075\ \mathrm{rad}$ for the raw delayed measurement - less than half. The comparison isolates the estimator's value: the same controller fed the clean, current estimate stays inside the island that the raw stale signal would push it out of.
K6. With signal-dependent motor noise $q(k_p)=q_0\big(1+(k_p/k_{\rm ref})^2\big)$, sweep the gain and find the sway-minimising $k_p$. Why is there an interior optimum? Probes: Section 5; sensitivity + optimization.
Solution
Substitute the gain-dependent intensity into $\operatorname{Var}(\theta)=q(k_p)/[2(k_p-\omega_0^2)k_d]$ and minimise numerically. Sway falls at first (tighter control) then rises (more command means more motor noise), giving an interior optimum near $k_p\approx22\ \mathrm{s^{-2}}$ (RMS $\approx0.9^\circ$). A constant-noise model predicts monotonically less sway with gain; the signal dependence is exactly what turns "more gain is better" into a trade-off, invisible in the boxed variance alone.
K7. Sweep the critical delay against the position gain and the COM height. Which parameter dominates, and what does that imply for tall versus short balancers? Probes: Section 4; sensitivity.
Solution
Solve the boundary for $\tau_c$ over each parameter. The gain dominates: $\tau_c\approx197,\,137,\,99\ \mathrm{ms}$ for $k_p=14,20,28$ - higher gain buys speed at the cost of delay tolerance. Height barely matters: over $\ell=0.8$-$1.3\ \mathrm{m}$, $\tau_c$ moves only $140\to135\ \mathrm{ms}$, because raising $\ell$ lowers both $\omega_0$ and the boundary together. The design lesson is that the operating gain, not stature, sets how much delay the standing controller can absorb.
K8. After a push that requires a step, sweep the foot-placement error and map which placements still capture the fall. State the admissible window. Probes: Section 7; simulation.
Solution
With the ideal capture point at $x_{\rm foot}=\xi$, a placement error $e$ leaves the post-step divergent coordinate $\xi'=-e$. Capture requires $\xi'\in[-\text{BoS},0]$, i.e. $0\le e\le\text{BoS}\approx0.10\ \mathrm{m}$: overshooting forward up to a foot-length still catches, but undershooting ($e\lt 0$) fails immediately because $\xi$ stays ahead of the new base. The asymmetry - forward error tolerated, backward error fatal - is why reactive steps are aimed slightly long.
K9. Sweep the four aging parameters and compute the critical impulse, one at a time and combined, to quantify how the drifts compound. Probes: Lab D, §9; sensitivity.
Solution
From $J_{\rm crit}=m\omega_0 L_{\rm step}e^{-\omega_0(\tau+t_{\rm move})}$, the young set gives $51\ \mathrm{N\,s}$. Drifting one parameter to its aged value gives $\approx40$ (delay), $35$ (rate), or $36\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ (step length) - each a modest cut. Together they give $19\ \mathrm{N\,s}$, far below the sum of the individual losses, because the delays enter as a product of exponentials: the drifts multiply, so no single deficit needs to be large to make the combination dangerous.
K10. Compare slip outcomes across available friction and reaction delay: for which combinations does the recovery step arrive in time? Probes: Section 8; regime comparison.
Solution
Take a heel-strike required coefficient $\mu_{\rm req}\approx0.20$; the foot grips when $\mu_{\rm avail}\ge\mu_{\rm req}$ ($\mu_{\rm avail}=0.30$: no slip) and slides otherwise ($0.15,\,0.08$: slip). Once sliding, recovery is a race - the extrapolated COM diverges at rate $\omega_0$ while the step is prepared over the reaction delay $\tau$ - so success needs a step reachable within $\tau$, which the aging model of §9 shows shrinks with delay and weakness. The regime map has two failure routes: too little friction to grip, and too slow a step to recover once grip is lost.

Five diagnostics ↑ contents

Quick self-checks; the answer follows each.

  1. Units of the extrapolated COM velocity term. Why does $\dot x_{\rm com}$ get divided by $\omega_0$ (not multiplied) to add to a position?
    Answer
    $\omega_0$ has units of $1/\mathrm{time}$, so $\dot x_{\rm com}/\omega_0$ has units of length - a position increment. It is the distance the pendulum's unstable mode carries the COM in one time-constant, the natural weight the dynamics assign to velocity.
  2. Sign of the gravity term. In $\ddot\theta=+\omega_0^2\theta+u$, why is the coefficient of $\theta$ positive, unlike a hanging pendulum?
    Answer
    Upright is unstable: a forward tilt makes gravity's moment tilt it further forward, so the restoring term has the destabilising $+$ sign. A hanging pendulum has $-\omega_0^2\theta$ (gravity restores). The controller's job is to supply an effective negative term $-k_p\theta$ that overcomes it.
  3. Delay versus lag. A first-order low-pass also "slows" feedback. Why is a pure time delay far more destabilising?
    Answer
    A low-pass adds bounded phase lag (at most $90^\circ$); a pure delay adds phase $\Omega\tau$ that grows without bound with frequency, so at high enough frequency the correction is fully out of phase and pumps the oscillation. That unbounded phase is why a delay, not a lag, creates the stability island.
  4. Which way does a slip drop you? A forward-moving walker's stance foot slips. Forward or backward fall?
    Answer
    Backward. The foot shoots forward out from under the COM, which is left behind, so the body rotates backward about the fleeing contact. (A trip - swing foot blocked - is the forward one.)
  5. Why fusing sensors never hurts. Adding a noisier channel to the estimate - can it make the estimate worse?
    Answer
    No. Optimal fusion weights each channel by its inverse variance, so a very noisy channel simply gets a near-zero weight; stacking more rows onto $C$ cannot lower the observability rank and cannot raise the minimum-variance estimate's variance. Extra information, optimally weighted, is never harmful.

0. Motivation - the arm as a machine that positions and pushes

Reach out and pick up a coffee cup. In the second it takes, your arm solves four problems that the rest of this module makes precise. It must place the hand at the cup - a question of geometry: which shoulder and elbow angles put the fingertips there? It must get there smoothly and stop - a question of dynamics: which joint torques, fighting gravity and the arm's own inertia, produce that motion? Once the fingers close, it must not let the cup slip - a question of contact mechanics: how hard must the fingers squeeze so friction can carry the weight? And through all of it, it must choose one way to do the task out of infinitely many - a question of redundancy: the arm has more joints than the task strictly needs, and the surplus has to be spent somehow.

These are not four separate skills bolted together. They are four faces of a single object - the arm and hand read as a manipulator: a chain of rigid links (Section 1) connected by joints, ending in a hand that makes frictional contact with the world. Robotics built its whole vocabulary on exactly this object, and that vocabulary - forward kinematics, the Jacobian, inverse kinematics, the force-torque transpose, the friction cone - is the right language for the human arm too, because the human arm is the system the robots were abstracted from.

The spine of the module is a single mathematical thread pulled through the whole reach-grasp-hold act. We model the arm as a planar two-link chain and write its forward kinematics - joint angles in, hand position out (Section 1). Differentiating that map gives the Jacobian $J$, the matrix that turns joint angular velocities into hand velocity (Section 2). Inverting the map - hand position in, joint angles out - is inverse kinematics, and it immediately exposes two facts the geometry forces on us: a reachable point generally has two arm configurations, and at the edge of reach the arm goes singular and loses a direction of motion (Section 3). The same Jacobian, transposed, maps hand force to joint torque, $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$ - the reason holding a weight at arm's length is so costly, and the key to reading endpoint stiffness (Section 4).

With three links instead of two the arm becomes redundant - more joints than task dimensions - and we meet the null space: the family of joint motions that do not move the hand at all (Section 5). Then we put mass back in and derive the arm's inverse dynamics - the mass matrix, the velocity-dependent (Coriolis and centrifugal) terms, and the gravity torque - to compute the shoulder and elbow torques behind a wave (Section 6). A short structural section reads the shoulder as the price paid for the hand's enormous workspace: mobility bought with stability, and stabilised actively (Section 7). Finally the hand meets the object: the friction cone sets the grip force needed to hold the cup, precision and power grips are two ways of arranging contacts, and thumb opposition is what makes a human grasp force-closed (Section 8). Tactile afferents close the loop, setting grip force just above slip with a safety margin that collapses when the skin is numb (Section 9).

The thesis of this module. The arm and hand are a redundant manipulator: a serial chain whose geometry is captured by one matrix, the Jacobian $J$, that does double duty - $J$ maps joint rates to hand velocity, and $J^{\mathsf T}$ maps hand force to joint torque. Reaching is inverting $J$ (with two solutions and edge-of-reach singularities); holding still against a load is reading $J^{\mathsf T}$; moving with inertia is the arm's inverse dynamics; and gripping is a friction-cone inequality closed by tactile feedback. Redundancy - more joints than the task needs - is not a nuisance but the freedom the controller spends on comfort, accuracy, and avoiding its own limits, and it is the bridge to whole-body coordination in Module 12.

Where the pieces attach to earlier modules: the joint torques here are produced by the Hill-type muscle actuators of Module 5 pulling on the moment arms of Module 1; the shoulder and its cartilage are the constrained interface of Modules 3-4; and the tactile feedback that sets grip force is another instance of the noisy, delayed sensorimotor loop of Module 10. Module 12 takes the redundancy this module exposes and asks how the nervous system chooses among the infinitely many solutions - optimal control, impedance, and motor learning.

1. The arm as a kinematic chain: forward kinematics

Strip the arm to its mechanical essentials. Between the shoulder and the fingertip lie two long bones - the humerus (upper arm) and the forearm (radius and ulna, taken together) - joined at the elbow, a near-perfect hinge (Section 7 and Module 3). Hold the whole arm in a vertical plane - the sagittal plane of a forward reach - and the shoulder contributes one rotational degree of freedom in that plane, the elbow another. Two rigid links, two revolute (rotational) joints: this is the planar two-link arm, the hydrogen atom of manipulation, and every result in Sections 1-6 is built on it.

Definition 1 (planar two-link arm). Fix a coordinate frame at the shoulder, with $x$ horizontal (forward) and $y$ vertical (up). The upper arm has length $\ell_1$ and makes angle $\theta_1$ with the $x$-axis; the second link - the forearm with the hand lumped onto it, running from the elbow to the fingertips - has length $\ell_2$ and makes angle $\theta_2$ with the upper arm (the elbow angle, measured at the elbow so that $\theta_2=0$ is a straight arm). The joint-angle vector is $\mathbf q=(\theta_1,\theta_2)$; the configuration space is the set of all $\mathbf q$. The point at the end of the second link - the fingertip, which we take as the hand's reference point or endpoint - has position $\mathbf p=(x,y)$ in the shoulder frame. (With $\ell_2$ the elbow-to-fingertip length, the human forearm's $\sim0.27\,\mathrm{m}$ plus the hand give $\ell_2\approx0.45\,\mathrm{m}$.)

The forward kinematics is the map $\mathbf q\mapsto\mathbf p$: given the joint angles, where is the hand? Walk out the chain. The elbow sits at the end of the upper arm, at $(\ell_1\cos\theta_1,\,\ell_1\sin\theta_1)$. The forearm leaves the elbow at absolute angle $\theta_1+\theta_2$ (its own angle $\theta_2$ measured relative to the upper arm, added to the upper arm's $\theta_1$). Adding its contribution gives the endpoint.

Proposition 1.1 (forward kinematics of the two-link arm). The endpoint position is $$\boxed{\;\begin{aligned}x&=\ell_1\cos\theta_1+\ell_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2),\\ y&=\ell_1\sin\theta_1+\ell_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2).\end{aligned}\;}$$ The reachable set (the workspace) is the annulus $|\ell_1-\ell_2|\le\|\mathbf p\|\le \ell_1+\ell_2$ centred at the shoulder.
Place the shoulder at the origin. The upper-arm vector is $\ell_1(\cos\theta_1,\sin\theta_1)$, reaching the elbow. The forearm is a vector of length $\ell_2$ at absolute angle $\theta_1+\theta_2$, namely $\ell_2(\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2),\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2))$. The endpoint is the sum of these two vectors, giving the boxed expressions. For the workspace, the distance from shoulder to endpoint is $\|\mathbf p\|$; by the law of cosines applied to the triangle shoulder-elbow-endpoint with sides $\ell_1,\ell_2$ and included angle $\pi-\theta_2$, $\|\mathbf p\|^2=\ell_1^2+\ell_2^2+2\ell_1\ell_2\cos\theta_2$. As $\theta_2$ ranges over $[-\pi,\pi]$, $\cos\theta_2\in[-1,1]$, so $\|\mathbf p\|^2$ ranges from $(\ell_1-\ell_2)^2$ (arm folded, $\theta_2=\pm\pi$) to $(\ell_1+\ell_2)^2$ (arm straight, $\theta_2=0$); the reachable radii fill $[\,|\ell_1-\ell_2|,\,\ell_1+\ell_2\,]$. ∎

Two features of this innocent-looking map drive everything downstream. First, it is nonlinear in the angles - sines and cosines of sums - so nothing about the arm's behaviour will be as simple as "output proportional to input"; the local, linear picture is exactly what the Jacobian of Section 2 supplies. Second, the map is generally two-to-one onto the workspace interior: the same hand position is reached with the elbow bent one way or the other, the two solutions of Section 3. With realistic human segment lengths - $\ell_1\approx0.30\ \mathrm{m}$ for the upper arm, $\ell_2\approx0.45\ \mathrm{m}$ for the forearm-plus-hand to the fingertips - the workspace is an annulus of outer radius $0.75\ \mathrm{m}$ and inner radius $0.15\ \mathrm{m}$: the reach envelope you sweep when you circle your straight arm, with the small hole near the shoulder your hand cannot fold into.

A third link - the hand rotating at the wrist - adds $\theta_3$ and lets us control the hand's orientation as well as its position; we defer it to Section 5, where it makes the arm redundant. For now two links suffice to build the Jacobian.

2. The Jacobian: how joint rates become hand velocity

The forward map of Section 1 answers "where is the hand?" The next question is "which way, and how fast, does the hand move when the joints turn?" - and its answer is the single most useful object in manipulation. If the joints turn at angular rates $\dot\theta_1,\dot\theta_2$, the endpoint velocity $\dot{\mathbf p}=(\dot x,\dot y)$ follows by differentiating the forward kinematics with the chain rule. The bookkeeping of that differentiation is a matrix.

Definition 2 (the Jacobian). The Jacobian of the arm is the matrix of partial derivatives of the endpoint position with respect to the joint angles, $$J(\mathbf q)=\frac{\partial\mathbf p}{\partial\mathbf q}=\begin{bmatrix}\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial\theta_1}&\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial\theta_2}\\[1.2ex]\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial\theta_1}&\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial\theta_2}\end{bmatrix},$$ so that endpoint velocity and joint rates are related by $\dot{\mathbf p}=J(\mathbf q)\,\dot{\mathbf q}$. It depends on the configuration $\mathbf q$: the arm's velocity behaviour is different in every posture.

Write $s_1=\sin\theta_1$, $c_1=\cos\theta_1$, and $s_{12}=\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)$, $c_{12}=\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)$ for the sums. Differentiating Proposition 1.1 term by term gives the Jacobian in closed form.

Proposition 2.1 (the two-link Jacobian). For the arm of Definition 1, $$\boxed{\;J=\begin{bmatrix}-\ell_1 s_1-\ell_2 s_{12} & -\ell_2 s_{12}\\[.4ex] \ell_1 c_1+\ell_2 c_{12} & \ell_2 c_{12}\end{bmatrix},\qquad \det J=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\theta_2.\;}$$ The columns have a direct reading: the first column is the endpoint velocity produced by a unit shoulder rate (both links swing), the second by a unit elbow rate (only the forearm swings).
Differentiate $x=\ell_1 c_1+\ell_2 c_{12}$. Since $\partial c_1/\partial\theta_1=-s_1$ and $c_{12}=\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)$ depends on both angles with $\partial c_{12}/\partial\theta_1=\partial c_{12}/\partial\theta_2=-s_{12}$, we get $\partial x/\partial\theta_1=-\ell_1 s_1-\ell_2 s_{12}$ and $\partial x/\partial\theta_2=-\ell_2 s_{12}$. Likewise $\partial y/\partial\theta_1=\ell_1 c_1+\ell_2 c_{12}$ and $\partial y/\partial\theta_2=\ell_2 c_{12}$, giving the matrix. Its determinant is $(-\ell_1 s_1-\ell_2 s_{12})(\ell_2 c_{12})-(-\ell_2 s_{12})(\ell_1 c_1+\ell_2 c_{12})=-\ell_1\ell_2 s_1 c_{12}-\ell_2^2 s_{12}c_{12}+\ell_1\ell_2 s_{12}c_1+\ell_2^2 s_{12}c_{12}=\ell_1\ell_2(s_{12}c_1-s_1 c_{12})=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\big((\theta_1+\theta_2)-\theta_1\big)=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\theta_2$, using the sine subtraction identity. ∎

The determinant carries a warning that becomes Section 3's central fact. $\det J=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\theta_2$ vanishes exactly when $\theta_2=0$ (arm straight) or $\theta_2=\pm\pi$ (arm fully folded). At those postures $J$ is singular: it has a rank-deficient range, and there is a direction in which the hand cannot move no matter how the joints turn. With the arm straight out, that lost direction is radial - you cannot lengthen your reach by turning the joints, only swing the fingertip in an arc. Singularity is not a modelling artefact; it is the mathematics of "you can't reach any farther," and every controller that inverts $J$ must cope with it.

The Jacobian also quantifies dexterity. Feed the set of unit joint-rate vectors $\|\dot{\mathbf q}\|=1$ through $\dot{\mathbf p}=J\dot{\mathbf q}$ and the image is an ellipse in velocity space - the manipulability ellipsoid. Where it is fat the hand moves fast and easily; where it collapses toward a line the arm is near a singularity and moves freely in only one direction. Its area is proportional to $|\det J|=\ell_1\ell_2|\sin\theta_2|$, largest at $\theta_2=\pm\pi/2$ - the reason a mid-range, comfortably bent elbow is the posture of greatest control, and the straight-locked or fully-folded arm the clumsiest. The transpose of this same matrix, in Section 4, will turn the ellipsoid of easy velocities into an ellipsoid of easy forces - sharing the same axis directions but with reciprocal lengths, so its long and short axes are swapped (the major-axis direction turned ninety degrees) and the direction the hand moves most freely is the direction it pushes most weakly.

3. Inverse kinematics: two elbows and a wall

Reaching runs the map of Section 1 backward: the target hand position $\mathbf p=(x,y)$ is given - the cup is there - and the nervous system must find joint angles $\mathbf q=(\theta_1,\theta_2)$ that put the hand on it. This is inverse kinematics (IK). Unlike forward kinematics it need not have a unique solution, or any solution, and unpacking exactly when is the content of this section.

Proposition 3.1 (closed-form inverse kinematics). Let $r^2=x^2+y^2$ be the squared distance from shoulder to target. A target inside the workspace annulus ($|\ell_1-\ell_2|\le r\le\ell_1+\ell_2$) is reached by the joint angles $$\boxed{\;\theta_2=\pm\arccos\!\left(\frac{r^2-\ell_1^2-\ell_2^2}{2\ell_1\ell_2}\right),\qquad \theta_1=\operatorname{atan2}(y,x)-\operatorname{atan2}\!\big(\ell_2\sin\theta_2,\ \ell_1+\ell_2\cos\theta_2\big).\;}$$ The two signs of $\theta_2$ give two distinct configurations - elbow-down and elbow-up - reaching the same point; on the workspace boundary the two coincide.
From the law-of-cosines identity of Proposition 1.1, $r^2=\ell_1^2+\ell_2^2+2\ell_1\ell_2\cos\theta_2$, solve for $\cos\theta_2=(r^2-\ell_1^2-\ell_2^2)/(2\ell_1\ell_2)$; this lies in $[-1,1]$ precisely when $r$ is in the annulus, and $\theta_2=\pm\arccos(\cdot)$ gives the two elbow assemblies. For $\theta_1$, note the endpoint direction $\operatorname{atan2}(y,x)$ (the quadrant-aware two-argument arctangent, returning the polar angle of the point $(x,y)$) is the shoulder-to-target bearing, and the endpoint sits at angle $\operatorname{atan2}(\ell_2\sin\theta_2,\ell_1+\ell_2\cos\theta_2)$ above the upper-arm direction $\theta_1$ (the internal angle of the shoulder-elbow-endpoint triangle at the shoulder). Subtracting recovers $\theta_1$. When $r=\ell_1+\ell_2$ or $r=|\ell_1-\ell_2|$, $\cos\theta_2=\pm1$, so $\theta_2=0$ or $\pi$ and the two solutions merge - which is exactly where $\det J=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\theta_2=0$, the singularity of Section 2. ∎

Three lessons fall out of Proposition 3.1, and each recurs. Two solutions: almost every reachable point admits two arm postures, and choosing between them is the first, smallest instance of the redundancy problem - here a discrete choice (which elbow), in Section 5 a continuous one. Humans pick the elbow-down assembly for most tabletop reaches because it keeps the joints away from their limits and the arm out of its own way; the choice is made on comfort and obstacle-avoidance, not geometry, since geometry offers both freely.

The wall at the edge: as the target approaches the outer radius $\ell_1+\ell_2$, the two solutions collapse and the arm straightens. Here the Jacobian is singular, and - crucially for control - a small further step outward in $\mathbf p$ demands an unbounded change in $\mathbf q$: near the boundary, $\theta_2$ depends on $r$ with an infinite slope (the $\arccos$ has a vertical tangent at its endpoints). This is why reaching for something at the very limit of your reach feels frantic and imprecise - the joint-angle cost of the last centimetre blows up. Robots feel it too, as enormous commanded joint velocities near a singularity.

No solution outside the annulus: a target beyond $\ell_1+\ell_2$ (too far) or inside $|\ell_1-\ell_2|$ (too close to the shoulder to fold into) has $|\cos\theta_2|\gt 1$ and no arm solution at all. The body's response is to stop being a two-link arm - it leans the trunk, shrugs the shoulder girdle, or takes a step, recruiting extra degrees of freedom. That escalation is the whole-body-coordination story of Module 12; here it marks the honest boundary of the two-link idealisation.

4. Statics: the force-torque duality $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$

So far the arm has only moved. Now hold it still and make it push: press the hand against a table, or simply hold a cup out against gravity. The hand exerts a force on the world; to keep the arm in equilibrium the shoulder and elbow muscles must supply matching torques. The relationship between the endpoint force and the joint torques is governed - remarkably - by the transpose of the very same Jacobian that governed velocity. This is the force-torque duality, and it is proved cleanly by the principle of virtual work.

Proposition 4.1 (force-torque duality). If the hand applies a force $\mathbf F=(F_x,F_y)$ to the environment (equivalently, the environment applies $-\mathbf F$ to the hand), the joint torques needed to hold the arm in static equilibrium are $$\boxed{\;\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F,\qquad \begin{bmatrix}\tau_1\\ \tau_2\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}-\ell_1 s_1-\ell_2 s_{12} & \ell_1 c_1+\ell_2 c_{12}\\ -\ell_2 s_{12} & \ell_2 c_{12}\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}F_x\\ F_y\end{bmatrix}.\;}$$
Consider a virtual joint displacement $\delta\mathbf q$, infinitesimal and consistent with the constraints. The endpoint moves by $\delta\mathbf p=J\,\delta\mathbf q$ (the differential form of the forward kinematics). The work done by the joint torques is $\delta U_{\rm joint}=\boldsymbol\tau^{\mathsf T}\delta\mathbf q$; the work done by the hand against the endpoint force is $\delta U_{\rm end}=\mathbf F^{\mathsf T}\delta\mathbf p=\mathbf F^{\mathsf T}J\,\delta\mathbf q$ (we write $\delta U$ for virtual work to keep $W$ for weight). In static equilibrium the net virtual work vanishes for every admissible $\delta\mathbf q$: $\boldsymbol\tau^{\mathsf T}\delta\mathbf q=\mathbf F^{\mathsf T}J\,\delta\mathbf q$ for all $\delta\mathbf q$, hence $\boldsymbol\tau^{\mathsf T}=\mathbf F^{\mathsf T}J$, i.e. $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$. Substituting $J$ from Proposition 2.1 and transposing gives the component form. ∎

The duality is not a coincidence of notation; it is the same geometry seen from two sides. Velocities push forward through $J$ (joint rates $\to$ hand velocity); forces pull back through $J^{\mathsf T}$ (hand force $\to$ joint torque). One immediate consequence: the force ellipsoid - the set of endpoint forces reachable with bounded joint torque - shares the axis directions of the velocity (manipulability) ellipsoid of Section 2 but with reciprocal lengths, so its long axis lies along that ellipsoid's short one (the major-axis direction turned ninety degrees). The direction in which the hand moves most easily is the direction in which it pushes most feebly, and vice versa. This is why you brace a heavy push along a nearly straight arm (near-singular, terrible for velocity, superb for force - the load runs down the bones, not through the muscles) and why fine, fast hand movements happen with a bent elbow.

Put numbers on the everyday case: holding a cup of mass $m=0.4\ \mathrm{kg}$ (a full mug) at the hand, arm horizontal and straight ahead, $\theta_1=0,\theta_2=0$. The endpoint force the hand must supply is $\mathbf F=(0,+mg)$ upward, $mg=0.4\times9.81\approx3.9\ \mathrm{N}$. With $\theta_1=\theta_2=0$ we have $s_1=s_{12}=0$, $c_1=c_{12}=1$, so $J^{\mathsf T}=\begin{bmatrix}0&\ell_1+\ell_2\\0&\ell_2\end{bmatrix}$ and $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}(0,mg)=\big((\ell_1+\ell_2)mg,\ \ell_2\,mg\big)$. The shoulder torque is $(\ell_1+\ell_2)mg=0.75\times3.9\approx2.9\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ and the elbow torque $\ell_2\,mg=0.45\times3.9\approx1.8\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ - the shoulder carries more because it has the longer moment arm to the load. Add the arm's own weight (Module 1's segment masses, restored in the inverse dynamics of Section 6) and these roughly double. This single matrix multiplication is the reason a waiter's tray held close to the body is far less tiring than the same tray held at arm's length: $J^{\mathsf T}$ scales the required torque with the moment arm baked into the geometry.

The transpose also delivers endpoint stiffness. If the joints behave like torsional springs with stiffness matrix $K_q$ (from muscle co-contraction, the impedance theme of Module 12), a small endpoint displacement $\delta\mathbf p$ produces a restoring endpoint force $\delta\mathbf F=K_x\,\delta\mathbf p$ with $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_q J^{-1}$. The hand thus presents a programmable stiffness to the world - stiff along the line you are pushing, compliant across it - which is how you keep a drill bit on target while letting it wander harmlessly sideways. We return to it in Section 9 and Module 12.

5. Redundancy and the null space

Touch your fingertip to a fixed spot on the desk and, without lifting the finger, raise and lower your elbow. The hand stays put while the arm rearranges itself behind it. That everyday freedom - move the joints without moving the hand - is kinematic redundancy, and it appears the moment the arm has more joints than the task has dimensions. A planar reach fixes two numbers (the hand's $x$ and $y$); the real arm brings far more than two joints to bear (shoulder, elbow, wrist, and the shoulder girdle behind them). The surplus is not waste. It is the reservoir the nervous system draws on to keep away from joint limits, dodge obstacles, and distribute effort - and the mathematics that describes it is the null space of the Jacobian.

Add the hand as a third link, length $\ell_3$, rotating at the wrist by $\theta_3$, so $\mathbf q=(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$. The endpoint (fingertip) position is still two numbers, so the Jacobian $J=\partial\mathbf p/\partial\mathbf q$ is now $2\times3$: two rows (the task), three columns (the joints). The velocity relation $\dot{\mathbf p}=J\dot{\mathbf q}$ is a system of two equations in three unknowns - underdetermined. It has a whole line of solutions, and the structure of that solution set is the point.

Definition 3 (null space and self-motion). The null space of $J$ is $\mathcal N(J)=\{\dot{\mathbf q}:J\dot{\mathbf q}=\mathbf 0\}$ - the joint velocities that produce no endpoint velocity. A motion $\dot{\mathbf q}\in\mathcal N(J)$ is a self-motion (or internal motion): the joints move, the hand does not. For a $2\times3$ Jacobian of full rank, $\mathcal N(J)$ is one-dimensional - a single degree of internal freedom, the elbow's swing with the fingertip pinned.
Proposition 5.1 (general redundancy resolution). Let $J$ be $2\times3$ of full row rank and let $J^{+}=J^{\mathsf T}(JJ^{\mathsf T})^{-1}$ be its Moore-Penrose right pseudoinverse (so $JJ^{+}=I_2$). Every joint velocity producing the desired endpoint velocity $\dot{\mathbf p}$ has the form $$\boxed{\;\dot{\mathbf q}=J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}+\big(I-J^{+}J\big)\mathbf z,\;}$$ where $\mathbf z\in\mathbb R^{3}$ is arbitrary. The first term is the unique minimum-norm solution (smallest $\|\dot{\mathbf q}\|$); the second lies in $\mathcal N(J)$ and is the self-motion, tuned through $\mathbf z$ to serve a secondary goal without disturbing the hand.
First, $J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}$ solves the task: $J(J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p})=(JJ^{+})\dot{\mathbf p}=\dot{\mathbf p}$. Second, $(I-J^{+}J)\mathbf z$ is a null-space vector: $J(I-J^{+}J)\mathbf z=(J-JJ^{+}J)\mathbf z=(J-J)\mathbf z=\mathbf 0$ since $JJ^{+}J=J$. So any $\dot{\mathbf q}$ of the boxed form satisfies $J\dot{\mathbf q}=\dot{\mathbf p}$. Conversely, if $J\dot{\mathbf q}=\dot{\mathbf p}$, write $\dot{\mathbf q}=J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}+(\dot{\mathbf q}-J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p})$; the remainder $\mathbf r=\dot{\mathbf q}-J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}$ satisfies $J\mathbf r=\dot{\mathbf p}-\dot{\mathbf p}=\mathbf 0$, so $\mathbf r\in\mathcal N(J)$, and every null-space vector is expressible as $(I-J^{+}J)\mathbf z$ because $I-J^{+}J$ is the orthogonal projector onto $\mathcal N(J)$. Minimum norm: the two terms are orthogonal ($J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}\in\mathcal R(J^{\mathsf T})\perp\mathcal N(J)$), so $\|\dot{\mathbf q}\|^2=\|J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}\|^2+\|(I-J^{+}J)\mathbf z\|^2\ge\|J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}\|^2$, with equality iff the null-space part is zero. ∎

Proposition 5.1 is the skeleton of every redundancy-resolution scheme. Choosing $\mathbf z$ is choosing how to be redundant. Set $\mathbf z=\mathbf 0$ and the arm takes the laziest instantaneous move (minimum joint speed). Set $\mathbf z=-\nabla H(\mathbf q)$ for a cost $H$ - distance-into-a-joint-limit, proximity to an obstacle, metabolic effort - and the self-motion continuously slides the elbow toward comfort while the hand tracks its target undisturbed. This is exactly what your arm does when you thread a needle with your elbow drifting to a natural angle: the fingertip is servoed, the elbow is free, and the freedom is spent on a secondary objective.

But Proposition 5.1 also exposes the deep problem it cannot solve. The pseudoinverse picks minimum joint velocity; a different secondary cost picks a different solution; and there is no purely kinematic reason to prefer one over another - the task does not determine the trajectory. This underdetermination, multiplied across the whole body's hundreds of degrees of freedom, is Bernstein's degrees-of-freedom problem, the founding question of motor control. Module 12 answers it not with a pseudoinverse but with optimal control: the nervous system resolves redundancy by minimising a cost over the whole movement (jerk, effort, end-point variance), and the null space becomes the space it optimises within. Here we simply mark that the freedom exists and is structured; there the freedom gets a principle.

6. Inverse dynamics: the torques behind a wave

Statics (Section 4) held the arm still. Waving to a friend does not: the forearm accelerates back and forth, and accelerating mass takes torque beyond what gravity alone demands. To find the shoulder and elbow torques behind a real movement we need the arm's equations of motion - and running them backward, from a prescribed motion to the torques that caused it, is inverse dynamics, the same operation Module 8 used to read joint torques off a measured gait.

Restore the mass the kinematic sections ignored. Link $i$ (upper arm $i=1$, forearm-plus-hand $i=2$) has mass $m_i$, length $\ell_i$, centre of mass a distance $\ell_{ci}$ from its proximal joint, and moment of inertia $I_i$ about its own centre of mass. The state is $\mathbf q=(\theta_1,\theta_2)$ with velocity $\dot{\mathbf q}$ and acceleration $\ddot{\mathbf q}$. The equations follow from the Lagrangian $L=T-V$, kinetic minus potential energy, exactly as the constrained dynamics of Module 3.

Proposition 6.1 (two-link arm dynamics). The planar two-link arm in a vertical gravity field obeys $$\boxed{\;M(\mathbf q)\,\ddot{\mathbf q}+C(\mathbf q,\dot{\mathbf q})\,\dot{\mathbf q}+\mathbf g(\mathbf q)=\boldsymbol\tau,\;}$$ with symmetric mass matrix, Coriolis/centrifugal matrix, and gravity vector $$M=\begin{bmatrix}I_1+I_2+m_1\ell_{c1}^2+m_2(\ell_1^2+\ell_{c2}^2+2\ell_1\ell_{c2}c_2) & I_2+m_2(\ell_{c2}^2+\ell_1\ell_{c2}c_2)\\ I_2+m_2(\ell_{c2}^2+\ell_1\ell_{c2}c_2) & I_2+m_2\ell_{c2}^2\end{bmatrix},$$ $$C=\begin{bmatrix}-h\,\dot\theta_2 & -h(\dot\theta_1+\dot\theta_2)\\ h\,\dot\theta_1 & 0\end{bmatrix},\quad h=m_2\ell_1\ell_{c2}s_2,\quad \mathbf g=\begin{bmatrix}(m_1\ell_{c1}+m_2\ell_1)g\,c_1+m_2\ell_{c2}g\,c_{12}\\ m_2\ell_{c2}g\,c_{12}\end{bmatrix},$$ where $c_2=\cos\theta_2$, $s_2=\sin\theta_2$, $c_1=\cos\theta_1$, $c_{12}=\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)$.
The elbow is at $(\ell_1 c_1,\ell_1 s_1)$; link 1's centre of mass at $\ell_{c1}(c_1,s_1)$, link 2's at $(\ell_1 c_1+\ell_{c2}c_{12},\ \ell_1 s_1+\ell_{c2}s_{12})$. Differentiating, link 1's centre-of-mass speed satisfies $v_1^2=\ell_{c1}^2\dot\theta_1^2$; link 2's is $v_2^2=\ell_1^2\dot\theta_1^2+\ell_{c2}^2(\dot\theta_1+\dot\theta_2)^2+2\ell_1\ell_{c2}c_2\,\dot\theta_1(\dot\theta_1+\dot\theta_2)$. The kinetic energy is $T=\tfrac12 m_1 v_1^2+\tfrac12 I_1\dot\theta_1^2+\tfrac12 m_2 v_2^2+\tfrac12 I_2(\dot\theta_1+\dot\theta_2)^2$; collecting terms as $T=\tfrac12\dot{\mathbf q}^{\mathsf T}M\dot{\mathbf q}$ reads off the stated $M$. The potential energy is $V=m_1 g\,\ell_{c1}s_1+m_2 g(\ell_1 s_1+\ell_{c2}s_{12})$, and $\mathbf g=\partial V/\partial\mathbf q$ gives the stated gravity vector. The Coriolis matrix follows from the Christoffel symbols $C_{kj}=\sum_i\tfrac12(\partial M_{kj}/\partial q_i+\partial M_{ki}/\partial q_j-\partial M_{ij}/\partial q_k)\dot q_i$. The only configuration dependence in $M$ is through $c_2$, giving $\partial M_{11}/\partial\theta_2=-2m_2\ell_1\ell_{c2}s_2=-2h$, $\partial M_{12}/\partial\theta_2=-h$, $\partial M_{22}/\partial\theta_2=0$ and every $\partial M_{kj}/\partial\theta_1=0$, where $h=m_2\ell_1\ell_{c2}s_2$. Evaluating one entry explicitly, only the $i=2$ term survives in $C_{11}$ and $C_{11}=\tfrac12(\partial M_{11}/\partial\theta_2)\dot\theta_2=\tfrac12(-2h)\dot\theta_2=-h\dot\theta_2$; the remaining sums evaluate the same way to $C_{12}=-h(\dot\theta_1+\dot\theta_2)$, $C_{21}=h\dot\theta_1$, $C_{22}=0$, the matrix as written. Substituting into the Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{\mathbf q}}-\frac{\partial L}{\partial\mathbf q}=\boldsymbol\tau$ gives the boxed form. ∎

Three physical readings make the algebra speak. The mass matrix $M(\mathbf q)$ says the arm's effective inertia depends on its posture - a bent arm ($c_2$ small or negative) is "lighter" to swing at the shoulder than a straight one, because the forearm's mass is tucked closer to the shoulder axis. The off-diagonal $M_{12}$ is dynamic coupling: accelerating the elbow alone throws a reaction torque back onto the shoulder, which is why a sharp forearm flick makes the whole arm want to counter-rotate. The Coriolis term $h$ collects the velocity-dependent forces that a rotating, reconfiguring linkage feels - zero when the arm is straight ($s_2=0$) and largest at a right-angle elbow.

Now wave. Idealise a wave as the shoulder roughly fixed ($\theta_1$ nearly constant, holding the upper arm up) while the elbow oscillates, $\theta_2(t)=\theta_2^0+A\sin(2\pi f t)$, at frequency $f\approx1.5\ \mathrm{Hz}$ and amplitude $A\approx0.5\ \mathrm{rad}$. Inverse dynamics substitutes this prescribed $\theta_2(t)$ (and its derivatives) into Proposition 6.1 and reads off $\boldsymbol\tau(t)$. Two forces share the elbow torque: a gravitational part, $g_2=m_2\ell_{c2}g\,c_{12}$, that rises and falls as the forearm swings through the horizontal, and an inertial part, $M_{22}\ddot\theta_2=-M_{22}A(2\pi f)^2\sin(2\pi f t)$, that grows with the square of the wave frequency. At a leisurely wave the gravity term dominates; wave twice as fast and the inertial term quadruples, quickly becoming the larger - which is why a fast, vigorous wave tires the arm out of proportion to its amplitude. The lab in Section 10 computes the full $\boldsymbol\tau(t)$ and plots the split; Fig. 7 previews it.

7. The shoulder: mobility bought with stability

The two-link model treated the shoulder as an ideal revolute joint - a clean pivot at the origin. The real shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body, and it pays for that mobility in a currency the model hides: stability. Understanding the trade is the difference between "the shoulder is a ball-and-socket" (Module 3's classification) and knowing why the shoulder, almost alone among joints, dislocates.

The glenohumeral joint is the ball-and-socket between the head of the humerus (the ball) and the glenoid, a shallow dish on the scapula (shoulder blade). Anatomically the fit is nothing like a hip: the glenoid is a saucer that cradles only about a third of the humeral head, so the bones supply almost no passive containment. Three things make up the deficit. The labrum, a fibrocartilage rim, deepens the socket by roughly half. The capsule and glenohumeral ligaments tighten at the extremes of motion (the passive constraints of Module 6). And - the active workhorse - the four rotator cuff muscles wrap the head and pull it into the socket, a mechanism called concavity-compression: press the ball into the dish and friction-free geometry alone resists a sideways dislocating force up to a limit set by how steep the socket walls are.

Proposition 7.1 (concavity-compression stability ratio). Model the seated humeral head as a ball pressed into a spherical socket of effective half-arc $\alpha$ (the angle from the socket's deepest point to its rim) by a compressive force $F_c$ directed into the socket. A tangential (dislocating) force $F_t$ fails to unseat the head if and only if $$\boxed{\;\frac{F_t}{F_c}\le\tan\alpha.\;}$$ The right-hand side is the stability ratio; a deeper socket (larger $\alpha$) resists a larger fraction of dislocating load, and the rotator cuff raises the tolerable $F_t$ by raising $F_c$.
Resolve forces on the head at the point of incipient dislocation, where the resultant of $F_c$ (into the socket, along the socket axis) and $F_t$ (tangential, toward the rim) must point no farther out than the rim to keep the contact reaction pushing the head back in. The resultant makes angle $\beta=\arctan(F_t/F_c)$ with the socket axis. The head remains seated as long as this resultant is contained within the socket, i.e. $\beta\le\alpha$, the rim half-arc; equivalently $\arctan(F_t/F_c)\le\alpha$, i.e. $F_t/F_c\le\tan\alpha$. Once $\beta\gt \alpha$ the resultant clears the rim, the reaction can no longer redirect the head inward, and it rides up and out. ∎

The numbers explain the clinical fact. A healthy glenoid-plus-labrum gives an effective half-arc of order $\alpha\approx25^\circ$, so the stability ratio is $\tan25^\circ\approx0.47$: the seated head tolerates a dislocating force up to about half the compressive force the cuff supplies. Tear the labrum and $\alpha$ drops, the ratio with it, and a shove that a healthy shoulder shrugs off now levers the head over the rim - recurrent instability, the commonest sequel to a first dislocation. Fatigue the cuff (so $F_c$ falls) and the tolerable $F_t$ falls in proportion. The shoulder, in short, is a joint that trades bony stability for range of motion and buys the stability back actively, muscle by muscle - which is exactly why shoulder rehabilitation is cuff strengthening, and why a tired or injured shoulder is an unstable one.

One more structural fact earns the arm its reach: the shoulder is not a single joint but a linkage. Raising the arm overhead combines glenohumeral rotation with rotation of the whole scapula on the ribcage, in a roughly $2\!:\!1$ ratio (about two degrees at the glenohumeral joint for every one of scapular rotation) - the scapulohumeral rhythm. This is a redundant linkage in the sense of Section 5: two joints cooperating to orient one segment, distributing the range so neither is driven to its painful extreme. It is the small-scale prototype of the whole-body coordination Module 12 takes up.

8. Grasp mechanics and the friction cone

The arm has delivered the hand to the cup; now the hand must hold it. Holding is a contact-mechanics problem, and its governing object is the friction cone. A fingertip pressed on the cup's surface can push (a normal force $N$, into the surface) and can drag along the surface (a tangential, friction force $F_t$), but Coulomb's law caps the drag: $|F_t|\le\mu N$, where $\mu$ is the coefficient of friction between skin and cup. Geometrically, the total contact force - normal plus tangential - must lie within a cone about the surface normal.

Definition 4 (friction cone). At a contact with friction coefficient $\mu$ and outward surface normal $\hat{\mathbf n}$, the set of forces the contact can transmit without slipping is the friction cone: all forces $\mathbf f$ that push into the surface and make an angle no greater than $\varphi=\arctan\mu$ with the inward normal. A contact force on the cone's surface is at the verge of slipping; a force outside it cannot be sustained - the finger slides. $\varphi$ is the friction angle.

The first question is quantitative: how hard must the fingers squeeze to keep the cup from sliding out of the grip? Take the simplest human grasp, the precision pinch - thumb pad on one side, index finger pad on the other, pressing toward each other with equal and opposite grip force $F_g$ (the normal force at each pad). Gravity pulls the cup down with weight $W=mg$; the only thing holding it up is friction at the two pads.

Proposition 8.1 (minimum grip force). A cup of weight $W$ held in a two-pad pinch, each pad pressing with normal force $F_g$ against vertical faces with friction coefficient $\mu$, does not slip if and only if $$\boxed{\;F_g\ \ge\ \frac{W}{2\mu}.\;}$$ Halving the friction (a wet or greasy cup) doubles the grip force needed; the safety margin humans keep above this floor is the subject of Section 9.
The cup is in vertical equilibrium: the total upward friction must equal the weight, $F_{t,1}+F_{t,2}=W$. By symmetry each pad carries half, $F_{t}=W/2$. Each pad's friction is capped by Coulomb's law, $F_{t}\le\mu F_g$. No slip requires $W/2\le\mu F_g$, i.e. $F_g\ge W/(2\mu)$. Conversely, if $F_g\ge W/(2\mu)$ then friction up to $\mu F_g\ge W/2$ is available at each pad, enough to supply the required $W/2$, so equilibrium holds and the grip is stable. ∎

Numbers: a full glass, $m=0.5\ \mathrm{kg}$, $W\approx4.9\ \mathrm{N}$. Dry skin on glass has $\mu\approx0.5$, so $F_g\ge 4.9/(2\times0.5)\approx4.9\ \mathrm{N}$ per pad - a light pinch. Wet the glass and $\mu$ falls toward $0.2$; now $F_g\ge 4.9/(2\times0.2)\approx12\ \mathrm{N}$, two and a half times harder - the lived experience of a soapy glass squirting out of your hand. This inverse-in-$\mu$ scaling is why we instinctively grip low-friction objects harder, and why sweat, soap, and smooth surfaces are the enemies of a secure hold.

The pinch works because the two contacts oppose - each friction cone reaches toward the other. This is the geometric heart of a secure grasp, made precise as force closure: a grasp is force-closed if its contacts can resist an arbitrary external push or twist, not merely gravity. For two frictional fingertips the condition is a clean statement about the cones.

Proposition 8.2 (force closure of a two-finger pinch). A planar grasp by two hard fingertips with friction angle $\varphi=\arctan\mu$ is force-closed if and only if the line segment joining the two contact points lies inside both friction cones - that is, each contact can direct a force at the other. Equivalently, letting $\gamma$ be the angle between the contact normal and the line to the other contact at each finger, closure requires $\gamma\lt \varphi$ at both.
Squeezing generates an internal force along the line joining the contacts: finger 1 pushes toward finger 2 and vice versa, and these balance, loading the object without moving it. This internal force is realisable only if each contact force - directed along the joining line - lies within that contact's friction cone, i.e. $\gamma\lt \varphi$ at both. Grant that. Then the two cones overlap in orientation, and the pair of contacts can generate contact forces spanning a full $2$-D set of directions: to resist an arbitrary external wrench (a planar force $\mathbf F_{\rm ext}$ together with a torque $\tau_{\rm ext}$) the contacts must generate contact wrenches that positively span $\mathbb R^3=\{(f_x,f_y,\tau)\}$. Each frictional point contact contributes the cone of wrenches spanned by its two friction-cone edges applied at the contact point, so the two fingers together give four edge-wrenches. When $\gamma\lt\varphi$ at both, the two force cones open toward each other and their edges cover every planar force direction, while the two contact points lie on opposite sides of any line through the object, so equal squeezes produce opposite-signed torques; the four edge-wrenches therefore strictly surround the origin of $\mathbb R^3$, i.e. the origin lies in the interior of their positive span. Positive span of $\mathbb R^3$ is exactly force closure: every external wrench $-(\mathbf F_{\rm ext},\tau_{\rm ext})$ is a nonnegative combination of the edge-wrenches, hence balanced by admissible contact forces. If instead $\gamma\ge\varphi$ at either finger, the joining-line direction is outside that friction cone: the squeeze itself would slip, no pre-load can be maintained, and an external force along the escaping direction cannot be resisted - closure fails. ∎

Proposition 8.2 is why the thumb is the keystone of the human hand. Opposition - the thumb's ability to rotate at its saddle joint (the trapeziometacarpal joint, a saddle joint in Module 3's taxonomy) to face the finger pads - is precisely what places the thumb's friction cone pointing back at the fingers, satisfying $\gamma\lt \varphi$ and closing the grasp. Lose thumb opposition (a median-nerve injury, or simply taping the thumb flat) and the hand can still touch objects but can no longer securely pinch them: the cones no longer oppose, and force closure is gone. The power grip - fingers wrapped around a handle into the palm - achieves closure differently, by wrapping the contacts more than halfway around so the object is caged by form as much as friction, trading the precision pinch's dexterity for a hold that survives low friction and large loads. Precision and power are the two ends of a continuum, and both are readings of the friction-cone geometry.

9. Tactile sensing and the grip-load loop

Proposition 8.1 gives a floor, $F_g\ge W/(2\mu)$, but leaves a question it cannot answer: how does the hand know $\mu$? You cannot see friction. Grip a chilled bottle and a dry mug with the same visual information and you will, correctly, squeeze the slippery one harder - because the hand measures friction by feel, at the contact, in real time. The tactile system closes the grip loop, and it is the same noisy, delayed sensorimotor control loop of Module 10, now at the fingertip.

Humans do not grip at the minimum. They set grip force a fraction above the slip boundary - a safety margin - so a small unexpected drop in friction or jolt in load does not cause an immediate slip.

Definition 5 (grip-force safety margin). With a tangential load $L$ (the force tending to slide the object from the grasp - the weight when holding still, or weight plus inertial load when lifting or moving) the slip boundary for the opposing two-pad pinch is $F_g^{\min}=L/(2\mu)$ (each of the two pads must supply half the load $L$, and its friction is capped at $\mu F_g$); humans hold $F_g=(1+s)\,F_g^{\min}$, where the dimensionless safety margin $s$ is typically $0.1$ to $0.4$. A larger $s$ wastes muscular effort and fatigues the hand; a smaller $s$ risks dropping the object.

Two mechanisms set and defend the margin, and locating each one in the tissue is the biology this section adds. First, feedforward (anticipatory) control: before and during a lift, the brain runs an internal model of the object - learned from its felt weight, remembered from the last lift, cued by its look - and drives grip force in parallel with the anticipated load. This is why grip and load force rise together in a tight, near-synchronous coupling when you lift a familiar object (Fig. 9): the grip is not reacting to the load, it is predicting it. Get the prediction wrong - a milk carton you thought was full but is empty - and the hand overshoots, the arm flies up, before feedback corrects: direct evidence that the first grip command is feedforward.

Second, feedback correction from tactile afferents. Fast-adapting mechanoreceptors in the fingertip skin - Meissner corpuscles (FA-I) and Pacinian corpuscles (FA-II) - are exquisitely sensitive to the tiny, localised micro-slips that precede gross sliding: a patch of skin at the contact edge slips first, and its vibration is detected. The signal triggers an automatic grip-force increase at a latency of roughly $70\ \mathrm{ms}$ - faster than a voluntary reaction, a genuine reflex - which restores the margin. The controller thus keeps grip force just above slip by probing the boundary: it lets the margin erode until a micro-slip fires, then bumps the force up. The felt coefficient $\mu$ is estimated from how much force it took to stop slipping.

The clinical reading is immediate and matches Module 10's theme that a control loop is only as good as its sensors. Anaesthetise the fingertips (a dental-style ring block, or the numbness of neuropathy) and the slip reflex is gone: subjects either drop objects or, compensating, grip them with a hugely inflated margin - $s$ balloons - crushing eggs and fatiguing fast, because without feedback the only safe policy is to over-squeeze. Aging thins the afferent population and slows the reflex, quietly raising the effort of every grip. The margin $s$ is, in the end, a purchased insurance against sensor noise and prediction error - the hand's local version of the stability-margin budget the whole body keeps against a push (Section 0; Module 10).

Lab A - reaching: inverse kinematics along a straight hand path

Physical question. To carry the hand in a straight line from lap to cup, what must the shoulder and elbow do - and how do the two elbow assemblies differ? Model. The two-link arm of Section 1; a straight-line endpoint path sampled in time; inverse kinematics (Proposition 3.1) at each sample. Parameters. $\ell_1=0.30\ \mathrm{m}$, $\ell_2=0.45\ \mathrm{m}$; start $(0.10,-0.30)\ \mathrm{m}$, goal $(0.45,0.10)\ \mathrm{m}$. Equations. $\cos\theta_2=(r^2-\ell_1^2-\ell_2^2)/(2\ell_1\ell_2)$, then $\theta_1$ from the bearing minus the internal angle.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

l1, l2 = 0.30, 0.45              # upper arm, forearm+hand (m)


def ik(x, y, elbow=+1):
    """Inverse kinematics; elbow=+1 up, -1 down. Returns (th1, th2)."""
    c2 = (x*x + y*y - l1**2 - l2**2)/(2*l1*l2)
    th2 = elbow*np.arccos(np.clip(c2, -1.0, 1.0))
    th1 = np.arctan2(y, x) - np.arctan2(l2*np.sin(th2),
                                        l1 + l2*np.cos(th2))
    return th1, th2


start, goal = np.array([0.10, -0.30]), np.array([0.45, 0.10])
s = np.linspace(0.0, 1.0, 60)
path = (1 - s)[:, None]*start + s[:, None]*goal
down = np.array([ik(px, py, elbow=-1) for px, py in path])
up = np.array([ik(px, py, elbow=+1) for px, py in path])

fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 4))
ax[0].plot(path[:, 0], path[:, 1], 'k--', label='hand path')
ax[1].plot(s, np.degrees(down[:, 0]), label='shoulder th1')
ax[1].plot(s, np.degrees(down[:, 1]), label='elbow th2')
for a in ax:
    a.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Interpretation. The hand tracks a straight line, but the joint angles trace curved, non-uniform trajectories - the nonlinearity of the forward map made visible. The shoulder rotates through about $63^\circ$ and the elbow through about $37^\circ$ over the reach; neither moves at constant rate even though the hand does, because $J$ changes with posture. Sensitivity. Move the goal outward toward $\ell_1+\ell_2=0.75\ \mathrm{m}$ and the elbow-angle curve steepens near the end - the boundary singularity of Section 3 - so the last centimetres demand disproportionate joint motion. Failure modes. A goal outside the annulus makes $|\cos\theta_2|\gt 1$; the clip keeps the code from crashing but the returned pose no longer reaches the target - the honest signal that the arm alone cannot get there. Extension. Replace the constant-speed path with a minimum-jerk time profile (Module 12) and re-plot the joint rates; the hand path is unchanged but the joint velocities become bell-shaped.

Lab B - waving: inverse dynamics of an oscillating forearm

Physical question. What shoulder and elbow torques drive a wave, and how do the gravitational and inertial shares change with wave speed? Model. The two-link dynamics of Proposition 6.1; shoulder held at $\theta_1=100^\circ$, elbow oscillating $\theta_2(t)=\theta_2^0+A\sin(2\pi f t)$. Parameters. $A=0.5\ \mathrm{rad}$, $\theta_2^0=-1.0\ \mathrm{rad}$, $f=1.5\ \mathrm{Hz}$; masses and inertias from the Appendix. Equations. $\boldsymbol\tau=M\ddot{\mathbf q}+C\dot{\mathbf q}+\mathbf g$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

g = 9.81
m1, m2 = 2.1, 1.65               # upper arm, forearm+hand (kg)
l1 = 0.30
lc1, lc2 = 0.15, 0.22            # com distances (m)
I1, I2 = 0.016, 0.025            # com inertias (kg m^2)


def tau(th1, th2, dth2, ddth2):
    """Elbow torque tau2 for fixed shoulder (dth1=ddth1=0)."""
    c2, s2 = np.cos(th2), np.sin(th2)
    c12 = np.cos(th1 + th2)
    M22 = I2 + m2*lc2**2
    h = m2*l1*lc2*s2
    grav = m2*lc2*g*c12
    cor = h*0.0                  # dth1=0 kills the elbow Coriolis term
    return M22*ddth2 + cor + grav, M22*ddth2, grav


t = np.linspace(0, 2.0, 400)
f, A, th20, th1 = 1.5, 0.5, -1.0, np.radians(100)
w = 2*np.pi*f
th2 = th20 + A*np.sin(w*t)
dth2 = A*w*np.cos(w*t)
ddth2 = -A*w*w*np.sin(w*t)
tot, inert, grav = np.vectorize(tau)(th1, th2, dth2, ddth2)

plt.plot(t, tot, label='total elbow torque')
plt.plot(t, inert, label='inertial part')
plt.plot(t, grav, label='gravity part')
plt.xlabel('time (s)')
plt.ylabel('torque (N m)')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Interpretation. At $f=1.5\ \mathrm{Hz}$ the inertial ($\approx4.7\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ peak) and gravitational ($\approx3.5\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ peak) contributions to the elbow torque are comparable, and they beat against each other so the total peaks near $8\ \mathrm{N\,m}$. Sensitivity. Because the inertial term scales as $f^2$ while gravity is unchanged, doubling the wave frequency to $3\ \mathrm{Hz}$ quadruples the inertial part; it comes to dominate ($\approx19\ \mathrm{N\,m}$) and the total torque amplitude roughly triples to $\approx22\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ - the quantitative reason a fast wave tires the arm out of proportion to its amplitude. Failure modes. The idealisation of a perfectly fixed shoulder is false: a real wave leaks torque into $\theta_1$ through the coupling $M_{12}$, so this underestimates shoulder effort. Extension. Release $\theta_1$ and integrate the full coupled dynamics with the shoulder held by a PD controller; watch the reaction torque $M_{12}\ddot\theta_2$ appear at the shoulder.

Lab C - gripping: minimum grip force and the grip-load loop

Physical question. How does the grip force needed to hold a cup depend on friction, and how must grip force be modulated while lifting so the safety margin stays constant? Model. Proposition 8.1 for the static floor; a lift with time-varying load $L(t)=m(g+a(t))$ for the coupling. Parameters. $m=0.5\ \mathrm{kg}$; $\mu$ swept $0.1$-$0.8$; safety margin $s=0.3$; a lift acceleration pulse peaking at $a=4\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$. Equations. $F_g=(1+s)\,L/(2\mu)$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

g, m, s = 9.81, 0.5, 0.30        # gravity, mass, safety margin

mu = np.linspace(0.1, 0.8, 100)
Fg_static = (1 + s)*m*g/(2*mu)   # per pad, with margin

t = np.linspace(0, 1.5, 300)
a = 4.0*np.exp(-((t - 0.6)/0.12)**2)   # lift acceleration pulse
load = m*(g + a)                        # tangential load during lift
Fg_lift = (1 + s)*load/(2*0.6)          # mu = 0.6 during the lift

fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 4))
ax[0].plot(mu, Fg_static)
ax[0].set_xlabel('friction mu')
ax[0].set_ylabel('grip force per pad (N)')

ax[1].plot(t, Fg_lift, label='grip force')
ax[1].legend()

plt.show()

Interpretation. The grip-force floor is a hyperbola in $\mu$: at $\mu=0.6$ it is about $5.3\ \mathrm{N}$ per pad, but as $\mu\to0.1$ it climbs past $32\ \mathrm{N}$ - the numerical face of "slippery things must be gripped hard." In the lift, grip force tracks the load pulse in lock-step, exactly the anticipatory grip-load coupling of Section 9. Sensitivity. The static curve's steepness near small $\mu$ means grip effort is most sensitive to friction precisely where friction is lowest - a dangerous regime, since a small further drop demands a large force increase. Failure modes. If the internal model underestimates the lift acceleration, the grip curve lags the load and the margin momentarily vanishes - a slip. Extension. Add a $70\ \mathrm{ms}$-delayed feedback bump triggered when the instantaneous margin drops below zero, and watch the reflex catch the slip.

Lab D - dexterity: the manipulability and force ellipsoids

Physical question. Where in its workspace is the arm most dexterous, and why is the direction of easy motion the direction of weak force? Model. The Jacobian of Proposition 2.1; the velocity ellipsoid is the image of the unit joint-rate sphere under $J$, the force ellipsoid the image under $J^{-\mathsf T}$. Parameters. $\ell_1=0.30$, $\ell_2=0.45\ \mathrm{m}$; ellipsoids drawn at three elbow angles. Equations. manipulability $w=|\det J|=\ell_1\ell_2|\sin\theta_2|$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

l1, l2 = 0.30, 0.45


def jac(th1, th2):
    s1, s12 = np.sin(th1), np.sin(th1 + th2)
    c1, c12 = np.cos(th1), np.cos(th1 + th2)
    return np.array([[-l1*s1 - l2*s12, -l2*s12],
                     [l1*c1 + l2*c12, l2*c12]])


phi = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 100)
circle = np.array([np.cos(phi), np.sin(phi)])   # unit joint-rate set
for th2 in (0.3, np.pi/2, 2.6):
    J = jac(np.radians(30), th2)
    vel = J @ circle                 # velocity ellipsoid
    frc = np.linalg.inv(J).T @ circle   # force ellipsoid
    w = abs(np.linalg.det(J))
    print('th2=%.2f  manipulability=%.4f' % (th2, w))
    plt.plot(vel[0], vel[1])
    plt.plot(frc[0], frc[1], '--')
plt.axis('equal')
plt.show()

Interpretation. The velocity ellipse is fattest at $\theta_2=\pi/2$ (manipulability $w=\ell_1\ell_2=0.135$) and collapses toward a line as the elbow straightens ($\theta_2\to0$) or folds ($\theta_2\to\pi$) - the singularities. At every posture the force ellipse (dashed) is the velocity ellipse rotated by $90^\circ$: its long axis lies along the velocity ellipse's short axis. Sensitivity. $w=\ell_1\ell_2|\sin\theta_2|$ is stationary at $\theta_2=\pi/2$, so dexterity is robust to small posture changes in mid-range but falls off steeply near the singular postures. Failure modes. At exactly $\theta_2=0$ or $\pi$, $J$ is singular and $J^{-\mathsf T}$ does not exist - the force ellipsoid degenerates to a line of infinite length, the bones taking the load the muscles cannot. Extension. Map $w$ as a heat field over the whole reachable annulus to see the ridge of maximal dexterity that reaching movements prefer to stay on.

Conceptual ↑ contents

C1 - the waiter's tray. A waiter carries a tray on a flat hand. Explain, in terms of $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$, why holding it tucked against the chest is far less tiring than holding it at arm's length, even though the tray's weight is identical. Probes: the force-torque transpose and the moment arm baked into $J$; Section 4.
Solution
The tray applies a downward force $\mathbf F=(0,-mg)$ at the hand regardless of posture. The joint torques needed to hold it are $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$, and the entries of $J^{\mathsf T}$ are the moment arms from each joint to the hand: the shoulder torque is (horizontal distance from shoulder to hand)$\times mg$. Tucked to the chest that horizontal distance is a few centimetres; at arm's length it is $\ell_1+\ell_2\approx0.75\ \mathrm{m}$. The torque - and the muscle force, and the metabolic cost - scales with that distance, so the outstretched tray can demand ten times the shoulder torque of the tucked one. Nothing about the weight changed; the geometry in $J^{\mathsf T}$ did.
C2 - which elbow. Inverse kinematics offers two arm configurations - elbow-up and elbow-down - for almost every reachable point. When you reach for a mug on a table you almost always pick elbow-down. Why is the choice not made by geometry, and what does make it? Probes: multiplicity of IK solutions and the first taste of redundancy; Section 3, Section 5.
Solution
Geometry supplies both solutions with equal validity - both put the hand exactly on the mug - so it cannot break the tie. The tie is broken by criteria outside the position task: the elbow-down assembly keeps the elbow and shoulder joints away from their range-of-motion limits, keeps the forearm clear of the table and the body, and is metabolically cheaper in that posture. This is the discrete, two-choice ancestor of the continuous redundancy problem: when the task under-determines the configuration, the surplus freedom is spent on secondary objectives (comfort, obstacle clearance, effort), exactly the null-space logic of Section 5 made binary.
C3 - bend to place, straighten to brace. A surgeon threading a fine suture works with the elbow well bent; a person shoving a stuck door leans into it with a nearly straight arm. Explain both using the manipulability and force ellipsoids. Probes: the $90^\circ$ duality between the velocity and force ellipsoids; Section 2, Section 4.
Solution
Fine placement needs the hand to move quickly and equally in every direction - a fat, round velocity (manipulability) ellipse - which occurs at a mid-range elbow ($\theta_2\approx\pi/2$), where $w=\ell_1\ell_2|\sin\theta_2|$ is maximal. Bracing needs large force along one line, and the force ellipse is the velocity ellipse rotated $90^\circ$: its long (strong) axis lies along the velocity ellipse's short axis. As the arm straightens toward a singularity the velocity ellipse collapses to a line (almost no free motion) while the force ellipse stretches out along the arm - enormous force capacity down the line of the bones. So you straighten the arm to push hard (force ellipse long) and bend it to move precisely (velocity ellipse round). The same $J$ governs both; the transpose swaps easy-motion for easy-force.
C4 - the soapy glass. Why does a wet or soapy glass demand a much firmer grip than a dry one, and why is the extra effort so steep - a small drop in friction forcing a large jump in grip force? Probes: the friction cone and the $1/\mu$ scaling of grip force; Section 8.
Solution
Friction caps the tangential (holding) force at $\mu F_g$, so to carry a fixed weight the grip force must satisfy $F_g\ge W/(2\mu)$ - inversely proportional to $\mu$. Wetting or soaping drops $\mu$ from about $0.5$ toward $0.1$-$0.2$; because $F_g\propto1/\mu$, halving $\mu$ doubles the required grip, and a fivefold drop quintuples it. The relationship is a hyperbola, steepest where $\mu$ is smallest, so in the slippery regime a further small loss of friction demands a large grip increase - which is exactly the frantic over-squeeze of a bar of soap about to shoot out of your hand.
C5 - the taped thumb. Tape a person's thumb flat against the side of the hand and they can still touch objects but can no longer securely pinch them. Explain using force closure. Probes: force closure and why opposition is essential; Section 8.
Solution
A secure pinch is force-closed: the contacts can resist a push from any direction. Proposition 8.2 says two frictional fingertips close the grasp only if the line joining the contacts lies inside both friction cones - each contact must be able to aim a force at the other. Thumb opposition rotates the thumb pad to face the finger pads, pointing its friction cone back along the joining line and satisfying the condition. Tape the thumb flat and its cone no longer opposes the fingers; the joining line leaves the cones; the squeeze itself would slip; and force closure is lost. The hand can press on an object (transmit a normal force) but cannot hold it against an arbitrary disturbance - it can touch, not grip.
C6 - the numb fingertip. After a dental anaesthetic block of the fingertips, people either drop objects or grip them so hard they crush eggs. Explain both failure modes with one mechanism. Probes: the tactile feedback loop and the safety margin; Section 9.
Solution
Grip force is normally set just above the slip floor $F_g^{\min}=L/\mu$ with a small safety margin, and defended by a fast-adapting tactile reflex that detects micro-slips and bumps the grip up within $\sim70\ \mathrm{ms}$. Anaesthesia removes the slip signal. With no feedback there are only two safe policies, and both are pathological: grip at the remembered floor and risk that any unexpected friction drop or load jolt causes an undetected, uncorrected slip - the object falls; or abandon the floor entirely and grip with a hugely inflated margin so that no plausible disturbance can cause slip - crushing fragile objects and fatiguing fast. The single missing ingredient is the feedback that let the healthy hand hold grip force just above slip.
C7 - why the shoulder, not the hip. The shoulder dislocates far more often than the hip, though both are ball-and-socket joints. Explain in terms of the concavity-compression stability ratio, and say what the shoulder gives up in exchange. Probes: socket depth, the stability ratio, and the mobility-stability trade; Section 7.
Solution
The stability ratio $F_t/F_c\le\tan\alpha$ says a seated head resists a dislocating force up to $\tan\alpha$ times the compressive force, where $\alpha$ is the socket's half-arc. The hip's acetabulum is deep (large $\alpha$), cupping more than half the femoral head, so $\tan\alpha$ is large and the joint is intrinsically stable. The glenoid is a shallow saucer (small $\alpha$) holding about a third of the humeral head, so $\tan\alpha\approx0.5$ and the joint relies on the labrum and the actively-compressing rotator cuff for stability. What the shoulder buys with that shallow socket is range of motion - the hand's enormous workspace. It is a deliberate trade: bony stability sacrificed for mobility, the stability bought back muscle by muscle, which is why a fatigued or labrum-torn shoulder dislocates and why rehabilitation is cuff strengthening.
C8 - the vigorous wave. A brisk, fast wave tires the arm out of all proportion to how far the hand actually travels. Which term in the arm's dynamics is responsible, and how does it scale with wave frequency? Probes: the inertial vs gravitational split and $f^2$ scaling; Section 6.
Solution
The elbow torque has a gravitational part (set by posture, independent of speed) and an inertial part $M_{22}\ddot\theta_2$. For an oscillation $\theta_2=\theta_2^0+A\sin(2\pi ft)$ the angular acceleration amplitude is $A(2\pi f)^2$, so the inertial torque scales as $f^2$. Doubling the wave frequency quadruples the inertial torque while gravity is unchanged; past a crossover near $1.3\ \mathrm{Hz}$ the inertial term dominates. The hand's travel (amplitude $A$) is fixed, but the torque - hence muscle force and metabolic cost - grows with the square of frequency, so a fast wave is disproportionately tiring. This is the same reason athletes' fast limb movements are dominated by inertial, not gravitational, loads.
C9 - the pinned fingertip. Press your fingertip on a fixed spot and you can still raise and lower your elbow. What mathematical object is this freedom, and why does it exist only because the arm has more joints than the task has dimensions? Probes: the null space and self-motion; Section 5.
Solution
The freedom is a self-motion in the null space of the Jacobian: a joint velocity $\dot{\mathbf q}$ with $J\dot{\mathbf q}=\mathbf 0$, moving the joints while the fingertip stays put. It exists because fixing the fingertip's position imposes only two scalar constraints (its $x$ and $y$), while the arm-plus-wrist offers three or more joint degrees of freedom. Two constraints on three unknowns leaves a one-dimensional solution space - the line of null-space motions - so the elbow can swing through a one-parameter family of postures with the fingertip pinned. Remove the surplus (a strictly two-joint arm reaching a 2-D point) and $J$ is square and generically invertible: no null space, no self-motion, the elbow locked once the fingertip is placed.
C10 - the frantic last centimetre. Reaching for something at the very limit of your reach feels frantic and imprecise, as if the arm were fighting itself. Explain using the boundary singularity of the workspace. Probes: the singularity at full extension and the blow-up of joint velocity; Section 3, Section 2.
Solution
At full extension the arm is at a singularity: $\det J=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\theta_2=0$ because $\theta_2=0$. The inverse kinematics has $\theta_2=\arccos(\cdot)$ with the argument at its endpoint $+1$, where $\arccos$ has an infinite slope - so a small further outward step in hand position demands an unbounded change in joint angle, and to cover it in fixed time requires very large joint velocities. The hand also loses a degree of freedom: near the singularity it can move freely only tangentially (in an arc), not radially (outward), so any radial error is expensive to correct. Large commanded joint speeds plus lost radial control is exactly the frantic, imprecise feeling - and robots exhibit the identical pathology, spiking joint velocities near a singularity.

Derivational ↑ contents

D1 - forward kinematics and the workspace. Derive the endpoint position of the two-link arm and show the reachable set is the annulus $|\ell_1-\ell_2|\le\|\mathbf p\|\le\ell_1+\ell_2$. Probes: vector addition along the chain and the law of cosines; Section 1.
Solution
The upper arm reaches the elbow at $\ell_1(\cos\theta_1,\sin\theta_1)$; the forearm adds $\ell_2(\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2),\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2))$, giving $x=\ell_1c_1+\ell_2c_{12}$, $y=\ell_1s_1+\ell_2s_{12}$. Then $\|\mathbf p\|^2=x^2+y^2=\ell_1^2+\ell_2^2+2\ell_1\ell_2(c_1c_{12}+s_1s_{12})=\ell_1^2+\ell_2^2+2\ell_1\ell_2\cos\theta_2$ using $\cos(A-B)=\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B$ with $A-B=(\theta_1+\theta_2)-\theta_1=\theta_2$. As $\cos\theta_2$ runs over $[-1,1]$, $\|\mathbf p\|^2$ runs over $[(\ell_1-\ell_2)^2,(\ell_1+\ell_2)^2]$, so $\|\mathbf p\|\in[\,|\ell_1-\ell_2|,\ell_1+\ell_2\,]$: a full annulus, swept as $\theta_1$ turns. (Full derivation: Proposition 1.1.)
D2 - the Jacobian and its determinant. Differentiate the forward kinematics to obtain $J$, and show $\det J=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\theta_2$. Interpret the vanishing of the determinant. Probes: the chain rule and the geometric meaning of singularity; Section 2.
Solution
Partial-differentiate $x,y$ with respect to $\theta_1,\theta_2$ (Proposition 2.1) to get $J=\begin{bmatrix}-\ell_1s_1-\ell_2s_{12}&-\ell_2s_{12}\\ \ell_1c_1+\ell_2c_{12}&\ell_2c_{12}\end{bmatrix}$. Expanding the determinant, the $\ell_2^2$ terms cancel and the cross terms give $\ell_1\ell_2(s_{12}c_1-s_1c_{12})=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\theta_2$. It vanishes at $\theta_2=0$ (straight arm) and $\theta_2=\pi$ (folded): there the two columns of $J$ become parallel, $J$ drops rank, and the hand loses a direction of instantaneous motion - a singularity. Physically the lost direction at full extension is radial: you cannot lengthen your reach by turning the joints.
D3 - inverse kinematics. Given a target $(x,y)$ inside the workspace, derive the two joint-angle solutions and show they coincide on the workspace boundary. Probes: inverting the forward map, the two-to-one structure; Section 3.
Solution
From D1, $\cos\theta_2=(r^2-\ell_1^2-\ell_2^2)/(2\ell_1\ell_2)$ with $r^2=x^2+y^2$, so $\theta_2=\pm\arccos(\cdot)$: two solutions, elbow-up and elbow-down. For $\theta_1$, the target bearing is $\operatorname{atan2}(y,x)$ and the endpoint lies at internal angle $\operatorname{atan2}(\ell_2\sin\theta_2,\ell_1+\ell_2\cos\theta_2)$ above the upper arm, so $\theta_1=\operatorname{atan2}(y,x)-\operatorname{atan2}(\ell_2\sin\theta_2,\ell_1+\ell_2\cos\theta_2)$. On the boundary $r=\ell_1+\ell_2$ (or $|\ell_1-\ell_2|$), $\cos\theta_2=\pm1$, so $\theta_2=0$ or $\pi$ and the $\pm$ solutions merge - the same place $\det J=0$. (Proposition 3.1.)
D4 - the force-torque transpose. Prove $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$ from the principle of virtual work. Probes: virtual work and the duality of velocity and force maps; Section 4.
Solution
For a virtual joint displacement $\delta\mathbf q$, the endpoint moves $\delta\mathbf p=J\,\delta\mathbf q$. The joint torques do work $\boldsymbol\tau^{\mathsf T}\delta\mathbf q$; the hand does work $\mathbf F^{\mathsf T}\delta\mathbf p=\mathbf F^{\mathsf T}J\,\delta\mathbf q$ against the endpoint force. Static equilibrium means the net virtual work is zero for every admissible $\delta\mathbf q$: $(\boldsymbol\tau^{\mathsf T}-\mathbf F^{\mathsf T}J)\delta\mathbf q=0\ \forall\delta\mathbf q$, so $\boldsymbol\tau^{\mathsf T}=\mathbf F^{\mathsf T}J$, i.e. $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$. The velocity map pushes forward through $J$; the force map pulls back through $J^{\mathsf T}$. (Proposition 4.1.)
D5 - force ellipsoid $\perp$ velocity ellipsoid. Show that the endpoint force ellipsoid (image of the unit torque sphere under $J^{-\mathsf T}$) has its principal axes aligned with those of the velocity ellipsoid (image of the unit joint-rate sphere under $J$), but with reciprocal lengths - so the long axis of one is the short axis of the other. Probes: the singular value decomposition of $J$; Section 2, Section 4.
Solution
Write the SVD $J=U\Sigma V^{\mathsf T}$, with $\Sigma=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1,\sigma_2)$. The velocity ellipsoid $\{J\dot{\mathbf q}:\|\dot{\mathbf q}\|=1\}$ has principal axes along the columns of $U$ with semi-axis lengths $\sigma_1,\sigma_2$. The force ellipsoid uses $J^{-\mathsf T}=U\Sigma^{-1}V^{\mathsf T}$ (since $J^{-\mathsf T}=(U\Sigma V^{\mathsf T})^{-\mathsf T}=U\Sigma^{-1}V^{\mathsf T}$), so $\{J^{-\mathsf T}\boldsymbol\tau:\|\boldsymbol\tau\|=1\}$ has the same axis directions (columns of $U$) but semi-axis lengths $1/\sigma_1,1/\sigma_2$. Where the velocity ellipsoid is long ($\sigma$ large) the force ellipsoid is short ($1/\sigma$ small), and vice versa. In 2-D with the two axes swapping which is longer, the force ellipse looks like the velocity ellipse turned $90^\circ$ - the easy-motion direction is the weak-force direction.
D6 - minimum grip force. Derive the least grip force that holds a cup of weight $W$ in a two-pad pinch with friction coefficient $\mu$. Probes: force balance and Coulomb friction; Section 8.
Solution
Vertical equilibrium of the cup: the two friction forces sum to the weight, $F_{t,1}+F_{t,2}=W$; by symmetry each carries $W/2$. Coulomb's law caps each pad's friction at $\mu F_g$. No slip requires $W/2\le\mu F_g$, i.e. $F_g\ge W/(2\mu)$, with equality the minimum. The grip scales inversely with friction, so slippery objects need proportionally harder grips. (Proposition 8.1.)
D7 - the stability ratio. Derive the concavity-compression condition $F_t/F_c\le\tan\alpha$ for a humeral head seated in a socket of half-arc $\alpha$. Probes: force resolution at the rim of the socket; Section 7.
Solution
The head stays seated while the resultant of the compressive force $F_c$ (along the socket axis) and the tangential force $F_t$ points no farther out than the rim, so the contact reaction can still redirect the head inward. That resultant makes angle $\beta=\arctan(F_t/F_c)$ with the axis; containment requires $\beta\le\alpha$, i.e. $\arctan(F_t/F_c)\le\alpha$, i.e. $F_t/F_c\le\tan\alpha$. Beyond it the resultant clears the rim and the head rides out - dislocation. A deeper socket (larger $\alpha$) or a larger compressive force (a strong cuff, larger $F_c$) both raise the tolerable $F_t$. (Proposition 7.1.)
D8 - redundancy resolution. For a full-row-rank $2\times3$ Jacobian, derive the general solution of $J\dot{\mathbf q}=\dot{\mathbf p}$ and show the pseudoinverse term is the minimum-norm solution and $(I-J^{+}J)$ projects onto the null space. Probes: the pseudoinverse and orthogonal decomposition; Section 5.
Solution
With $J^{+}=J^{\mathsf T}(JJ^{\mathsf T})^{-1}$ we have $JJ^{+}=I_2$, so $J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}$ solves the task. For any $\mathbf z$, $J(I-J^{+}J)\mathbf z=(J-J)\mathbf z=\mathbf 0$, so the second term is in $\mathcal N(J)$; conversely any solution differs from $J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}$ by a null-space vector, and $I-J^{+}J$ is the orthogonal projector onto $\mathcal N(J)$ (it is symmetric and idempotent, with range $\mathcal N(J)$). Since $J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}\in\mathcal R(J^{\mathsf T})=\mathcal N(J)^{\perp}$, the two parts are orthogonal, giving $\|\dot{\mathbf q}\|^2=\|J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}\|^2+\|\text{null part}\|^2\ge\|J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}\|^2$: the pseudoinverse term is minimum-norm. (Proposition 5.1.)
D9 - the mass matrix. Derive the mass matrix $M(\mathbf q)$ of the two-link arm from its kinetic energy, and explain why $M_{11}$ depends on $\theta_2$ but $M_{22}$ does not. Probes: kinetic energy of a linkage and configuration-dependent inertia; Section 6.
Solution
Link 1's centre-of-mass speed is $v_1=\ell_{c1}\dot\theta_1$; link 2's centre, at $(\ell_1c_1+\ell_{c2}c_{12},\ \ell_1s_1+\ell_{c2}s_{12})$, has $v_2^2=\ell_1^2\dot\theta_1^2+\ell_{c2}^2(\dot\theta_1+\dot\theta_2)^2+2\ell_1\ell_{c2}c_2\dot\theta_1(\dot\theta_1+\dot\theta_2)$. Then $T=\tfrac12 m_1v_1^2+\tfrac12 I_1\dot\theta_1^2+\tfrac12 m_2v_2^2+\tfrac12 I_2(\dot\theta_1+\dot\theta_2)^2=\tfrac12\dot{\mathbf q}^{\mathsf T}M\dot{\mathbf q}$; reading off coefficients gives $M$ (Proposition 6.1). $M_{11}$ carries the $2\ell_1\ell_{c2}c_2$ cross term because a shoulder rotation swings link 2 about the shoulder at a radius that depends on how folded the elbow is ($c_2$); $M_{22}$ is link 2 rotating about the elbow alone, whose inertia $I_2+m_2\ell_{c2}^2$ is fixed regardless of $\theta_2$.
D10 - endpoint stiffness. Starting from $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$ and joint stiffness $\delta\boldsymbol\tau=-K_q\,\delta\mathbf q$, derive the endpoint stiffness $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_q J^{-1}$ (near a non-singular posture, to first order). Probes: linearising the force map, programmable hand stiffness; Section 4.
Solution
A small endpoint displacement $\delta\mathbf p$ maps to joint change $\delta\mathbf q=J^{-1}\delta\mathbf p$ (differential kinematics inverted, $J$ square and non-singular). The joints resist with $\delta\boldsymbol\tau=-K_q\delta\mathbf q=-K_qJ^{-1}\delta\mathbf p$. The resulting endpoint restoring force, to first order (holding $J$ fixed), is $\delta\mathbf F=J^{-\mathsf T}\delta\boldsymbol\tau=-J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}\delta\mathbf p$, so the endpoint stiffness is $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}$. Because $J$ depends on posture, the same joint stiffnesses present different hand stiffnesses in different configurations - the arm can be stiff along a working direction and compliant across it, the basis of impedance control (Module 12).

Each computational problem requires simulation, optimisation, an inverse solve, a sensitivity sweep, or a regime comparison - not substitution into a boxed formula. Numbers quoted are those the code produces.

K1 - the cost of the last centimetre (sensitivity sweep). A straight-line reach of fixed duration $T=0.6\ \mathrm{s}$ starts at $(0.10,-0.30)\ \mathrm{m}$. Sweep the goal outward toward the workspace boundary $\ell_1+\ell_2=0.75\ \mathrm{m}$ and, at each goal, solve IK along the path and finite-difference the joint angles to find the peak elbow angular velocity. Show how it blows up near the singularity. Probes: near-singular joint-velocity blow-up; Section 3.
Solution
With goal $x$-coordinates $0.45,0.60,0.70,0.73\ \mathrm{m}$ (radii $r=0.461,0.608,0.707,0.737\ \mathrm{m}$) the peak elbow rate is $2.55,4.37,8.79,16.0\ \mathrm{rad/s}$ - it more than doubles over the last $3\ \mathrm{cm}$ of approach to the boundary, and would diverge as $r\to0.75$. The mechanism is $\theta_2=\arccos(\cdot)$ acquiring an infinite slope at full extension: the same hand-speed demands ever-larger joint speed. The lesson is that reaching to the very edge of the workspace is not merely "a bit harder" but singular - which is why the body recruits trunk lean rather than truly straightening the arm to a target at its limit.
K2 - inertial vs gravitational torque (regime comparison + root find). For the wave $\theta_2(t)=\theta_2^0+A\sin(2\pi ft)$ ($A=0.5\ \mathrm{rad}$), compute the peak elbow torque and its inertial and gravitational parts as functions of frequency $f$. Find the crossover frequency where the two parts are equal, and compare the total peak at $1.5$ and $3\ \mathrm{Hz}$. Probes: the $f^2$ inertial law and the gravity/inertia crossover; Section 6.
Solution
The gravitational peak is fixed at $m_2\ell_{c2}g\approx3.5\ \mathrm{N\,m}$; the inertial peak is $M_{22}A(2\pi f)^2$, quadratic in $f$. Setting them equal gives the crossover $f_c=\frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{m_2\ell_{c2}g/(M_{22}A)}\approx1.31\ \mathrm{Hz}$. Below $f_c$ gravity dominates; above it inertia does. At $f=1.5\ \mathrm{Hz}$ the parts are comparable ($4.7$ vs $3.5\ \mathrm{N\,m}$) and the total peaks at $\approx8.1\ \mathrm{N\,m}$; at $f=3\ \mathrm{Hz}$ the inertial part quadruples to $\approx18.6\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ and the total reaches $\approx22.1\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ - roughly triple, for the same hand travel. This quantifies why a vigorous wave is disproportionately tiring.
K3 - grip force with a slip reflex (simulation + sensitivity). Sweep $\mu$ from $0.1$ to $0.8$ and compute the grip force (with $30\%$ safety margin) needed to hold a $0.5\ \mathrm{kg}$ cup; then simulate a lift with a time-varying load and modulate grip in parallel. Report the grip force at $\mu=0.6$, $0.2$, and $0.1$. Probes: the $1/\mu$ floor and grip-load coupling; Section 8, Section 9.
Solution
The per-pad grip force $F_g=(1+s)mg/(2\mu)$ with $s=0.3$ gives $5.31\ \mathrm{N}$ at $\mu=0.6$, $15.9\ \mathrm{N}$ at $\mu=0.2$, and $31.9\ \mathrm{N}$ at $\mu=0.1$ - a hyperbola whose steepness near small $\mu$ means grip effort is most sensitive to friction exactly where friction is scarcest. In the lift, grip force tracks the load pulse $m(g+a(t))$ in lock-step, keeping the margin roughly constant, the anticipatory grip-load coupling of Section 9. Sensitivity: $dF_g/d\mu=-(1+s)mg/(2\mu^2)$, so the marginal grip cost of a friction drop grows as $1/\mu^2$.
K4 - the most dexterous reach distance (optimisation). Manipulability along a radial reach is $w(r)=\ell_1\ell_2|\sin\theta_2(r)|$ with $\cos\theta_2=(r^2-\ell_1^2-\ell_2^2)/(2\ell_1\ell_2)$. Find the reach distance $r$ that maximises $w$, and the maximum value. Probes: dexterity as an optimisation over posture; Section 2.
Solution
$w$ is maximal when $|\sin\theta_2|=1$, i.e. $\theta_2=\pi/2$, where $\cos\theta_2=0$ and thus $r^2=\ell_1^2+\ell_2^2$, giving $r=\sqrt{0.30^2+0.45^2}=0.541\ \mathrm{m}$ and $w_{\max}=\ell_1\ell_2=0.135\ \mathrm{m^2}$. This is the right-angle-elbow posture: reaching to a target about $0.54\ \mathrm{m}$ from the shoulder puts the arm at peak dexterity, which is why comfortable working distances (a desk, a steering wheel) sit near this radius rather than at full extension. The peak is broad: solving $|\sin\theta_2|\ge0.95$ gives $\theta_2\in[71.8^\circ,108.2^\circ]$, i.e. any reach $r\in[0.46,0.61]\ \mathrm{m}$ - a $\sim16\ \mathrm{cm}$ band - keeps $w$ within $5\%$ of its maximum, so dexterity is robust to reach errors near the sweet spot and falls off only toward the singular extremes.
K5 - resolving redundancy off a joint limit (inverse solve with secondary cost). A three-link arm ($\ell_i=0.25\ \mathrm{m}$) holds its fingertip fixed with the elbow near a soft limit at $2.0\ \mathrm{rad}$. Using the null-space projector, drive the elbow toward a comfortable $1.0\ \mathrm{rad}$ while holding the fingertip. Report the elbow motion and the fingertip error. Probes: null-space optimisation of a secondary objective; Section 5.
Solution
Integrating $\dot{\mathbf q}=J^{+}(k_e\,\mathbf e)-(I-J^{+}J)\,k_H\nabla H$ with $H=\tfrac12(\theta_2-1.0)^2$ and a fingertip-error feedback $\mathbf e$ drives the elbow from $1.90$ to $1.005\ \mathrm{rad}$ - off the limit and onto the comfortable target - while the fingertip position error stays at $1.3\times10^{-9}\ \mathrm{m}$, i.e. exactly fixed to numerical precision. The self-motion reconfigures the arm without disturbing the task, the continuous version of C2's discrete elbow choice, and the mechanism by which the nervous system keeps joints out of their painful extremes during sustained holds.
K6 - strongest push in a direction (constrained optimisation). At the pose reaching $(0.45,0.10)\ \mathrm{m}$ (elbow-down), with each joint torque bounded by $|\tau_i|\le15\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, find the maximum vertical endpoint force the hand can exert. Probes: the force polytope under torque limits; Section 4.
Solution
From $\mathbf F=J^{-\mathsf T}\boldsymbol\tau$, the vertical force is $F_y=(J^{-\mathsf T})_{2,:}\cdot\boldsymbol\tau$, a linear function of $\boldsymbol\tau$ over the box $|\tau_i|\le15$. Its maximum is $F_y^{\max}=15\big(|(J^{-\mathsf T})_{21}|+|(J^{-\mathsf T})_{22}|\big)=34.5\ \mathrm{N}$, attained by driving both joint torques to the sign-matched corner of the box. This is about $7\times$ the weight of the $0.5\ \mathrm{kg}$ cup - ample - but at a near-straight bracing pose the same torque box would yield far more vertical force still, because $J^{-\mathsf T}$ blows up toward the singularity (C3's bracing intuition, quantified).
K7 - a self-motion that moves nothing (null-space simulation). For the same three-link arm, integrate a pure null-space joint velocity (the smallest right-singular vector of $J$) and confirm the fingertip does not move while the elbow reconfigures. Probes: the null space as measured self-motion; Section 5.
Solution
Integrating $\dot{\mathbf q}=n$, the unit null vector of the $2\times3$ Jacobian (from its SVD), over a short interval moves the three joints by $(+10.0,-24.5,+21.8)^\circ$ and swings the elbow angle $\theta_1+\theta_2$ by $-14.5^\circ$, while the fingertip drifts by only $4.4\times10^{-5}\ \mathrm{m}$ - the residual of the finite step size, vanishing as the step shrinks. This is C9's pinned-fingertip freedom measured: the joints traverse a one-parameter family of postures with the endpoint held fixed, the internal motion the nervous system exploits for comfort and obstacle avoidance.
K8 - a slip and its reflex (event-driven simulation). Simulate lifting a cup whose weight the internal model badly under-predicts, so the feedforward grip is too low. Detect the resulting slip and add a $70\ \mathrm{ms}$-delayed reflex that boosts the grip. Report when the margin goes negative and when the reflex catches it. Probes: feedforward error, slip detection, delayed feedback; Section 9, Module 10.
Solution
With the model predicting a lift acceleration of $1.5\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ but the true value $9\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$, the feedforward grip provides only $(2\mu F_g)$ that falls $22\%$ short of the load at the acceleration peak (minimum margin $-0.22$); the slip is detected at $t=0.425\ \mathrm{s}$ and the reflex, arriving $70\ \mathrm{ms}$ later at $t\approx0.495\ \mathrm{s}$, boosts the grip $30\%$ above the instantaneous floor and arrests it. The episode reproduces the everyday "lighter than I thought / heavier than I thought" jolt and shows the tactile loop as a delayed-feedback catch on a feedforward error - the fingertip instance of Module 10's delayed balance loop.
K9 - the best link-length ratio (design optimisation). Holding total arm length $\ell_{\rm tot}=\ell_1+\ell_2$ fixed, find the ratio $\ell_2/\ell_1$ that maximises manipulability at a reach of $0.6\,\ell_{\rm tot}$. Interpret. Probes: sensitivity of dexterity to segment proportions; Section 2.
Solution
Sweeping the ratio with $\ell_{\rm tot}$ fixed and evaluating $w=\ell_1\ell_2|\sin\theta_2|$ at $r=0.6\,\ell_{\rm tot}$, the maximum is at $\ell_2/\ell_1=1.0$ - equal-length links - giving $w=0.135\ \mathrm{m^2}$ (with $\ell_{\rm tot}=0.75\ \mathrm{m}$). Equal segments maximise the product $\ell_1\ell_2$ for fixed sum, and the reach at $0.6L$ keeps the elbow comfortably bent. The human upper arm and forearm-plus-hand are not far from equal, a proportion consistent with (though not proven by) a dexterity criterion; the real arm also optimises reach, strength, and packing, so this is one pressure among several.
K10 - pinch versus power grip (capstan-amplified regime comparison). A precision pinch holds by friction at two pads; a power grip also wraps the fingers and palm around the handle over a wrap angle $\beta$, so the squeeze is amplified by the capstan (belt-friction) law $e^{\mu\beta}$. With a maximum squeeze of $30\ \mathrm{N}$ and a half-wrap $\beta=\pi$, compute and compare the heaviest object each grip holds versus friction $\mu$, and show how the power-grip advantage scales with $\mu$ and $\beta$. Probes: the capstan law and a nonlinear friction-regime comparison; Section 8.
Solution
Model the pinch as $W_{\rm pinch}=2\mu F_g$ (two pads at the friction floor of Proposition 8.1), and the power grip as the same squeeze wrapped over angle $\beta$, whose holding capacity is amplified by the capstan law: $W_{\rm power}=2\mu F_g\,e^{\mu\beta}$. With $F_g=30\ \mathrm{N}$ and a half-wrap $\beta=\pi$: at $\mu=0.2$, pinch $12\ \mathrm{N}$ versus power $22\ \mathrm{N}$ (amplification $e^{0.2\pi}=1.87$); at $\mu=0.5$, $30$ versus $144\ \mathrm{N}$ ($e^{0.5\pi}=4.81$); at $\mu=0.8$, $48$ versus $593\ \mathrm{N}$ ($e^{0.8\pi}=12.3$). Unlike a fixed multiple, the capstan amplification $e^{\mu\beta}$ grows exponentially in both friction and wrap angle: at $\mu=0.5$, opening the wrap from $\beta=\pi/2$ to $3\pi/2$ raises the power-grip capacity from $66$ to $317\ \mathrm{N}$. So the power grip beats the pinch not by a constant factor but by a margin that explodes as the object gets slippery or the wrap deepens - the mechanical reason you carry a heavy suitcase with a wrapped power grip, not a fingertip pinch, which keeps only the dexterity of its two independent contacts.

Five diagnostics ↑ contents

  1. Units check. The Jacobian $J$ maps joint rates (rad/s) to hand velocity (m/s); what are the units of its entries, and of $J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$?
    Solution
    Entries of $J$ are metres (length), so $J\dot{\mathbf q}$ has units $\mathrm{m}\cdot\mathrm{rad/s}=\mathrm{m/s}$ (radians dimensionless). $J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$ has units $\mathrm{m}\cdot\mathrm{N}=\mathrm{N\,m}$ - torque, as required.
  2. Sign of the determinant. Why does $\det J=\ell_1\ell_2\sin\theta_2$ change sign at $\theta_2=0$?
    Solution
    The sign of $\det J$ encodes the handedness of the mapping; it flips as the elbow crosses full extension because the two IK branches (elbow-up/down) swap, and the arm passes through the singularity where $J$ is rank-deficient.
  3. Grip direction. In the pinch, why must the two grip forces be equal and opposite?
    Solution
    Horizontal equilibrium of the cup: the two normal forces are the only horizontal forces, so they must cancel, forcing them equal and opposite; their common magnitude is the grip force $F_g$.
  4. Redundancy count. How many null-space dimensions has a planar three-link arm doing a position-only reach? A four-link arm?
    Solution
    Null-space dimension $=$ joints $-$ task dimension. Three-link, 2-D position: $3-2=1$. Four-link: $4-2=2$.
  5. Stability ratio. If a labral tear halves the effective socket half-arc $\alpha$ (small-angle), roughly what happens to the stability ratio?
    Solution
    For small $\alpha$, $\tan\alpha\approx\alpha$, so halving $\alpha$ roughly halves the stability ratio $F_t/F_c$ the shoulder can tolerate - a large loss of security, the mechanics of recurrent instability.

0. Motivation - from one-dimensional models to a continuum

This course has been quietly modelling tissues as one-dimensional objects. Module 2 treated a bone as an Euler-Bernoulli beam - a line with a bending stiffness. Module 4 treated cartilage as a stack of springs and dashpots squeezing fluid through a one-dimensional column. Module 6 treated a tendon as a nonlinear spring, a single force-length curve. Each reduction was deliberate and powerful, and each threw away the same thing: that a tissue is a three-dimensional body, deforming in every direction at once, with a stress that is not a single number but a state at each point.

A one-dimensional model cannot answer the questions that decide whether a tissue fails. When a bone is loaded off-axis, where is the stress highest across its cross-section? When a tendon is stretched along its fibres, how much does it bulge sideways, and why does that matter for its stiffness? When cartilage is pressed, how does the load partition between the solid matrix and the pressurised fluid, point by point? These are questions about fields - stress and strain that vary through the body's volume - and to answer them we need the language those fields are written in: continuum mechanics.

The plan runs from geometry to law to computation. We first make deformation precise - the displacement field, the deformation gradient, and the strain tensor that measures local stretching (Section 1). We then define stress as a tensor and impose equilibrium (Section 2), and connect the two with a constitutive law - linear elasticity and its strain energy (Section 3). With the physics in place, we turn to computation: the bar and beam elements that are the atoms of finite-element analysis (Section 4), and how they assemble into a global system solved for the stress field (Section 5). The last third makes the laws realistic for tissue: anisotropy and fibre reinforcement (Section 6), hyperelasticity at finite strain (Section 7), and the time-dependent viscoelastic and poroelastic behaviours (Section 8) that make bone a composite, tendon a fibre-reinforced solid, and cartilage a biphasic material (Section 9). Two labs and a problem set close it. The reward is that every idealisation of the earlier modules becomes a special case of one framework - and its limits become visible.

1. Deformation: the displacement field, gradient, and strain

Continuum mechanics begins not with force but with geometry: a precise account of how a body changes shape. Label each material point by its position $\mathbf X$ in the undeformed (reference) body. When the body deforms, that point moves to a new position $\mathbf x$. The map $\mathbf x=\boldsymbol\chi(\mathbf X)$ is the deformation, and its local behaviour - how a tiny material fibre is stretched and rotated - is captured by a single object.

Definition 1.1 (displacement field and deformation gradient). The displacement of a material point is $\mathbf u(\mathbf X)=\mathbf x-\mathbf X$, the vector from where the point was to where it is. The deformation gradient is the Jacobian of the deformation map, $$\mathbf F=\frac{\partial\mathbf x}{\partial\mathbf X}=\mathbf I+\frac{\partial\mathbf u}{\partial\mathbf X},$$ a second-order tensor (in two dimensions a $2\times2$ matrix). It maps an infinitesimal material fibre $d\mathbf X$ in the reference body to its image $d\mathbf x=\mathbf F\,d\mathbf X$ in the deformed body, encoding the local stretch and rotation (Fig. 2). Its determinant $J=\det\mathbf F$ is the local volume ratio (deformed volume over reference volume); $J=1$ is an isochoric (volume-preserving) deformation.

The deformation gradient contains rotation as well as stretch, and rotation is not deformation - a rigid spin of the whole body strains nothing. To measure only the stretching, we form a quantity that is blind to rotation. The Green-Lagrange strain tensor does this exactly.

Definition 1.2 (strain). The Green-Lagrange strain is $$\mathbf E=\tfrac12\big(\mathbf F^{\!\top}\mathbf F-\mathbf I\big),$$ which vanishes for any rigid-body motion (where $\mathbf F$ is a pure rotation, $\mathbf F^{\!\top}\mathbf F=\mathbf I$) and measures the change in squared length of material fibres. When displacements and their gradients are small - the regime of bone and of small tissue strains - the quadratic term is negligible and $\mathbf E$ reduces to the infinitesimal (small) strain tensor $$\boldsymbol\varepsilon=\tfrac12\big(\nabla\mathbf u+\nabla\mathbf u^{\!\top}\big),\qquad \varepsilon_{ij}=\tfrac12\Big(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial X_j}+\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial X_i}\Big),$$ whose diagonal entries $\varepsilon_{11},\varepsilon_{22},\varepsilon_{33}$ are the fractional stretches along each axis (the familiar $\Delta L/L$) and whose off-diagonal entries are the shear strains.
Proposition 1.1 (small strain is the linearisation of Green-Lagrange strain). For small displacement gradients $\nabla\mathbf u$, the Green-Lagrange strain equals the infinitesimal strain to first order: $$\boxed{\;\mathbf E=\boldsymbol\varepsilon+O\big(\lVert\nabla\mathbf u\rVert^2\big),\qquad \boldsymbol\varepsilon=\tfrac12\big(\nabla\mathbf u+\nabla\mathbf u^{\!\top}\big).\;}$$
Write $\mathbf F=\mathbf I+\mathbf H$ with $\mathbf H=\nabla\mathbf u$ the displacement gradient. Then $$\mathbf F^{\!\top}\mathbf F=(\mathbf I+\mathbf H)^{\!\top}(\mathbf I+\mathbf H)=\mathbf I+\mathbf H+\mathbf H^{\!\top}+\mathbf H^{\!\top}\mathbf H,$$ so $\mathbf E=\tfrac12(\mathbf F^{\!\top}\mathbf F-\mathbf I)=\tfrac12(\mathbf H+\mathbf H^{\!\top})+\tfrac12\mathbf H^{\!\top}\mathbf H$. The first term is exactly $\boldsymbol\varepsilon$; the second is quadratic in $\mathbf H=\nabla\mathbf u$ and is dropped when $\lVert\nabla\mathbf u\rVert\ll1$. Hence $\mathbf E=\boldsymbol\varepsilon+O(\lVert\nabla\mathbf u\rVert^2)$. The linear theory of Section 3 uses $\boldsymbol\varepsilon$; the finite-strain theory of Section 7 keeps the full $\mathbf E$. ∎

A concrete case anchors the tensor. Under a uniaxial stretch of ratio $\lambda$ along $X_1$ with lateral contraction ratio governed by Poisson's ratio $\nu$ (Section 3), the deformation is $x_1=\lambda X_1$, $x_2=(1-\nu(\lambda-1))X_2$, so $\mathbf F=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda,\,1-\nu(\lambda-1))$ and, for small $\lambda-1$, the axial small strain is $\varepsilon_{11}=\lambda-1$ and the transverse strain is $\varepsilon_{22}=-\nu\varepsilon_{11}$ - the sideways bulge of a stretched tendon quantified. This is the geometry the next section loads.

2. Stress: traction and the Cauchy stress tensor

Deformation is geometry; stress is force. Cut a loaded body across an internal surface and the two sides push on each other. The force per unit area transmitted across that cut is the traction, and its value depends on the orientation of the cut - a fact that forces stress, like strain, to be a tensor rather than a number.

Definition 2.1 (traction and the Cauchy stress tensor). At a point, imagine a small planar cut with unit normal $\mathbf n$. The material on the positive-$\mathbf n$ side exerts on the other side a force per unit area, the traction $\mathbf t(\mathbf n)$. Cauchy's theorem states that this dependence on $\mathbf n$ is linear: there is a single second-order tensor $\boldsymbol\sigma$, the Cauchy stress, with $$\boxed{\;\mathbf t(\mathbf n)=\boldsymbol\sigma\,\mathbf n,\qquad t_i=\sigma_{ij}n_j.\;}$$ The component $\sigma_{ij}$ is the $i$-th force component per unit area on the face whose normal points along $j$; the diagonal entries are normal stresses (tension/compression), the off-diagonal entries shear stresses.
Proposition 2.1 (the stress tensor is symmetric). Balance of angular momentum forces $$\boxed{\;\boldsymbol\sigma=\boldsymbol\sigma^{\!\top},\qquad \sigma_{ij}=\sigma_{ji},\;}$$ so of its nine components only six are independent - three normal and three shear.
Take a material cube of side $h$ centred at the point and sum moments about its centre. A shear stress $\sigma_{ij}$ ($i\neq j$) acts on the two faces normal to $j$ with opposite sign, forming a couple of moment $\sim\sigma_{ij}h^2\cdot h=\sigma_{ij}h^3$ about the axis perpendicular to the $i$-$j$ plane; the complementary shear $\sigma_{ji}$ forms an opposing couple $\sim\sigma_{ji}h^3$. The body's rotational inertia scales as $\rho h^5$ and its angular acceleration is finite, so the net moment must vanish faster than $h^3$: dividing the moment balance by $h^3$ and letting $h\to0$ leaves $\sigma_{ij}-\sigma_{ji}=0$. Hence $\boldsymbol\sigma$ is symmetric. ∎

Stress must also balance force. For a body in static equilibrium under a body force per unit volume $\mathbf b$ (gravity, say), summing forces on an infinitesimal element gives the equilibrium equation

$$\nabla\!\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma+\mathbf b=\mathbf 0,\qquad \frac{\partial\sigma_{ij}}{\partial x_j}+b_i=0,$$

three scalar equations (one per direction) that the stress field must satisfy at every interior point, with the surface traction $\boldsymbol\sigma\mathbf n$ matching the applied load on the boundary. Equilibrium, the strain-displacement relation of Section 1, and the constitutive law of Section 3 together close the system: three ingredients - kinematics, balance, and material law - determine the fields. Two scalars extracted from $\boldsymbol\sigma$ recur in tissue failure: the principal stresses (its eigenvalues, the extreme normal stresses over all cut orientations, which decide brittle fracture in bone) and the von Mises stress (a scalar built from the shear parts, which governs ductile yield). Both are invariants of the same tensor.

3. Linear elasticity and the strain-energy function

Kinematics gave strain, balance gave stress; a constitutive law ties them together, encoding what the material is. The simplest - accurate for bone and for small strains of any tissue - is linear elasticity: stress proportional to strain, through a fourth-order stiffness tensor.

Definition 3.1 (generalised Hooke's law; isotropic elasticity). Linear elasticity sets $\sigma_{ij}=C_{ijkl}\,\varepsilon_{kl}$, with $\mathbf C$ the stiffness tensor. For an isotropic material - no preferred direction, the case for bone matrix at the tissue scale - $\mathbf C$ reduces to two constants, and $$\boxed{\;\boldsymbol\sigma=\lambda\,(\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol\varepsilon)\,\mathbf I+2\mu\,\boldsymbol\varepsilon,\;}$$ with $\lambda,\mu$ the Lamé constants ($\mu$ the shear modulus). Equivalently the response is set by Young's modulus $E$ and Poisson's ratio $\nu$: for uniaxial stress $\sigma$, the axial strain is $\varepsilon=\sigma/E$ and the transverse strain is $-\nu\varepsilon$, with $\mu=\dfrac{E}{2(1+\nu)}$ and $\lambda=\dfrac{E\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)}$.
Proposition 3.1 (stored energy and its positivity). A linear-elastic material stores a strain-energy density $$\boxed{\;W=\tfrac12\,\boldsymbol\sigma:\boldsymbol\varepsilon=\tfrac12\,\varepsilon_{ij}C_{ijkl}\varepsilon_{kl},\;}$$ the work per unit volume done in straining it, and stress is its gradient, $\sigma_{ij}=\partial W/\partial\varepsilon_{ij}$. Stability of the material requires $W\gt0$ for every non-zero strain, which for the isotropic law holds iff $\mu\gt0$ and the bulk modulus $K=\lambda+\tfrac23\mu\gt0$ (equivalently $E\gt0$ and $-1\lt\nu\lt\tfrac12$).
Load the element quasi-statically along a straight path $\boldsymbol\varepsilon(s)=s\,\bar{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}$, $s:0\to1$. The stress is $\boldsymbol\sigma(s)=s\,\mathbf C:\bar{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}$, and the work per volume is $W=\int_0^1\boldsymbol\sigma:d\boldsymbol\varepsilon=\int_0^1 s\,(\mathbf C:\bar{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}):\bar{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}\,ds=\tfrac12\bar{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}:\mathbf C:\bar{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}=\tfrac12\boldsymbol\sigma:\boldsymbol\varepsilon$, and differentiating $W=\tfrac12\varepsilon_{ij}C_{ijkl}\varepsilon_{kl}$ (with $\mathbf C$ symmetric) gives $\partial W/\partial\varepsilon_{ij}=C_{ijkl}\varepsilon_{kl}=\sigma_{ij}$. Splitting any strain into a volumetric part $\tfrac13(\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol\varepsilon)\mathbf I$ and a deviatoric (shape-changing) part $\mathbf e$, the isotropic energy separates as $W=\tfrac12 K(\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol\varepsilon)^2+\mu\,\mathbf e:\mathbf e$; both terms are non-negative for all strains iff $K\gt0$ and $\mu\gt0$, and each vanishes only at zero strain, giving positive-definiteness. ∎

The two constants span an enormous range across the tissues of this course, which is why their mechanical roles differ so sharply.

But three of these four tissues are poorly served by the isotropic, linear, time-independent law: tendon is strongly anisotropic (stiff along fibres, soft across), tendon and cartilage are nonlinear and time-dependent, and cartilage is biphasic. The rest of the module builds those laws - but first, the linear law is enough to make the continuum computable, which is the business of the finite element.

4. The bar and beam elements: finite-element intuition

The continuum equations of the last three sections - strain from displacement, stress from strain, equilibrium of stress - are partial differential equations, solvable in closed form only for simple geometries. The finite-element method makes them computable for any shape by a single idea: chop the body into small pieces (elements), assume a simple displacement variation within each, and require equilibrium of the pieces. The atom of the method is the bar element, and it is nothing more than Hooke's law for a rod.

Definition 4.1 (the axial bar element). A straight rod of length $L$, cross-section $A$, and modulus $E$ carries axial displacements $u_1,u_2$ at its two end nodes, with axial forces $f_1,f_2$. Assuming the displacement varies linearly along the rod, the strain is the constant $\varepsilon=(u_2-u_1)/L$, the stress $\sigma=E\varepsilon$, and the internal force $N=\sigma A=\dfrac{EA}{L}(u_2-u_1)$. Force balance at the nodes gives the element stiffness relation $$\boxed{\;\begin{pmatrix}f_1\\ f_2\end{pmatrix}=\frac{EA}{L}\begin{pmatrix}1&-1\\-1&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}u_1\\ u_2\end{pmatrix}=\mathbf k_e\,\mathbf u_e,\;}$$ with $k=EA/L$ the axial stiffness (the spring constant of Modules 2 and 6, here derived from the continuum).
Proposition 4.1 (the element matrix is the Hessian of its strain energy). The bar element's stiffness matrix $\mathbf k_e$ is symmetric positive-semidefinite, and equals the second derivative of the element strain energy $W_e=\tfrac12\,\dfrac{EA}{L}(u_2-u_1)^2$ with respect to the nodal displacements; its single zero eigenvalue is the rigid translation $u_1=u_2$.
The stored energy is the strain-energy density $\tfrac12 E\varepsilon^2$ times the volume $AL$: $W_e=\tfrac12 E\big(\tfrac{u_2-u_1}{L}\big)^2 AL=\tfrac12\tfrac{EA}{L}(u_2-u_1)^2$. Its gradient gives the nodal forces, $f_1=\partial W_e/\partial u_1=-\tfrac{EA}{L}(u_2-u_1)$, $f_2=\partial W_e/\partial u_2=+\tfrac{EA}{L}(u_2-u_1)$, which is the boxed relation, and its Hessian $\partial^2 W_e/\partial u_i\partial u_j=\tfrac{EA}{L}\big(\begin{smallmatrix}1&-1\\-1&1\end{smallmatrix}\big)=\mathbf k_e$. This matrix is symmetric with eigenvalues $0$ (eigenvector $(1,1)$, a rigid shift that strains nothing) and $2EA/L$ (eigenvector $(1,-1)$, pure stretch), hence positive-semidefinite. The zero eigenvalue is why a structure needs a support: without a fixed node, $\mathbf K$ is singular and the displacement is undetermined up to a rigid motion. ∎

Bending needs one more element. A beam element resists transverse deflection, and because bending couples deflection to rotation (Module 2's Euler-Bernoulli theory), each node carries two degrees of freedom - a transverse displacement $w$ and a slope $\theta=dw/dx$ - so the element stiffness is a $4\times4$ matrix built from the bending stiffness $EI$,

$$\mathbf k_{\rm beam}=\frac{EI}{L^3}\begin{pmatrix}12&6L&-12&6L\\6L&4L^2&-6L&2L^2\\-12&-6L&12&-6L\\6L&2L^2&-6L&4L^2\end{pmatrix},$$

whose entries are exactly the shear-force and bending-moment reactions Module 2 computed for a loaded beam, now packaged so that a bone modelled as a chain of beam elements reproduces its deflection under any load. The bar carries axial force, the beam carries bending; a general frame element superposes the two. With one element understood, the method is defined by how elements combine.

5. Assembling and solving a toy finite-element model

A body is meshed into many elements sharing nodes. The rule that combines them is physical: the total strain energy is the sum of the elements' energies, so the global stiffness is the sum of the element stiffnesses, each added into the rows and columns of the nodes it touches.

Proposition 5.1 (assembly and solution). For a mesh of elements $e$ with stiffness matrices $\mathbf k_e$ acting on their nodal displacements, the global equilibrium is $$\boxed{\;\mathbf K\,\mathbf u=\mathbf f,\qquad \mathbf K=\sum_e \mathbf L_e^{\!\top}\mathbf k_e\mathbf L_e,\;}$$ where $\mathbf L_e$ selects an element's nodes from the global list. $\mathbf K$ is symmetric positive-semidefinite; imposing enough displacement boundary conditions to remove rigid-body motion makes the reduced system positive-definite and uniquely solvable for $\mathbf u$, after which each element's stress follows from its nodal displacements.
The total strain energy is $W=\sum_e W_e=\sum_e\tfrac12\mathbf u_e^{\!\top}\mathbf k_e\mathbf u_e=\tfrac12\mathbf u^{\!\top}\big(\sum_e\mathbf L_e^{\!\top}\mathbf k_e\mathbf L_e\big)\mathbf u=\tfrac12\mathbf u^{\!\top}\mathbf K\mathbf u$, using $\mathbf u_e=\mathbf L_e\mathbf u$. Equilibrium is stationarity of the total potential energy $\Pi=W-\mathbf f^{\!\top}\mathbf u$: $\partial\Pi/\partial\mathbf u=\mathbf K\mathbf u-\mathbf f=\mathbf 0$, the boxed system. $\mathbf K$ inherits symmetry and positive-semidefiniteness from the $\mathbf k_e$ (a sum of such matrices), with rigid-body motions its null space (Proposition 4.1); fixing those degrees of freedom leaves a positive-definite reduced $\mathbf K$, which is invertible, so $\mathbf u$ is unique. The element strain, then stress $\sigma=E\varepsilon$, is read back from the solved nodal displacements. ∎

The whole method is short enough to read. The code below meshes a tapered bar into bar elements, assembles $\mathbf K$, applies a fixed end and an end load, solves $\mathbf K\mathbf u=\mathbf f$, and recovers the element stresses.

import numpy as np

E, L, P, A0, nel = 17e9, 0.10, 2000.0, 2e-4, 8
xn = np.linspace(0, L, nel + 1)
Ael = A0*(1 - 0.5*(xn[:-1] + xn[1:])/2/L)     # taper A0 -> A0/2
K = np.zeros((nel + 1, nel + 1))
for e in range(nel):                           # assemble
    k = E*Ael[e]/(xn[e + 1] - xn[e])
    K[e:e + 2, e:e + 2] += k*np.array([[1, -1], [-1, 1]])
f = np.zeros(nel + 1)
f[-1] = P                                      # end load
free = np.arange(1, nel + 1)                    # node 0 fixed
u = np.zeros(nel + 1)
u[free] = np.linalg.solve(K[np.ix_(free, free)], f[free])
sig = np.array([E*(u[e + 1] - u[e])/(xn[e + 1] - xn[e])
                for e in range(nel)])
print("tip displacement = %.4f mm" % (u[-1]*1e3))
print("stress %.1f -> %.1f MPa" % (sig[0]/1e6, sig[-1]/1e6))

The output is exact where it can be and convergent where it cannot. The tip displacement is $0.082\ \mathrm{mm}$ and the element stresses rise from $10\ \mathrm{MPa}$ at the wide end to $19\ \mathrm{MPa}$ at the narrow end, straddling the true $P/A(x)$ hyperbola that climbs from $10$ to $20\ \mathrm{MPa}$ (Fig. 6). Each element carries a single constant stress - the price of the linear-displacement assumption - so the solution is a staircase; refining the mesh shrinks the steps and the staircase converges to the exact curve. This is the whole of finite-element analysis in miniature: a boundary-value problem no formula solves becomes a matrix system a computer does, with an accuracy set by the mesh. The remaining sections make the material law inside each element realistic for tissue.

6. Anisotropy and fibre reinforcement

The isotropic law of Section 3 assumes the material responds the same in every direction. That is fine for bone matrix but badly wrong for tendon, ligament, and muscle, which are built of fibres running one way. A tendon pulled along its collagen is a stiff cable; pulled across, it is soft putty. Capturing this needs a law with a built-in direction.

Definition 6.1 (transversely isotropic, fibre-reinforced material). Let $\mathbf a$ be the unit vector of the fibre direction in the reference body. A material with one such preferred direction is transversely isotropic - isotropic in the plane perpendicular to $\mathbf a$ - and its stiffness needs five constants instead of two. The simplest useful form is a fibre-reinforced strain energy, an isotropic matrix plus a term that stiffens only along the fibres, $$\boxed{\;W=W_{\rm iso}(\boldsymbol\varepsilon)+W_{\rm fib}(\varepsilon_a),\qquad \varepsilon_a=\mathbf a\cdot\boldsymbol\varepsilon\,\mathbf a,\;}$$ where $\varepsilon_a$ is the strain resolved along the fibre. The matrix carries load in every direction; the fibre term adds stiffness only when the tissue is stretched along $\mathbf a$.
Proposition 6.1 (fibre reinforcement is uniaxial). The extra stress from the fibre term acts purely along the fibre direction: $$\boxed{\;\boldsymbol\sigma_{\rm fib}=\frac{\partial W_{\rm fib}}{\partial\varepsilon_a}\,\mathbf a\otimes\mathbf a,\;}$$ so a fibre-reinforced material is stiff for loads along $\mathbf a$ and reduces to its (soft) isotropic matrix for loads perpendicular to $\mathbf a$.
The fibre strain is $\varepsilon_a=\mathbf a\cdot\boldsymbol\varepsilon\,\mathbf a=a_i\varepsilon_{ij}a_j$, so $\partial\varepsilon_a/\partial\varepsilon_{ij}=a_ia_j=(\mathbf a\otimes\mathbf a)_{ij}$. The fibre stress is the gradient of the fibre energy (Proposition 3.1 generalised), $(\boldsymbol\sigma_{\rm fib})_{ij}=\partial W_{\rm fib}/\partial\varepsilon_{ij}=W_{\rm fib}'(\varepsilon_a)\,\partial\varepsilon_a/\partial\varepsilon_{ij}=W_{\rm fib}'(\varepsilon_a)\,a_ia_j$, i.e. $\boldsymbol\sigma_{\rm fib}=W_{\rm fib}'(\varepsilon_a)\,\mathbf a\otimes\mathbf a$. This tensor has its only non-zero action along $\mathbf a$: for a load with normal $\mathbf n\perp\mathbf a$, $\boldsymbol\sigma_{\rm fib}\mathbf n=W_{\rm fib}'(\varepsilon_a)(\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf n)\mathbf a=\mathbf 0$, so across the fibres only the isotropic matrix responds. ∎

Figure 7 makes the consequence quantitative: the effective stiffness of tendon is about $1.5\ \mathrm{GPa}$ along the fibres and roughly a tenth of that across them - the anisotropy that lets a tendon be a taut cable in one direction and fold freely in another. The same $\mathbf a\otimes\mathbf a$ structure describes ligament, the annulus of an intervertebral disc, and the helical fibres of muscle and heart wall; changing $\mathbf a$ (or adding a second fibre family) tailors the law to each tissue. But tendon is also grossly nonlinear - its stiffness rises with stretch - and a strain measured as $\Delta L/L$ breaks down once the stretch is large. Both need the finite-strain framework of the next section.

7. Hyperelasticity: finite strain and strain-energy density

Linear elasticity assumes strains are small enough that $\boldsymbol\varepsilon=\tfrac12(\nabla\mathbf u+\nabla\mathbf u^{\!\top})$ and that stress is proportional to it. Soft tissues violate both: a tendon stretches several per cent, skin and muscle tens of per cent, and their stress-stretch curves bend. Hyperelasticity keeps the full deformation gradient $\mathbf F$ (Section 1) and derives stress from a stored-energy function, so large deformations and nonlinearity are built in from the start.

Definition 7.1 (hyperelastic material). A material is hyperelastic if its stress derives from a strain-energy density $W(\mathbf F)$ per unit reference volume, a function of the deformation gradient alone. The work-conjugate stress is its gradient - the (first Piola-Kirchhoff) nominal stress $\mathbf P=\partial W/\partial\mathbf F$, or equivalently the Cauchy stress $\boldsymbol\sigma=J^{-1}\mathbf P\mathbf F^{\!\top}$. The archetype is the neo-Hookean solid, $$W=\tfrac{\mu}{2}\big(\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf F^{\!\top}\mathbf F)-3\big)-\mu\ln J+\tfrac{\lambda}{2}(\ln J)^2,$$ whose two constants $\mu,\lambda$ are the same Lamé constants as the linear law, now governing arbitrarily large strain.
Proposition 7.1 (hyperelasticity reduces to linear elasticity at small strain). Expanding a hyperelastic law about the undeformed state $\mathbf F=\mathbf I$ recovers linear elasticity to first order: for the neo-Hookean solid, $$\boxed{\;\boldsymbol\sigma=\lambda(\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol\varepsilon)\mathbf I+2\mu\boldsymbol\varepsilon+O(\lVert\nabla\mathbf u\rVert^2),\;}$$ so the linear constants are the small-strain limit of the hyperelastic ones, and the two theories agree exactly where strains are small.
Write $\mathbf F=\mathbf I+\mathbf H$, $\mathbf H=\nabla\mathbf u$ small. Then $\mathbf F^{\!\top}\mathbf F=\mathbf I+2\boldsymbol\varepsilon+O(\mathbf H^2)$ with $\boldsymbol\varepsilon=\tfrac12(\mathbf H+\mathbf H^{\!\top})$, and $J=\det\mathbf F=1+\operatorname{tr}\mathbf H+O(\mathbf H^2)$, so $\ln J=\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol\varepsilon+O(\mathbf H^2)$. The nominal stress of the neo-Hookean energy is $\mathbf P=\mu(\mathbf F-\mathbf F^{-\top})+\lambda\ln J\,\mathbf F^{-\top}$; substituting $\mathbf F=\mathbf I+\mathbf H$ and $\mathbf F^{-\top}=\mathbf I-\mathbf H^{\!\top}+O(\mathbf H^2)$ gives $\mathbf P=\mu(\mathbf H+\mathbf H^{\!\top})+\lambda(\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol\varepsilon)\mathbf I+O(\mathbf H^2)=2\mu\boldsymbol\varepsilon+\lambda(\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol\varepsilon)\mathbf I+O(\mathbf H^2)$. To this order $\boldsymbol\sigma=\mathbf P$, the isotropic linear law of Definition 3.1. ∎

Figure 8 contrasts the laws over a $10\%$ stretch. The linear and neo-Hookean curves rise together and stay close - a modest nonlinearity. The fibre-reinforced curve is different in kind: it is nearly flat at first (the toe region, where crimped collagen straightens at almost no stress - exactly the J-shaped law Module 6 derived) and then climbs steeply once the fibres are taut, crossing above the linear line. Modelling that toe needs a fibre strain energy that grows exponentially with fibre stretch (a term like $W_{\rm fib}\propto e^{k(\varepsilon_a)}-1$, combining the fibre reinforcement of Section 6 with the finite strain of this section). The toe is not a curiosity: it is why tendons are compliant shock-absorbers at low load and stiff force-transmitters at high load, the dual role Module 6 built a whole spring model around. With anisotropy and nonlinearity in hand, one behaviour remains - time dependence - which is where the fluid in tissue enters.

8. Viscoelasticity and poroelasticity

An elastic material, linear or hyperelastic, responds to load instantly and stores all the work as recoverable energy. Real tissue does neither: hold a tendon at a fixed stretch and its stress slowly falls; press cartilage and it keeps sinking for minutes. This time dependence has two physical origins, and the continuum captures both.

Definition 8.1 (linear viscoelasticity). A viscoelastic material's stress depends on the whole history of strain, not its current value. For small strains this is the hereditary integral $$\boxed{\;\sigma(t)=\int_{-\infty}^{t} G(t-s)\,\frac{d\varepsilon}{ds}\,ds,\;}$$ with $G(t)$ the relaxation modulus - the stress remaining a time $t$ after a unit step of strain. $G$ falls from an instantaneous value $G(0)=G_0$ to an equilibrium $G(\infty)=G_\infty$; the standard-linear-solid model (a spring in parallel with a spring-and-dashpot, Module 6) gives $G(t)=G_\infty+(G_0-G_\infty)e^{-t/\tau}$ with relaxation time $\tau$.

The second origin is fluid. Cartilage and, on longer scales, bone are biphasic: a porous solid matrix saturated with interstitial fluid. Load is shared between a stress in the solid and a pressure in the fluid, and the fluid can only move by seeping through the matrix (Darcy's law), which takes time.

Definition 8.2 (biphasic / poroelastic material). In a biphasic material the total stress splits into a fluid pressure and a solid (effective) stress, $$\boxed{\;\boldsymbol\sigma=-p\,\mathbf I+\boldsymbol\sigma_{\rm s},\;}$$ where $p$ is the interstitial fluid pressure and $\boldsymbol\sigma_{\rm s}$ the stress carried by the solid matrix. Combining this split with Darcy flow and mass conservation yields a consolidation (diffusion) equation for the matrix displacement $u$, $\partial u/\partial t=c_v\,\partial^2u/\partial z^2$, with consolidation coefficient $c_v=H_A k$ ($H_A$ the aggregate modulus, $k$ the permeability) - the very equation Module 4 derived for cartilage.
Proposition 8.1 (instantaneous stiffness exceeds equilibrium stiffness). A biphasic tissue is stiffer the faster it is loaded: its instantaneous modulus $G_0$ (fluid pressurised, carrying the load) exceeds its equilibrium modulus $G_\infty$ (fluid drained, matrix alone), and the relaxation modulus $G(t)$ decreases monotonically between them, $$\boxed{\;G_0\ge G(t)\ge G_\infty\ge0,\qquad G'(t)\le0.\;}$$
At the instant load is applied there is no time for fluid to move; the fluid is nearly incompressible, so it pressurises and carries a large share of the load, and the tissue resists with its full instantaneous stiffness $G_0$. As $t\to\infty$ the fluid pressure gradient drives seepage until $p\to$ its drained value; the fluid no longer supports load, which now rests entirely on the matrix, giving the smaller equilibrium stiffness $G_\infty$. For the standard-linear-solid form $G(t)=G_\infty+(G_0-G_\infty)e^{-t/\tau}$, $G'(t)=-\tfrac1\tau(G_0-G_\infty)e^{-t/\tau}\le0$ since $G_0\ge G_\infty$, so $G$ decreases monotonically from $G_0$ to $G_\infty$; both bounds are non-negative by material stability (Proposition 3.1). The same monotone decay describes the viscoelastic and the biphasic tissue - two mechanisms, one continuum signature. ∎

Figure 9 shows both faces of time dependence: the relaxing stress of a viscoelastic tendon and the draining load-partition of biphasic cartilage. They are not merely analogous - a biphasic tissue is viscoelastic, its relaxation time $\tau\sim L^2/c_v$ set by how long fluid takes to seep a distance $L$ (Module 4's diffusion timescale). This is why cartilage feels rubbery under a quick tap and yielding under a sustained press, and why impact loading - too fast for the fluid to escape - is carried safely by pressure while a slow crushing load falls on the matrix and damages it. The continuum with a relaxation modulus, or a biphasic split, captures the whole range.

9. The three tissues as continua, and what the models miss

The framework now closes the loop with the tissues the course began from. Each earlier one-dimensional model is a projection of a specific continuum law, and naming that law says exactly what the reduction kept and dropped.

Two threads unify the table. Every model is a strain-energy function plus a balance law: change the energy (isotropic, fibre-reinforced, hyperelastic, biphasic) and one framework spans bone to muscle. And every earlier module is recovered as a limit - the beam is the continuum under a slender-body assumption, the spring is the fibre law in one dimension, the biphasic column is the consolidation equation in one dimension. The continuum is not a different theory; it is the parent of the ones already used.

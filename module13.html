0. Motivation - the day as applied mechanics

An ordinary morning is a sequence of mechanical problems the body solves without a thought. You rise from bed and from a chair; you carry a full kettle; you climb the stairs and come back down; you lift a box, turn a corner, catch your balance on a rug that slips, open a stubborn jar, push a heavy door, and walk up the hill to the bus. Each of these is a movement this course has already given the tools to analyze - and the point of this module is to use them, turning the machinery of Modules 1-12 on the concrete acts of daily life (Fig. 1) and reading off the forces, torques, powers, and stresses the body actually meets.

The value of doing this is threefold. First, it makes the abstractions concrete: the low-back torque of Module 1 becomes the spinal compression that decides whether lifting a box is safe; the inverted pendulum of Modules 7-8 becomes the chair rise an aging parent finds hard; the friction cone of Module 11 becomes the grip that opens a jar. Second, it reveals margins: how close each everyday act runs to the limits of strength, balance, or tissue tolerance - and therefore why a small loss of capacity turns an easy task into an impossible or dangerous one. Third, it names failure modes: for each case, exactly which parameter (strength, range of motion, reaction time, balance margin, tissue strength) has to fall, and by how much, before the movement fails - which is the mechanical basis for aging, injury, and the training that pushes the margins back out.

This module is deliberately not a new theory but a disciplined application. Each case runs the same short pipeline - idealize, draw the free-body diagram, write the governing equation, put in human numbers, simulate, and find the failure mode - a template we set up once in Section 1 and then apply to nine everyday movements. The recurring lesson is how often ordinary tasks run near a limit: lifting a modest box loads the spine past its recommended tolerance, rising from a low chair demands nearly all an older adult's knee strength, and catching a stumble spends most of the balance budget of Module 10. Daily life is not comfortably inside the body's envelope; it lives at the edge, which is why the edge moving inward - with age or injury - is felt so sharply.

The thesis of this module. Every ordinary movement is a solvable mechanics problem, and solving it exposes how near the body runs to its limits. A single template - idealize to links and joints, balance moments or momenta, estimate with human numbers, simulate, and locate the failure mode - turns rising, climbing, lifting, turning, gripping, and walking a slope into quantitative statements about joint torque, spinal load, balance margin, and metabolic power. The margins are thin, and reading them is the mechanics of independence: what keeps a person able to rise, carry, and catch themselves is a strength, range, and balance reserve that this module measures case by case.

What each case draws on: rising and stairs use the joint torques and power of Modules 1, 5, and 8; lifting uses the low-back moment of Module 1 and the tissue tolerance of Modules 2 and 16; turning and stumble recovery use the balance margin of Module 10; jar and door use the grip and endpoint force of Module 11; and every case is coordinated by the controller of Module 12 and powered by the muscles of Module 5.

1. The case-study method

Before the cases, we fix the template that unifies them. It is a five-step reduction from a real movement to a number, and every later section is an instance of it.

Definition 1 (the case-study pipeline). To analyze a daily movement: (1) Idealize - replace the body with the minimal set of rigid links, joints, and point masses that carries the essential mechanics. (2) Free-body diagram - identify every external force (gravity, ground reaction, contact, load) and the internal joint torques. (3) Govern - write the balance law: static moment balance $\sum M=0$ for a slow (quasi-static) task, or the momentum/energy law for a fast (dynamic) one. (4) Estimate - insert human-scale masses, lengths, and moment arms and read off the number. (5) Test - simulate if the task is dynamic, and locate the failure mode: which parameter, reduced by how much, makes the movement fail.

Two governing laws recur, and choosing between them is the first judgement each case demands (Fig. 2). When the movement is slow enough that accelerations are negligible - holding a lift, pressing a door - the body is in quasi-static equilibrium and the relevant law is moment balance about a joint.

Quasi-static moment balance. About any joint, the muscle torque equals the sum of external moments: $$\tau_{\rm muscle}=F_{\rm muscle}\,d_{\rm muscle}=\sum_i W_i\,d_i,$$ where each external weight $W_i$ acts at horizontal moment arm $d_i$ from the joint and the muscle acts at its (usually short) moment arm $d_{\rm muscle}$. The short muscle arm is why internal forces dwarf external loads (the low back of Section 4).

When the movement is fast - rising briskly, climbing at cadence, catching a stumble - accelerations matter and the relevant laws are work-energy and impulse-momentum: the work done by joint torques changes the body's mechanical energy, and the impulse of the ground reaction changes its momentum.

Work, power, and impulse. A joint torque $\tau$ turning at angular velocity $\omega$ delivers power $P=\tau\,\omega$ and, over an angle, work $U=\int\tau\,d\theta$; positive work is concentric (lifting the body), negative is eccentric (braking it, as in stair descent). A ground-reaction impulse $\int\mathbf F\,dt$ equals the change in the body's momentum $m\,\Delta\mathbf v$ (the turn and stumble of Section 5).

The third recurring quantity is a margin: the ratio of what a task demands to what the body can supply. A strength margin is the demanded joint torque over the maximum voluntary torque; a balance margin is the extrapolated centre of mass's distance inside the base of support (Module 10); a tissue margin is the applied stress over the tissue's tolerance (Modules 2, 16). A movement is possible when every margin exceeds one and safe with room to spare; aging and injury shrink the numerators' headroom, and the cases below measure by how much. With the template fixed, we turn it on the first movement of the day.

2. Rising from a chair

Standing up from a chair is the movement that most often separates independent living from assisted care, because it runs closest to the limit of a common capacity - knee extensor strength. Watch it and it has two phases: a flexion-momentum phase, where the trunk pitches forward to bring the centre of mass over the feet and build forward velocity, and an extension phase, where the legs straighten to lift the body. The mechanically critical instant is seat-off, when the thighs leave the seat and the entire body weight, until then partly supported by the chair, passes through the feet.

Idealize the body at seat-off as two segments: the shanks, fixed at the ankles, and everything above the knees - thighs, pelvis, trunk, head, arms, the HAT (head, arms, trunk) plus thigh mass $m_{\rm up}\approx0.8\,m$ - pivoting about the knees. Its centre of mass sits a horizontal distance $d$ ahead of the knee joint, and gravity's moment there must be balanced by the knee extensors (the quadriceps, through the patellar tendon of Module 6).

Proposition 2.1 (peak knee torque at seat-off). In the quasi-static limit, the knee extensor torque required at seat-off is $$\boxed{\;\tau_{\rm knee}=m_{\rm up}\,g\,d,\;}$$ where $m_{\rm up}$ is the body mass above the knees and $d$ the horizontal distance from the knee to that mass's centre of mass. For $m_{\rm up}=0.8\times70=56\ \mathrm{kg}$, $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$, $d=0.20\ \mathrm{m}$, this is $\tau_{\rm knee}\approx110\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, or $1.57\ \mathrm{N\,m/kg}$ of body mass.
Take moments about the knee joint for the upper body treated as a rigid segment pivoting there (the shanks are fixed, so the knee is the working joint). The only external moment is gravity acting at the upper body's centre of mass, at horizontal distance $d$ from the knee, giving a flexing moment $m_{\rm up}g\,d$. In the quasi-static limit (slow rise, negligible angular acceleration) the net moment is zero, so the knee extensors must supply an equal and opposite extensor moment $\tau_{\rm knee}=m_{\rm up}g\,d$. Substituting the values gives $56\times9.81\times0.20\approx110\ \mathrm{N\,m}$. ∎

That $1.57\ \mathrm{N\,m/kg}$ (Fig. 3) is most of what a healthy adult can produce - peak voluntary knee extensor torque is about $2$-$3\ \mathrm{N\,m/kg}$ in the young and falls toward $1$-$1.5\ \mathrm{N\,m/kg}$ in the old. The strength margin for a chair rise is therefore small, and two everyday facts follow directly from the formula. A lower or softer chair increases $d$ (the knee starts more flexed and further behind the COM), raising the required torque - the reason a low sofa is far harder to leave than a tall stool. And the momentum strategy is not a stylistic quirk but a torque-reducing necessity: pitching the trunk forward first gives the COM an upward-forward velocity at seat-off, so part of the body's rise is already underway and the legs need supply only a fraction of the full quasi-static torque. When even that is not enough, people recruit the arms - pushing on the armrests adds an external upward force at the hands, subtracting directly from the knee's share.

Failure mode. Sit-to-stand fails when the demanded torque exceeds the available: a strength margin below one. Aging lowers the numerator's headroom (sarcopenia, Module 14) while low chairs raise the demand, so the same person who rises easily at 40 may, at 80, be unable to leave a low armchair without pushing off - and the clinical five-times-sit-to-stand test times exactly this margin. The mechanical fixes all target the formula: a higher, firmer chair (smaller $d$), armrests (an added external force), and strength training (a larger available torque).

3. Stairs: ascent and descent

Stairs make gravity's bookkeeping vivid because every step changes the body's height by a fixed amount. Ascending, the legs do positive work to raise the centre of mass; descending, they do negative work to lower it under control. The two are not mirror images - one is powered, the other braked - and the asymmetry is why descent, not ascent, is the greater fall risk.

Proposition 3.1 (work and power on stairs). Raising the body by one step of height $h$ requires mechanical work against gravity $$\boxed{\;U_{\rm step}=m\,g\,h,\qquad P=m\,g\,h\,f=m\,g\,v_{\rm vert},\;}$$ where $f$ is the step frequency and $v_{\rm vert}=hf$ the average vertical speed. For $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, $h=0.17\ \mathrm{m}$: $U_{\rm step}\approx117\ \mathrm{J}$ per step; at $f=1.8\ \mathrm{steps/s}$ ($v_{\rm vert}\approx0.31\ \mathrm{m/s}$), $P\approx210\ \mathrm{W}$ of gravitational power.
By the work-energy theorem, the mechanical work done against gravity to raise the centre of mass by $h$ (with negligible net change in kinetic energy step to step, since the body returns to the same speed each cycle) equals the gain in gravitational potential energy, $U_{\rm step}=mgh$. Power is work per unit time; at step frequency $f$ the body climbs $f$ steps per second, so $P=U_{\rm step}f=mghf$, and since the vertical speed is $v_{\rm vert}=hf$, $P=mg\,v_{\rm vert}$. Substituting gives $70\times9.81\times0.17\approx117\ \mathrm{J}$ and $117\times1.8\approx210\ \mathrm{W}$. ∎

This gravitational power (Fig. 4) is the minimum mechanical output; the muscles supply more, because they also swing the legs and are less than perfectly efficient (Module 8's metabolic cost). Inverse dynamics (Module 8) distributes the work across the joints: on ascent the hip and knee extensors do most of the positive work at push-off, the ankle contributing a final propulsive burst. The $\approx210\ \mathrm{W}$ figure - sustained - is why a long staircase is aerobically taxing: it is comparable to a brisk cycling effort, delivered by the legs alone.

Descent inverts the sign. To lower the body one step, the legs do negative work $-mgh$: the knee extensors contract while lengthening (eccentric action, Module 5), absorbing the potential energy the body gives up rather than adding to it. This is metabolically cheaper than ascent but mechanically more demanding of control - an eccentric brake must dissipate $117\ \mathrm{J}$ per step smoothly, and if the knee gives way the energy converts to an impact instead. That is exactly why descent dominates stair-fall statistics and why knee pain (osteoarthritis, Module 4) is felt most on the way down: eccentric loading concentrates high contact stress on the patellofemoral joint at a flexed knee.

Failure mode. Ascent fails on power - insufficient extensor strength or aerobic capacity to sustain $mgv_{\rm vert}$ - and people slow their cadence to keep within it. Descent fails on control - insufficient eccentric strength or too slow a reflex to arrest a buckling knee - and the failure is a fall, not merely a stop. The training implications differ: ascent wants strength and endurance, descent wants eccentric control and quadriceps capacity, which is why rehabilitation after knee injury drills the controlled step-down specifically.

4. Lifting and carrying: the low back

Lifting a box off the floor is the movement that most reliably injures, and the reason is a lever with a cruel ratio. The muscles that extend the trunk - the erector spinae, running just behind the spine - act at a moment arm of only about $5\ \mathrm{cm}$ from the intervertebral discs, while the load in the hands and the weight of the trunk itself act at moment arms several times larger. To balance a modest external moment, the back muscles must pull with a large force, and that force presses the vertebrae together: the spine pays for the lever disadvantage in compression.

Idealize the lifter, bent forward, as a lever pivoting at the L5/S1 disc (the lumbosacral junction, the most loaded level). Three forces create moments about it: the upper-body weight $W_{\rm tr}$ at horizontal moment arm $d_{\rm tr}$, the load $W_L$ in the hands at moment arm $d_L$, and the erector spinae force $F_m$ at the short arm $d_m$. Moment balance fixes $F_m$; force balance then gives the disc compression.

Proposition 4.1 (spinal compression in a lift). Balancing moments about L5/S1, the erector spinae force and the resulting disc compression are $$\boxed{\;F_m=\frac{W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_L d_L}{d_m},\qquad F_{\rm comp}\approx F_m+(W_{\rm tr}+W_L)\cos\phi,\;}$$ with $\phi$ the trunk's inclination from vertical. For a $20\ \mathrm{kg}$ load at $d_L=0.40\ \mathrm{m}$, trunk mass $W_{\rm tr}=0.5mg$ at $d_{\rm tr}=0.30\ \mathrm{m}$, and $d_m=0.05\ \mathrm{m}$: $F_m\approx3630\ \mathrm{N}$ and $F_{\rm comp}\approx4200\ \mathrm{N}$ (a conservative upper bound - see the proof) - above the NIOSH (US National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health) action limit of $3400\ \mathrm{N}$.
Take moments about the L5/S1 disc. The external flexing moments are $W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}$ (trunk weight) and $W_L d_L$ (load); the erector spinae supplies the balancing extensor moment $F_m d_m$. Quasi-static balance $\sum M=0$ gives $F_m d_m=W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_L d_L$, hence $F_m=(W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_L d_L)/d_m$. For the compression, resolve forces along the spine's long axis: the muscle force $F_m$ acts nearly parallel to the spine and the segment plus load weights contribute their axial component $(W_{\rm tr}+W_L)\cos\phi$, so the disc reaction (compression) is $F_{\rm comp}=F_m+(W_{\rm tr}+W_L)\cos\phi$. Substituting $W_{\rm tr}=0.5\times70\times9.81=343\ \mathrm{N}$, $W_L=196\ \mathrm{N}$: $F_m=(343\times0.30+196\times0.40)/0.05=(103+78)/0.05\approx3630\ \mathrm{N}$, and, taking the axial weight term at its upper bound $\cos\phi\le1$, $F_{\rm comp}\le3630+(343+196)\approx4200\ \mathrm{N}$; at a deep forward lean $\phi\to90^\circ$ this term shrinks toward zero (the weight becomes shear rather than compression), so the true compression lies between $F_m\approx3630\ \mathrm{N}$ and this bound - either way above the NIOSH limit. ∎

Four thousand newtons (Fig. 5) is more than five times body weight, pressed through a disc, to lift a load a fifth of body weight - the fivefold amplification is entirely the $d_L/d_m$ lever ratio. Two everyday rules fall straight out of the formula. Keep the load close: $F_m\propto d_L$, so halving the horizontal distance from the spine to the load halves the muscle force and nearly halves the compression - the single most effective thing a lifter can do. Squat, don't stoop: a squat lift keeps the trunk more upright (smaller $d_{\rm tr}$) and lets the load stay closer (smaller $d_L$), while a stoop lift with straight legs maximizes both moment arms; our numbers give $\approx3600\ \mathrm{N}$ squatting versus $\approx4800\ \mathrm{N}$ stooping the same load, the difference between borderline and clearly hazardous.

Carrying shifts the geometry sideways. A bag in one hand adds a load at a lateral moment arm about the spine, which the contralateral trunk side-flexors must balance - so a one-sided carry both compresses and side-bends the spine, and people instinctively lean away from the bag to bring the combined centre of mass back over the pelvis, trading a lateral moment for a small compression increase. Splitting the load into two bags, one per hand, cancels the lateral moments and is mechanically far kinder.

Failure mode. Lifting fails when disc compression (or shear) exceeds tissue tolerance: acute failure is disc herniation or, in osteoporotic bone (Module 2), a vertebral compression fracture; chronic overload is the mechanics behind much low-back pain. Tolerance falls with age as discs dehydrate and bone thins, while the demand is set by load, distance, and posture - which is why safe-lifting guidance is entirely about shrinking $d_L$ and $d_{\rm tr}$, and why a young back tolerating a careless stoop lift is running the same $4200\ \mathrm{N}$ that fractures an old one.

5. Turning and catching a stumble

Straight-line walking (Module 8) is only part of getting about; real locomotion is a sequence of turns, and turns - together with the stumbles that interrupt them - are where the balance machinery of Module 10 is spent hardest. Both are momentum problems: a turn redirects the body's momentum, a stumble recovery arrests an unplanned one, and both are governed by impulse.

To turn while walking, the horizontal velocity must change direction. Redirecting a speed $v$ through a turn angle $\beta$ changes the velocity vector even at constant speed, and that change of momentum must be delivered as a sideways impulse by the ground.

Proposition 5.1 (impulse to turn). Redirecting the horizontal velocity through angle $\beta$ at constant speed $v$ requires a ground-reaction impulse of magnitude $$\boxed{\;J=m\,|\Delta\mathbf v|=2\,m\,v\,\sin(\beta/2),\;}$$ directed toward the inside of the turn. For $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, $v=1.3\ \mathrm{m/s}$, $\beta=60^\circ$: $J\approx91\ \mathrm{N\,s}$.
The horizontal momentum before the turn is $m\mathbf v_1$ and after is $m\mathbf v_2$, with $|\mathbf v_1|=|\mathbf v_2|=v$ and the angle between them $\beta$. By the impulse-momentum theorem the ground-reaction impulse equals the momentum change, $J=m|\mathbf v_2-\mathbf v_1|$. For two equal-length vectors separated by angle $\beta$, $|\mathbf v_2-\mathbf v_1|=2v\sin(\beta/2)$ (the base of the isosceles triangle of sides $v$). Hence $J=2mv\sin(\beta/2)$, directed along $\mathbf v_2-\mathbf v_1$, i.e. toward the inside of the turn. Substituting, $2\times70\times1.3\times\sin30^\circ=91\ \mathrm{N\,s}$. ∎

That impulse (Fig. 6) is delivered over a step, and while it is being delivered the body's extrapolated centre of mass (Module 10) swings - crucially - toward the inside of the turn and must be kept over a base of support that is itself being repositioned by the stepping foot. A turn is thus a coupled problem: generate the lateral impulse and keep the XcoM captured, all while the base is in motion. This is why turning is a disproportionate fall trigger in the elderly - it demands lateral balance control at the moment the base is least stable - and why "turning steps" are a specific focus of balance rehabilitation.

A stumble is the uninvited version of the same physics. A trip decelerates the swinging foot and pitches the trunk forward, throwing the XcoM toward and past the toe - exactly Module 10's condition for a mandatory change of support. Recovery is a reactive step that must plant a new base ahead of the racing XcoM, and its required length is set by the capturability result of Module 10.

Stumble recovery (imported from Module 10). After a trip, a fixed-support (ankle/hip) response suffices only while the extrapolated COM stays within the foot; once $\xi=x_{\rm com}+\dot x_{\rm com}/\omega_0$ (with $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$ the pendulum rate and $\ell$ the COM height; Module 10) passes the toe, a recovery step of length at least the XcoM's forward excursion is mandatory. Early in swing the body uses an elevating strategy (lift the tripped foot over the obstacle and reach forward); late in swing a lowering strategy (plant the foot down fast and take a compensatory step with the other leg). The choice is timing-critical, and a delay of tens of milliseconds - the reflex slowing of Module 10 and aging - is the difference between a caught stumble and a fall.

Failure mode. Turning fails when lateral balance control cannot keep the XcoM captured over the moving base - a lateral balance-margin shortfall - and stumble recovery fails when the reactive step is too short, too slow, or too weak to plant ahead of the XcoM. Both degrade with the same Module 10 parameters (reflex delay, step speed and length, torque capacity), which is why turning and tripping, not straight walking, dominate real-world falls.

6. Opening a jar, pushing a door

The hand's daily tasks are torque problems delivered through the manipulator of Module 11. Two are archetypal: unscrewing a jar lid, limited by grip friction, and pushing a door, limited by reach geometry. Both convert a hand action into a torque about an axis, and both reveal why the everyday design of lids and handles is really applied mechanics.

A jar lid is held by friction (and often a vacuum seal) that resists rotation with some torque $\tau_{\rm resist}$. To open it the hand grips the lid rim (radius $r$) and twists; friction between skin and lid converts the inward grip force $F_{\rm grip}$ into a tangential force $\mu F_{\rm grip}$ around the rim, and that tangential force at radius $r$ produces the opening torque.

Proposition 6.1 (grip-limited jar torque). A hand gripping a lid of radius $r$ with total effective normal force $F_{\rm grip}$ and skin-lid friction $\mu$ can apply an opening torque up to $$\boxed{\;\tau_{\rm open}=\mu\,F_{\rm grip}\,r,\;}$$ so opening a lid that resists with $\tau_{\rm resist}$ requires $F_{\rm grip}\ge\tau_{\rm resist}/(\mu r)$. For $\tau_{\rm resist}=2\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, $r=0.035\ \mathrm{m}$, $\mu=0.7$: a tangential force of $\approx57\ \mathrm{N}$ around the rim, needing a grip force of $\approx82\ \mathrm{N}$.
The friction available at the grip is $F_t\le\mu F_{\rm grip}$ (Coulomb's law, Module 11), acting tangent to the lid rim. A tangential force $F_t$ at radius $r$ exerts a torque $\tau=F_t\,r$ about the lid axis; maximizing $F_t$ gives $\tau_{\rm open}=\mu F_{\rm grip}r$. Opening requires $\tau_{\rm open}\ge\tau_{\rm resist}$, i.e. $\mu F_{\rm grip}r\ge\tau_{\rm resist}$, so $F_{\rm grip}\ge\tau_{\rm resist}/(\mu r)=2/(0.7\times0.035)\approx82\ \mathrm{N}$; the tangential force is $\mu F_{\rm grip}\approx57\ \mathrm{N}$, equivalently $\tau_{\rm resist}/r$. ∎

The formula names every jar-opening aid (Fig. 7). A rubber pad or a dry hand raises $\mu$; a wider lid or a jar wrench raises $r$; both cut the grip force needed in inverse proportion. The task is grip-limited, and grip strength is among the first capacities to fall with age and the worst hit by hand arthritis - so a stuck jar is a classic threshold task, trivial at $150\ \mathrm{N}$ of grip and impossible at $60\ \mathrm{N}$, which is why grip strength is a validated proxy for whole-body frailty.

Pushing a door is the complementary case - not grip-limited but geometry-limited. A door resists opening with a torque about its hinge (a latch, a seal, a self-closer), and the hand's force $F_{\rm hand}$ at distance $d$ from the hinge supplies the opening torque $F_{\rm hand}d$; balance requires $F_{\rm hand}=\tau_{\rm door}/d$ - a one-line lever balance beside the jar's friction subtlety, needing no separate proposition. For a self-closer of $\tau_{\rm door}\approx20\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ and a handle at $d=0.8\ \mathrm{m}$, a modest $\approx25\ \mathrm{N}$ suffices - which is exactly why handles are placed far from the hinge, and why a handle near the hinge (or a door pushed near its edge) feels immovable. The arm delivers $F_{\rm hand}$ through the endpoint-force relation $\boldsymbol\tau=J^{\mathsf T}\mathbf F$ of Module 11, bracing best with a near-straight arm along the push - the force-ellipsoid lesson made domestic.

7. Walking up and down a slope

A slope tilts gravity's bookkeeping. On level ground the net work against gravity over a stride is zero (the COM returns to its height); on a grade, every stride nets a height change, and the component of gravity along the path either resists progress (uphill) or drives it (downhill). The mechanics is a one-line resolution of the weight vector, but its consequences - why uphill is aerobically hard and downhill is hard on the knees - are opposite.

Proposition 7.1 (power on a slope). Walking at speed $v$ along a slope of inclination $\theta$, the extra mechanical power against gravity beyond level walking is $$\boxed{\;P_{\rm slope}=m\,g\,v\,\sin\theta,\;}$$ positive (supplied by the muscles) uphill and negative (absorbed, braked) downhill. For $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, $v=1.0\ \mathrm{m/s}$: $P_{\rm slope}\approx60,\,119,\,178\ \mathrm{W}$ at $\theta=5^\circ,10^\circ,15^\circ$ ($9\%,18\%,27\%$ grade).
Resolve the weight $mg$ into components parallel and perpendicular to the slope. The parallel component is $mg\sin\theta$, directed down the slope. Walking up the slope at speed $v$ moves the body against this component at rate $v$, so the power the muscles must add is force times velocity, $P_{\rm slope}=mg\sin\theta\cdot v$. Equivalently, the vertical speed is $v\sin\theta$ and the gravitational power is $mg\times v\sin\theta$, the same result. Downhill the parallel component aids motion, so the muscles must absorb $mg\sin\theta\cdot v$ eccentrically to hold speed constant. Substituting $\theta=10^\circ$: $70\times9.81\times1.0\times\sin10^\circ\approx119\ \mathrm{W}$. ∎

Uphill (Fig. 8), the extra $60$-$180\ \mathrm{W}$ is added on top of level walking's cost, which is why a moderate hill quickly raises heart rate and why people slow down to hold power within their aerobic limit - the same power ceiling that caps stair ascent (Section 3). Downhill flips the demand from supply to absorb: the legs must dissipate $mg\sin\theta\,v$ as eccentric muscle work step after step, concentrating high forces on the quadriceps and the patellofemoral joint at a flexed knee - the reason a long descent leaves the thighs sore and aggravates knee osteoarthritis (Module 4) far more than the climb did. Steep enough, and eccentric braking cannot keep up: the walker must switchback, sidestep, or descend slowly, converting an absorption problem back into a controllable one.

Failure mode. Uphill fails on aerobic/strength power - the same ceiling as stairs; downhill fails on eccentric control and joint tolerance, the same as stair descent. Slopes are simply stairs with the risers spread out, and they inherit both failure modes.

8. Aging, injury, and training across the cases

Read the nine cases together and a small set of parameters recurs as the deciding margin in every one. This is the module's unifying payoff: daily independence is not a single capacity but a handful of reserves - strength, range of motion, balance margin, reaction time, and tissue tolerance - each of which some ordinary task drives to its limit, and each of which aging and injury erode. Three cross-cutting lessons emerge (Fig. 9). The reserves are shared: knee extensor strength decides the chair, the stairs, and the downhill; balance margin decides the turn, the stumble, and the slope - so a single deficit (weak quadriceps, slow reflexes) shows up across many tasks at once, which is why frailty is felt as a general loss of capability, not a list of separate failures. The margins are thin to begin with: a healthy chair rise already uses most of available knee torque and a careless lift already exceeds the recommended spinal load, so even a modest decline crosses a threshold. Training is specific and mechanical: because each case's binding margin is known, the effective intervention is the one that raises that exact quantity - leg strength for rising, eccentric control for descending, grip for jars, reactive stepping for stumbles - which is the mechanical rationale for targeted, task-specific exercise over generic activity. Module 14 develops the aging changes; here the point is that each is read through a margin some daily movement was already spending.

9. Computational labs

Four labs put numbers under the cases: the chair-rise torque and how momentum lowers it, stair power against a strength ceiling, spinal compression across lifting postures, and the recovery step a stumble demands. Each states its physical question, model, parameters, and equations; every plotted number was produced by the code shown.

Lab A - the chair rise: torque and the momentum strategy

Physical question. How does the required knee torque depend on chair height, and how much does trunk-flexion momentum lower the peak? Model. Proposition 2.1 for the quasi-static torque; a point-mass COM raised by $H$ in time $T$ for the dynamic version, whose peak vertical ground reaction sets the extra torque. Parameters. $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, $m_{\rm up}=0.8m$, knee-to-COM distance $d$ from $0.15$ to $0.28\ \mathrm{m}$ (higher to lower chair); rise height $H=0.40\ \mathrm{m}$, $T=1.2\ \mathrm{s}$. Equations. $\tau=m_{\rm up}gd$; dynamic vGRF $\approx m(g+a)$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

g, m = 9.81, 70.0
m_up = 0.8*m
d = np.linspace(0.15, 0.28, 40)          # knee-to-COM distance (m)
tau = m_up*g*d                            # quasi-static knee torque
tau_max = 1.5*m                           # available ~1.5 N m/kg (older adult)

print("torque at d=0.20 m: %.0f N m (%.2f N m/kg)"
      % (m_up*g*0.20, m_up*g*0.20/m))
plt.plot(d, tau, label='required')
plt.axhline(tau_max, ls='--', label='available (older)')
plt.xlabel('knee-to-COM distance d (m)')
plt.ylabel('knee torque (N m)')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Interpretation. The required knee torque rises linearly with $d$: at a high firm chair ($d=0.15\ \mathrm{m}$) it is $\approx82\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, at a low soft one ($d=0.28\ \mathrm{m}$) $\approx154\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, crossing an older adult's $\approx105\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ ceiling near $d=0.19\ \mathrm{m}$ - so the same person can rise from the tall chair but not the low one. Sensitivity. Torque is linear in $d$ and in $m_{\rm up}$, so raising the seat or losing weight both help directly. Failure mode. When required exceeds available the rise is impossible without arms or momentum. Extension. Add the dynamic momentum term: a brisk rise ($T=0.9\ \mathrm{s}$) needs peak vGRF $\approx1.3\ \mathrm{BW}$ but front-loads it into the momentum phase, lowering the extension-phase torque - quantify the trade.

Lab B - stair power against a ceiling

Physical question. How does stair-climbing power scale with cadence, and where does it meet an aerobic/strength ceiling? Model. Proposition 3.1. Parameters. $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, $h=0.17\ \mathrm{m}$, cadence $0.5$-$2.5\ \mathrm{steps/s}$; ceiling $\approx250\ \mathrm{W}$ of sustained leg power. Equations. $P=mgh\,f$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

g, m, h = 9.81, 70.0, 0.17
f = np.linspace(0.5, 2.5, 40)             # steps per second
P = m*g*h*f                                # gravitational power
ceiling = 250.0

f_max = ceiling/(m*g*h)
print("work/step %.0f J; max sustainable cadence %.2f steps/s"
      % (m*g*h, f_max))
plt.plot(f, P)
plt.axhline(ceiling, ls='--')
plt.xlabel('cadence (steps/s)')
plt.ylabel('gravity power (W)')
plt.show()

Interpretation. Power is linear in cadence: $117\ \mathrm{J}$ per step means the $250\ \mathrm{W}$ ceiling is reached at $\approx2.1\ \mathrm{steps/s}$, above which the climb cannot be sustained and cadence must drop. Sensitivity. Power scales with body mass and step height, so a heavier person or a steeper stair hits the ceiling at lower cadence. Failure mode. Exceeding the ceiling forces a slow-down (ascent) - the benign failure; the dangerous one is descent, where the same energy must be absorbed. Extension. Split the $117\ \mathrm{J}$ across hip, knee, and ankle using Module 8's inverse-dynamics fractions and find which joint saturates first.

Lab C - the safe-lift envelope

Physical question. How does spinal compression vary from a squat to a stoop, and where does it cross the safety limit? Model. Proposition 4.1. Parameters. trunk $0.5m$ at $d_{\rm tr}=0.30\ \mathrm{m}$, muscle arm $d_m=0.05\ \mathrm{m}$; load $10$-$25\ \mathrm{kg}$; load distance $d_L$ from $0.20$ (squat) to $0.55\ \mathrm{m}$ (stoop). Equations. $F_{\rm comp}=(W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_L d_L)/d_m+(W_{\rm tr}+W_L)$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

g, m = 9.81, 70.0
W_tr, d_tr, d_m = 0.5*m*g, 0.30, 0.05
dL = np.linspace(0.22, 0.55, 40)          # load distance (m)
NIOSH = 3400.0

for mL in (10, 20):
    W_L = mL*g
    F = (W_tr*d_tr + W_L*dL)/d_m + (W_tr + W_L)
    plt.plot(dL, F, label='%d kg' % mL)
    print('%d kg: squat %.0f N, stoop %.0f N'
          % (mL, (W_tr*d_tr+W_L*0.25)/d_m+(W_tr+W_L),
             (W_tr*d_tr+W_L*0.55)/d_m+(W_tr+W_L)))
plt.axhline(NIOSH, ls='--')
plt.xlabel('load distance dL (m)')
plt.ylabel('spinal compression (N)')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Interpretation. Compression rises linearly with $d_L$; a $20\ \mathrm{kg}$ load runs $\approx3600\ \mathrm{N}$ squatting ($d_L=0.25\ \mathrm{m}$) and $\approx4800\ \mathrm{N}$ stooping ($0.55\ \mathrm{m}$), over the $3400\ \mathrm{N}$ NIOSH limit even for the squat - while a $10\ \mathrm{kg}$ load stays under ($\approx3000\ \mathrm{N}$) squatting but exceeds the limit ($\approx3600\ \mathrm{N}$) stooping. Sensitivity. The slope is $W_L/d_m$, so the tiny $5\ \mathrm{cm}$ muscle arm is the amplifier: every centimetre the load moves out adds $W_L/d_m\approx4\ \mathrm{N}$ of muscle force per newton-metre of extra moment. Failure mode. Crossing tissue tolerance - lower in aged discs and osteoporotic bone. Extension. Add intra-abdominal pressure as a second extensor moment and quantify how much it offloads the discs.

Lab D - the recovery step a stumble demands

Physical question. After a trip, how long a recovery step is needed as a function of how fast the body is pitching forward? Model. Module 10's extrapolated centre of mass; the step must reach the XcoM. Parameters. COM height $\ell=1.0\ \mathrm{m}$ ($\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$), forward COM velocity $\dot x$ from $0.3$ to $1.5\ \mathrm{m/s}$, initial COM offset $x_0=0.05\ \mathrm{m}$. Equations. $\xi=x_0+\dot x/\omega_0$; required step $\ge\xi$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

g, ell, x0 = 9.81, 1.0, 0.05
w0 = np.sqrt(g/ell)
xdot = np.linspace(0.3, 1.5, 40)          # forward COM speed (m/s)
xi = x0 + xdot/w0                          # extrapolated COM = min step
max_step = 0.60                            # reachable step length (m)

print('at xdot=1.0 m/s: XcoM=%.2f m; max reachable %.2f m'
      % (x0 + 1.0/w0, max_step))
plt.plot(xdot, xi, label='required step (XcoM)')
plt.axhline(max_step, ls='--', label='max reachable')
plt.xlabel('forward COM speed (m/s)')
plt.ylabel('recovery step length (m)')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Interpretation. The required step grows linearly with forward speed: at $\dot x=1.0\ \mathrm{m/s}$ the XcoM is $\approx0.37\ \mathrm{m}$ ahead, within a $0.60\ \mathrm{m}$ reachable step, but past $\dot x\approx1.72\ \mathrm{m/s}$ the demand exceeds any single step and a fall (or a second step) is unavoidable. Sensitivity. Step demand scales as $\dot x/\omega_0$, so a taller person (larger $\ell$, smaller $\omega_0$) needs a proportionally longer step for the same speed. Failure mode. When the required step exceeds the reachable one - shortened by age, weakness, or a delayed reaction - recovery fails. Extension. Add a reaction delay $\Delta t$ during which the XcoM keeps advancing ($\xi\to\xi\,e^{\omega_0\Delta t}$): a $100\ \mathrm{ms}$ slowing drops the maximum recoverable speed from $1.72$ to $1.22\ \mathrm{m/s}$.

10. Problems, diagnostics, and limitations

Jump to: Conceptual · Derivational · Computational · Diagnostics · Limitations · Repayment

Thirty problems - ten conceptual, ten derivational, ten computational - plus five diagnostics. Each carries a Probes note and a collapsible worked solution; computational solutions quote numbers the code produces.

Conceptual ↑ contents

C1 - the low soft chair. Why is a low, soft armchair so much harder to leave than a tall, firm stool, for the same person? Probes: the moment arm $d$ in the chair-rise torque; Section 2.
Solution
The knee torque needed at seat-off is $\tau=m_{\rm up}gd$, linear in the horizontal knee-to-COM distance $d$. A low seat starts the knees more flexed and the COM further behind and below them, enlarging $d$; a soft cushion sinks the pelvis, enlarging it further. Since available knee torque is fixed, a larger $d$ can push the demand past it - the mechanical reason a low sofa defeats someone who rises easily from a kitchen stool. Raising and firming the seat (smaller $d$) is the first, cheapest intervention.
C2 - down is worse than up. Stair falls happen far more on descent than ascent. Why, in terms of what the muscles must do? Probes: eccentric control versus power; Section 3.
Solution
Ascent requires positive work - the muscles add energy to raise the body - and its failure is benign: run out of power and you simply slow or stop. Descent requires negative work - the knee extensors lengthen under load (eccentric) to absorb $mgh$ per step - and its failure is catastrophic: if the eccentric brake gives way, the absorbed energy becomes an impact and a fall. Control, not power, is binding on the way down, and control degrades with reflex slowing and weak quadriceps, so descent dominates stair-fall statistics.
C3 - keep it close. Safe-lifting advice says hold the load close to the body. Why is this the single most effective rule? Probes: the $d_L$ lever in spinal compression; Section 4.
Solution
Spinal compression is dominated by the erector spinae force $F_m=(W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_Ld_L)/d_m$, and the muscle arm $d_m\approx5\ \mathrm{cm}$ is tiny, so the load term $W_Ld_L/d_m$ amplifies the load's moment about fortyfold per metre. Halving the horizontal load distance $d_L$ nearly halves the muscle force and the compression. No other single change - not strength, not a back belt - moves the compression as much, because the compression is set by geometry (the lever), and $d_L$ is the geometry the lifter controls.
C4 - squat versus stoop. Why does squatting to lift load the spine less than stooping with straight legs? Probes: both moment arms in the compression formula; Section 4.
Solution
A stoop lift (bend at the waist, straight legs) maximizes both external moment arms: the trunk pitches far forward (large $d_{\rm tr}$) and the load hangs far from the spine (large $d_L$). A squat lift (bend the knees, keep the trunk upright) minimizes both: the trunk stays near vertical (small $d_{\rm tr}$) and the load can be tucked close (small $d_L$). Since compression $\propto W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_Ld_L$, the squat's smaller arms give a lower compression - our numbers, $\approx3600\ \mathrm{N}$ squat versus $\approx4800\ \mathrm{N}$ stoop for $20\ \mathrm{kg}$, the difference between borderline and clearly hazardous.
C5 - the lean away. Why do you instinctively lean away from a heavy bag carried in one hand? Probes: the lateral spinal moment of an asymmetric carry; Section 4.
Solution
A bag in one hand hangs at a lateral moment arm from the spine, creating a side-bending moment the trunk muscles must balance. Leaning the trunk away shifts the body's own weight to the opposite side, creating an equal and opposite moment that cancels the bag's - bringing the combined centre of mass back over the pelvis and the base of support. The lean trades a large muscular side-bending effort for a small increase in symmetric compression; splitting the load into two bags removes the lateral moment entirely and is mechanically kinder.
C6 - falls happen at corners. Why do turns and trips, rather than straight walking, account for most falls? Probes: dynamic balance and the moving base; Section 5, Module 10.
Solution
Straight walking keeps the extrapolated COM tracking forward over a base repositioned in the direction of travel - a well-practised, stable pattern. A turn demands a lateral impulse ($2mv\sin(\beta/2)$) while the XcoM swings sideways and the base is being repositioned in a new direction, taxing lateral balance exactly when the base is least stable. A trip throws the XcoM past the foot, demanding a fast, accurate reactive step (Module 10). Both draw heavily on the balance margin and are timing-critical, so both, not steady walking, are where balance fails.
C7 - the rubber jar-opener. Why does a thin rubber pad let you open a jar you could not open bare-handed? Probes: friction in the grip-limited torque; Section 6.
Solution
The opening torque is $\tau_{\rm open}=\mu F_{\rm grip}r$, limited by the friction coefficient $\mu$ between hand and lid. Skin on a smooth or damp lid has low $\mu$, so most of your grip force cannot be converted into turning force. A rubber pad roughly doubles $\mu$, doubling the achievable torque for the same grip - often enough to cross the threshold from "won't budge" to "opens." It adds no strength; it converts more of the strength you already have into torque.
C8 - handles far from hinges. Why is a door handle placed at the edge farthest from the hinge, and why does pushing near the hinge feel impossible? Probes: the moment arm about the hinge; Section 6.
Solution
Opening a door is a torque about the hinge: $F_{\rm hand}d=\tau_{\rm door}$, so the hand force needed is $\tau_{\rm door}/d$, inversely proportional to the distance $d$ from the hinge. At the far edge $d$ is largest and the force smallest (a gentle $\approx25\ \mathrm{N}$); pushing near the hinge makes $d$ tiny, so the required force blows up toward infinity as $d\to0$. Placing the handle far from the hinge is the free mechanical advantage designers hand the user.
C9 - lungs up, knees down. Why does walking uphill tax the lungs while walking downhill taxes the knees? Probes: positive versus negative work on a slope; Section 7.
Solution
Uphill, the muscles do positive work against gravity's along-slope component, adding energy at rate $mgv\sin\theta$ - a metabolic (aerobic) demand that raises heart rate and breathing. Downhill, that component aids motion, so the muscles must absorb the same power eccentrically to hold speed constant; eccentric braking is metabolically cheap but concentrates high forces on the quadriceps and the knee/patellofemoral joint at a flexed knee. Same $mgv\sin\theta$, opposite sign, opposite organ system: supply uphill, absorb downhill.
C10 - one weakness, many failures. Why does a single deficit like weak quadriceps impair rising, stair-climbing, and downhill walking all at once? Probes: shared reserves across cases; Section 8.
Solution
Different daily tasks draw on the same small set of reserves. Knee extensor strength is the binding margin for the chair rise ($m_{\rm up}gd$), for stair ascent power, and for the eccentric control of stair and slope descent - so one deficit in that reserve shows up simultaneously across every task that spends it. This is why frailty presents as a general loss of capability rather than a list of unrelated failures, and why training the shared reserve (leg strength) restores several tasks at once - the mechanical basis of targeted exercise.

Derivational ↑ contents

D1 - chair-rise torque. Derive the quasi-static knee extensor torque at seat-off. Probes: moment balance about the knee; Section 2.
Solution
Treat the body above the knees as a rigid segment pivoting at the knee (shanks fixed). Gravity acts at its centre of mass, horizontal distance $d$ from the knee, giving a flexing moment $m_{\rm up}gd$. Quasi-static balance $\sum M=0$ requires the knee extensors to supply an equal extensor moment, so $\tau_{\rm knee}=m_{\rm up}gd$ (Proposition 2.1).
D2 - stair work and power. Derive the work per step and the climbing power. Probes: the work-energy theorem; Section 3.
Solution
Each step raises the COM by $h$; with kinetic energy unchanged step to step, the work equals the potential-energy gain $U_{\rm step}=mgh$. At step frequency $f$, power is $P=U_{\rm step}f=mghf=mgv_{\rm vert}$ with $v_{\rm vert}=hf$ (Proposition 3.1).
D3 - spinal compression. Derive the erector spinae force and disc compression when lifting. Probes: moment and force balance about L5/S1; Section 4.
Solution
Moment balance about L5/S1: $F_md_m=W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_Ld_L$, so $F_m=(W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_Ld_L)/d_m$. Force balance along the spine adds the axial weight component: $F_{\rm comp}=F_m+(W_{\rm tr}+W_L)\cos\phi$ (Proposition 4.1). The short $d_m$ makes $F_m$, and hence the compression, large.
D4 - turning impulse. Derive the ground impulse needed to redirect the walk through angle $\beta$. Probes: impulse-momentum for a vector velocity change; Section 5.
Solution
Momentum changes from $m\mathbf v_1$ to $m\mathbf v_2$ with equal speeds $v$ and included angle $\beta$; the impulse is $J=m|\mathbf v_2-\mathbf v_1|$. Two equal vectors at angle $\beta$ differ by $2v\sin(\beta/2)$, so $J=2mv\sin(\beta/2)$, directed inward (Proposition 5.1).
D5 - jar torque. Derive the maximum opening torque a grip can apply and the grip force to beat a resisting torque. Probes: friction converted to torque; Section 6.
Solution
Friction gives tangential force $\le\mu F_{\rm grip}$ at the lid rim (radius $r$), so torque $\le\mu F_{\rm grip}r$. Opening needs $\mu F_{\rm grip}r\ge\tau_{\rm resist}$, i.e. $F_{\rm grip}\ge\tau_{\rm resist}/(\mu r)$ (Proposition 6.1).
D6 - slope power. Derive the extra power of walking on a grade. Probes: resolving weight along the slope; Section 7.
Solution
The along-slope weight component is $mg\sin\theta$; walking up at speed $v$ works against it at rate $P=mg\sin\theta\,v$ (equivalently $mg\times v\sin\theta$, the vertical power). Downhill the sign flips and the muscles absorb it eccentrically (Proposition 7.1).
D7 - the momentum strategy. Show why pitching the trunk forward before rising reduces the extension-phase knee/hip torque. Probes: momentum transfer at seat-off; Section 2.
Solution
Flexing the trunk forward builds forward (and, as the COM passes over the feet, upward) momentum before seat-off. At seat-off the COM already has velocity $\dot{\mathbf x}_0$, so the extension phase need only supply the remaining impulse to complete the rise: from impulse-momentum, the extensor impulse need only make up the shortfall $m\,\Delta v-m\dot x_0$ between the momentum the rise requires and the momentum $m\dot x_0$ the flexion already supplied. The legs thus supply less than the full quasi-static impulse; the trade is that the momentum must be generated and safely arrested, which is why the strategy needs trunk control. (The full reduction requires the coupled two-segment model; the point here is the momentum offset.)
D8 - the safe-lift envelope. From the compression limit, derive the maximum safe load as a function of its distance. Probes: inverting the compression formula; Section 4.
Solution
Set $F_{\rm comp}=F_{\rm lim}$ and solve $ (W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}+W_Ld_L)/d_m+(W_{\rm tr}+W_L)=F_{\rm lim}$ for $W_L$: $W_L\big(d_L/d_m+1\big)=F_{\rm lim}-W_{\rm tr}d_{\rm tr}/d_m-W_{\rm tr}$, so $W_L^{\max}=\dfrac{F_{\rm lim}-W_{\rm tr}(d_{\rm tr}/d_m+1)}{d_L/d_m+1}$. The safe load falls as $\sim1/d_L$: with $F_{\rm lim}=3400\ \mathrm{N}$ it is $\approx17\ \mathrm{kg}$ close ($d_L=0.25$) and $\approx9\ \mathrm{kg}$ at arm's length ($d_L=0.50$).
D9 - the recovery step. Derive the minimum recovery-step length after a trip from the extrapolated COM. Probes: capturability; Section 5, Module 10.
Solution
The free inverted pendulum diverges along $\xi=x_{\rm com}+\dot x_{\rm com}/\omega_0$ with $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$ (Module 10). A recovery step arrests the fall only if the new base of support reaches the extrapolated COM, so the minimum step length is $\xi=x_0+\dot x/\omega_0$. It grows linearly with forward speed and, with a reaction delay $\Delta t$, to $\xi\,e^{\omega_0\Delta t}$.
D10 - the carry lean. Derive the trunk lean that recentres the COM over the pelvis when carrying a one-hand load. Probes: cancelling a lateral moment; Section 4.
Solution
A bag of weight $W_b$ at lateral arm $d_b$ makes a moment $W_bd_b$ about the spine. Leaning the trunk shifts the body weight $W_{\rm tr}$ to the far side by $x_{\rm lean}$, an opposing moment $W_{\rm tr}x_{\rm lean}$. Setting them equal, $x_{\rm lean}=W_bd_b/W_{\rm tr}$: a $10\ \mathrm{kg}$ bag at $0.30\ \mathrm{m}$ needs a $\approx0.09\ \mathrm{m}$ COM shift. Two symmetric bags cancel the lateral moment and need no lean.

Computational ↑ contents

Each requires a sweep, an inverse solve, an optimisation, a simulation, or a regime comparison - not substitution into a boxed formula. Numbers quoted are those the code produces.

K1 - the chair an older adult can leave (threshold + inverse). Sweep the knee-to-COM distance $d$ and find the highest chair (smallest $d$) that a person with a given peak knee torque can rise from; compare a typical older adult ($1.5\ \mathrm{N\,m/kg}$) and a frail one ($1.0\ \mathrm{N\,m/kg}$). Probes: the strength-margin threshold; Section 2.
Solution
Setting $m_{\rm up}gd=\tau_{\max}$ and inverting, the largest tolerable $d$ is $d_{\max}=\tau_{\max}/(m_{\rm up}g)$. For $m_{\rm up}=56\ \mathrm{kg}$: a typical older adult ($\tau_{\max}=105\ \mathrm{N\,m}$) rises up to $d=0.19\ \mathrm{m}$ (most chairs), but a frail adult ($70\ \mathrm{N\,m}$) only up to $d=0.13\ \mathrm{m}$ - a high, firm chair. The inverse gives the design target: to keep the frail adult independent, seats must hold $d\le0.13\ \mathrm{m}$, i.e. be tall and firm. The threshold, not the average, is what independence turns on.
K2 - the cadence ceiling (inverse + sensitivity). Find the maximum sustainable stair cadence for a given power ceiling, and how it changes with body mass. Probes: inverting the power law; Section 3.
Solution
From $P=mghf$, the maximum cadence at a $250\ \mathrm{W}$ ceiling is $f_{\max}=250/(mgh)$: $2.14\ \mathrm{steps/s}$ at $70\ \mathrm{kg}$ but only $1.67\ \mathrm{steps/s}$ at $90\ \mathrm{kg}$ (same $h=0.17\ \mathrm{m}$). Heavier climbers hit the ceiling at lower cadence - a $29\%$ mass increase costs $22\%$ of cadence - which is why load carriage and obesity slow stair climbing measurably, and why the sensitivity $\partial f_{\max}/\partial m=-250/(m^2gh)$ steepens at low power ceilings.
K3 - the safe-lift envelope (inverse solve + regime). Compute the maximum load that keeps spinal compression under $3400\ \mathrm{N}$ as a function of load distance, and contrast squat and stoop. Probes: inverting the compression limit; Section 4.
Solution
Inverting Proposition 4.1 at $F_{\rm comp}=3400\ \mathrm{N}$: $W_L^{\max}=[3400-W_{\rm tr}(d_{\rm tr}/d_m+1)]/(d_L/d_m+1)$. With $W_{\rm tr}=343\ \mathrm{N}$, $d_{\rm tr}=0.30$, $d_m=0.05$, the safe load is $\approx17\ \mathrm{kg}$ held close ($d_L=0.25\ \mathrm{m}$, squat) but only $\approx9\ \mathrm{kg}$ at arm's length ($d_L=0.50\ \mathrm{m}$, stoop) - the squat nearly doubles the safe load. The envelope is a hyperbola in $d_L$: safe load falls as $\sim1/d_L$, which is the quantitative safe-lifting chart.
K4 - the recoverable envelope (2-parameter sweep). Compute the maximum forward speed from which a trip can be recovered in one step, as a function of reaction delay. Probes: how delay shrinks the balance envelope; Section 5, Module 10.
Solution
With a maximum step $L=0.60\ \mathrm{m}$, the recoverable speed satisfies $(x_0+\dot x/\omega_0)e^{\omega_0\Delta t}\le L$, so $\dot x_{\max}=\omega_0(Le^{-\omega_0\Delta t}-x_0)$. For $\omega_0=3.13\ \mathrm{rad/s}$: no delay gives $\dot x_{\max}=1.72\ \mathrm{m/s}$, but a $100\ \mathrm{ms}$ reaction delay drops it to $1.22\ \mathrm{m/s}$ - a $29\%$ loss of the recoverable envelope from a tenth-second slowing. This quantifies why reflex slowing (Module 10, aging) so sharply raises trip-fall risk: the envelope shrinks exponentially in delay.
K5 - the sharpest safe turn (friction limit). Find the maximum turn angle achievable in one step before the foot must slip, as a function of walking speed. Probes: the friction-limited lateral impulse; Section 5.
Solution
The lateral impulse $2mv\sin(\beta/2)$ delivered over a step of duration $t_{\rm step}$ needs an average lateral force $2mv\sin(\beta/2)/t_{\rm step}\le\mu mg$ (no slip). Solving, $\sin(\beta/2)\le\mu g\,t_{\rm step}/(2v)$. For $\mu=0.7$, $t_{\rm step}=0.35\ \mathrm{s}$: at $v=1.0\ \mathrm{m/s}$ any turn is possible ($\le180^\circ$), at $v=1.3\ \mathrm{m/s}$ up to $\beta\approx135^\circ$, but at $v=2.0\ \mathrm{m/s}$ only $\beta\approx74^\circ$ before the foot slips. Fast turns must be gentle - the mechanics behind slowing to round a sharp corner, and behind slip-turn falls on low-friction floors.
K6 - which aid opens the jar? (regime comparison). For a weak grip ($60\ \mathrm{N}$), compute the opening torque bare-handed, with a rubber pad, and with a jar wrench, and see which crosses a $2\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ threshold. Probes: the two levers $\mu$ and $r$; Section 6.
Solution
Opening torque is $\mu F_{\rm grip}r$. With $F_{\rm grip}=60\ \mathrm{N}$: bare hand ($\mu=0.5$, $r=0.035\ \mathrm{m}$) gives $1.05\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ - fails the $2\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ lid; a rubber pad ($\mu=1.0$) gives $2.10\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ - just opens; a jar wrench ($\mu=0.7$, $r=0.060\ \mathrm{m}$) gives $2.52\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ - opens comfortably. Both aids attack the product $\mu r$, and either alone can cross the threshold without any gain in grip strength - the mechanical reason such aids restore independence to arthritic hands.
K7 - how fast can you climb the hill? (inverse + regime). Given a sustained-power ceiling, compute the maximum walking speed up slopes of increasing grade, and contrast the downhill braking demand. Probes: inverting the slope-power law; Section 7.
Solution
The gravity power budget $mgv\sin\theta\le P$ gives $v_{\max}=P/(mg\sin\theta)$ for the climbing share. At a $250\ \mathrm{W}$ ceiling: $v_{\max}\approx4.2,\,2.1,\,1.4\ \mathrm{m/s}$ at $\theta=5^\circ,10^\circ,15^\circ$ (adding level-walking cost lowers these further). The speed falls as $1/\sin\theta$, so doubling the grade halves the sustainable speed - the reason steep trails are walked slowly. Downhill flips supply to absorb: the same $mgv\sin\theta$ must be dissipated eccentrically, so the binding limit becomes joint tolerance and control, not aerobic power.
K8 - does a brisk rise cost more torque? (dynamic simulation + regime). Simulate a chair rise with a minimum-jerk COM path and compare the peak knee torque of a slow (quasi-static) and a brisk rise. Probes: the inertial cost of speed; Section 2.
Solution
Driving the COM up by $H=0.40\ \mathrm{m}$ on a minimum-jerk path in $T=1.2\ \mathrm{s}$ adds a vertical acceleration $a_z(t)$, so the knee torque becomes $m_{\rm up}(g+a_z)d$. The quasi-static peak is $110\ \mathrm{N\,m}$; the brisk rise peaks higher at $\approx128\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ (during the accelerate phase) and dips to $\approx92\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ later - so speed costs a higher transient torque. This is why the pure vertical model shows a penalty; the genuine flexion-momentum benefit (transferring horizontal trunk momentum to the rise, reducing the extension-phase demand) needs the coupled two-segment model of the extension - a real distinction the simple simulation makes visible.
K9 - splitting a carried load (optimisation). A total load is split between the two hands, each share hanging at lateral distance $d_b$ from the spine. Sweep the split fraction and find the distribution that minimises the lateral spinal moment - and hence the trunk lean, and the extra compression that lean itself creates, needed to cancel it. Probes: optimising a load distribution; Section 4.
Solution
With a fraction $\alpha$ of the total $10\ \mathrm{kg}$ load in one hand and $1-\alpha$ in the other (each at $\pm d_b=0.30\ \mathrm{m}$), the net lateral moment about the spine is $|2\alpha-1|\,M_Lg\,d_b$, and the trunk lean needed to cancel it, $x_{\rm lean}=|2\alpha-1|M_Lg\,d_b/W_{\rm tr}$, grows with the imbalance - itself adding symmetric compression. Both the residual side-flexor effort and the lean's compression penalty increase with $|2\alpha-1|$, so sweeping $\alpha$ traces a V with its minimum at the even split $\alpha=0.5$ (net moment $0$, no lean); the one-handed extreme $\alpha=1$ is the worst case at $29\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ of lateral moment and a $0.09\ \mathrm{m}$ lean. The even two-bag split is thus the optimum along a continuum, not merely better than one bag - and any deviation costs in proportion to the imbalance.
K10 - which task fails first? (multi-margin sweep). As overall leg strength declines from a young baseline, simulate which everyday task first becomes impossible. Probes: shared reserves and thresholds; Section 8.
Solution
Scale peak knee torque from a young baseline $2.5\ \mathrm{N\,m/kg}$ ($175\ \mathrm{N\,m}$) downward and compare against each task's demand. The chair rise (demand $110\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ from a standard seat) is crossed first, at $\approx63\%$ of young strength; stair ascent power and descent control follow as strength falls further. So of the strength-limited tasks, rising from a low chair is the first casualty of declining leg strength - which matches the clinical observation that sit-to-stand difficulty is an early marker of functional decline, and pinpoints the reserve whose training delays the whole cascade.

Five diagnostics ↑ contents

  1. Units of compression. Check that $(W\,d)/d_m$ has units of force.
    Solution
    $W\,d$ is $\mathrm{N\cdot m}$ (a moment); dividing by $d_m$ in $\mathrm{m}$ gives $\mathrm{N}$ - a force, as required.
  2. Direction of the turn impulse. Which way does the ground push to turn you left?
    Solution
    Toward the inside of the turn (to your left): the impulse $2mv\sin(\beta/2)$ points along $\mathbf v_2-\mathbf v_1$, inward.
  3. Sign of stair work. Ascending versus descending, what is the sign of the muscles' work?
    Solution
    Positive (concentric) ascending - the muscles add energy; negative (eccentric) descending - they absorb it.
  4. Chair-height fix. A person cannot rise from a $0.40\ \mathrm{m}$ chair. Name two mechanical changes that help.
    Solution
    Raise/firm the seat (smaller $d$) and add armrests (an external upward force); both reduce the knee's share of $m_{\rm up}gd$.
  5. Slope power sign. At $\theta=10^\circ$ downhill, is $mgv\sin\theta$ supplied or absorbed?
    Solution
    Absorbed: gravity aids the descent, so the muscles must brake it eccentrically at that rate.

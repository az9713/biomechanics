0. Motivation - the degrees-of-freedom problem

Sign your name on a slip of paper. Now sign it again, twice the size, on a whiteboard. The two signatures are unmistakably yours - the same loops, the same slant, the same idiosyncratic flourish - yet the first was written with small movements of the fingers and wrist, the second with large sweeps of the elbow and shoulder. Entirely different muscles, joints, and forces produced the same shape. This everyday miracle, called motor equivalence (Fig. 1), is the doorway to the deepest problem in movement science: the body has vastly more ways to perform a task than the task specifies, and something must choose among them.

The Russian physiologist Nikolai Bernstein named it the degrees-of-freedom problem. Count the freedoms. The arm alone has about seven joint degrees of freedom (Section 1); the task of putting the hand at a point in space fixes only three. Below the joints, some fifty muscles cross them, so the number of ways to produce a given set of joint torques is larger still. Below the muscles, thousands of motor units can be recruited in countless patterns. At every level the controller faces the same surplus: more actuators than the task has constraints. A reach does not have a solution; it has an infinite-dimensional family of them.

Module 11 met the smallest version of this - the null space of the arm's Jacobian, the joint motions that leave the hand fixed - and deliberately left the choice open, resolving it with a pseudoinverse or an arbitrary secondary cost. That was a placeholder. The nervous system does not pick a solution arbitrarily or by minimum joint speed; it picks movements that are strikingly stereotyped - reaches follow nearly straight hand paths with smooth, bell-shaped speed profiles, the same shape whether the reach is fast or slow, near or far. Stereotypy is the fingerprint of an optimization: out of the infinite family, the brain consistently selects the movement that minimizes some cost. Identifying that cost, and the control laws that realize it, is the whole of this module.

The thesis of this module. Movement is the solution of an optimal-control problem: the nervous system resolves the surplus degrees of freedom by minimizing a cost - smoothness, effort, or endpoint error - over the whole movement, subject to the body's dynamics, its noisy sensors and actuators, and its feedback delays. This single principle generates the observed regularities: the minimum-jerk trajectory of a reach (Section 3), the linear-quadratic feedback that stabilizes it (Section 4), the impedance the arm presents to the world (Section 5), the internal models that predict ahead of the delay (Section 6), and - the unifying idea - optimal feedback control, which corrects only task-relevant errors and leaves the redundant directions free, explaining both synergies and the structured variability of real movement (Section 7).

The arc of this module. We formalize the degrees-of-freedom problem and the need for a selection principle (Section 1), set up movement as the minimization of a cost functional (Section 2), derive the minimum-jerk trajectory and its bell-shaped speed (Section 3), build the LQR optimal feedback law (Section 4), read the arm as a programmable impedance for contact tasks (Section 5), add the internal models that beat the sensorimotor delay and learn from error (Section 6), unify these under optimal feedback control and the minimal-intervention principle (Section 7), reduce dimensionality with synergies and central pattern generators (Section 8), and contrast human and robot control (Section 9) before the labs and problems.

Where this attaches to the course: the plant being controlled is the two-link arm of Module 11 (its Jacobian, its dynamics $M\ddot{\mathbf q}+C\dot{\mathbf q}+\mathbf g=\boldsymbol\tau$) driven by the Hill muscles of Module 5; the noise and delay are Module 10's; the impedance rests on Module 11's endpoint stiffness $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}$. This module supplies the controller those modules assumed.

1. Redundancy, formalized: the null space needs a principle

To choose well we must first say precisely what there is to choose among. Redundancy appears at three nested levels, each a map from more variables to fewer, and each with a null space of task-neutral variation.

Definition 1 (kinematic, actuation, and neural redundancy). A system is redundant for a task when the map from its control variables to the task variables has a nontrivial null space - a set of control changes that leave the task unchanged. Three levels stack: kinematic (joint angles $\mathbf q\in\mathbb R^{n}$ to hand pose $\mathbf p\in\mathbb R^{m}$, $n\gt m$, null space of the Jacobian $J$); actuation (muscle forces to joint torques, more muscles than joints); and neural (motor-unit activity to muscle force). At each level the null space is the freedom the controller must spend (Fig. 2).

Module 11 quantified the first level: for the arm's Jacobian $J$ ($m\times n$, full row rank), any joint velocity realizing a desired hand velocity $\dot{\mathbf p}$ has the form $\dot{\mathbf q}=J^{+}\dot{\mathbf p}+(I-J^{+}J)\mathbf z$, where $J^{+}=J^{\mathsf T}(JJ^{\mathsf T})^{-1}$ is the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse (the minimum-norm right inverse, $JJ^{+}=I$) and the second term ranges over the $(n-m)$-dimensional null space as $\mathbf z$ varies. The pseudoinverse picks minimum joint speed; but that is a mathematical default, not a biological law, and it makes the wrong prediction. Consider the classic demonstration.

Proposition 1.1 (redundancy admits infinitely many exact solutions). Let $J\in\mathbb R^{m\times n}$ have full row rank $m\lt n$. Then for every task velocity $\dot{\mathbf p}\in\mathbb R^{m}$ the solution set $\{\dot{\mathbf q}:J\dot{\mathbf q}=\dot{\mathbf p}\}$ is an affine subspace of dimension $n-m\ge1$; no purely kinematic criterion singles out a point of it. A selection requires an added objective.
Since $J$ has full row rank $m$, the system $J\dot{\mathbf q}=\dot{\mathbf p}$ is consistent for every $\dot{\mathbf p}$ (the range of $J$ is all of $\mathbb R^{m}$). Its solution set is $\dot{\mathbf q}_0+\mathcal N(J)$ where $\dot{\mathbf q}_0$ is any particular solution and $\mathcal N(J)=\{\mathbf v:J\mathbf v=\mathbf 0\}$. By rank-nullity $\dim\mathcal N(J)=n-\operatorname{rank}J=n-m\ge1$, so the set is an affine subspace of positive dimension - a continuum of exact solutions. Any function that is constant on the task (depends on $\dot{\mathbf q}$ only through $J\dot{\mathbf q}$) takes the same value at all of them and cannot discriminate; a criterion that does discriminate must depend on $\dot{\mathbf q}$ off the task directions, i.e. it is an objective added from outside the kinematics. ∎

Proposition 1.1 is a licence and a demand: the kinematics permit any solution, so the controller is free to impose a preference, and it must impose one to act at all. The rest of the module is a sequence of increasingly principled preferences - smoothness (Section 3), a weighted trade of error against effort (Section 4), minimal task-variance under noise (Section 7) - each a cost whose minimizer picks one movement from Bernstein's infinite family. Crucially, the good principles do not collapse the null space to a point and freeze it; they leave the task-irrelevant freedom loose, which is why repetitions of a movement vary most in exactly the directions that do not matter (Section 7). We begin with the cost-functional machinery that makes "minimize a cost over a movement" precise.

2. Movement as optimal control

An optimal-control problem makes "the best movement" a precise object. We describe the body by a state $\mathbf x(t)$ (positions and velocities of the segments), acted on by a control $\mathbf u(t)$ (the neural commands, ultimately muscle activations), evolving by the dynamics $\dot{\mathbf x}=f(\mathbf x,\mathbf u)$ - the arm's equations of motion from Module 11. A movement is a control history $\mathbf u(\cdot)$ over $[0,T]$ and the state trajectory it produces; its quality is scored by a cost functional.

Definition 2 (optimal-control problem). Given dynamics $\dot{\mathbf x}=f(\mathbf x,\mathbf u)$, an initial state $\mathbf x(0)$, and a cost functional $$\mathcal J[\mathbf u]=\phi\big(\mathbf x(T)\big)+\int_0^T L\big(\mathbf x(t),\mathbf u(t),t\big)\,dt,$$ the optimal control is the $\mathbf u(\cdot)$ minimizing $\mathcal J$ subject to the dynamics and any boundary conditions. $L$ is the running cost (effort, error, jerk paid continuously) and $\phi$ the terminal cost (missing the target at the end). The minimizer selects one movement from all those the dynamics allow.

Two solution styles run through the module, and they answer two different questions. Open-loop (trajectory) optimization fixes the whole $\mathbf u(\cdot)$ in advance to shape the path - it answers "what movement?" and yields the minimum-jerk reach of Section 3. Feedback optimization seeks a control law $\mathbf u=\pi(\mathbf x,t)$ that reacts to the measured state - it answers "how to correct?" and yields the LQR of Section 4 and the optimal feedback controller of Section 7. Real movement needs both: a plan and a correction rule.

For open-loop problems whose cost depends on a trajectory and its derivatives, the minimizer is characterized by a differential equation from the calculus of variations - the tool we need for minimum jerk.

Lemma 2.1 (Euler-Poisson equation). Among scalar trajectories $x(t)$ on $[0,T]$ with fixed values of $x,\dot x,\dots,x^{(p-1)}$ at both endpoints, a minimizer of $\mathcal J[x]=\int_0^T L\big(t,x,\dot x,\dots,x^{(p)}\big)\,dt$ satisfies $$\sum_{k=0}^{p}(-1)^k\frac{d^k}{dt^k}\frac{\partial L}{\partial x^{(k)}}=0.$$
Let $x$ be a minimizer and $\eta(t)$ any variation vanishing with its first $p-1$ derivatives at both endpoints. Then $g(\varepsilon)=\mathcal J[x+\varepsilon\eta]$ has a minimum at $\varepsilon=0$, so $g'(0)=0$: $\int_0^T\sum_{k=0}^{p}\frac{\partial L}{\partial x^{(k)}}\eta^{(k)}\,dt=0$. Integrate the $k$-th term by parts $k$ times; the boundary terms vanish because $\eta$ and its derivatives up to order $p-1$ are zero at the endpoints, giving $\int_0^T\big[\sum_k(-1)^k\frac{d^k}{dt^k}\frac{\partial L}{\partial x^{(k)}}\big]\eta\,dt=0$. As this holds for every admissible $\eta$, the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations forces the bracket to vanish identically. ∎

3. The minimum-jerk trajectory

What cost does a reach minimize? Measured reaches are remarkably uniform: the hand travels a nearly straight path with a smooth, single-peaked, symmetric speed profile, and the profile keeps its shape when the movement is scaled in size or duration. Flash and Hogan proposed the cost that predicts exactly this - the total squared jerk, jerk being the third time-derivative of position, the rate of change of acceleration. Minimizing jerk is minimizing abruptness; the result is the smoothest movement that gets there.

Definition 3 (minimum-jerk cost). For a hand coordinate $x(t)$ moving over $[0,T]$, the minimum-jerk cost is $$\mathcal J[x]=\tfrac12\int_0^T\Big(\frac{d^3x}{dt^3}\Big)^2 dt,$$ minimized subject to fixed endpoints at rest: $x(0)=x_0$, $x(T)=x_f$, and $\dot x=\ddot x=0$ at both $t=0$ and $t=T$.
Proposition 3.1 (the minimum-jerk trajectory). The minimizer of Definition 3 is the fifth-order polynomial $$\boxed{\;x(t)=x_0+(x_f-x_0)\big(10\tau^3-15\tau^4+6\tau^5\big),\qquad \tau=t/T.\;}$$ Its speed $\dot x=\dfrac{30(x_f-x_0)}{T}\,\tau^2(1-\tau)^2$ is a symmetric, bell-shaped pulse peaking at mid-movement $\tau=\tfrac12$ with value $\dot x_{\max}=\dfrac{15}{8}\dfrac{x_f-x_0}{T}=1.875\,\dfrac{x_f-x_0}{T}$.
Here $L=\tfrac12(x^{(3)})^2$ depends only on $x^{(3)}$, so in Lemma 2.1 with $p=3$ the only nonzero derivative is $\partial L/\partial x^{(3)}=x^{(3)}$, and the Euler-Poisson equation reduces to $(-1)^3\frac{d^3}{dt^3}x^{(3)}=-x^{(6)}=0$, i.e. $x^{(6)}(t)=0$. Hence $x(t)$ is a polynomial of degree at most five, $x(t)=\sum_{i=0}^5 a_i\tau^i$ with $\tau=t/T$. The six boundary conditions fix the six coefficients: $x(0)=x_0$ gives $a_0=x_0$; $\dot x(0)=\ddot x(0)=0$ give $a_1=a_2=0$; and the three conditions at $\tau=1$ ($x=x_f$, $\dot x=0$, $\ddot x=0$) give the linear system $a_3+a_4+a_5=x_f-x_0$, $3a_3+4a_4+5a_5=0$, $6a_3+12a_4+20a_5=0$, whose solution is $a_3=10(x_f-x_0)$, $a_4=-15(x_f-x_0)$, $a_5=6(x_f-x_0)$. This is the boxed form. Differentiating, $\dot x=\frac{x_f-x_0}{T}(30\tau^2-60\tau^3+30\tau^4)=\frac{30(x_f-x_0)}{T}\tau^2(1-\tau)^2$, which is symmetric about $\tau=\tfrac12$ and vanishes at both ends; its maximum is at $\tau=\tfrac12$, value $\frac{30(x_f-x_0)}{T}\cdot\frac1{16}=\frac{15}{8}\frac{x_f-x_0}{T}$. ∎

Three predictions of Proposition 3.1 (Fig. 3) are borne out by measurement and give the model its authority. Scale invariance: the bracket $10\tau^3-15\tau^4+6\tau^5$ depends only on the fraction of the movement completed, so the speed profile has the identical bell shape for a $5\ \mathrm{cm}$ and a $50\ \mathrm{cm}$ reach, fast or slow - exactly the observed stereotypy that motivated the search. Straight paths: applied independently to the $x$ and $y$ hand coordinates with a common $T$, the trajectory is a straight line in space (both coordinates share the same time-bracket, so $y-y_0$ is a fixed multiple of $x-x_0$), matching the near-straight reaches people make even though curved joint paths would also reach the target. The $1.875$ ratio: peak speed is a fixed multiple of average speed $(x_f-x_0)/T$, a parameter-free number that reaching data reproduce. The cost has no free parameters beyond the endpoints and duration, which is why a one-line formula predicts a measured movement.

Minimum jerk is a kinematic smoothness principle - it never mentions forces, masses, or muscles, and it is really a description of the desired hand path, not yet a controller. Two questions remain. Why should the brain value kinematic smoothness at all - is there a deeper, dynamical reason? (Section 7 gives one: under signal-dependent noise, smoothness minimizes endpoint variance.) And how is such a trajectory actually tracked by a body with inertia, delay, and disturbances? That is a feedback problem, and its optimal solution is the linear-quadratic regulator.

4. The linear-quadratic regulator

A planned trajectory is useless without a rule to correct deviations from it - a gust, a slip, a misestimate of the load all push the state off plan, and the controller must pull it back. The cleanest optimal feedback law is the linear-quadratic regulator (LQR): for a linear plant and a quadratic cost, the optimal control is exact, in closed form, and is a constant-gain feedback on the state. It is the feedback counterpart of minimum jerk and the workhorse of Section 7.

Linearize the plant about the operating point (Module 11's arm about a posture, or a point-mass hand) to $\dot{\mathbf x}=A\mathbf x+B\mathbf u$, where $\mathbf x$ is the state deviation and $\mathbf u$ the control. Charge a quadratic running cost that prices deviation against effort.

Definition 4 (infinite-horizon LQR). For the linear plant $\dot{\mathbf x}=A\mathbf x+B\mathbf u$ with cost $$\mathcal J=\int_0^\infty\big(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}Q\,\mathbf x+\mathbf u^{\mathsf T}R\,\mathbf u\big)\,dt,\qquad Q=Q^{\mathsf T}\succeq0,\ R=R^{\mathsf T}\succ0,$$ the linear-quadratic regulator is the control $\mathbf u(\cdot)$ minimizing $\mathcal J$. $Q$ prices state error (each unit of position or velocity deviation), $R$ prices control effort; their ratio sets how aggressively errors are corrected.
Proposition 4.1 (LQR optimal feedback). The LQR optimal control is the constant-gain state feedback $$\boxed{\;\mathbf u=-K\mathbf x,\qquad K=R^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P,\;}$$ where $P=P^{\mathsf T}\succeq0$ solves the algebraic Riccati equation $$A^{\mathsf T}P+PA-PBR^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P+Q=0.$$ The minimum cost from state $\mathbf x$ is $\mathcal J^\star(\mathbf x)=\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x$.
Let $V(\mathbf x)=\min_{\mathbf u(\cdot)}\int_t^\infty(\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}Q\mathbf x+\mathbf u^{\mathsf T}R\mathbf u)\,dt'$ be the optimal cost-to-go; being time-invariant it depends on $\mathbf x$ alone. By Bellman's principle of optimality (the optimal cost-to-go equals the best one-step cost plus the optimal cost-to-go from the next state), $V$ satisfies the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation $0=\min_{\mathbf u}\big[\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}Q\mathbf x+\mathbf u^{\mathsf T}R\mathbf u+\nabla V(\mathbf x)^{\mathsf T}(A\mathbf x+B\mathbf u)\big]$. Try $V(\mathbf x)=\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x$ with $P=P^{\mathsf T}\succeq0$, so $\nabla V=2P\mathbf x$. The bracket is quadratic and convex in $\mathbf u$; setting its $\mathbf u$-gradient to zero, $2R\mathbf u+2B^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x=0$, gives $\mathbf u=-R^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x=-K\mathbf x$. Substituting back, $0=\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}Q\mathbf x+\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}PBR^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x+2\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}P A\mathbf x-2\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}PBR^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x$. Using $2\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}PA\mathbf x=\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}(A^{\mathsf T}P+PA)\mathbf x$ and combining the $PBR^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P$ terms, the bracket equals $\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\big(A^{\mathsf T}P+PA-PBR^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P+Q\big)\mathbf x$. For this to vanish for every $\mathbf x$, the symmetric matrix must be zero - the algebraic Riccati equation - and then $V(\mathbf x)=\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x$ is the optimal cost-to-go with feedback $\mathbf u=-K\mathbf x$. (A unique stabilizing $P=P^{\mathsf T}\succeq0$ exists provided $(A,B)$ is stabilizable and $(Q^{1/2},A)$ detectable, which we assume; the pendulum plant of Module 11 satisfies both.) ∎

The LQR makes the effort-accuracy trade quantitative. Increase $R$ (effort is dear) and the gains shrink: the arm corrects lazily, drifting back to target slowly with small torques - the strategy of a relaxed, unhurried movement. Increase $Q$ (error is dear) and the gains grow: fast, stiff correction at the cost of large, metabolically expensive torques that also amplify any noise in the command. There is no single "right" gain (Fig. 4); there is a family parameterized by $Q/R$, and the nervous system is observed to shift along it with the stakes of the task - stiffening (high gain) when precision matters, relaxing (low gain) when it does not. This tunable trade is the through-line to impedance control, where the same gains are read as a mechanical stiffness the hand presents to the world.

5. Impedance control: the arm as a programmable spring

So far the arm has moved through free space. The moment the hand touches the world - turning a key, wiping a surface, inserting a plug - a new difficulty appears that pure position control cannot handle. Command a stiff position controller to hold the hand at a point, then place a rigid wall a millimetre inside that point: the controller, seeing a persistent position error, drives ever-larger force into the wall. Position control against a stiff environment is unstable or damaging. The nervous system's answer, and robotics', is not to control position or force but the relationship between them - the hand's mechanical impedance.

Definition 5 (mechanical impedance and impedance control). The mechanical impedance of the hand is the dynamic map from an imposed displacement to the force the hand generates in response. Impedance control regulates this map so the hand behaves like a programmable spring-mass-damper about a moving equilibrium $\mathbf x_{\rm eq}(t)$: $$\mathbf F_{\rm int}=K_x\big(\mathbf x_{\rm eq}-\mathbf x\big)-D_x\dot{\mathbf x},$$ where $\mathbf F_{\rm int}$ is the force the hand exerts on the environment, $K_x$ is the endpoint stiffness matrix, and $D_x$ the endpoint damping. The controller commands the equilibrium and the stiffness, not the position directly.

The endpoint stiffness is not a free choice at the hand; it is set at the joints and transformed by the arm's geometry - the very relation Module 11 derived. Restating it here fixes what the controller can and cannot shape.

Proposition 5.1 (endpoint stiffness from joint stiffness). If the joints present a stiffness matrix $K_q$ (from muscle co-contraction and reflexes), the hand's endpoint stiffness in a non-singular posture is $$\boxed{\;K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_q\,J^{-1},\;}$$ so the hand's stiffness ellipse is set jointly by the neural gains $K_q$ and the configuration-dependent Jacobian $J$. Co-contraction scales $K_q$ up (a stiffer hand everywhere); posture rotates and reshapes the ellipse through $J$.
A small endpoint displacement $\delta\mathbf x$ produces joint displacement $\delta\mathbf q=J^{-1}\delta\mathbf x$ (differential kinematics inverted). The joints resist with torque $\delta\boldsymbol\tau=-K_q\delta\mathbf q=-K_qJ^{-1}\delta\mathbf x$. By the force-torque duality $\delta\mathbf F=J^{-\mathsf T}\delta\boldsymbol\tau$ (Module 11), the endpoint restoring force is $\delta\mathbf F=-J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}\delta\mathbf x$, so the endpoint stiffness is $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}$, holding $J$ fixed to first order. ∎

Impedance control turns contact tasks from impossible to routine (Fig. 5). Inserting a peg into a hole, you make the hand stiff along the insertion axis (to push) and compliant across it (so the peg slides into alignment instead of jamming) - a stiffness ellipse elongated along the axis, arranged by choosing the posture and the pattern of co-contraction that Proposition 5.1 converts into $K_x$. A wiping task wants the opposite: stiff normal to the surface (constant contact force), compliant tangentially. The controller never specifies force or position alone; it specifies the equilibrium trajectory $\mathbf x_{\rm eq}(t)$ (the minimum-jerk plan of Section 3) plus the impedance around it.

There is a deep stability reason the body prefers impedance. A mechanical impedance that is passive - one that can only dissipate or store the energy fed into it, never generate net energy - remains stable in contact with any passive environment, however stiff or unpredictable. A spring-damper with $K_x,D_x\succeq0$ is passive; so setting a positive-definite stiffness and damping guarantees safe, stable contact without knowing the environment in advance. This is why a relaxed, compliant limb is safe to bump into a wall while a high-gain position servo is not, and why co-contraction (which raises passive stiffness and damping together) is the body's first response to an uncertain or unstable contact.

6. Internal models and feedforward prediction

Every feedback law so far assumed the controller knows the current state. It does not - it knows only delayed, noisy sensations. Module 10 measured the delay in the balance loop at $100$-$200\ \mathrm{ms}$; the same delay afflicts the arm. A pure feedback controller acting on $150\ \mathrm{ms}$-old information about a fast reach is correcting where the hand was, not where it is, and high feedback gains on stale data oscillate (Module 10's delayed-feedback instability). The nervous system escapes the delay by predicting: it runs an internal model of its own body.

Definition 6 (forward and inverse internal models). A forward model predicts the sensory consequences of a motor command: given an efference copy of the command $\mathbf u$ and the current state estimate $\hat{\mathbf x}$, it outputs the predicted next state and sensory feedback. An inverse model runs the map backward: given a desired state trajectory, it outputs the feedforward command that would produce it. The forward model beats the delay (predict now what feedback will confirm later); the inverse model supplies the feedforward command that feedback then merely corrects.

The forward model does two jobs (Fig. 6). First, it furnishes a current state estimate by advancing the last delayed measurement forward through the known dynamics and command - the predictor that, combined with the Kalman correction of Module 10, gives a fast, accurate $\hat{\mathbf x}$ for the feedback laws of Sections 4-5 to act on. Second, it lets the brain cancel the sensory consequences of its own actions, keeping self-generated signals from swamping external ones.

Proposition 6.1 (reafference cancellation). Let the measured sensory signal be $\mathbf s=\mathbf s_{\rm self}(\mathbf u)+\mathbf s_{\rm ext}+\boldsymbol\nu$, the sum of a self-generated part driven by the command $\mathbf u$, an external part, and noise. If the forward model predicts the self-generated part as $\hat{\mathbf s}_{\rm self}=\mathbf s_{\rm self}(\mathbf u)$ from the efference copy and the nervous system forms the residual $\mathbf r=\mathbf s-\hat{\mathbf s}_{\rm self}$, then $\mathbf r=\mathbf s_{\rm ext}+\boldsymbol\nu$: the residual retains only externally-caused sensation. A perfectly predicted self-touch cancels to noise.
Subtract: $\mathbf r=\mathbf s-\hat{\mathbf s}_{\rm self}=\big(\mathbf s_{\rm self}(\mathbf u)+\mathbf s_{\rm ext}+\boldsymbol\nu\big)-\mathbf s_{\rm self}(\mathbf u)=\mathbf s_{\rm ext}+\boldsymbol\nu$. The self-generated term cancels exactly when the prediction is exact; any prediction error $\Delta=\mathbf s_{\rm self}-\hat{\mathbf s}_{\rm self}$ survives as $\mathbf r=\mathbf s_{\rm ext}+\boldsymbol\nu+\Delta$. ∎

This is why you cannot tickle yourself: the forward model predicts the touch your own hand will produce and cancels it, so the residual - the part that drives the ticklish response - is near zero, whereas another person's identical touch is unpredicted and survives in full. It is a direct, everyday readout of the forward model at work. Its counterpart, the inverse model, is what makes skilled movement look effortless: with practice the brain learns the command that produces the desired outcome, so feedback has little to correct - and the evidence that such a model exists and is learned comes from what happens when the world changes.

Strap a person's hand to a robot that pushes sideways in proportion to hand speed - a force field - and their first reaches hook badly off course. Over tens of trials the reaches straighten as the brain learns to pre-compensate. Remove the field without warning and the reaches hook the other way: an aftereffect. The aftereffect is the smoking gun of an internal model - the brain kept issuing the learned compensatory command into a field that was no longer there. Adaptation is the trial-to-trial updating of that model by error.

Proposition 6.2 (error-driven adaptation converges geometrically). Let $e_n$ be the movement error on trial $n$, produced by the mismatch between the internal model and the true perturbation, and suppose the model is updated by a fixed fraction of the observed error, $u_{n+1}=u_n+\eta\,e_n$, where $u_n$ is the compensatory command and $\eta\gt 0$ the learning rate. If error depends on the residual mismatch as $e_n=g\,(p-u_n)$ for a constant perturbation $p$ and error sensitivity $g\gt 0$ (a scalar constant, distinct from the plant's gravity term $\mathbf g$), then $$e_{n+1}=(1-\eta g)\,e_n,$$ so for $0\lt \eta g\lt 2$ the error decays geometrically to zero at rate $|1-\eta g|$, and the learned command converges to $u_\infty=p$; upon removing the perturbation ($p\to0$) the first error is $-u_\infty g=-pg$, the aftereffect.
From $e_n=g(p-u_n)$, $e_{n+1}=g(p-u_{n+1})=g(p-u_n-\eta e_n)=g(p-u_n)-\eta g\,e_n=e_n-\eta g\,e_n=(1-\eta g)e_n$. Hence $e_n=(1-\eta g)^n e_0\to0$ iff $|1-\eta g|\lt 1$, i.e. $0\lt \eta g\lt 2$, with geometric rate. At the fixed point $e_\infty=0$ forces $u_\infty=p$. Removing the perturbation sets the true field to $0$ while the command is still $u_\infty=p$; the resulting error is $e=g(0-u_\infty)=-pg$, equal and opposite to the initial, pre-adaptation error $e_0=gp$ - the full response the model had learned, now issued into an empty field - the aftereffect. ∎

Proposition 6.2 is the simplest quantitative model of motor learning, and it captures the coarse facts: an exponential learning curve, a rate set by $\eta g$, convergence of the command to the perturbation, and an aftereffect equal to what was learned. Real adaptation adds structure - fast and slow processes with different rates and retention (a two-state version of the same rule), savings on relearning, and generalization across the workspace - but all are elaborations of error-driven internal-model update, and all are downstream of the same principle: the brain does not just react, it models and predicts.

7. Optimal feedback control and the minimal-intervention principle

We now unify the module. Minimum jerk (Section 3) planned a trajectory but gave no dynamical reason to value smoothness and no correction rule; LQR (Section 4) corrected deviations but needed a trajectory to track; redundancy (Section 1) was left with an arbitrary null-space choice. Optimal feedback control (OFC), the framework of Todorov and Jordan, folds all three into one, and its central prediction - the minimal-intervention principle - is a statement about which errors the brain bothers to correct.

The key physiological fact OFC builds on is signal-dependent noise: the variability of a motor command grows with its magnitude, so larger, faster commands are noisier (Harris and Wolpert). This changes the optimization completely. There is no longer a noise-free trajectory to plan; every command adds variability scaled to its own size, and the sensible objective is not a smooth path but a small error at the goal.

Proposition 7.1 (smoothness from minimum endpoint variance). For a point-to-point movement of a system driven by commands corrupted by signal-dependent noise (command variance $\propto$ command squared), the control that minimizes the variance of the final position, subject to reaching the target on average, spreads the motor command smoothly over the whole movement rather than in abrupt bursts; passed through the limb's high-order (command-to-force) smoothing dynamics, that graded command yields a single-peaked velocity resembling the minimum-jerk trajectory of Section 3. Smoothness is then not an assumed cost but a consequence of minimizing endpoint error under realistic noise.
(Sketch; the quadratic-program step below is exact, and the bell shape follows once the plant's command-to-force smoothing is included.) Discretize the movement into steps with commands $u_k$; the state is a linear function of the commands, and the final position is $x_T=\sum_k b_k u_k$ for known weights $b_k$ (the plant's impulse response). Signal-dependent noise makes step $k$ contribute a variance $c\,u_k^2 w_k$ to the endpoint (for positive weights $w_k$ propagating that step's noise to the endpoint). Minimizing endpoint variance $\sum_k c\,w_k u_k^2$ subject to the mean-reaching constraint $\sum_k b_k u_k=x_T$ is a quadratic program; by Lagrange multipliers its solution spreads the command smoothly over the whole movement (each $u_k\propto b_k/w_k$) rather than concentrating it in large, noisy bursts. Large impulsive commands are penalized quadratically by the noise they inject, so the optimum spreads the command gradually; passed through the limb's command-to-force smoothing this graded command produces a single-peaked, bell-like velocity - the smoothness Section 3 imposed by fiat, here falling out of noise minimization. ∎

OFC then dispenses with the "plan a trajectory, track it" split entirely: it computes a single feedback law $\mathbf u=\pi(\hat{\mathbf x},t)$ (from the state estimate of Section 6) that directly minimizes the expected task cost plus effort under the noise. Because the cost is written on the task variables, not on a prescribed path, the controller has no reason to enforce any particular value of the redundant, task-irrelevant coordinates - and this is the minimal-intervention principle.

Proposition 7.2 (minimal intervention and the uncontrolled manifold). Consider two effectors whose outputs $y_1,y_2$ each carry independent noise of variance $\sigma^2$, with the task depending only on the task variable $t=y_1+y_2$ (e.g. total force or a summed position) and not on the null variable $n=y_1-y_2$. An optimal feedback controller that senses and corrects the task error, reducing task-direction variance by a loop factor $0\lt \rho\lt 1$, leaves the null variable uncorrected, so $$\operatorname{Var}(t)=\rho\cdot2\sigma^2,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(n)=2\sigma^2,\qquad \frac{\operatorname{Var}(n)}{\operatorname{Var}(t)}=\frac1\rho\gt 1.$$ Across repetitions, variability is largest in the task-irrelevant (null) direction - the uncontrolled-manifold signature - even though no controller ever "aims" for that structure.
Write $y_i=\mu_i+\varepsilon_i$ with $\varepsilon_i$ independent, variance $\sigma^2$. The null variable $n=y_1-y_2$ carries noise $\varepsilon_1-\varepsilon_2$ of variance $2\sigma^2$; since the task cost is independent of $n$, the optimal controller applies zero corrective gain there (correcting $n$ would spend effort - and, under signal-dependent noise, inject variance - for no reduction in task cost), so $\operatorname{Var}(n)=2\sigma^2$ is the uncontrolled level. The task variable $t=y_1+y_2$ carries noise $\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2$ of open-loop variance $2\sigma^2$; feedback that senses the task error suppresses it by a factor $\rho\lt 1$, giving $\operatorname{Var}(t)=\rho\cdot2\sigma^2$. The ratio is $1/\rho\gt 1$. ∎

Proposition 7.2 resolves the puzzle Bernstein left (Fig. 7). The nervous system does not "solve" the redundancy problem by eliminating the surplus degrees of freedom; it exploits them, correcting only what matters to the task and letting the rest vary freely. This is why skilled, repeated movements are variable precisely where variability is harmless - the pianist's finger trajectories differ trial to trial in ways that leave the struck key unchanged; the marksman's arm jitters along directions that do not move the sight line. The observed structure of motor variability - larger in task-irrelevant directions, the uncontrolled manifold of Scholz and Schöner - is a fingerprint of optimal feedback control, and synergies emerge as its consequence: coordinated patterns that hold the task variable steady while the null space breathes. Section 8 asks how such patterns are represented and generated.

8. Synergies and central pattern generators

Optimal feedback control explains which errors to correct, but a controller with a fifty-dimensional command still faces a representational burden. The nervous system lightens it by controlling not fifty muscles independently but a handful of coordinated patterns. Two ideas - muscle synergies for the spatial pattern and central pattern generators for the temporal - reduce the dimensionality of control to something a slow, noisy brain can manage.

Record the activity of many muscles during a repertoire of movements and stack it in a matrix $M$ (muscles $\times$ time). Empirically $M$ is close to low rank: a few fixed muscle groupings, scaled by a few time-varying coefficients, reconstruct most of the activity.

Definition 7 (muscle synergy). A muscle synergy is a fixed vector $\mathbf w_i\in\mathbb R^{\#\text{muscles}}$ of relative activations - a coordinated group of muscles recruited together. Under the synergy hypothesis the muscle-activation vector at time $t$ is a combination of a small number $k$ of synergies, $\mathbf m(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}c_i(t)\,\mathbf w_i$, with time-varying recruitment coefficients $c_i(t)\ge0$. The controller sets the $k$ coefficients, not the individual muscles.
Proposition 8.1 (synergies collapse the control dimension). If muscle activity over a task set is exactly a nonnegative combination of $k$ synergies, the muscle-activation matrix $M$ (muscles $\times$ samples) factors as $M=WC$ with $W$ (muscles $\times k$) and $C$ ($k\times$ samples), so $\operatorname{rank}M\le k$. The controller then needs only the $k$ rows of $C$ - the coefficients - to specify any activation in the set, reducing the command dimension from the number of muscles to $k$. The quality of the reduction is the variance accounted for, $\text{VAF}=1-\|M-WC\|_F^2/\|M\|_F^2$.
By hypothesis each column $\mathbf m(t_j)=\sum_{i=1}^k c_i(t_j)\mathbf w_i=W\mathbf c(t_j)$ where $W=[\mathbf w_1\ \cdots\ \mathbf w_k]$ and $\mathbf c(t_j)$ is the coefficient vector; stacking columns gives $M=WC$ with $C=[\mathbf c(t_1)\ \cdots]$. The column space of $M$ lies in the span of the $k$ columns of $W$, so $\operatorname{rank}M\le k$. Specifying $M$ therefore requires only $C$ once $W$ is fixed - $k$ numbers per time sample rather than one per muscle. When the factorization is approximate, $M\approx WC$ is the best rank-$k$ nonnegative fit and $\text{VAF}$ measures how much of the data's energy it captures. ∎

Synergies are extracted from data by nonnegative matrix factorization (the nonnegativity enforcing that muscles pull, never push) or, more loosely, by principal component analysis; a handful typically account for $80$-$90\%$ of the variance across postures and gaits. Whether synergies are hard-wired modules or an emergent low-rank shadow of optimal control (Proposition 7.2's structured variability) is debated - but either way they are the practical basis in which the brain writes commands, a low-dimensional coordinate system for the null space.

Rhythmic movements - walking (Module 8), chewing, breathing, swimming - add a temporal counterpart. Their timing is produced by central pattern generators (CPGs): networks of neurons, largely in the spinal cord and brainstem, that generate rhythmic output without rhythmic input. The decisive evidence is that a deafferented or isolated preparation, cut off from sensory feedback, still produces the rhythm - the oscillation is central, not a reflex chain. The minimal model is a set of coupled oscillators, and their essential behaviour is entrainment: coupled oscillators lock to a common rhythm.

Proposition 8.2 (two coupled oscillators entrain). Let two phase oscillators have intrinsic frequencies $\omega_1,\omega_2$ and sinusoidal coupling of strength $K\gt 0$: $$\dot\phi_1=\omega_1+K\sin(\phi_2-\phi_1),\qquad \dot\phi_2=\omega_2+K\sin(\phi_1-\phi_2).$$ Their phase difference $\psi=\phi_2-\phi_1$ obeys $\dot\psi=\Delta\omega-2K\sin\psi$ with $\Delta\omega=\omega_2-\omega_1$. A phase-locked state ($\dot\psi=0$) exists if and only if $$\boxed{\;|\Delta\omega|\le 2K,\;}$$ at $\psi^\star=\arcsin\!\big(\Delta\omega/2K\big)$; the locked solution is stable, so mismatched oscillators within the coupling bandwidth synchronize to a common frequency with a fixed phase lag.
Subtract the two equations: $\dot\psi=\dot\phi_2-\dot\phi_1=(\omega_2-\omega_1)+K\sin(\phi_1-\phi_2)-K\sin(\phi_2-\phi_1)=\Delta\omega-2K\sin\psi$, using $\sin(-\psi)=-\sin\psi$. A fixed point needs $\sin\psi^\star=\Delta\omega/(2K)$, solvable for real $\psi^\star$ iff $|\Delta\omega/(2K)|\le1$, i.e. $|\Delta\omega|\le2K$. Linearizing about $\psi^\star$, $\frac{d}{d\psi}(\Delta\omega-2K\sin\psi)=-2K\cos\psi^\star$; choosing the root with $\cos\psi^\star\gt 0$ makes this negative, so that fixed point is asymptotically stable. Hence within the locking bandwidth $2K$ the oscillators synchronize. ∎

Proposition 8.2 (Fig. 8) is the mechanism behind gait coordination, the entrainment of stepping to a metronome or a treadmill, and the stability of interlimb rhythms; the same Adler equation governs synchronizing fireflies and coupled clocks. It also explains why rhythmic movement is metabolically cheap to control: the CPG supplies the pattern open-loop, feedback only nudges its phase (Module 8's push-off timing), and the whole gait is specified by a few oscillator parameters rather than a moment-by-moment command. Together, synergies and CPGs are how the brain writes a low-dimensional program that the optimal feedback controller of Section 7 then shapes to the moment.

Lab A - the minimum-jerk reach

Physical question. What do the position, velocity, and acceleration of a smooth reach look like, and is the speed profile really scale-invariant? Model. The minimum-jerk polynomial of Proposition 3.1. Parameters. distance $D=0.4\ \mathrm{m}$, duration $T=0.5\ \mathrm{s}$. Equations. $x=D(10\tau^3-15\tau^4+6\tau^5)$, $\tau=t/T$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

D, T = 0.40, 0.50
t = np.linspace(0, T, 400)
tau = t/T
x = D*(10*tau**3 - 15*tau**4 + 6*tau**5)
v = D/T*(30*tau**2 - 60*tau**3 + 30*tau**4)
a = D/T**2*(60*tau - 180*tau**2 + 120*tau**3)

print("peak speed %.3f m/s = 1.875 D/T = %.3f" % (v.max(), 1.875*D/T))
fig, ax = plt.subplots(3, 1, sharex=True)
ax[0].plot(t, x)
ax[1].plot(t, v)
ax[2].plot(t, a)
for a_, lb in zip(ax, ("pos (m)", "vel (m/s)", "acc (m/s^2)")):
    a_.set_ylabel(lb)
plt.show()

Interpretation. Position is a smooth S-curve; velocity a symmetric bell peaking at $1.5\ \mathrm{m/s}=1.875\,D/T$ at mid-movement; acceleration an antisymmetric wave with equal accelerate/decelerate phases, peaking at $\pm9.24\ \mathrm{m/s^2}=5.77\,D/T^2$ near $\tau\approx0.21$ and $0.79$. Sensitivity. Every profile scales as $D/T^{k}$ for the $k$-th derivative, so halving the duration doubles peak speed and quadruples peak acceleration - the reason fast reaches demand disproportionately large forces (Module 11's inertial torque). Failure modes. The model prescribes a path, not a force; it ignores the arm's inertia and gravity, which the inverse dynamics of Module 11 must supply to realise it. Extension. Plot the speed profile of a $0.05\ \mathrm{m}$ and a $0.5\ \mathrm{m}$ reach on a normalised time axis and confirm they superimpose exactly - scale invariance.

Lab B - the LQR gain trade

Physical question. How does the optimal feedback gain, and the speed of recovery, change as effort is priced more dearly? Model. A point-mass hand (double integrator $\ddot x=u$) with LQR (Proposition 4.1), solving the Riccati equation with SciPy. Parameters. $Q=\operatorname{diag}(1,0)$ (penalise position error), $R\in\{0.01,1,100\}$. Equations. $K=R^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P$, $A^{\mathsf T}P+PA-PBR^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P+Q=0$.

import numpy as np
from scipy.linalg import solve_continuous_are
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

A = np.array([[0.0, 1.0], [0.0, 0.0]])
B = np.array([[0.0], [1.0]])
Q = np.diag([1.0, 0.0])
t = np.linspace(0, 10, 500)
dt = t[1] - t[0]
for R in (0.01, 1.0, 100.0):
    P = solve_continuous_are(A, B, Q, np.array([[R]]))
    K = (B.T @ P/R).reshape(1, 2)
    x = np.array([1.0, 0.0])           # start 1 m off target
    xs = []
    for _ in t:
        x = x + dt*(A - B @ K) @ x
        xs.append(x[0])
    print("R=%.2f  K=%s" % (R, np.round(K.flatten(), 3)))
    plt.plot(t, xs, label="R=%.2f" % R)
plt.legend()
plt.xlabel("time (s)")
plt.ylabel("position error (m)")
plt.show()

Interpretation. As $R$ falls from $100$ to $0.01$ the gains rise from $K=[0.1,0.45]$ to $[10,4.5]$ and the closed-loop poles move left from $-0.22\pm0.22i$ to $-2.24\pm2.24i$: cheap effort buys fast, tight correction; dear effort gives slow, gentle recovery. Sensitivity. For this double integrator the position gain scales as $K_1=\sqrt{1/R}$ and the recovery rate as $R^{-1/4}$, so a hundredfold change in the effort price shifts recovery speed only about threefold (the tenfold change is in the gain $K_1=\sqrt{1/R}$, not the recovery rate) - the trade has diminishing returns. Failure modes. Infinite gain ($R\to0$) is optimal only in this noise- and delay-free model; add Module 10's delay and the high-gain limit oscillates and destabilises, which is why real gains stay finite. Extension. Add signal-dependent noise to $u$ and find the $R$ that minimises endpoint variance - it is nonzero, the effort term earning its place.

Lab C - impedance versus position control in contact

Physical question. Why is a compliant (impedance) controller safe on contact while a high-gain position servo is dangerous? Model. A point mass commanded to a target $5\ \mathrm{cm}$ behind a rigid wall; compare an impedance controller and a stiff position servo. Parameters. wall at $0.10\ \mathrm{m}$, target $0.15\ \mathrm{m}$; impedance $K=200\ \mathrm{N/m}$, servo $K_p=5000\ \mathrm{N/m}$. Equations. $F=K(x_{\rm eq}-x)-D\dot x$.

import numpy as np

m, dt, xw, x_des = 1.0, 0.001, 0.10, 0.15


def run(kctrl, dctrl):
    x, v, fmax = 0.0, 0.0, 0.0
    for _ in range(4000):
        f = kctrl*(x_des - x) - dctrl*v
        fwall = -1e5*(x - xw) if x > xw else 0.0
        v += dt*(f + fwall)/m
        x += dt*v
        if x >= xw - 1e-4:
            fmax = max(fmax, f)
    return fmax


print("impedance push  %.0f N" % run(200.0, 30.0))
print("position servo  %.0f N" % run(5000.0, 150.0))

Interpretation. Both controllers reach the wall and cannot pass it, but the steady force they press with is $K(x_{\rm des}-x_{\rm wall})$: the impedance controller settles at $200\times0.05=10\ \mathrm{N}$, the stiff servo at $5000\times0.05=250\ \mathrm{N}$ - $25\times$ larger, enough to damage the object or the arm. Sensitivity. Contact force scales linearly with stiffness and with the penetration the controller "wants," so lowering stiffness or the commanded overshoot both cut the force. Failure modes. A truly rigid wall plus a high position gain is not just forceful but can go unstable (the discrete loop overshoots and rings); impedance with $K,D\succeq0$ is passive and stays stable. Extension. Give the wall a finite stiffness and plot contact force versus wall stiffness for both controllers; the impedance force saturates while the servo force climbs.

Lab D - the minimal-intervention signature

Physical question. Does correcting only the task variable really leave more variability in the task-irrelevant direction? Model. Two noisy effectors whose sum is the task (Proposition 7.2); feedback suppresses task-direction variance, the null direction is left free. Parameters. per-effector noise $\sigma=1$, residual task fraction $\rho=0.15$, $N=4000$ trials. Equations. $t=y_1+y_2$, $n=y_1-y_2$.

import numpy as np

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
N, sigma, rho = 4000, 1.0, 0.15
n1 = rng.normal(0, sigma, N)
n2 = rng.normal(0, sigma, N)
task = n1 + n2                       # task error before correction
corr = (1 - np.sqrt(rho))*task/2     # feedback shares task correction
y1, y2 = n1 - corr, n2 - corr
t, n = y1 + y2, y1 - y2
print("Var task  %.2f" % np.var(t))
print("Var null  %.2f" % np.var(n))
print("ratio     %.2f  (predicted 1/rho = %.2f)"
      % (np.var(n)/np.var(t), 1/rho))

Interpretation. The cloud of $(y_1,y_2)$ outcomes is an ellipse stretched along the null direction $y_1=-y_2$ (variance $2.01$) and squeezed along the task direction $y_1=y_2$ (variance $0.30$), a ratio of $6.65$ - matching the predicted $1/\rho=6.67$. Variability is largest exactly where it does not affect the task. Sensitivity. The ratio is set entirely by the loop's task suppression $\rho$; a tighter task loop (smaller $\rho$) makes the ellipse more elongated, more clearly aligned with the uncontrolled manifold. Failure modes. If the controller wastefully corrects the null direction too, the ellipse rounds out and the signature disappears - the hallmark of a non-optimal, over-constraining controller. Extension. Replace the summed task with a redundant reach (two joints, one hand coordinate) and confirm joint-angle variance aligns with the arm's null space.

11. Problems, diagnostics, and limitations

Jump to: Conceptual · Derivational · Computational · Diagnostics · Limitations · Repayment

Thirty problems - ten conceptual, ten derivational, ten computational - plus five diagnostics. Each carries a Probes note and a collapsible worked solution; computational solutions quote numbers the code produces.

Conceptual ↑ contents

C1 - the two signatures. Your signature on paper and on a whiteboard is the same shape, made with entirely different muscles and joints. What does this "motor equivalence" imply about the level at which the movement is planned? Probes: task-level control and the degrees-of-freedom problem; Section 0, Section 1.
Solution
It implies the plan is stored at the level of the task (the shape, a hand-path goal), not as a fixed set of muscle commands. Because many joint/muscle patterns realise the same shape (Proposition 1.1's continuum of solutions), the controller specifies the task variable and lets the redundant degrees of freedom be filled in by whatever effectors are recruited - fingers and wrist for the small version, elbow and shoulder for the large. Motor equivalence is direct evidence that the brain controls task variables and resolves redundancy downstream, exactly the picture optimal feedback control formalises.
C2 - fast or slow, same shape. A reach's speed profile is a symmetric bell whether the movement is quick or slow, near or far. Why does one cost - minimum jerk - predict this scale invariance? Probes: scale invariance of the minimum-jerk trajectory; Section 3.
Solution
The minimum-jerk trajectory is $x_0+(x_f-x_0)(10\tau^3-15\tau^4+6\tau^5)$ with $\tau=t/T$: the bracket depends only on the fraction of the movement completed, and the amplitude $(x_f-x_0)$ and duration $T$ enter only as multiplicative scales. So the normalised speed profile is identical for every reach; changing distance or duration stretches the axes but not the shape. Scale invariance is thus a structural consequence of the cost depending on jerk alone, and its match to data is why minimum jerk is credited as the reach's organising principle.
C3 - the failed self-tickle. You cannot tickle yourself, but another person's identical touch tickles. Explain with the forward model. Probes: reafference cancellation; Section 6.
Solution
A forward model predicts the sensory consequences of your own motor command from an efference copy, and the nervous system subtracts this prediction from the actual sensation (Proposition 6.1), leaving a residual of only externally-caused feeling. A self-touch is well predicted, so it cancels to near zero and does not drive the ticklish response; another person's touch is unpredicted, survives the subtraction in full, and tickles. The failed self-tickle is a direct readout of the forward model cancelling reafference.
C4 - stiffening in the dark. Faced with an unstable or unpredictable contact task, people co-contract, stiffening the limb. Why is this the right first response, and what does it cost? Probes: impedance, passivity, co-contraction; Section 5.
Solution
Co-contraction raises the joint stiffness $K_q$ and hence the endpoint stiffness $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}$ and damping. A positive-definite stiffness/damping is a passive impedance, which stays stable in contact with any passive environment without needing to know or predict it - so stiffening buys guaranteed stability against an uncertain or unstable interaction before any model is learned. It costs metabolic energy (both muscles working against each other) and it dulls fine control, which is why co-contraction fades as the task becomes predictable and a lower-gain, more efficient impedance suffices.
C5 - the servo against the wall. Why is a stiff, high-gain position servo dangerous when it touches a rigid wall, while a compliant limb is safe? Probes: impedance versus position control; Section 5.
Solution
A position servo, seeing a persistent error when the wall blocks its target, drives force proportional to its gain times the "wanted" penetration - a high gain means a huge, potentially damaging or unstable force (Lab C: $250\ \mathrm{N}$ vs $10\ \mathrm{N}$). An impedance-controlled limb instead presents a spring-damper of chosen stiffness; the contact force is bounded to (stiffness) $\times$ (commanded overshoot), and a passive impedance cannot inject energy, so it stays stable. Controlling the force-motion relationship, not position, is what makes contact safe.
C6 - the opposite error. After training in a force field, removing it makes reaches curve the other way. Why is this "aftereffect" proof of an internal model? Probes: internal-model learning and aftereffects; Section 6.
Solution
If the brain merely reacted to forces by feedback, removing the field would leave reaches straight. Instead they curve opposite to the trained field, because the brain has built a model of the field and issues a feedforward command that pre-compensates for it; with the field gone, that learned command is unopposed and pushes the hand the other way (Proposition 6.2, aftereffect $=-pg$). The aftereffect shows the compensation was internally generated and predictive, not a reflex - the signature of an internal model.
C7 - variable but accurate. A pianist's finger follows a slightly different path every time, yet the struck key is unchanged. How does optimal feedback control explain variability that spares the task? Probes: minimal intervention and the uncontrolled manifold; Section 7.
Solution
Optimal feedback control corrects only deviations that raise the task cost and leaves task-irrelevant deviations uncorrected (the minimal-intervention principle), because correcting them would spend effort - and inject signal-dependent noise - for no task benefit. So variability accumulates freely in the null space (directions that leave the struck key unchanged) and is suppressed in the task direction (Proposition 7.2). The finger path varies most where it does not matter; the note is held constant. Variability structured this way is the uncontrolled-manifold fingerprint of an optimal controller exploiting redundancy.
C8 - cheap walking. Rhythmic movements like walking are metabolically cheap to control, not just to power. Why? Probes: central pattern generators; Section 8.
Solution
A central pattern generator produces the rhythm open-loop - a spinal oscillator network generates the stepping pattern without moment-by-moment commands from the brain, and even without sensory feedback (deafferentation preserves the rhythm). The higher controller then only sets a few oscillator parameters (frequency, amplitude) and nudges the phase through feedback (Module 8's push-off timing), rather than specifying every muscle at every instant. Reducing control to a handful of oscillator parameters is what makes rhythmic movement cheap to command.
C9 - a few knobs, many muscles. Why does the nervous system control a handful of synergies rather than fifty muscles independently? Probes: dimensionality reduction and the DOF problem; Section 8.
Solution
Controlling fifty muscles independently is a fifty-dimensional command problem - Bernstein's degrees-of-freedom burden - too high-dimensional for a slow, noisy controller to search or learn efficiently. Muscle activity across tasks is empirically low-rank (Proposition 8.1): a few fixed synergies, scaled by a few time-varying coefficients, reconstruct most activation. Commanding the coefficients collapses the control dimension from muscles to synergies (often $3$-$5$), a low-dimensional coordinate system in which planning, learning, and noise are all more manageable.
C10 - why gains stay finite. The LQR says lower effort cost gives higher gain and faster correction, seemingly without limit. Why do real feedback gains stay modest? Probes: delay- and noise-bounded gain; Section 4, Section 6, Module 10.
Solution
The unbounded-gain optimum holds only in the idealised noise- and delay-free LQR. Real feedback acts on state that is delayed ($100$-$200\ \mathrm{ms}$) and noisy: high gains on stale information drive the delayed-feedback instability of Module 10 (a first-order delayed loop is stable only for gain $k\lt \pi/2\tau$), and under signal-dependent noise high gains amplify command noise into the movement. So the useful gain is capped by delay and noise, which is exactly why the brain leans on prediction (internal models, Section 6) to get a fresh state estimate rather than simply cranking the gain up.

Derivational ↑ contents

D1 - the Euler-Poisson equation. Derive the necessary condition a minimizer of $\int_0^T L(t,x,\dot x,\dots,x^{(p)})\,dt$ must satisfy. Probes: calculus of variations with higher derivatives; Section 2.
Solution
Perturb $x\to x+\varepsilon\eta$ with $\eta$ and its first $p-1$ derivatives vanishing at both ends. Stationarity $\frac{d}{d\varepsilon}\mathcal J|_0=0$ gives $\int_0^T\sum_k\frac{\partial L}{\partial x^{(k)}}\eta^{(k)}dt=0$. Integrate the $k$-th term by parts $k$ times; boundary terms vanish by the conditions on $\eta$, leaving $\int_0^T[\sum_k(-1)^k\frac{d^k}{dt^k}\frac{\partial L}{\partial x^{(k)}}]\eta\,dt=0$. Since $\eta$ is arbitrary, the bracket vanishes: $\sum_{k=0}^p(-1)^k\frac{d^k}{dt^k}\frac{\partial L}{\partial x^{(k)}}=0$ (Lemma 2.1).
D2 - the minimum-jerk polynomial. Show the jerk-minimizing trajectory with fixed endpoints at rest is a fifth-order polynomial, and find it. Probes: applying Euler-Poisson; Section 3.
Solution
With $L=\tfrac12(x^{(3)})^2$, the only nonzero derivative in Euler-Poisson ($p=3$) is $\partial L/\partial x^{(3)}=x^{(3)}$, giving $-\frac{d^3}{dt^3}x^{(3)}=-x^{(6)}=0$, so $x$ is degree $\le5$. Six boundary conditions ($x,\dot x,\ddot x$ at each end) fix the six coefficients; solving gives $x(t)=x_0+(x_f-x_0)(10\tau^3-15\tau^4+6\tau^5)$, $\tau=t/T$ (Proposition 3.1).
D3 - the 1.875 ratio. Derive the peak speed of a minimum-jerk reach and show it is $1.875$ times the average speed. Probes: differentiating the trajectory; Section 3.
Solution
Differentiate: $\dot x=\frac{x_f-x_0}{T}(30\tau^2-60\tau^3+30\tau^4)=\frac{30(x_f-x_0)}{T}\tau^2(1-\tau)^2$. The factor $\tau^2(1-\tau)^2$ is symmetric about $\tau=\tfrac12$ and maximal there, value $(\tfrac12)^2(\tfrac12)^2=\tfrac1{16}$. So $\dot x_{\max}=\frac{30}{16}\frac{x_f-x_0}{T}=\frac{15}{8}\frac{x_f-x_0}{T}=1.875\frac{x_f-x_0}{T}$, and since $(x_f-x_0)/T$ is the average speed, the peak-to-mean ratio is $1.875$.
D4 - the LQR gain. Derive the optimal LQR feedback and the algebraic Riccati equation via the HJB equation. Probes: dynamic programming, the value function; Section 4.
Solution
Take value $V=\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x$. HJB: $0=\min_{\mathbf u}[\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}Q\mathbf x+\mathbf u^{\mathsf T}R\mathbf u+2\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}P(A\mathbf x+B\mathbf u)]$. Minimizing over $\mathbf u$: $2R\mathbf u+2B^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x=0\Rightarrow\mathbf u=-R^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x=-K\mathbf x$. Substituting and symmetrising ($2\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}PA\mathbf x=\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}(A^{\mathsf T}P+PA)\mathbf x$) gives $\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}(A^{\mathsf T}P+PA-PBR^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P+Q)\mathbf x=0$ for all $\mathbf x$, hence the ARE $A^{\mathsf T}P+PA-PBR^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P+Q=0$ (Proposition 4.1).
D5 - endpoint stiffness. Derive $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}$ from joint stiffness and the force-torque duality. Probes: transforming impedance through the Jacobian; Section 5.
Solution
An endpoint displacement $\delta\mathbf x$ gives joint change $\delta\mathbf q=J^{-1}\delta\mathbf x$; the joints resist with $\delta\boldsymbol\tau=-K_q\delta\mathbf q=-K_qJ^{-1}\delta\mathbf x$; by the duality $\delta\mathbf F=J^{-\mathsf T}\delta\boldsymbol\tau=-J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}\delta\mathbf x$, so $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}$ (Proposition 5.1). Posture ($J$) rotates and reshapes the ellipse; co-contraction ($K_q$) scales it.
D6 - reafference cancellation. Show that subtracting a forward-model prediction of self-generated sensation leaves only externally-caused sensation plus noise. Probes: the prediction residual; Section 6.
Solution
With $\mathbf s=\mathbf s_{\rm self}(\mathbf u)+\mathbf s_{\rm ext}+\boldsymbol\nu$ and prediction $\hat{\mathbf s}_{\rm self}=\mathbf s_{\rm self}(\mathbf u)$, the residual is $\mathbf r=\mathbf s-\hat{\mathbf s}_{\rm self}=\mathbf s_{\rm ext}+\boldsymbol\nu$ (Proposition 6.1). A prediction error $\Delta$ survives as $\mathbf r=\mathbf s_{\rm ext}+\boldsymbol\nu+\Delta$, so accurate prediction is what makes cancellation clean.
D7 - adaptation dynamics. Derive the trial-to-trial error recursion for error-driven adaptation, its convergence condition, and the aftereffect. Probes: the learning recursion; Section 6.
Solution
With update $u_{n+1}=u_n+\eta e_n$ and error $e_n=g(p-u_n)$: $e_{n+1}=g(p-u_n-\eta e_n)=e_n-\eta g\,e_n=(1-\eta g)e_n$. So $e_n=(1-\eta g)^n e_0\to0$ iff $0\lt \eta g\lt 2$, geometric rate $|1-\eta g|$; the command converges to $u_\infty=p$, and removing the perturbation gives first error $-pg$, the aftereffect (Proposition 6.2).
D8 - the UCM variance ratio. For two noisy effectors whose sum is the task, derive the ratio of null-direction to task-direction variance under a task-correcting controller. Probes: minimal intervention; Section 7.
Solution
Independent noises of variance $\sigma^2$ give the null variable $n=y_1-y_2$ variance $2\sigma^2$ (uncorrected, since the task cost is $n$-independent) and the task variable $t=y_1+y_2$ open-loop variance $2\sigma^2$, suppressed by the loop to $\rho\cdot2\sigma^2$. The ratio $\operatorname{Var}(n)/\operatorname{Var}(t)=1/\rho\gt 1$ (Proposition 7.2): variability is largest in the task-irrelevant direction.
D9 - oscillator locking. Derive the phase-difference dynamics of two coupled oscillators and the condition for phase-locking. Probes: the Adler equation; Section 8.
Solution
Subtracting the two oscillator equations gives $\dot\psi=\Delta\omega-2K\sin\psi$ ($\psi=\phi_2-\phi_1$). A locked state $\dot\psi=0$ needs $\sin\psi^\star=\Delta\omega/2K$, solvable iff $|\Delta\omega|\le2K$; linearising, the root with $\cos\psi^\star\gt 0$ is stable. So oscillators within coupling bandwidth $2K$ synchronize at phase lag $\psi^\star=\arcsin(\Delta\omega/2K)$ (Proposition 8.2).
D10 - synergy rank bound. Show that if muscle activity is a combination of $k$ synergies, the activation matrix has rank at most $k$, and the control dimension collapses to $k$. Probes: low-rank factorization; Section 8.
Solution
Each activation column is $\mathbf m(t_j)=W\mathbf c(t_j)$ with $W$ the $(\text{muscles}\times k)$ synergy matrix; stacking gives $M=WC$, whose column space lies in the $k$ columns of $W$, so $\operatorname{rank}M\le k$. Fixing $W$, specifying $M$ needs only the $k$ coefficients per sample - the command dimension drops from the number of muscles to $k$ (Proposition 8.1).

Computational ↑ contents

Each requires simulation, optimisation, an inverse solve, a sensitivity sweep, or a regime comparison - not substitution into a boxed formula. Numbers quoted are those the code produces.

K1 - which smoothness cost? (regime comparison). Compute and compare the speed profiles that minimise squared jerk versus squared acceleration for the same reach, and their peak-to-mean speed ratios. Which matches reaching data? Probes: distinguishing smoothness costs by their signature; Section 3.
Solution
Minimum jerk gives the fifth-order polynomial with peak/mean $=1.875$; minimum acceleration (Euler-Poisson $p=2\Rightarrow x^{(4)}=0$, a cubic) gives $\dot x\propto\tau(1-\tau)$, a flatter parabola with peak/mean $=1.500$. Measured reaches show peak/mean near $1.7$-$1.9$, so the minimum-jerk profile - the more sharply peaked one - fits, while minimum acceleration is too flat. The peak-to-mean ratio is a parameter-free discriminator between candidate costs.
K2 - tuning the LQR (sensitivity + inverse). For the point-mass reach, sweep the effort weight $R$ and compute the closed-loop settling time; then find the $R$ giving a target $\sim1.8\ \mathrm{s}$ settling. Probes: the effort-speed trade and inverting for a target; Section 4.
Solution
Solving the Riccati equation and taking the $2\%$ settling time $\approx4/|\mathrm{Re}\,\lambda|$: $R=0.01\to1.79\ \mathrm{s}$, $R=0.1\to3.18\ \mathrm{s}$, $R=1\to5.66\ \mathrm{s}$, $R=10\to10.1\ \mathrm{s}$. Settling time scales as $R^{1/4}$, so a hundredfold change in effort price shifts speed only $\sim3.2\times$. To hit $\sim1.8\ \mathrm{s}$ one needs $R\approx0.01$ - a low effort price, i.e. an aggressive, high-gain controller. The inverse problem picks the cost that yields the observed movement speed.
K3 - orienting the stiffness ellipse (optimisation). For a peg-in-hole task with a given insertion axis, search postures/co-contraction to orient the endpoint stiffness ellipse $K_x=J^{-\mathsf T}K_qJ^{-1}$ stiff along the axis and compliant across it. Probes: shaping impedance by posture; Section 5.
Solution
Compute $K_x$ over a range of elbow angles (with $K_q$ from co-contraction), diagonalise it, and score the alignment of its stiff eigenvector with the insertion axis and the ratio of stiff-to-compliant eigenvalues. The optimum is the posture whose Jacobian rotates the joint-stiffness ellipse so its long axis lies along the insertion direction; there the peg is pushed firmly in while sliding compliantly into alignment. The search shows the arm shapes its mechanical impedance by where it works, not only by how hard it co-contracts - a computation with a clear optimum, not a plug-in.
K4 - force-field adaptation (simulation + stability sweep). Simulate error-driven adaptation to a constant perturbation, plot the learning curve and aftereffect, and sweep the learning rate $\eta$ to find where learning diverges. Probes: the learning recursion and its stability bound; Section 6.
Solution
Iterating $u_{n+1}=u_n+\eta e_n$, $e_n=g(p-u_n)$ with $g=1$, $p=1$: $\eta=0.2$ decays at rate $0.80$ per trial, $\eta=0.5$ at $0.50$, $\eta=1.0$ in a single step (rate $0$); all converge to $u_\infty=p=1$ with aftereffect $-1$. At $\eta=2.5$, $\eta g=2.5\gt 2$ and the error diverges (rate $1.5\gt 1$). The stability boundary is exactly $\eta g\lt 2$; the fastest stable learning is $\eta g=1$ (deadbeat). The sweep locates the boundary a plug-in never would.
K5 - fast and slow learning (simulation). Simulate a two-state adaptation model (a fast, poorly-retaining process plus a slow, well-retaining one) and reproduce spontaneous recovery: after adaptation and a brief reversal, the error rebounds toward the trained state. Probes: multi-rate internal-model learning; Section 6.
Solution
Let $x=x_f+x_s$ with $x_{f,n+1}=A_f x_{f,n}+B_f e_n$ (small retention $A_f$, large gain $B_f$) and $x_{s,n+1}=A_s x_{s,n}+B_s e_n$ (large $A_s$, small $B_s$). During adaptation both rise; the fast process leads. On a sudden reversal the fast process flips quickly and cancels the slow, driving net error to zero; then, with error near zero, the fast process decays (poor retention) while the slow persists, so the net command rebounds toward the original adaptation - spontaneous recovery, which a single-rate model cannot produce. The simulation surfaces a qualitative phenomenon (rebound) invisible to the one-state formula.
K6 - smoothness from noise (optimisation). With signal-dependent command noise, numerically find the command sequence that minimises endpoint variance subject to reaching the target, and show the resulting speed profile is bell-shaped. Probes: the noise justification for smoothness; Section 7.
Solution
Discretise the reach; endpoint variance is $\sum_k c\,w_k u_k^2$ (each command's noise $\propto u_k^2$ propagated to the endpoint), minimised subject to $\sum_k b_k u_k=x_T$. The quadratic program's solution spreads the command smoothly over the movement ($u_k\propto b_k/w_k$) rather than in large bursts, producing a graded, bell-shaped velocity close to minimum jerk. Solving the optimisation shows smoothness emerges from minimising variance under noise - it need not be assumed, answering C2's "why smooth?" at a deeper level.
K7 - the uncontrolled manifold in a redundant reach (joint-space simulation). A two-link arm reaches a target that fixes only the hand's horizontal position - a one-dimensional task, leaving a one-dimensional joint null space. Add independent motor noise to each joint under a controller that corrects only the hand-position error, and measure how joint-angle variability splits between the null and task-relevant joint combinations. Probes: the minimal-intervention signature realised in joint space; Section 7, Module 11.
Solution
At the reach posture the hand-position row of the Jacobian has null vector $(0.89,0.45)$ - the joint change that leaves the hand fixed. Simulating $N=4000$ noisy reaches ($0.02\ \mathrm{rad}$ per joint) with a controller that nulls the hand-position error, the joint-angle scatter is an ellipse aligned with that null direction: the variance in the null combination ($3.9\times10^{-4}\ \mathrm{rad^2}$) is $6.4\times$ the variance in the task-relevant combination ($6\times10^{-5}\ \mathrm{rad^2}$). The two joints co-vary to hold the hand steady while their task-irrelevant combination wanders freely - Bernstein's redundancy resolved in the arm itself, the uncontrolled manifold of Section 7 realised in joint space rather than the abstract two-effector sum of Lab D.
K8 - entrainment bandwidth (simulation + inverse). Integrate two coupled oscillators; find the maximum frequency mismatch that still locks as a function of coupling $K$, and the locked phase lag. Probes: the locking bandwidth and phase lag; Section 8.
Solution
Integrating $\dot\psi=\Delta\omega-2K\sin\psi$, the oscillators lock whenever $|\Delta\omega|\le2K$ and drift (beat) beyond it: bandwidth $=2K$ ($=0.5,1.0,2.0\ \mathrm{rad/s}$ for $K=0.25,0.5,1.0$). At $\Delta\omega=K$ the integrated phase lag settles to $\arcsin(1/2)=30^\circ$, matching the prediction. The inverse - the critical mismatch for locking - is read directly off the bandwidth, and the simulation confirms the sharp lock/drift transition.
K9 - counting synergies (dimensionality / inverse). Generate synthetic multi-muscle activity from a few synergies plus noise, extract components, and find how many synergies are needed to reach $90\%$ variance accounted for. Probes: dimensionality of control; Section 8.
Solution
For eight muscles generated from three synergies with $5\%$ noise, the cumulative variance accounted for is $0.785$ (k=1), $0.951$ (k=2), $0.998$ (k=3): two-to-three components exceed $90\%$, recovering the true low dimensionality. The VAF-versus-$k$ curve's elbow identifies the number of synergies - an inverse/dimensionality result that reduces an eight-muscle command to a three-number one, the practical face of Proposition 8.1.
K10 - gain limited by delay (regime comparison). For a delayed feedback loop, compute the maximum stable feedback gain as a function of the delay, and connect it to Module 10's balance loop. Probes: why prediction, not gain, beats delay; Section 4, Module 10.
Solution
A first-order delayed loop $\dot x=-k\,x(t-\tau)$ is stable iff $k\tau\lt \pi/2$, so the maximum stable gain is $k_{\max}=\pi/(2\tau)$: $31.4,15.7,10.5,7.9\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$ for $\tau=0.05,0.10,0.15,0.20\ \mathrm{s}$. Doubling the delay halves the usable gain - the same delay-bounded-gain law that set Module 10's stability island. The comparison shows that beyond the delay-set ceiling, increasing gain destabilises rather than helps, which is why the nervous system invests in prediction (Section 6) to act on a fresh state estimate instead.

Five diagnostics ↑ contents

  1. Units of jerk. What are the units of jerk and of the minimum-jerk cost $\int(\dddot x)^2dt$?
    Solution
    Jerk is $\mathrm{m/s^3}$; its square is $\mathrm{m^2/s^6}$; integrated over time ($\mathrm{s}$) the cost has units $\mathrm{m^2/s^5}$.
  2. Riccati symmetry. Why must the LQR cost matrix $R$ be positive-definite, not merely semidefinite?
    Solution
    The optimal control $\mathbf u=-R^{-1}B^{\mathsf T}P\mathbf x$ needs $R^{-1}$; $R\succ0$ guarantees invertibility (a zero-cost control direction would permit unbounded effort).
  3. Passivity. Why does a spring-damper impedance with $K_x,D_x\succeq0$ guarantee stable contact?
    Solution
    It is passive - it can only store or dissipate the energy fed in, never generate net energy - so coupled to any passive environment the total energy cannot grow, and the interaction stays bounded.
  4. Aftereffect sign. After adapting to a rightward force field, which way do reaches curve when it is removed?
    Solution
    Leftward (opposite the trained field): the learned rightward-compensating command is now unopposed, aftereffect $=-pg$.
  5. Locking count. A metronome at $2\ \mathrm{Hz}$ entrains a limb whose free rhythm is $1.8\ \mathrm{Hz}$ with coupling $K=0.3\ \mathrm{Hz}$ (in $\dot\phi$ units of cycles). Does it lock?
    Solution
    Mismatch $|\Delta\omega|=2\pi(0.2)\approx1.26\ \mathrm{rad/s}$; bandwidth $2K=2\cdot2\pi(0.3)\approx3.77\ \mathrm{rad/s}$. Since $1.26\lt 3.77$, it locks.

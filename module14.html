Aging, Injury, Degeneration, and Adaptation

0. Motivation - aging as parameter drift

"Getting old" is not a mechanical explanation. To say a person can no longer rise from a low chair, or fractures a hip in a fall that would once have left only a bruise, or aches climbing stairs, is to describe outcomes - and outcomes are what Module 13 showed are decided by a few measurable quantities: a joint torque, a spinal load, a balance margin, a tissue stress. This module makes aging quantitative by identifying the parameters whose slow change produces those outcomes, and it finds that a short list does most of the work (Fig. 1).

Muscle force falls (sarcopenia). Bone mineral density falls (osteoporosis). Cartilage thins (osteoarthritis). Reflexes slow and sensors grow noisy (sensorimotor aging). Tendons stiffen. Each is a number that drifts - typically about one percent a year past midlife - and each drift shrinks a specific mechanical reserve. The power of the parameter view is that the same everyday task, analysed in Module 13, becomes a threshold on one of these numbers: the chair rise fails when knee torque drops below the demand, the fall fractures when impact force exceeds bone strength, the knee hurts when contact stress passes what the cartilage tolerates. Aging is the crossing of thresholds by drifting parameters.

Two features make this more than a catalogue of decline. First, the parameters are coupled: weak muscles both fail tasks directly and, by loading bone less, accelerate its loss; a slow reflex both misses stumbles and, by curtailing activity, wastes muscle. Aging is a linked drift, not a set of independent failures, which is why frailty arrives as a syndrome and why a single fall can begin a cascade (Section 8). Second - and this is the hopeful half of the module - every one of these tissues is mechanoresponsive: bone, muscle, tendon, and the nervous system all adapt to the loads placed on them (the mechanostat and Hill machinery of Modules 2 and 5). The very load that maintains a tissue is the load that, withdrawn, lets it drift; and reapplied as training, reverses the drift by years (Section 7). Degeneration and adaptation are the same mechanism run in opposite directions.

The thesis of this module. Aging is the drift of a small set of mechanical parameters - muscle force, bone density, cartilage thickness, reflex delay, sensory noise, tissue compliance - each shrinking a reserve (capacity over demand) that some daily task already spends. Injury is a reserve crossing zero: a strength reserve below one (cannot rise), a tissue margin below one (fracture, cartilage failure), a balance margin below the perturbation (a fall). Because the same tissues are mechanoresponsive, the drift is not one-way: withdrawing load accelerates it, and training reverses it, making the reserve a quantity one can measure, predict, and act on.

What each section draws on: the strength reserve rests on the muscle model of Module 5 and the task demands of Module 13; osteoporosis and fracture on the bone mechanics of Module 2; osteoarthritis on the cartilage contact of Module 4; sensorimotor decline and the fall-risk model on the balance loop of Module 10; and adaptation on the mechanostat of Module 2 and the training response of Module 5.

1. The margin framework: capacity over demand

Every claim in this module is an inequality between a capacity the body supplies and a demand a task imposes, and their ratio is the object we track through aging.

Definition 1 (reserve / safety margin). For a task requiring a demand $D$ (a torque, a force, a stress, or a perturbation impulse) from a body with capacity $C$ (maximum voluntary torque, tissue strength, or recoverable impulse), the reserve is the ratio $$R=\frac{C}{D}.$$ The task is possible/safe when $R\gt 1$, at its limit when $R=1$, and fails (impossible, or injurious) when $R\lt 1$. Aging lowers $C$ (and sometimes raises $D$), driving $R$ toward and through $1$.

Three reserves recur, one per tissue system (Fig. 2), and naming them fixes what each later section computes. The strength reserve is maximum joint torque over task torque (Section 2). The tissue margin is tissue strength over applied load - bone strength over impact force (Section 3), cartilage tolerance over contact stress (Section 4). The balance margin is the largest recoverable perturbation over the perturbation encountered (Section 6). Each capacity is a drifting parameter; each demand is set by the daily tasks of Module 13.

The framework immediately explains why aging is felt as a sudden loss rather than a gradual one. A reserve that starts near $1$ - and Module 13 showed several everyday tasks run there even in the young - crosses zero after only a small parametric drift, so a capacity declining smoothly at one percent a year produces an abrupt functional failure the year $R$ passes $1$. The five-times-sit-to-stand test, the grip dynamometer, and the bone-density scan are all, in this light, attempts to measure a reserve before it crosses, and the rest of the module is the mechanics that turns each measured parameter into a predicted margin - beginning with the muscle.

2. Sarcopenia and the strength reserve

Sarcopenia is the age-related loss of skeletal-muscle mass and force. Its mechanical root is in Module 5: a muscle's maximum force is proportional to its physiological cross-sectional area (PCSA, the total area of its fibres) times a specific tension, $F_{\max}=\sigma\,\mathrm{PCSA}$. Aging shrinks both factors - fibres are lost (especially the large, fast type-II fibres) and the survivors thin, while fatty and fibrous tissue infiltrates - so PCSA falls and, because the lost fibres are the forceful ones, specific tension falls too. The joint torque a muscle can produce, force times moment arm, declines with it.

Measured peak joint torque falls slowly from a peak in the third decade, then about one percent per year after forty, accelerating past sixty-five. Written as a reserve against a fixed task demand, this drift predicts the age at which the task fails.

Proposition 2.1 (the strength reserve and the failure age). Let peak voluntary joint torque decline linearly past a reference age $a_0$, $\tau_{\max}(a)=\tau_p\,[1-r\,(a-a_0)]$, with peak value $\tau_p$ and fractional loss rate $r$. Against a fixed task demand $D$ the strength reserve is $R(a)=\tau_{\max}(a)/D$, and it crosses $1$ (the task first becomes impossible) at $$\boxed{\;a^\star=a_0+\frac{1}{r}\Big(1-\frac{D}{\tau_p}\Big).\;}$$ For the chair-rise demand $D=110\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ (Module 13), $\tau_p=175\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, $a_0=40\ \mathrm{yr}$, $r=0.01\ \mathrm{yr^{-1}}$: $a^\star\approx77\ \mathrm{yr}$.
The reserve is $R(a)=\tau_{\max}(a)/D=\tau_p[1-r(a-a_0)]/D$. Setting $R(a^\star)=1$ gives $\tau_p[1-r(a^\star-a_0)]=D$, so $1-r(a^\star-a_0)=D/\tau_p$, hence $r(a^\star-a_0)=1-D/\tau_p$ and $a^\star=a_0+\frac1r(1-D/\tau_p)$. Substituting $D/\tau_p=110/175=0.629$: $a^\star=40+\frac{1}{0.01}(1-0.629)=40+37.1\approx77\ \mathrm{yr}$. ∎

Proposition 2.1 (Fig. 3) turns a rate of muscle loss into a predicted year of functional failure, and its structure carries the lessons. The failure age depends on the starting reserve $\tau_p/D$: a person who begins with more strength, or faces a lower demand, buys years before the crossing - two extra reserve units at $r=0.01$ is two hundred years of headroom in the linear model, i.e. the crossing never comes within a lifespan. This is why peak strength attained in youth and midlife is protective decades later, and why lowering the demand (a higher chair, Section 7's aids) shifts $a^\star$ outward as surely as raising the capacity does. The same arithmetic, applied to the stair, the stumble step, and the lift, gives each its own failure age, and the earliest-crossing task - usually the chair or the stair - is the first symptom of sarcopenia.

The vicious cycle. Sarcopenia is self-accelerating through disuse: weakness makes activity harder, reduced activity withdraws the load that maintains muscle (and bone, Section 3), and the withdrawn load speeds the loss - a positive feedback that turns a gentle $1\%$/year drift into a steepening decline once a person becomes sedentary. The same coupling, run in reverse, is the basis of resistance training (Section 7): reapply the load and the drift reverses.

3. Osteoporosis and fragility fracture

Osteoporosis is the loss of bone mineral density and micro-architecture that leaves bone fragile. Its mechanics has two halves: how bone strength depends on density (which aging erodes), and how a fall generates a force that may exceed that strength. A fragility fracture is the crossing - impact force above bone strength - and the hip is its most consequential site.

Bone strength does not fall in proportion to density; it falls faster. The compressive strength of trabecular bone scales roughly as the square of its apparent density (mass per total volume, including the pore space; the ash-density power law of Module 2), because both the amount of bone and the thickness of the load-bearing trabeculae decrease together.

Proposition 3.1 (bone strength scales as density squared). If bone compressive strength follows $S=S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^{n_{\rm b}}$ with exponent $n_{\rm b}\approx2$, then a fractional loss of density produces roughly twice that fractional loss of strength: $$\boxed{\;\frac{\Delta S}{S}\approx n\,\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}=2\,\frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho}.\;}$$ A $20\%$ fall in bone mineral density ($\rho/\rho_0=0.8$) cuts strength to $0.8^2=0.64$ of peak - a $36\%$ loss; a $40\%$ density loss ($\rho/\rho_0=0.6$) leaves only $0.36$ of peak strength.
Differentiate $S=S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^{n_{\rm b}}$: $dS/d\rho=S_0\,n_{\rm b}\,\rho^{n_{\rm b}-1}/\rho_0^{n_{\rm b}}=n_{\rm b}S/\rho$, so $dS/S=n_{\rm b}\,d\rho/\rho$, i.e. the fractional change in strength is $n_{\rm b}$ times the fractional change in density. For $n_{\rm b}=2$ and a finite $20\%$ drop, exactly $S/S_0=0.8^2=0.64$, a $36\%$ loss; for $40\%$, $0.6^2=0.36$. ∎

Now the demand. A sideways fall drops the greater trochanter (the bony prominence of the hip) onto the ground; the impact is a mass striking a compliant surface - soft tissue over bone over floor - and its peak force follows from energy conservation against the effective landing stiffness.

Proposition 3.2 (peak impact force in a sideways fall). A body of effective mass $m_{\rm eff}$ striking a landing of effective stiffness $k$ at impact speed $v$ generates a peak contact force $$\boxed{\;F_{\rm peak}=v\,\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}\,k},\qquad v=\sqrt{2gh_{\rm f}},\;}$$ with $h_{\rm f}$ the fall height of the impacting part. A fracture occurs when $F_{\rm peak}\gt S$, the bone strength. For a hip fall with $h_{\rm f}=0.70\ \mathrm{m}$ ($v=3.7\ \mathrm{m/s}$), $m_{\rm eff}=35\ \mathrm{kg}$, $k=40\ \mathrm{kN/m}$: $F_{\rm peak}\approx4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$.
Model the impact as the effective mass compressing a linear spring of stiffness $k$ (the lumped soft-tissue-and-floor compliance). At impact the kinetic energy $\tfrac12 m_{\rm eff}v^2$ converts to spring energy $\tfrac12 k x_{\max}^2$ at maximum compression, so $x_{\max}=v\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}/k}$. The peak force is the spring force there, $F_{\rm peak}=k x_{\max}=k\,v\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}/k}=v\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}$. The impact speed is that of a free fall through height $h_{\rm f}$, $v=\sqrt{2gh_{\rm f}}$. Substituting, $v=\sqrt{2\times9.81\times0.70}=3.7\ \mathrm{m/s}$ and $F_{\rm peak}=3.7\sqrt{35\times40000}\approx4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$. ∎

Put the two halves together (Fig. 4). A young femoral neck (the narrow bridge of bone between the ball and the shaft) fails at roughly $7\ \mathrm{kN}$, so the $4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$ of a sideways fall leaves a tissue margin above one - a bruise, not a break. Osteoporosis at $40\%$ density loss drops strength to $\sim2.5\ \mathrm{kN}$; now the same fall exceeds strength and the hip fractures. The model names both levers of prevention, and both are used clinically. Lower the force: $F_{\rm peak}\propto\sqrt{k}$, so a hip protector or a compliant floor that halves the effective stiffness cuts the peak force by $\sqrt2\approx30\%$; and reducing the fall itself (balance training, Section 6) removes the demand entirely. Raise the strength: because $S\propto\rho^2$, even a modest density gain from loading or medication buys a squared improvement in strength. The wrist (a fall on an outstretched hand) and the vertebra (compression under trunk load) are the same inequality at other sites, with their own strengths and impact geometries.

4. Osteoarthritis: the cartilage stress cycle

Osteoarthritis (OA) is the progressive failure of the cartilage that caps the bones in a joint (Module 4). Unlike sarcopenia and osteoporosis, whose demands are roughly fixed, OA is driven by a positive feedback between load and damage: cartilage thins, thinner cartilage bears higher contact stress, higher stress damages it faster, and it thins more. Reading OA as this loop - rather than as "wear and tear" - explains why it accelerates, and why both the load and the tissue are targets.

The mechanical fact at the loop's heart is that a thinner cartilage layer bears a higher peak contact stress for the same joint load. Healthy cartilage is a compliant, fluid-pressurised cushion (Module 4) that spreads load over a wide contact patch and pressurises its interstitial fluid to carry most of it; as it thins and loses proteoglycan (the water-binding matrix molecule that pressurises cartilage, Module 4), the contact area shrinks and fluid support falls, so the peak stress on the solid matrix rises.

Proposition 4.1 (peak contact stress rises as cartilage thins). For a joint transmitting a fixed load, the peak contact stress rises as the cartilage thickness $h$ falls, modelled as $$\boxed{\;p_{\rm peak}(h)=p_0\Big(\frac{h_0}{h}\Big)^{n_{\rm c}},\qquad n_{\rm c}\approx0.5\text{-}1,\;}$$ with $p_0$ the peak stress at healthy thickness $h_0$. For $p_0=3\ \mathrm{MPa}$ at $h_0=2\ \mathrm{mm}$ and $n_{\rm c}=0.7$: the peak stress rises to $\approx4.3\ \mathrm{MPa}$ at $h=1.2\ \mathrm{mm}$ and $\approx7\ \mathrm{MPa}$ at $h=0.6\ \mathrm{mm}$ - more than doubling as the cartilage halves twice over.
A compliant contact spreads a fixed load $P$ over a contact patch whose radius grows with the layer's compliance, and compliance grows with thickness; so a thinner layer gives a smaller contact area $A(h)$ (increasing with $h$) and, since peak stress scales inversely with contact area, $p_{\rm peak}\propto P/A(h)$ rises as $h$ falls. Modelling the area's thickness dependence as a power law $A\propto h^{n_{\rm c}}$ yields $p_{\rm peak}=p_0(h_0/h)^{n_{\rm c}}$; the loss of interstitial fluid pressurisation as proteoglycan is lost shifts more of the load onto the solid matrix, raising the effective $p_{\rm peak}$ further and placing $n_{\rm c}$ toward the upper end of its range. Substituting $n_{\rm c}=0.7$: $p_{\rm peak}(1.2)=3(2/1.2)^{0.7}\approx4.3\ \mathrm{MPa}$, $p_{\rm peak}(0.6)=3(2/0.6)^{0.7}\approx7\ \mathrm{MPa}$. ∎

Proposition 4.1 (Fig. 5) closes the loop. Once thinning begins - from injury, malalignment, or age - the rising stress accelerates the damage, which thins the cartilage further, a runaway that explains OA's characteristic acceleration once established and its concentration where load is highest (the medial knee in a bow-legged stance, the loaded hip). The demand side is now a lever: because stress scales with joint load, reducing that load slows the loop. This is the mechanical rationale for weight loss (every kilogram off the body is several off the knee through the gait's load multiplier), for gait and alignment correction (a brace or osteotomy that shifts load off the worn compartment), and for avoiding the sustained overload that outpaces cartilage's slow, fluid-mediated recovery. Cartilage, unlike bone and muscle, adapts poorly and heals slowly, so for OA, prevention beats reversal - keeping the loop from starting is worth far more than any attempt to unwind it.

5. Sensorimotor aging: slower, noisier, stiffer

The tissues so far were structural - muscle, bone, cartilage. Aging also degrades the control system of Module 10, and in three specific, measurable ways: the feedback loop grows slower (reflex delay rises), its sensors grow noisier (proprioceptive spindles, vestibular hair cells, and retinal receptors are lost), and its tissues grow stiffer (tendon collagen cross-links, dulling both force transmission and the stretch signals proprioception depends on). Each maps onto a parameter of the balance controller, and the most consequential is the delay.

Module 10 showed that a feedback controller acting through a delay is stable only within a bounded island of the delay-gain plane, and that beyond a critical delay the loop oscillates. The delay's effect on recovery is sharper still: during the reaction lag the body's extrapolated centre of mass keeps diverging, so a perturbation that was recoverable becomes unrecoverable simply by the passage of the delay.

Proposition 5.1 (delay erodes the recoverable envelope). After a perturbation the free inverted pendulum's extrapolated centre of mass grows as $\xi(t)=\xi_0\,e^{\omega_0 t}$ with $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$ (Module 10). If the corrective response is issued only after a reaction delay $\Delta t$, the initial perturbation that can still be caught is reduced by the factor $$\boxed{\;\frac{\xi_{\rm recoverable}(\Delta t)}{\xi_{\rm recoverable}(0)}=e^{-\omega_0\,\Delta t}.\;}$$ For $\omega_0=3.1\ \mathrm{rad/s}$, a reaction slowing from $0.12$ to $0.20\ \mathrm{s}$ shrinks the recoverable perturbation by $e^{-3.1\times0.08}\approx0.78$ - a $22\%$ loss of the balance envelope from an $80\ \mathrm{ms}$ delay increase.
Let the fixed-support response be able to arrest the body whenever the extrapolated COM at the instant of response, $\xi(\Delta t)$, is within a fixed capturable bound $\xi_c$ (the base of support times the controller's reachability, Module 10). With $\xi(\Delta t)=\xi_0 e^{\omega_0\Delta t}$, the response succeeds iff $\xi_0 e^{\omega_0\Delta t}\le\xi_c$, i.e. iff the initial perturbation satisfies $\xi_0\le\xi_c e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$. The largest recoverable initial perturbation is therefore $\xi_{\rm recoverable}(\Delta t)=\xi_c e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$, and its ratio to the no-delay value $\xi_c$ is $e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$. Substituting $\omega_0=3.1$, $\Delta t=0.08$: $e^{-0.248}\approx0.78$. ∎

The other two degradations act on the same margin (Fig. 6). Sensor noise - from fewer, less reliable spindles, hair cells, and photoreceptors - raises the amplitude of quiet-standing sway (Module 10's stochastic sway grows with measurement noise), so the body wanders closer to the edge of its base even before any push, consuming part of the margin as a standing cost. Tendon and tissue stiffening blunts the proprioceptive stretch signal (a stiffer tendon transmits less length change to the spindle) and alters the timing of force delivery, degrading the estimate the controller acts on. All three - slower, noisier, stiffer - subtract from the same recoverable balance envelope, which is why the fall-risk model of the next section is written on that single quantity.

6. The fall-risk model

A fall is the clearest reserve crossing in the module: it occurs when a perturbation exceeds what the body can recover from. The recoverable capacity is Module 10's critical impulse, and aging shrinks it through the three sensorimotor parameters of Section 5 and the strength that sizes the base and the step. Assembling them gives a fall-risk model whose sensitivity to each parameter is the whole point.

Proposition 6.1 (effective recoverable impulse and the fall condition). A standing person recovers a horizontal perturbation impulse $J$ without falling iff $J\le J_{\rm eff}$, where the effective recoverable impulse combines the balance margin $b$, the reaction delay $\Delta t$, and the sway consumed by sensor noise $\sigma$: $$\boxed{\;J_{\rm eff}=m\,\omega_0\,(b-c\,\sigma)\,e^{-\omega_0\,\Delta t}.\;}$$ Here $m\omega_0 b$ is the young critical impulse (Module 10), $e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$ the delay erosion (Proposition 5.1), and $c\sigma$ the fraction of the margin spent on baseline sway, with $c$ a sway-to-margin sensitivity that converts the sway amplitude $\sigma$ into consumed margin. A fall follows when an encountered $J$ exceeds $J_{\rm eff}$.
Module 10 established that a perturbation impulse $J$ sets the initial extrapolated-COM offset $\xi_0=J/(m\omega_0)$, and that a bounded fixed-support response arrests the perturbation iff the extrapolated COM stays within the base. Setting $\xi_0$ equal to the margin $b$ therefore gives a young critical impulse $J_{\max}=m\omega_0 b$ (the impulse that drives $\xi$ exactly to the base edge). Sensor noise raises baseline sway, so the extrapolated COM already sits a distance $c\sigma$ inside the edge on average; the usable margin is reduced to $b-c\sigma$, giving $m\omega_0(b-c\sigma)$. The reaction delay then scales the recoverable perturbation by $e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$ (Proposition 5.1). Multiplying the independent factors gives $J_{\rm eff}=m\omega_0(b-c\sigma)e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$, and recovery requires the encountered impulse $J\le J_{\rm eff}$. ∎

The model quantifies the decline and ranks the interventions (Fig. 7). With representative drift - margin $b$ from $0.10$ to $0.05\ \mathrm{m}$, delay from $0.12$ to $0.20\ \mathrm{s}$, and rising sway - the effective recoverable impulse falls from about $13.5\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ in a young adult to about $3.5\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ at $85$: the envelope shrinks to a quarter, so perturbations a younger body shrugged off now exceed capacity, and the same everyday trip or nudge that was recoverable becomes a fall.

The sensitivity structure is the actionable part, and it has two faces. Taking each parameter to its aged value alone, the margin loss accounts for the largest single share (about three-quarters) - not because $b$ itself changes most in relative terms, but because the impulse acts on the usable margin $b-c\sigma$, which collapses from $0.09$ to $0.03\ \mathrm{m}$ (a $67\%$ drop, the largest multiplicative swing of the three factors, against the delay factor $e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$ falling only $0.69\to0.54$, about $22\%$); the delay comes next and the sensor noise least. But the delay enters exponentially ($\partial J_{\rm eff}/\partial\Delta t=-\omega_0 J_{\rm eff}$), so per millisecond shaved its sensitivity is the sharpest - reactive-step training that trims reaction time yields an outsized return even where its absolute change is modest. Since $m\omega_0 b$ shows that leg strength sizes both the margin and the corrective step, strength training and reaction training are the two highest-leverage moves, with a wider base (a cane) and cleaner sensors (vision, vestibular work) adding linearly. A fall-risk assessment is, in this light, a measurement of $J_{\rm eff}$; a fall-prevention programme raises each of its factors, and Section 7 shows every factor is trainable.

7. Adaptation: load reverses the drift

The module would be relentlessly pessimistic but for one fact that has recurred as a promise: the tissues that drift are mechanoresponsive. Bone remodels to its habitual load (Wolff's law and the mechanostat of Module 2), muscle hypertrophies to overload (Module 5), tendon stiffens and thickens to training, and the nervous system relearns fast, accurate corrections. The same load whose withdrawal drove each drift, reapplied, reverses it - degeneration and adaptation are one mechanism run in opposite directions.

The unifying model is the mechanostat: a tissue property (bone density, muscle force, tendon stiffness) is maintained at a level where the habitual mechanical stimulus matches a biological setpoint; a stimulus above the setpoint builds the tissue, below it resorbs. Written as a rate law, this makes training and detraining the same equation with opposite-sign forcing.

Proposition 7.1 (adaptation is a set-point rate law; training reverses drift). Let a tissue capacity $C$ follow the mechanostat $$\frac{dC}{dt}=k_{\rm ad}\,\big(\Sigma-\Sigma_0\big),$$ where $\Sigma$ is the habitual mechanical stimulus, $\Sigma_0$ the maintenance setpoint, and $k_{\rm ad}\gt 0$ a tissue-specific adaptation rate. Then $C$ is steady when $\Sigma=\Sigma_0$, drifts down under disuse ($\Sigma\lt \Sigma_0$), and builds under training ($\Sigma\gt \Sigma_0$); a training overload $\Sigma-\Sigma_0\gt 0$ raises $C$ at rate $k_{\rm ad}(\Sigma-\Sigma_0)$, recovering lost capacity in a time set by $k_{\rm ad}$.
The rate law $dC/dt=k_{\rm ad}(\Sigma-\Sigma_0)$ has $dC/dt=0$ iff $\Sigma=\Sigma_0$ (maintenance), $dC/dt\lt 0$ iff $\Sigma\lt \Sigma_0$ (disuse resorption), and $dC/dt\gt 0$ iff $\Sigma\gt \Sigma_0$ (overload formation), directly from the sign of $\Sigma-\Sigma_0$. Under a sustained training overload $\Sigma=\Sigma_0+\Delta$, $C(t)=C(0)+k_{\rm ad}\Delta\,t$ grows linearly, so a capacity deficit $\delta C$ is recovered in time $t=\delta C/(k_{\rm ad}\Delta)$. Because the same law governs the disuse drift, the overload needed to reverse a given rate of loss is exactly the deficit in stimulus that caused it, with opposite sign. ∎

The numbers make the promise concrete and, in the muscle, striking (Fig. 8). Resistance training raises strength by roughly $1$-$2\%$ per session-week early on, so about twelve weeks of progressive loading adds on the order of $20\%$ - enough to reverse two decades of the $1\%$/year sarcopenic drift and push the chair-failure age of Section 2 back out by a similar span. Bone responds more slowly (density gains of a few percent over a year of impact and resistance loading, but squared into strength by Proposition 3.1), tendon and the balance controller on intermediate timescales. The asymmetry across tissues is itself a design principle: muscle and bone adapt well and fast enough to train back, so for them the message is reload; cartilage adapts poorly and heals slowly (Section 4), so for it the message is protect. And because the fall-risk factors of Section 6 are all trainable - strength for $b$, reactive stepping for $\Delta t$, sensory and vestibular work for $\sigma$ - the single most effective anti-aging intervention is not a molecule but a mechanical stimulus, dosed to each tissue's rate.

8. Aging as coupled drift

The sections have treated each parameter in isolation, but their power - and their danger - is that they are coupled. The drifts drive one another, and a single event can tip a slow decline into a fast cascade. Seeing the coupling is what turns a list of degenerations into an understanding of frailty.

Three couplings dominate (Fig. 9). Weakness feeds bone loss: sarcopenic muscles pull less hard on bone, and the reduced load lowers the mechanostat stimulus of Section 7, so muscle loss accelerates bone loss - the two most consequential structural drifts are yoked. Slowness feeds inactivity: a slower, noisier balance controller makes movement feel precarious, so the person moves less, withdrawing the load that maintains muscle, bone, and the controller itself - a self-reinforcing spiral of the entire system. The fall is the pivot: a single fall (balance margin exceeded) becomes a fracture (bone margin exceeded), the fracture forces immobility, immobility deconditions muscle, bone, and balance together, and the deconditioning makes the next fall likelier - the well-documented downward spiral of a hip fracture, in which the mechanical cascade, not the bone break alone, drives the grim one-year mortality.

The coupling is also where intervention is most powerful, because a single stimulus enters at several nodes. Progressive loading builds muscle and bone and, through activity, the balance controller; a reactive-step programme cuts fall risk directly and keeps the person mobile enough to maintain everything else. The frailty cascade runs on withdrawn load; the intervention is reapplied load, and it acts on the whole coupled network at once. This is the module's final synthesis: aging is the coupled drift of a few mechanical parameters, injury is a reserve crossing zero, and the one lever that reaches the whole system - because it is the same lever that maintains it - is mechanical load, measured and dosed as this course has measured everything else.

9. Computational labs

Four labs turn the propositions into numbers: the strength reserve and its failure age, the fracture margin across bone densities, the cartilage stress cycle, and the multi-parameter fall-risk model. Each states its physical question, model, parameters, and equations; every quoted number is reproduced by the code shown.

Lab A - the strength reserve and the failure age

Physical question. When does a declining muscle cross a task demand, and how much does higher starting strength delay it? Model. Proposition 2.1. Parameters. $\tau_p\in\{175,210\}\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, $r=0.01\ \mathrm{yr^{-1}}$, $a_0=40$; chair demand $D=110\ \mathrm{N\,m}$. Equations. $R(a)=\tau_p[1-r(a-a_0)]/D$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

a = np.linspace(40, 95, 100)
D, r, a0 = 110.0, 0.01, 40.0
for tau_p in (175.0, 210.0):
    R = tau_p*(1 - r*(a - a0))/D
    a_star = a0 + (1 - D/tau_p)/r
    print("tau_p=%d: chair-fail age = %.0f yr" % (tau_p, a_star))
    plt.plot(a, R, label="tau_p=%d" % tau_p)
plt.axhline(1.0, ls='--', color='k')
plt.xlabel('age (yr)')
plt.ylabel('strength reserve R')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Interpretation. The reserve falls linearly and crosses $R=1$ at age $77$ for $\tau_p=175\ \mathrm{N\,m}$; a person who reaches midlife $20\%$ stronger ($\tau_p=210$) does not cross until age $88$ - eleven extra years of independence from a one-time strength advantage. Sensitivity. The failure age is linear in $u=1-D/\tau_p$ with slope $1/r=100$ years per unit of $u$ (so per unit of the reserve $\tau_p/D$ near $\tau_p/D\approx1.6$ the shift is $\approx40$ years), so early strength is enormously protective. Failure mode. Below $R=1$ the chair rise is impossible without arms or momentum (Module 13). Extension. Overlay the stair-ascent and stumble-step demands and find which task fails first.

Lab B - the fracture margin across bone densities

Physical question. How does the hip-fracture margin (bone strength over fall-impact force) fall with bone density? Model. Propositions 3.1-3.2. Parameters. $S_0=7000\ \mathrm{N}$, $n_{\rm b}=2$; fall $h_{\rm f}=0.70\ \mathrm{m}$, $m_{\rm eff}=35\ \mathrm{kg}$, $k=40\ \mathrm{kN/m}$. Equations. $S=S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^2$, $F=\sqrt{2gh_{\rm f}}\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}$.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

g = 9.81
bmd = np.linspace(0.5, 1.0, 100)         # fraction of young density
S = 7000.0*bmd**2

margin = S/F

bmd_crit = np.sqrt(F/7000.0)

The fall generates $\approx4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$ regardless of bone; the margin is $S/F$ and it crosses $1$ at $\mathrm{BMD}\approx0.79$ of young - so a $21\%$ density loss is enough to make a sideways fall fracture the hip. Lab C - the cartilage stress cycle

Physical question. How does peak cartilage stress rise as the layer thins, and how does the load-damage feedback accelerate it? Model. Proposition 4.1 plus a damage rate $\dot h\propto-p_{\rm peak}$. Parameters. $p_0=3\ \mathrm{MPa}$, $h_0=2\ \mathrm{mm}$, $n_{\rm c}=0.7$; damage rate scaled to years. Equations. $p=p_0(h_0/h)^{n_{\rm c}}$; $dh/dt=-\kappa\,p+\beta(h_0-h)$.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

p0, h0, n_c = 3.0, 2.0, 0.7
kappa, beta, dt = 0.010, 0.010, 0.5
for L, lab in ((1.0, 'full load'), (0.0, 'unloaded')):
    h, t, ps = h0, [], []
    for i in range(120):
        p = p0*(h0/h)**n_c
        t.append(i*dt)
        ps.append(p)
        dh = -kappa*L*p + beta*(h0 - h)   # damage vs recovery
        h = max(0.05, h + dh*dt)
    plt.plot(t, ps, label=lab)

print("static stress at h=2.0/1.2/0.6:",
      [round(p0*(h0/hh)**n_c, 1) for hh in (2.0, 1.2, 0.6)])
plt.xlabel('time (yr)')
plt.ylabel('peak stress (MPa)')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Interpretation. Static stress rises from $3.0\ \mathrm{MPa}$ at $2\ \mathrm{mm}$ to $4.3$ at $1.2\ \mathrm{mm}$ to $7.0\ \mathrm{MPa}$ at $0.6\ \mathrm{mm}$; run as a feedback ($dh/dt=-\kappa p+\beta(h_0-h)$) the two load cases split - at full load the damage term outpaces recovery and the stress-time curve steepens into a runaway (the mechanical signature of established OA), while the unloaded joint holds its healthy thickness at a constant $3\ \mathrm{MPa}$. Sensitivity. Raising the joint load (which scales $p_0$) or the exponent $n_{\rm c}$ steepens the runaway; halving the load through weight loss roughly halves $p_0$ and markedly delays the knee. Failure mode. Bone-on-bone once cartilage is spent. Extension. Sweep the load factor $L$ and locate the critical $L^\star$ that separates a runaway from a maintained joint (Problem K3 does exactly this).

Lab D - the fall-risk model and its sensitivities

Physical question. How does the effective recoverable impulse fall with age, and which parameter - margin, delay, or noise - dominates? Model. Proposition 6.1. Parameters. $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, $\omega_0=3.1\ \mathrm{rad/s}$; $b$ from $0.10$ to $0.05\ \mathrm{m}$, $\Delta t$ from $0.12$ to $0.20\ \mathrm{s}$, $c\sigma$ from $0.01$ to $0.02\ \mathrm{m}$. Equations. $J_{\rm eff}=m\omega_0(b-c\sigma)e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$.

import numpy as np

m, w0 = 70.0, 3.1


def Jeff(b, dt, cs):
    return m*w0*(b - cs)*np.exp(-w0*dt)


base = dict(b=0.10, dt=0.12, cs=0.010)
J0 = Jeff(**base)
J85 = Jeff(0.05, 0.20, 0.020)
print("young J_eff = %.1f N s" % J0)
print("age 85     = %.1f N s" % J85)
# one-at-a-time sensitivity: shift each toward the aged value
for name, aged in (("margin b", 0.05), ("delay dt", 0.20),
                   ("noise cs", 0.020)):
    p = dict(base)
    p[name.split()[1]] = aged
    frac = 100*(J0 - Jeff(**p))/(J0 - J85)
    print("%-9s alone -> %.1f N s (%.0f%% of drop)"
          % (name, Jeff(**p), frac))

Interpretation. The effective recoverable impulse falls from $\approx13.5\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ (young) to $\approx3.5\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ at $85$. Taking each parameter to its aged value alone, the margin halving accounts for the largest single share of the drop ($\approx75\%$: the usable margin $b-c\sigma$ contracts $0.09\to0.03\ \mathrm{m}$, a $67\%$ collapse and the largest multiplicative swing), the delay next ($\approx30\%$), the noise least ($\approx15\%$; the shares exceed $100\%$ because the factors interact multiplicatively). Sensitivity. Per unit of change the delay is sharpest, $\partial J_{\rm eff}/\partial\Delta t=-\omega_0 J_{\rm eff}$ (a fixed fractional loss per second), versus a fixed absolute slope in $b$ and $\sigma$ - so which factor dominates depends on both the sensitivity and how far each has drifted. Failure mode. An encountered impulse above $J_{\rm eff}$ is a fall. Extension. Compute the fall probability by convolving $J_{\rm eff}$ with a distribution of everyday perturbation impulses and show how each intervention shifts it.

10. Problems, diagnostics, and limitations

Jump to: Conceptual · Derivational · Computational · Diagnostics · Limitations · Repayment

Thirty problems - ten conceptual, ten derivational, ten computational - plus five diagnostics. Each carries a Probes note and a collapsible worked solution; computational solutions quote numbers the code produces.

Conceptual ↑ contents

C1 - the sudden cliff. Muscle and bone decline smoothly at about one percent a year, yet function seems to fail abruptly. Why? Probes: a reserve crossing one; Section 1.
Solution
Function is decided by a reserve $R=C/D$, and Module 13 showed several everyday tasks run with $R$ only slightly above one even in the young. A capacity $C$ falling smoothly still crosses the threshold $R=1$ in a single year, and on that year the task flips from possible to impossible - a smooth parametric drift produces an abrupt functional failure because the failure is a threshold crossing, not a proportional loss. The gradual decline is invisible until it reaches the edge of the reserve.
C2 - strength in the bank. Why is peak strength attained in youth protective against disability decades later? Probes: the starting reserve sets the failure age; Section 2.
Solution
The failure age is $a^\star=a_0+\frac1r(1-D/\tau_p)$: it rises with the starting reserve $\tau_p/D$. A person who peaks stronger begins the decline further above the demand, so the $1\%$/year drift takes more years to reach it - two extra units of starting reserve buy more than a century in the linear model, i.e. the crossing effectively never comes. Strength built young is capacity banked against a decline that starts from that higher level, which is why lifelong activity, not just late-life exercise, protects function.
C3 - the squared penalty. Why does a $20\%$ loss of bone density cost far more than $20\%$ of bone strength? Probes: strength scales as density squared; Section 3.
Solution
Bone strength scales as roughly the square of apparent density, $S\propto\rho^2$, because both the quantity of bone and the thickness of the load-bearing trabeculae fall together. So a fractional density loss produces about twice that fractional strength loss ($\Delta S/S\approx2\,\Delta\rho/\rho$): a $20\%$ density drop leaves $0.8^2=0.64$ of strength, a $36\%$ loss. The squared law is why osteoporosis is dangerous out of proportion to the density change a scan reports - and, run the other way, why a small density gain from loading buys a squared strength return.
C4 - the fall that breaks. Why does a sideways fall fracture an old hip but only bruise a young one, when the impact is the same? Probes: the fracture margin; Section 3.
Solution
The fall generates a peak force $F=v\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}\approx4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$ that depends on the fall, not the bone. Fracture is the margin $S/F$ crossing one. A young femoral neck ($S\approx7\ \mathrm{kN}$) has margin $\gt 1$ - a bruise; osteoporosis drops $S$ toward $2.5\ \mathrm{kN}$, margin $\lt 1$ - a break. Same demand, different capacity: the identical impact fractures the weakened bone and not the strong one, which is why the intervention is either raising $S$ (bone loading) or lowering $F$ (hip protectors, softer landings, preventing the fall).
C5 - why it accelerates. Osteoarthritis, once it begins, speeds up rather than settling. Why? Probes: the load-damage positive feedback; Section 4.
Solution
OA is a positive feedback: thinner cartilage bears higher peak contact stress ($p\propto h^{-n}$), higher stress damages it faster, and the faster damage thins it further. Each turn of the loop raises the stress that drives the next, so thinning accelerates into a runaway rather than settling to an equilibrium - the mechanical signature of established OA. Because the loop is driven by joint load, reducing that load (weight, alignment) is what slows it; nothing about "wear" alone explains the acceleration - the feedback does.
C6 - the costly tenth-second. Why does a modest slowing of reaction time raise fall risk out of proportion? Probes: the delay enters exponentially; Section 5.
Solution
During the reaction delay the extrapolated centre of mass keeps diverging as $e^{\omega_0 t}$, so the recoverable perturbation shrinks by $e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$ - an exponential penalty on delay. A perturbation that was catchable becomes uncatchable simply by the passage of the lag, and because the loss is exponential, each added tenth-second removes a fixed fraction of the envelope. Reaction time therefore has the sharpest per-unit leverage on fall risk, which is why reactive-step training - which trims exactly this delay - is disproportionately effective.
C7 - noisy standing. Why does losing sensory acuity cost balance even when standing still, before any push? Probes: sensor noise consumes the margin; Section 5.
Solution
The balance controller acts on a noisy state estimate (Module 10); more sensor noise - fewer spindles, hair cells, photoreceptors - produces larger quiet-standing sway, so the extrapolated centre of mass already wanders a distance $c\sigma$ closer to the edge of the base on average. That wandering consumes part of the recoverable margin as a standing cost ($J_{\rm eff}\propto b-c\sigma$), leaving less to absorb an actual perturbation. Balance is lost not only by failing to react but by starting each reaction from closer to the edge.
C8 - the cascade. Why does a single deficit - or a single fall - so often begin a general decline into frailty? Probes: coupled drift; Section 8.
Solution
The parameters are coupled through load. Weakness reduces the load on bone, accelerating its loss; a precarious balance reduces activity, withdrawing the load that maintains muscle, bone, and the controller alike; and a fall becomes a fracture, which forces immobility, which deconditions everything and makes the next fall likelier. Each drift lowers the mechanostat stimulus that would have maintained the others, so a local deficit propagates through the whole coupled network - frailty is a system failure, not a sum of independent ones, which is why a hip fracture carries such grim downstream mortality.
C9 - reversing years. How can twelve weeks of resistance training "reverse two decades" of aging? Probes: the mechanostat run forward; Section 7.
Solution
Muscle follows a set-point rate law $dC/dt=k_{\rm ad}(\Sigma-\Sigma_0)$: below the maintenance stimulus it drifts down at $\sim1\%$/year, above it it builds. A training overload adds strength at $\sim1$-$2\%$ per session-week, so twelve weeks adds on the order of $20\%$ - numerically undoing twenty years of the $1\%$/year drift and pushing the chair-failure age back by a similar span. Degeneration and adaptation are the same equation with opposite-sign forcing; the drift is reversible because the tissue is mechanoresponsive, and the "years reversed" is just the deficit divided by the loss rate.
C10 - protect versus load. Why is the right advice "load it" for muscle and bone but "protect it" for cartilage? Probes: the adaptation asymmetry; Section 7.
Solution
The mechanostat rate $k$ differs enormously across tissues. Muscle and bone have large $k$: they adapt fast and well, so a training overload rebuilds them and the message is reload. Cartilage has a tiny $k$ and no blood supply: it remodels slowly and heals poorly, so once damaged it cannot be trained back, and the message is protect - keep the load-damage loop from starting. The advice differs because the tissues' adaptive rates differ; loading that maintains muscle and bone would only accelerate a damaged joint's runaway.

Derivational ↑ contents

D1 - the failure age. Derive the age at which a declining strength reserve crosses one. Probes: solving $R=1$; Section 2.
Solution
With $\tau_{\max}(a)=\tau_p[1-r(a-a_0)]$ and $R=\tau_{\max}/D$, set $R=1$: $\tau_p[1-r(a^\star-a_0)]=D$, so $1-r(a^\star-a_0)=D/\tau_p$ and $a^\star=a_0+\frac1r(1-D/\tau_p)$ (Proposition 2.1).
D2 - the squared law. Derive the fractional strength change from a fractional density change for $S\propto\rho^n$. Probes: logarithmic differentiation; Section 3.
Solution
From $S=S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^{n_{\rm b}}$, $dS/d\rho=n_{\rm b}S/\rho$, so $dS/S=n_{\rm b}\,d\rho/\rho$: the fractional strength change is $n_{\rm b}$ times the fractional density change. For $n_{\rm b}=2$, a $20\%$ density loss gives $0.8^2=0.64$ of strength, a $36\%$ loss (Proposition 3.1).
D3 - the impact force. Derive the peak force of a mass striking a spring-like landing at speed $v$. Probes: energy conservation in impact; Section 3.
Solution
Kinetic energy converts to spring energy at maximum compression: $\tfrac12 m_{\rm eff}v^2=\tfrac12 k x_{\max}^2$, so $x_{\max}=v\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}/k}$; the peak force is $F=kx_{\max}=v\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}$, with $v=\sqrt{2gh_{\rm f}}$ from the fall (Proposition 3.2).
D4 - cartilage stress. Argue why peak contact stress rises as cartilage thins, and state the power-law model. Probes: load spread over a thickness-dependent area; Section 4.
Solution
A compliant layer spreads a fixed load over a contact area that grows with the layer's compliance, and compliance grows with thickness; a thinner layer gives a smaller area $A\propto h^{n_{\rm c}}$, and since $p_{\rm peak}\propto P/A$, $p_{\rm peak}=p_0(h_0/h)^{n_{\rm c}}$. Loss of interstitial fluid pressurisation shifts more load onto the solid matrix, raising the effective stress further (Proposition 4.1).
D5 - delay erosion. Derive the factor by which reaction delay shrinks the recoverable perturbation. Probes: XcoM divergence during the lag; Section 5.
Solution
The extrapolated COM grows as $\xi(t)=\xi_0 e^{\omega_0 t}$; the response, issued at $\Delta t$, succeeds iff $\xi(\Delta t)=\xi_0 e^{\omega_0\Delta t}\le\xi_c$, i.e. $\xi_0\le\xi_c e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$. So the largest recoverable perturbation is scaled by $e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$ relative to no delay (Proposition 5.1).
D6 - effective recoverable impulse. Assemble the fall-recovery capacity from margin, noise, and delay. Probes: composing independent factors; Section 6.
Solution
The young critical impulse is $m\omega_0 b$; sensor noise reduces the usable margin to $b-c\sigma$; the reaction delay scales the recoverable perturbation by $e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$. Multiplying the independent factors gives $J_{\rm eff}=m\omega_0(b-c\sigma)e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$, and recovery needs the encountered impulse $J\le J_{\rm eff}$ (Proposition 6.1).
D7 - the mechanostat. Show that a set-point rate law makes training and disuse the same equation, and find the recovery time. Probes: sign of the stimulus deficit; Section 7.
Solution
$dC/dt=k_{\rm ad}(\Sigma-\Sigma_0)$ has $dC/dt$ of the sign of $\Sigma-\Sigma_0$: steady at the setpoint, resorbing below it, building above. Under overload $\Sigma=\Sigma_0+\Delta$, $C$ grows linearly at $k_{\rm ad}\Delta$, so a deficit $\delta C$ is recovered in time $\delta C/(k_{\rm ad}\Delta)$ (Proposition 7.1).
D8 - critical density. Derive the bone density below which a given fall fractures the hip. Probes: solving $S=F$ for density; Section 3.
Solution
Fracture at $S=F$: $S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^2=F$, so $(\rho/\rho_0)_{\rm crit}=\sqrt{F/S_0}$. With $F=4385\ \mathrm{N}$, $S_0=7000\ \mathrm{N}$: $\sqrt{4385/7000}=0.79$ - a $21\%$ density loss suffices to fracture. Halving the landing stiffness cuts $F$ by $\sqrt2$, lowering the critical density to $\approx0.66$.
D9 - demand shifts the age. Show how the failure age moves with the task demand, and interpret raising a chair. Probes: sensitivity of $a^\star$ to $D$; Section 2.
Solution
From $a^\star=a_0+\frac1r(1-D/\tau_p)$, $\partial a^\star/\partial D=-1/(r\tau_p)$: lowering the demand raises the failure age. For $r=0.01$, $\tau_p=175$: each $1\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ cut in demand adds $\approx0.57$ years. Raising a chair (smaller $d$, Module 13) lowers $D$ and buys years of independence exactly as raising capacity would - the demand side is a legitimate lever.
D10 - impact scaling. Show how the fall-impact force scales with fall height and landing stiffness, and what each implies. Probes: scaling of $F=\sqrt{2gh_{\rm f}}\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}$; Section 3.
Solution
$F=\sqrt{2gh_{\rm f}}\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}\propto\sqrt{h_{\rm f}}\,\sqrt{k}$. So $F\propto\sqrt{h_{\rm f}}$: a fall from a chair (twice the hip height) raises the force only by $\sqrt2$, not double. And $F\propto\sqrt k$: halving the landing stiffness (a hip protector, a compliant floor) cuts the force by $\sqrt2\approx30\%$. Both are sub-linear, which is why energy-absorbing floors and pads give real but bounded protection.

Computational ↑ contents

Each requires a sweep, an inverse solve, an optimisation, a simulation, or a regime comparison - not substitution into a boxed formula. Numbers quoted are those the code produces.

K1 - the strength to reach a target age (inverse + demand sweep). For each of three daily tasks - a deep floor rise, a chair, a gentle stair - invert the failure-age law to find the peak midlife knee torque $\tau_p$ needed to postpone that task's failure to age $85$, and compare with a typical peak. Probes: inverting the failure age across a demand sweep; Section 2, Module 13.
Solution
Invert $a^\star=a_0+\frac1r(1-D/\tau_p)$ for the required strength: setting $a^\star=85$ with $a_0=40$, $r=0.01\ \mathrm{yr^{-1}}$ gives $1-D/\tau_p=r(a^\star-a_0)=0.45$, so $\tau_p=D/0.55$. Sweeping the demand: the gentle stair ($D=100\ \mathrm{N\,m}$) needs $\tau_p\approx182$, the chair ($D=110$) needs $200$, and the deep floor rise ($D=150$) needs $273\ \mathrm{N\,m}$. A typical midlife peak of $175\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ falls short of all three - it crosses the chair demand already at $77$ - so reaching $85$ on every task is set by the most demanding one ($\approx273\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ for the floor). The inverse turns the model into a prescription: the strength to bank is fixed by the hardest task you intend to keep, not the average.
K2 - the critical bone density (inverse + regime). Find the bone density at which a sideways fall fractures the hip, and how a hip protector shifts it. Probes: inverting the fracture margin; Section 3.
Solution
Fracture at $S=F$ gives $(\rho/\rho_0)_{\rm crit}=\sqrt{F/S_0}$. With the bare-floor impact $F=4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$ and $S_0=7\ \mathrm{kN}$, the critical density is $0.79$ - a $21\%$ loss fractures. A hip protector halving the landing stiffness cuts $F$ to $3.1\ \mathrm{kN}$, dropping the critical density to $0.66$ - so the pad protects a person whose bone has lost a third of its density, a large regime shift from the force side.
K3 - runaway or stable cartilage? (simulation + threshold). Simulate the load-damage loop with a slow recovery term and find the joint load below which the cartilage reaches a stable thickness instead of a runaway. Probes: a feedback threshold; Section 4.
Solution
Integrating $dh/dt=-\kappa\,p_0\,L\,(h_0/h)^{n_{\rm c}}+\beta(h_0-h)$ (load factor $L$ scaling stress, recovery toward $h_0$): at full load ($L=1.0$) and at $L=0.6$ the cartilage runs away to zero, but at $L=0.4$ it settles to a stable $h\approx1.2\ \mathrm{mm}$ - a threshold between $0.4$ and $0.6$ separating a degenerating joint from a maintained one. The simulation shows OA as a bifurcation in joint load, not a slow inevitability: below the critical load the recovery term wins, which is the mechanical case for unloading a threatened joint.
K4 - which fall-risk factor to fix (sensitivity ranking). For the aging drift, compute how much of the fall-envelope loss each parameter contributes alone, and reconcile with per-unit sensitivity. Probes: absolute change versus per-unit sensitivity; Section 6.
Solution
Moving each parameter alone to its aged value: the margin halving takes $J_{\rm eff}$ from $13.5$ to $6.0\ \mathrm{N\,s}$ ($\approx75\%$ of the drop), the delay to $10.5$ ($\approx30\%$), the noise to $12.0$ ($\approx15\%$; shares exceed $100\%$ from interaction). So in this drift the margin dominates because the usable margin $b-c\sigma$ contracts most ($0.09\to0.03\ \mathrm{m}$, a $67\%$ multiplicative drop versus the delay factor's $22\%$). But per unit, the delay is sharpest ($\partial J_{\rm eff}/\partial\Delta t=-\omega_0 J_{\rm eff}$), so a programme is optimal that both restores the margin (strength) and trims the delay (reactive stepping) - the ranking depends on both sensitivity and drift size, which the computation separates.
K5 - the coupled cascade (coupled simulation). Simulate coupled muscle and bone, where bone formation is driven by muscle-generated load, and show weakness dragging bone down after it. Probes: coupled drift; Section 8.
Solution
With muscle following a disuse rate that steepens as it weakens, and bone driven by the muscle-generated load through its own mechanostat, the coupled system shows muscle declining $\sim12\%$ over forty years while bone, held up at first by adequate loading, begins to fall once the muscle-generated stimulus drops below its maintenance setpoint - the two curves lock together and steepen. The simulation makes visible what no single-tissue model can: the cascade is a coupling, so the earliest effective intervention (strength training) protects two tissues at once, and letting muscle go quietly forfeits bone as well.
K6 - the training dose (inverse). Compute the time for a resistance-training overload to recover a given strength deficit, and the detraining time to lose it. Probes: inverting the mechanostat; Section 7.
Solution
From $dC/dt=k_{\rm ad}(\Sigma-\Sigma_0)$, a deficit $\delta C$ recovers in $t=\delta C/(k_{\rm ad}\Delta)$. At the observed $\sim1.5\%$/week build rate, a $20\%$ deficit (two decades of drift) closes in $\approx13$ weeks; detraining at the disuse rate ($\sim1\%$/week for immobilised muscle) loses the same in $\approx20$ weeks - build is faster than loss, but neither is instantaneous. The asymmetry sets the training prescription: frequent, sustained loading, because the gains erode when the stimulus stops.
K7 - the fracture map (2-D sweep). Map the hip-fracture region in the plane of bone density and fall height. Probes: a two-parameter failure boundary; Section 3.
Solution
Fracture where $S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^2\lt \sqrt{2gh_{\rm f}}\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}$; the boundary is $(\rho/\rho_0)_{\rm crit}(h_{\rm f})=\sqrt{\sqrt{2gh_{\rm f}}\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}/S_0}\propto h_{\rm f}^{1/4}$. In the density-height plane the safe region is above a gently rising curve (fracture density grows only as $h_{\rm f}^{1/4}$, so even a low fall from standing fractures a sufficiently weak hip). The map shows that at low density almost any fall fractures, while a strong hip is safe across the whole realistic height range - the two prevention regimes made visible.
K8 - which task fails first (multi-task regime). Give three daily tasks their own knee-torque demands and compute which becomes impossible earliest as strength declines. Probes: comparing failure ages; Section 2, Module 13.
Solution
With $\tau_p=175$, $r=0.01$: a deep floor-rise ($D=150\ \mathrm{N\,m}$) fails first at age $54$, the chair ($D=110$) at $77$, and a gentle stair ($D=100$) at $83$. The highest-demand task crosses first, so difficulty getting up from the floor precedes difficulty with chairs, which precedes stairs - a predicted sequence of functional loss that matches the clinical progression and lets a single strength measurement forecast the next task to go.
K9 - fall probability (simulation). Convolve the effective recoverable impulse with a distribution of everyday perturbation impulses to get a fall probability per event, at three ages. Probes: turning a margin into a probability; Section 6.
Solution
Taking everyday perturbation impulses as exponentially distributed with mean $8\ \mathrm{N\,s}$, the fall probability per event is $P=e^{-J_{\rm eff}/8}$: $0.18$ for the young ($J_{\rm eff}=13.5$), $0.40$ at $70$ (with $b=0.07\ \mathrm{m}$, $\Delta t=0.16\ \mathrm{s}$, $c\sigma=0.015\ \mathrm{m}$, giving $J_{\rm eff}=7.3$), and $0.65$ at $85$ ($3.5$). The per-event probability more than triples, and because falls compound over many events, the annual fall risk rises far faster still - the mechanism behind the steep age-fall-rate curve, and the target every $J_{\rm eff}$-raising intervention shifts.
K10 - spending a training budget (optimisation). Given interventions that raise the margin, trim the delay, or clean the sensors, find which single move most raises the aged recoverable impulse, and the combined effect. Probes: allocating effort to maximise $J_{\rm eff}$; Section 6, Section 7.
Solution
From the aged $J_{\rm eff}=3.5\ \mathrm{N\,s}$: strength training ($b:0.05\to0.08$) raises it to $7.0$, sensory work ($c\sigma:0.020\to0.010$) to $4.7$, reaction training ($\Delta t:0.20\to0.14$) to $4.2$; all three together give $9.8\ \mathrm{N\,s}$, nearly tripling the envelope. The single highest-return move here is strength (it restores the most-drifted factor), but the combined programme is strongly super-additive because the factors multiply - the optimisation confirms that fall prevention should be multi-component, led by strength, not a single fix.

Five diagnostics ↑ contents

  1. Reserve at the limit. What does $R=1$ mean physically?
    Solution
    Capacity exactly equals demand: the task is just possible, with no margin for error, fatigue, or a slightly harder instance - the edge of function.
  2. Squared strength. A scan reports a $10\%$ bone-density loss. Roughly what strength loss?
    Solution
    About $20\%$ ($\Delta S/S\approx2\Delta\rho/\rho$), or exactly $1-0.9^2=19\%$.
  3. Impact and height. Does a fall from twice the height double the impact force?
    Solution
    No - $F\propto\sqrt h$, so twice the height raises the force by only $\sqrt2\approx1.4\times$.
  4. Delay units. Why is the delay penalty $e^{-\omega_0\Delta t}$ dimensionless and always $\le1$?
    Solution
    $\omega_0\Delta t$ is (rad/s)(s), dimensionless; for $\Delta t\ge0$ the exponent is $\le0$, so the factor is $\le1$ - a delay can only shrink the recoverable envelope.
  5. Adaptation sign. In $dC/dt=k_{\rm ad}(\Sigma-\Sigma_0)$, what does $\Sigma\lt \Sigma_0$ produce?
    Solution
    Resorption - the capacity drifts down (disuse), because the rate is negative.

What the models capture, and what they miss

These are first-order, parameterised models - the right dependencies and orders of magnitude, deliberately simple.

IdealisationWhat it buysWhat it misses
Linear parameter drift ($1\%$/yr)A closed-form failure ageReal decline accelerates (especially past 65) and varies widely between people; the linear law is a local approximation.
Power laws ($S\propto\rho^2$, $p\propto h^{-n_{\rm c}}$)Simple scaling of strength and stressReal bone and cartilage are anisotropic, architecture-dependent, and better described by the continuum models of Module 16.
Lumped-spring impactA one-line peak forceImpact is a distributed, rate-dependent, nonlinear soft-tissue event; the true force depends on posture and muscle activation.
Single-parameter reservesClear thresholds per systemFunction is multi-factorial; a reserve near one interacts with fatigue, comorbidity, and psychology, not modelled here.
Set-point mechanostatTraining and disuse in one lawAdaptation saturates, has genetic ceilings, and depends on nutrition and hormones (endocrine effects on bone and muscle) beyond a single rate $k_{\rm ad}$.

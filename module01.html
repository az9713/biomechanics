0. Motivation — why a coffee cup gets heavy

Hold a half-litre coffee cup against your chest and you can stand there indefinitely. Now extend your arm straight out and hold the same cup horizontally: within a minute your shoulder burns. The cup did not gain mass. Gravity did not strengthen. What changed is purely geometric — the distance from your shoulder to the cup — and that distance enters the mechanics through a quantity called the moment (torque).

This module makes that intuition exact. We will derive, from Newton's laws, the formula for the torque a joint must supply to hold a load, show it scales with the cup's distance and with $\cos\theta$ as you lower the arm, and then convert that joint torque into the much larger force inside the muscle — the price of the body's lever geometry. By the end you can put a number on every "hold," "lean," and "lift" in daily life, and every later module (bone stress, muscle activation, balance, gait) is built on this same free-body bookkeeping.

1. Definitions, notation, and the governing law

We work in SI units throughout: length in metres (m), mass in kilograms (kg), force in newtons (N $=\mathrm{kg\,m\,s^{-2}}$), torque in newton-metres (N·m). Define every term at first use.

Definition 1.1 (scalar, vector). A scalar is a single number with units (e.g. mass $m$). A vector is a quantity with both magnitude and direction, written $\mathbf{v}$, with magnitude $\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert$. In the sagittal plane (the side-view plane that splits the body into left and right) a vector has two components, $\mathbf{v}=(v_x,v_y)$.
Definition 1.2 (force, weight, gravitational field). A force $\mathbf{F}$ (N) is a push or pull; by Newton's second law it changes a body's momentum. The gravitational field near Earth's surface is $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$ pointing down. The weight of a mass $m$ is the force $\mathbf{W}=m\mathbf{g}$, with magnitude $W=mg$, directed downward.
Definition 1.3 (line of action, position vector). The line of action of a force is the infinite straight line through the point where the force is applied, parallel to the force. The position vector $\mathbf{r}$ of a point, relative to a chosen reference point $O$, is the displacement from $O$ to that point.
Definition 1.4 (center of mass). The center of mass (COM) of a body is the mass-weighted average position, $\mathbf{r}_{\text{COM}}=\frac{1}{M}\sum_i m_i\mathbf{r}_i$ (or the integral $\frac{1}{M}\int \mathbf{r}\,dm$). For computing gravitational moments, the entire weight of a segment may be replaced by a single force $W=mg$ acting downward through its COM. This replacement is exact for a uniform field and is the workhorse of the whole module.
Definition 1.5 (free-body diagram). A free-body diagram (FBD) isolates one body, replaces everything it touches by the force/torque that contact exerts, and shows the body's own weight at its COM. Every analysis below starts by drawing (in text) the FBD.

The governing law for this module is the static specialization of Newton's laws.

Law 1 (static equilibrium). A rigid body is in static equilibrium (no linear and no angular acceleration) if and only if the net force and the net moment about any point vanish: $$\boxed{\;\sum_i \mathbf{F}_i = \mathbf{0}, \qquad \sum_i \mathbf{M}_i = \mathbf{0}.\;}\tag{1.1}$$ The moment $\mathbf{M}_i$ of force $\mathbf{F}_i$ is defined in §2.
Newton's second law for the center of mass reads $\sum_i\mathbf F_i = M\mathbf a_{\text{COM}}$, and the angular-momentum principle about a fixed point reads $\sum_i\mathbf M_i = \dot{\mathbf L}$, with $\mathbf L$ the angular momentum. Static means no motion, so $\mathbf a_{\text{COM}}=\mathbf 0$ and $\mathbf L$ is constant, giving $\dot{\mathbf L}=\mathbf 0$; both right-hand sides vanish, which is (1.1). It remains to justify "about any point." The moment sum about a point $A$ is $\sum_i(\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_A)\times\mathbf F_i$; subtracting the sum about a second point $B$, $$\sum_i(\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_B)\times\mathbf F_i-\sum_i(\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_A)\times\mathbf F_i=(\mathbf r_A-\mathbf r_B)\times\sum_i\mathbf F_i.$$ Once the first equation $\sum_i\mathbf F_i=\mathbf 0$ holds, the right-hand side is $\mathbf 0$, so the net moment is the same about every point: vanishing about one point forces it to vanish about all. This is what lets us take moments about the joint axis in every derivation below, killing the unknown joint force. ∎

2. The moment of a force

With force and position defined in §1, we now define the quantity that makes the extended arm hard to hold.

Definition 2.1 (moment / torque, moment arm). The moment (torque) of a force $\mathbf{F}$ applied at a point with position $\mathbf{r}$ relative to reference $O$ is the cross product $$\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F},\qquad \text{magnitude}\quad M=\lVert\mathbf{r}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{F}\rVert\sin\varphi,\tag{2.1}$$ where $\varphi$ is the angle between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{F}$. The moment arm $d=\lVert\mathbf{r}\rVert\sin\varphi$ is the perpendicular distance from $O$ to the force's line of action, so $$\boxed{\,M = d\,\lVert\mathbf{F}\rVert\,}\tag{2.2}$$ — "moment $=$ moment arm $\times$ force." In the sagittal plane $\mathbf{M}$ points out of the plane and reduces to the signed scalar $M=x F_y - y F_x$.

The key consequence, used everywhere below: for a downward weight, the moment arm is simply the horizontal distance from the pivot to the weight's line of action. Move the weight farther out horizontally and the moment grows in direct proportion.

3. Static equilibrium of a body segment

We model a limb segment as a rigid link: a rigid body hinged at a joint. Combining Law 1 with the moment definition (2.2), and taking moments about the joint axis (a convenient choice, because the unknown joint contact force passes through that axis and so contributes zero moment), gives a single scalar equation that determines the muscle's required moment.

Proposition 3.1 (moment balance about a joint). For a segment hinged at joint $O$, in static equilibrium, the net muscle moment $\tau$ about $O$ balances the moments of all gravitational and external loads: $$\tau \;=\; \sum_k W_k\,d_k,\tag{3.1}$$ where $W_k=m_k g$ is the weight of the $k$-th mass (segment or external load) and $d_k$ is its horizontal moment arm from $O$.
Isolate the segment (Definition 1.5). The forces on it are: the joint contact force $\mathbf{J}$ at $O$; each weight $\mathbf{W}_k=m_k\mathbf g$ acting downward through its COM (Definition 1.4); and the net muscle force, whose moment about $O$ we call $\tau$. Apply the moment equation of Law 1, $\sum \mathbf M=\mathbf 0$, taking moments about $O$. The joint force has zero moment arm ($\mathbf r=\mathbf 0$ at $O$), so $\mathbf J$ drops out. Each weight is downward, so by (2.2) its moment magnitude is $W_k d_k$ with $d_k$ the horizontal distance from $O$, and all gravitational moments share the same rotational sense (they pull the segment down). Balancing the muscle moment against them gives $\tau-\sum_k W_k d_k=0$, i.e. (3.1). ∎

4. Main result — joint torque to hold a load

We now apply Proposition 3.1 to the motivating task: the forearm held out, an object in the hand. This is the elbow version of "holding the coffee cup," and the result generalizes verbatim to every joint in §6.

Definition 4.1 (model parameters). Let $m_s$ be the mass of the forearm-plus-hand segment, with COM a distance $r_s$ from the elbow axis; let $m_L$ be the mass of the held load, gripped a distance $r_L$ from the elbow. Let $\theta$ be the angle of the forearm below the horizontal ($\theta=0$: forearm horizontal; $\theta=90^\circ$: forearm hanging straight down).
Theorem 4.2 (required joint torque). The muscle moment the elbow must supply to hold the segment and load statically at angle $\theta$ is $$\boxed{\;\tau(\theta)\;=\;g\cos\theta\,\bigl(m_s r_s + m_L r_L\bigr).\;}\tag{4.1}$$ It is maximal when the forearm is horizontal ($\theta=0$) and zero when the forearm hangs vertically ($\theta=90^\circ$).
By Proposition 3.1, taking moments about the elbow, the two downward weights are $W_s=m_sg$ and $W_L=m_Lg$. When the forearm makes angle $\theta$ below horizontal, a point at distance $r$ along the forearm sits a horizontal distance $r\cos\theta$ from the elbow (the projection onto the horizontal). Hence the moment arms are $d_s=r_s\cos\theta$ and $d_L=r_L\cos\theta$. Substituting into (3.1): $$\tau(\theta)=W_s d_s + W_L d_L = m_s g\,(r_s\cos\theta)+m_L g\,(r_L\cos\theta).$$ Factor out $g\cos\theta$ to obtain (4.1). At $\theta=0$, $\cos\theta=1$ (maximum); at $\theta=90^\circ$, $\cos\theta=0$, so $\tau=0$ — a hanging arm needs no holding torque. ∎

The $\cos\theta$ factor is exactly the everyday effect from §0: the load is hardest to hold when the limb is horizontal and costs nothing when it hangs straight down. The animation traces the shrinking moment arm $d_L=r_L\cos\theta$ as the forearm lowers.

5. From joint torque to muscle force: mechanical disadvantage

Theorem 4.2 gives the torque a joint must supply. But muscles produce force, and they attach close to the joint — at a small muscle moment arm $d_m$, defined (exactly as in Definition 2.1) as the perpendicular distance from the joint axis to the muscle's line of action (typically a few centimetres). Re-using (2.2) for the muscle's own moment, $\tau=F_m\,d_m$, gives the internal force.

Proposition 5.1 (muscle force). A muscle whose line of action lies a perpendicular distance $d_m$ from the joint axis must generate force $$\boxed{\;F_m=\frac{\tau}{d_m}\;}\tag{5.1}$$ to supply the joint torque $\tau$. Since $d_m\ (\sim 0.03\ \mathrm m)$ is far smaller than the load's moment arm $r_L\ (\sim 0.35\ \mathrm m)$, $F_m$ is many times the load weight.
Isolate the segment as in Proposition 3.1 and take moments about the joint axis $O$. Besides the gravitational loads (whose net moment is $\tau=\sum_k W_k d_k$ by (3.1)), the only remaining moment is that of the muscle force $\mathbf F_m$, whose line of action passes a perpendicular distance $d_m$ from $O$; by (2.2) its moment magnitude is $F_m d_m$, and it opposes the load moment (the muscle holds the segment up). The moment equation of Law 1 about $O$ therefore reads $F_m d_m-\tau=0$, i.e. $F_m d_m=\tau$, and dividing by $d_m\gt0$ gives (5.1). ∎

The muscle force is not the only internal force the joint carries. Vertical force balance — the $\sum\mathbf F=\mathbf 0$ half of Law 1, so far unused — fixes the compressive load the bones press through the joint.

Proposition 5.2 (joint reaction force). For the horizontal segment of §4, with a vertical muscle force $F_m$ up and the weights $W_s,W_L$ down, the joint contact force $\mathbf J$ the neighbouring bone exerts on the segment is vertical with magnitude $$\boxed{\;J=F_m-(W_s+W_L)\;}\tag{5.2}$$ directed downward onto the segment (equivalently, the two bones press together across the joint with compressive force $J$).
Take the same free body as Proposition 5.1, now applying the force half of Law 1, $\sum\mathbf F=\mathbf 0$. In the horizontal pose every force is vertical: the muscle pulls up ($+F_m$), the two weights pull down ($-W_s,-W_L$), and the neighbouring bone contributes the unknown joint reaction $\mathbf J=(0,J_y)$. Summing the $y$-components, $J_y+F_m-W_s-W_L=0$, so $J_y=-(F_m-W_s-W_L)$. Because $F_m\gg W_s+W_L$ (the lever disadvantage of Proposition 5.1), $J_y\lt0$: the reaction points down on the segment, with magnitude $J=F_m-(W_s+W_L)$ as in (5.2). By Newton's third law the segment presses up on the bone with the same magnitude — the two bones are squeezed together, so $J$ is a joint compression. ∎

Equation (5.2) is why the internal forces do not simply cancel: the muscle must overcome the load with a large upward pull, and almost all of that pull is passed straight into the joint as compression. We put a number on it in §8, and Module 2 turns that compression into a bone stress.

7. Standing on the ground — reaction force, center of pressure, dynamic equilibrium

Every free body above has been a single segment, closed off at a joint by the joint force $\mathbf J$. But the whole body meets the outside world at one place — the ground — and the force there has its own name, its own point of application, and (once the body moves) its own inertial correction. These three ideas — ground reaction force, center of pressure, and dynamic equilibrium — are the bridge from this static module to standing (Module 7), walking (Module 8), and jumping (Module 9).

Definition 7.1 (ground reaction force). The ground reaction force (GRF) $\mathbf F_{\text{GRF}}$ is the force the ground exerts on the body through the feet. By Newton's third law it is equal and opposite to the force the feet press onto the ground. In quiet standing it is vertical and, by force balance, equals body weight; during push-off or landing it also carries horizontal (shear) components and can exceed body weight severalfold.
Definition 7.2 (center of pressure). Contact with the ground is not a point but a pressure distribution $p(\mathbf x)\ge 0$ spread over the sole. The center of pressure (COP) is the pressure-weighted centroid of that distribution — the single point $\mathbf x_{\text{COP}}=\bigl(\int \mathbf x\,p\,dA\bigr)/\bigl(\int p\,dA\bigr)$ at which the resultant GRF may be taken to act, reproducing the true distribution's net force and moment. It is the ground-contact analogue of the center of mass (Definition 1.4).
Definition 7.3 (base of support). The base of support is the convex region of the ground enclosed by the contact points (for two feet, the outline around both soles). The COP can only lie inside it — pressure cannot be negative, so the resultant cannot act outside the contact patch.
Proposition 7.4 (standing requires COP under COM). For a person standing statically (no acceleration), the horizontal position of the center of pressure must coincide with the horizontal position of the center of mass: $$\boxed{\;x_{\text{COP}}=x_{\text{COM}}.\;}\tag{7.1}$$ Equilibrium is therefore possible only while $x_{\text{COM}}$ stays over the base of support.
Take the whole body as the free body. Two external forces act: weight $W=Mg$ down through the COM at horizontal position $x_{\text{COM}}$, and the resultant GRF up through the COP at $x_{\text{COP}}$ (Definitions 7.1–7.2). Force balance (the $\sum\mathbf F=\mathbf 0$ half of Law 1) gives $F_{\text{GRF}}=W$. Now take moments about the COP: the GRF has zero arm there, so only the weight contributes, with moment $W\,(x_{\text{COM}}-x_{\text{COP}})$. Static equilibrium demands $\sum\mathbf M=\mathbf 0$, so $W\,(x_{\text{COM}}-x_{\text{COP}})=0$; since $W\gt0$, $x_{\text{COP}}=x_{\text{COM}}$, which is (7.1). If the COM drifts past the edge of the base of support, no admissible COP (which must stay inside it, Definition 7.3) can satisfy (7.1): the couple $W\!\cdot\!(x_{\text{COM}}-x_{\text{COP}})$ can no longer be cancelled and the body starts to topple. ∎

Worked example — leaning forward while standing

Stand upright, then lean the trunk forward without moving the feet. The whole-body COM shifts forward by some horizontal offset $d_{\text{COM}}$ from the ankle. To stay in balance the ankle plantarflexors must supply a torque holding the body against the toppling moment of gravity, and the COP slides forward under the new COM. For a $70\ \mathrm{kg}$ person ($W=687\ \mathrm N$) who leans until the COM sits $d_{\text{COM}}=0.05\ \mathrm m$ ahead of the ankle axis:

the ankle torque from Proposition 3.1 is $\tau_{\text{ankle}}=W\,d_{\text{COM}}=687\times0.05\approx 34\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, and by Proposition 7.4 the COP must sit $5\ \mathrm{cm}$ forward of the ankle to match. A typical foot extends only $\sim 12\ \mathrm{cm}$ from the ankle to the toe, so the COM can travel at most that far before the COP hits the toe — beyond it the only recovery is a step. This is the static skeleton of the postural-sway and stepping analyses in Modules 7 and 10.

Definition 7.5 (dynamic equilibrium). When the body accelerates, Newton's laws keep their full form: $\sum\mathbf F=M\mathbf a_{\text{COM}}$ and $\sum\mathbf M=\dot{\mathbf L}$ (with $\mathbf L$ the angular momentum, $\dot{\mathbf L}=I\alpha$ for a rigid body of moment of inertia $I$ and angular acceleration $\alpha$). Moving the inertial terms to the force side defines the d'Alembert inertial force $-M\mathbf a_{\text{COM}}$ and inertial moment $-\dot{\mathbf L}$: with these added as fictitious loads, a moving body satisfies the same $\sum\mathbf F=\mathbf 0$, $\sum\mathbf M=\mathbf 0$ bookkeeping as a static one.

Static equilibrium (Law 1) is exactly the special case $\mathbf a_{\text{COM}}=\mathbf 0$, $\dot{\mathbf L}=\mathbf 0$. The consequence for the ground: in quiet standing $F_{\text{GRF}}=W$, but the instant the COM accelerates upward (rising onto the toes, the take-off of a jump) the vertical GRF becomes $F_{\text{GRF}}=W+M a_y\gt W$, and the COP no longer sits under the COM but under the COM plus the inertial force. Every later dynamic module is this one correction — an added $M\mathbf a$ — layered onto the free-body bookkeeping built here.

8. Worked numerical example

Take a $70\ \mathrm{kg}$ adult holding a $m_L=5\ \mathrm{kg}$ object (a full grocery bag) with the forearm horizontal ($\theta=0$). Anthropometric values (Appendix): forearm+hand mass $m_s=0.022\times70=1.54\ \mathrm{kg}$, segment COM at $r_s=0.116\ \mathrm m$, grip at $r_L=0.35\ \mathrm m$, flexor moment arm $d_m=0.03\ \mathrm m$, $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$.

Required elbow torque from (4.1) with $\cos 0=1$: $$\tau = 9.81\,(1.54\times0.116 + 5\times0.35)=9.81\,(0.1786+1.75)=9.81\times1.9286 \approx 18.9\ \mathrm{N\,m}.$$ Muscle force from (5.1): $$F_m=\frac{\tau}{d_m}=\frac{18.9}{0.03}\approx 631\ \mathrm N \approx 64\ \mathrm{kgf}.$$ Here $1\ \mathrm{kgf}$ (kilogram-force) denotes the weight of a $1\ \mathrm{kg}$ mass, $\approx 9.81\ \mathrm N$. Joint compression from (5.2), with $W_s=m_s g=15.1\ \mathrm N$ and $W_L=m_L g=49.1\ \mathrm N$: $$J=F_m-(W_s+W_L)=631-(15.1+49.1)\approx 567\ \mathrm N.$$

Interpretation. Holding a 5 kg bag (weight $\approx 49$ N) at arm's length, the biceps/brachialis must pull with $\approx 630$ N — about 13 times the bag's weight, and roughly the force of hanging a 64 kg mass from the tendon. Almost all of that pull re-enters the elbow as a compressive joint reaction of $\approx 567$ N — about $0.8\times$ the person's body weight ($687$ N) crushing the elbow just to hold a light bag. The "heaviness" you feel is this internal amplification, set by the lever ratio $r_L/d_m\approx 12$, not by the bag itself; the joint compression $J$ is the load Module 2 turns into bone stress.

9. Computational lab and sensitivity analysis

Physical question: how does the required joint torque vary with forearm angle $\theta$ and load $m_L$? Model: Theorem 4.2. Parameters: as in §8. The script below evaluates (4.1) over $\theta\in[0,90^\circ]$ for four loads and reports the worst case and the implied muscle force.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

    return g*np.cos(theta)*(m_s*r_s + m_L*r_L)


theta = np.linspace(0, np.pi/2, 200)
for m_L in [0, 2, 5, 10]:

plt.legend()
plt.grid(alpha=0.3)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("m1_torque.png")

    print(f"load {m_L:>2} kg -> tau={t0:5.2f} N*m, F_m={t0/d_m:6.0f} N")

Conceptual problems (C1–C10)

C1. Two people hold identical 4 kg dumbbells: one with the arm horizontal ($\theta=0$), one with the forearm $60^\circ$ below horizontal. Without computing, rank their elbow torques and their biceps forces, and justify with (4.1) and (5.1). Probes: the $\cos\theta$ dependence and the torque-to-force conversion.

Show solution

By (4.1) the elbow torque scales as $\cos\theta$: horizontal gives $\cos0=1$, the bent arm $\cos60^\circ=0.5$, so the horizontal arm needs twice the torque. By (5.1) muscle force is $F_m=\tau/d_m$; if $d_m$ is treated as constant the biceps force also ranks horizontal $\gt$ bent by the same factor of two. (In reality $d_m$ varies with elbow angle — problem K1 — which can shift the force ranking; here we hold $d_m$ fixed.) So both the torque and the force are larger for the horizontal arm.

C2. The §0 coffee cup: explain, using the moment arm, why the identical cup is easy against the chest but burns at arm's length — even though its weight never changes. Probes: moment $=$ moment arm $\times$ force, and that only the horizontal distance matters.

Show solution

The shoulder torque is $\tau=W_L\,d$, where $d$ is the horizontal distance from the shoulder axis to the cup's line of action (Definition 2.1, and the horizontal-distance corollary of §2). Against the chest $d$ is a few centimetres; extended, $d\approx0.5$ m — an order of magnitude larger. The weight $W_L$ is identical, so the torque (and hence the muscle force, by (5.1)) grows in direct proportion to $d$. "Heaviness" is torque, not weight.

C3. In the segment free body, the $\sum\mathbf M=0$ equation determines the muscle moment while the joint force drops out, but the $\sum\mathbf F=0$ equation determines the joint reaction. Explain which unknown each half of Law 1 exposes, and why you cannot get the joint compression from moments about the joint alone. Probes: the complementary roles of the force and moment balances (Prop 5.1 vs 5.2).

Show solution

Taking moments about the joint axis kills the joint force (zero moment arm), leaving one equation in one unknown — the muscle moment $\tau$ (Prop 3.1/5.1). The joint reaction $\mathbf J$ never appears there, so moments about the joint carry no information about it. To recover $\mathbf J$ you must use the other half of Law 1, $\sum\mathbf F=0$ (Prop 5.2): with $F_m$ now known, force balance gives $J=F_m-(W_s+W_L)$. Each equilibrium equation exposes a different internal force; you need both.

C4. Classify the elbow-flexion lever and the ankle-plantarflexion (push-off) lever as first-, second-, or third-class. Identify the pivot, effort, and load in each, and state which arrangement multiplies force and which multiplies speed. Probes: the lever-class taxonomy and the force-vs-speed trade the §5 caption corrects.

Show solution

Elbow flexion is a third-class lever: pivot at the elbow, effort (biceps) applied between pivot and load (hand). Effort arm $\lt$ load arm, so $F_m\gt W_L$ — force is sacrificed to gain hand speed and range. Ankle push-off is a second-class lever: pivot at the toe/ball, body-weight load between pivot and the calf effort at the heel. Here effort arm $\gt$ load arm, so the muscle force is less than the supported load — force is multiplied. The blanket claim "human limbs are third-class levers" is therefore wrong for the ankle at push-off.

C5. A person stands quietly. Explain why the center of pressure must sit directly under the center of mass, and what physically happens as the COM drifts toward the toe edge of the base of support. Probes: Prop 7.4 and the base-of-support limit.

Show solution

With only weight (down at COM) and the resultant GRF (up at COP), a moment balance about the COP leaves only $W(x_{\text{COM}}-x_{\text{COP}})$; static equilibrium forces it to zero, so $x_{\text{COP}}=x_{\text{COM}}$ (Prop 7.4). The COP is the pressure centroid and cannot leave the contact patch. As the COM nears the toe, the COP piles up at the toe; once the COM passes the toe, no admissible COP can cancel the weight's moment, an uncancelled toppling couple appears, and the person must step (or fall).

C6. In quiet standing the vertical ground reaction force equals body weight. Explain why, at the instant you accelerate the body upward (rising onto the toes, jump take-off), the GRF must exceed body weight, and relate this to dynamic equilibrium. Probes: dynamic equilibrium $F_{\text{GRF}}=M(g+a)$ as the $a\neq0$ generalization of statics.

Show solution

Newton's second law for the COM in the vertical direction reads $F_{\text{GRF}}-W=Ma_y$ (Definition 7.5). In quiet standing $a_y=0$, so $F_{\text{GRF}}=W$. To accelerate the COM upward ($a_y\gt0$) the ground must push harder: $F_{\text{GRF}}=W+Ma_y\gt W$. The extra force is exactly the inertial term; static equilibrium is the $a_y=0$ special case, and every dynamic module adds this $Ma$ correction to the same bookkeeping.

C7. Lifting even a light object from the floor with a straight-legged stoop can load the lumbar spine to thousands of newtons. Using the low-back geometry of §6, explain why the compression is so large and why "keep the load close" helps most. Probes: the tiny erector-spinae moment arm and the dominance of horizontal reach.

Show solution

The extensor torque about L5/S1 is $g(m_{\text{ub}}r_t+m_L r_L)$ with reaches of $0.3$–$0.4$ m, but the erector spinae balance it through an arm of only $d_{\text{es}}\approx0.05$ m. By (5.1) the muscle force is the torque divided by that small arm — an $\sim8\times$ amplification — and almost all of it becomes disc compression (Prop 5.2). Because the torque is proportional to horizontal reach, halving $r_L$ (load against the belly) or squatting to shrink $r_t$ cuts the compression nearly in half, far more than trimming the object's weight.

C8. You carry a heavy box either pressed against your belly or held out in front at arm's length. Which posture loads the low back more, and why? Frame the answer with the same moment-arm argument as C2. Probes: transferring the moment-arm intuition to whole-trunk loading.

Show solution

The box's contribution to the L5/S1 torque is $W_L$ times its horizontal distance from the spine. Held out front, that distance is large ($\sim0.4$ m); pressed to the belly it is small ($\sim0.1$ m), so the spinal torque — and, through the small erector-spinae arm, the compression — is roughly four times smaller when the load is kept close. Same physics as the coffee cup, scaled up to the trunk.

C9. A child has limb segments half the length of an adult's but geometrically and proportionally similar (same shape, same density). Argue how the load moment arm and the gravitational holding torque scale with the linear size $L$. Probes: dimensional/scaling reasoning ahead of the formal derivation in D8.

Show solution

Moment arms are lengths, so $r\propto L$. Segment mass scales with volume, $m\propto L^3$ (density fixed). The self-weight holding torque $\tau\sim m g r\propto L^3\cdot L=L^4$. Halving $L$ therefore cuts the segment's own holding torque by $2^4=16$. This steep scaling (derived formally in D8) is why small animals and children are relatively strong for their size — their limbs' self-loading falls faster than their muscle cross-section ($\propto L^2$).

C10. Pushing a door with the same force produces no rotation at the hinge but swings it wide at the handle. Explain via the cross-product definition of moment why direction and point of application matter, not just force magnitude. Probes: $\mathbf M=\mathbf r\times\mathbf F$ and the role of the moment arm.

Show solution

The moment is $\mathbf M=\mathbf r\times\mathbf F$, magnitude $\lVert\mathbf r\rVert\lVert\mathbf F\rVert\sin\varphi$ (Definition 2.1). At the hinge $\mathbf r=\mathbf 0$, so $\mathbf M=\mathbf 0$ no matter how hard you push. At the handle $\lVert\mathbf r\rVert$ is the door width and, pushing perpendicular ($\varphi=90^\circ$), the full $\lVert\mathbf r\rVert\lVert\mathbf F\rVert$ becomes torque. A push aimed along $\mathbf r$ ($\varphi=0$) again gives zero. Thus turning effect depends on where and in what direction the force acts, through the moment arm $d=\lVert\mathbf r\rVert\sin\varphi$.

D2. Derive the shoulder torque for the whole upper limb: an upper-arm segment (mass $m_a$, COM at $r_a$) plus forearm-hand and a hand load, all treated as point masses at their horizontal arms. Assume the limb is straight and horizontal. Probes: summing gravitational moments over several segments.

Show solution

Each mass contributes $W_k$ times its horizontal arm. With the limb horizontal ($\theta=0$) and taking moments about the shoulder, $$\tau_{\text{sh}}=g\bigl(m_a r_a+m_s r_{s}'+m_L r_L'\bigr),$$ where $r_s'$, $r_L'$ are the forearm-COM and grip distances measured from the shoulder. D3. Prove that when $\sum\mathbf F=\mathbf 0$ the net moment is the same about every reference point, and use it to argue that taking moments about the joint is the computationally smartest choice for a limb segment. Probes: reference-point independence (the second half of the Law 1 proof).

Show solution

For points $A$, $B$, the moment sums differ by $$\textstyle\sum_i(\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_B)\times\mathbf F_i-\sum_i(\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_A)\times\mathbf F_i=(\mathbf r_A-\mathbf r_B)\times\sum_i\mathbf F_i.$$ If $\sum_i\mathbf F_i=\mathbf 0$ the difference vanishes, so the net moment is reference-independent. Choosing the joint axis as the reference sets the (unknown) joint force's arm to zero, removing it from the moment equation and leaving a single equation for the muscle moment — the smallest possible system.

D4. The elbow flexor does not pull straight up but along a line at angle $\varphi$ to the forearm. For a horizontal forearm holding load $W_L$ at $r_L$, with muscle insertion at $r_m$, derive the muscle force and both components of the joint reaction, and show the reaction has a compressive component along the bone. Probes: vector force balance with a non-vertical muscle line (extends Prop 5.2).

Show solution

Moments about the elbow: only the muscle's transverse component $F_m\sin\varphi$ has a moment arm $r_m$, so $F_m\sin\varphi\,r_m=W_L r_L$, giving $F_m=W_L r_L/(r_m\sin\varphi)$. Force balance: along the forearm (x), $J_x+F_m\cos\varphi=0\Rightarrow J_x=-F_m\cos\varphi$ (a compression pointing into the joint); transverse (y), $J_y+F_m\sin\varphi-W_L=0\Rightarrow J_y=W_L-F_m\sin\varphi$. The axial term $J_x=-F_m\cos\varphi$ is the bone compression the vertical-muscle model of Prop 5.2 folds into $J$; a shallow insertion (small $\varphi$) makes it large.

D5. Model the stooped lift as a trunk flexed at angle $\beta$ from vertical, carrying upper-body weight $m_{\text{ub}}g$ at along-trunk distance $\ell_t$ and a load $m_L g$ at along-trunk distance $\ell_L$. Derive the L5/S1 compression as a function of $\beta$ and find $\partial(\text{compression})/\partial\beta$. Probes: parameterizing the low-back load by posture and differentiating.

Show solution

Horizontal arms are the along-trunk distances projected: $r=\ell\sin\beta$. The extensor torque is $\tau(\beta)=g\sin\beta\,(m_{\text{ub}}\ell_t+m_L\ell_L)$, the erector force $F_{\text{es}}=\tau/d_{\text{es}}$, and the compression adds the along-spine weight component: $$C(\beta)=\frac{g\sin\beta}{d_{\text{es}}}(m_{\text{ub}}\ell_t+m_L\ell_L)+(m_{\text{ub}}+m_L)g\cos\beta.$$ Then $\displaystyle\frac{dC}{d\beta}=\frac{g\cos\beta}{d_{\text{es}}}(m_{\text{ub}}\ell_t+m_L\ell_L)-(m_{\text{ub}}+m_L)g\sin\beta.$ The first (muscle) term dominates because of the tiny $d_{\text{es}}$, so $C$ rises steeply as the trunk tips from upright — bending deeper is costly.

D6. A person stands on two feet and holds a load off to one side. Modeling the two foot-contacts as the ends of the base of support, derive the center-of-pressure position and show it shifts toward the loaded foot. Probes: the COP as a weighted average of contact reactions.

Show solution

Let the feet be at $x_L$ and $x_R$ carrying vertical reactions $R_L$, $R_R$ with $R_L+R_R=W_{\text{tot}}$. The COP is the reaction-weighted centroid $x_{\text{COP}}=(R_L x_L+R_R x_R)/W_{\text{tot}}$. By Prop 7.4 it must equal $x_{\text{COM}}$; adding a load on the right shifts $x_{\text{COM}}$ right, which (through moment balance about each foot) raises $R_R$ relative to $R_L$, pushing $x_{\text{COP}}$ toward the loaded foot. It stays inside $[x_L,x_R]$ as long as balance holds.

D7. During the upward phase of rising from a squat the COM accelerates upward with $a(t)$. Derive the vertical ground reaction force $F_{\text{GRF}}(t)$ and, by impulse–momentum, the COM velocity gained over the push. Probes: dynamic equilibrium and the impulse–momentum theorem.

Show solution

Vertical Newton's law: $F_{\text{GRF}}(t)-Mg=Ma(t)$, so $F_{\text{GRF}}(t)=M\,(g+a(t))$. Integrating over the push $[0,T]$, the net vertical impulse is $\int_0^T (F_{\text{GRF}}-Mg)\,dt=M\int_0^T a\,dt=M\,\Delta v$, giving $\Delta v=\int_0^T a\,dt$ — the area under the acceleration pulse. The GRF above body weight is precisely what accelerates the COM; K7 evaluates it for a measured pulse.

D8. For geometrically similar bodies of linear size $L$ (same shape and density), derive the scaling of the gravitational holding torque and of the ratio (torque demand)/(muscle capacity), where muscle force capacity scales with cross-sectional area. Interpret for children and small animals. Probes: dimensional analysis and allometric scaling.

Show solution

Mass $\propto L^3$, moment arm $\propto L$, so self-weight torque $\tau_{\text{demand}}\propto L^3\cdot L=L^4$. Muscle force capacity $\propto$ physiological cross-section $\propto L^2$, and its moment arm $\propto L$, so torque capacity $\tau_{\text{cap}}\propto L^2\cdot L=L^3$. The safety ratio $\tau_{\text{cap}}/\tau_{\text{demand}}\propto L^3/L^4=L^{-1}$: it grows as size shrinks. Smaller bodies are relatively stronger — a child or an ant lifts many times its body weight, a scaling a large animal cannot match.

D9. Standing, a person leans the trunk forward so the whole-body COM moves a horizontal distance $d_{\text{COM}}$ ahead of the ankle. Derive the required ankle torque and the COP position, and find the maximum lean before the COP reaches the toe (foot length $L_f$ ankle-to-toe). Probes: the standing balance limit (Prop 7.4) as a geometric constraint.

Ankle torque balances gravity's toppling moment: $\tau_{\text{ankle}}=W\,d_{\text{COM}}$ (Prop 3.1, whole body). By Prop 7.4 the COP sits at $x_{\text{COP}}=d_{\text{COM}}$ ahead of the ankle. The COP cannot pass the toe, so the constraint is $d_{\text{COM}}\le L_f$; the maximum static lean is $d_{\text{COM}}^{\max}=L_f$, giving $\tau_{\text{ankle}}^{\max}=W L_f$. D10. Estimate the mechanical power of stair climbing. K2. You measure noisy elbow torque at 25 forearm angles while a subject holds an unknown mass at an unknown grip distance. Recover the load parameter $m_L r_L$ (and $m_L$ if $r_L$ is known) by least-squares regression of $\tau$ on $g\cos\theta$. Probes: an inverse problem — parameter recovery from noisy data.

Show solution

Since $\tau=g\cos\theta\,(m_s r_s+m_L r_L)$ is linear in $g\cos\theta$, the regression slope is the bracket $A=m_s r_s+m_L r_L$. g, m_s, r_s = 9.81, 0.022*70, 0.116

A = np.sum(X*tau)/np.sum(X*X)          # least-squares slope

Show solution

Upright is an unstable equilibrium: linearizing gravity's toppling moment gives $\ddot x=\omega_0^2 x$ with $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}=3.13\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$, whose solution grows as $x(t)=x_0\cosh(\omega_0 t)$. Stepping the ODE numerically from $x_0=0.02$ m, $\dot x_0=0$ and watching for $x=0.12$ m, the COP reaches the toe at $t\approx0.79$ s (analytically $t=\operatorname{acosh}(6)/\omega_0=0.79$ s). Unlike a linear drift, the fall accelerates — most of the excursion happens in the final fraction of a second, which is why balance recovery is so time-critical (Modules 7, 10).

import numpy as np

g, L, toe = 9.81, 1.0, 0.12
w2 = g/L                         # inverted-pendulum growth rate^2
x, v, t, dt = 0.02, 0.0, 0.0, 1e-4
while x < toe:
    v += w2*x*dt                 # x'' = (g/L) x  (unstable)
    x += v*dt
    t += dt
print(f"step needed at t = {t:.2f} s")
# step needed at t = 0.79 s

K7. A force-plate study gives the vertical COM acceleration during a jump take-off as $a(t)=15\sin(\pi t/0.3)\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$ over $0\le t\le0.3$ s. Compute the GRF$(t)=M(g+a)$, integrate to get take-off velocity, and predict jump height. Probes: numerical integration of measured dynamics.

Show solution

The GRF peaks at $M(g+15)\approx1.74$ kN $\approx2.5\times$ body weight. Integrating the acceleration, $v_{\text{TO}}=\int_0^{0.3}a\,dt\approx2.86\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, and $h=v_{\text{TO}}^2/2g\approx0.42$ m. The take-off velocity is entirely built by the GRF exceeding body weight — the dynamic-equilibrium correction of D7.

import numpy as np

g, M = 9.81, 70.0
t = np.linspace(0, 0.3, 3001)
a = 15*np.sin(np.pi*t/0.3)
GRF = M*(g + a)
v = np.trapezoid(a, t)
print(f"peak GRF {GRF.max():.0f} N = {GRF.max()/(M*g):.2f} BW")
print(f"v_takeoff {v:.2f} m/s, height {v**2/(2*g)*100:.0f} cm")
# peak GRF 1737 N = 2.53 BW ; v 2.86 m/s, height 42 cm

K8. Estimate stair-climbing mechanical power $P=Mghf$ (the rate of gravitational-PE gain derived in D10) for $M=70$ kg. Sweep cadence $f$ and, for step heights $h=0.15,\,0.18,\,0.20\ \mathrm m$, find the maximum cadence that keeps the power under a sustainable $250\ \mathrm W$ ceiling. Which step height limits the pace? Probes: a two-parameter sweep against a power ceiling (regime comparison over step height).

Show solution

Power is linear in cadence, $P=Mghf$, so taller steps reach the ceiling at a lower cadence. Setting $P=250$ W and solving $f=250/(Mgh)$ gives a maximum sustainable cadence of $2.43$, $2.02$, $1.82$ steps/s at $h=0.15,\,0.18,\,0.20$ m: the tallest steps are limiting, so on steep stairs you must slow down to hold the same power. (A moderate $0.17$ m step at $1.5$ steps/s draws $\approx175$ W, comfortably inside the ceiling.)

import numpy as np

M, g, ceil = 70.0, 9.81, 250.0
for h in [0.15, 0.18, 0.20]:
    f_max = ceil/(M*g*h)         # P = M g h f, solved at the ceiling
    print(f"h={h} m -> {M*g*h:.0f} W/(step/s), max f={f_max:.2f}/s")
# 2.43, 2.02, 1.82 steps/s ; tallest step limits the pace

K9. A subject's maximum voluntary elbow torque, measured across forearm angles, does not simply track $\cos\theta$, because the flexor moment arm changes with angle. Given the noisy measured max-voluntary torque at 20 angles and a known constant flexor force capacity $F_{\max}=2000$ N, recover the moment-arm model $d_m(\theta)=d_0+d_1\sin\theta$ by least squares. Report $d_m$ at the horizontal forearm and near $\theta=90^\circ$. Probes: a two-parameter least-squares inverse for an angle-varying moment arm (and the identifiability that requires the sweep).

Show solution

The measured torque is $\tau_{\max}(\theta)=F_{\max}\,d_m(\theta)=F_{\max}(d_0+d_1\sin\theta)$, linear in the two unknowns $d_0,d_1$. Regressing $\tau_{\max}$ on the columns $[F_{\max},\,F_{\max}\sin\theta]$ recovers $d_0\approx3.0$ cm and $d_1\approx0.008$ m from noisy data (true $0.030,\,0.008$), so the moment arm rises from $\approx3.0$ cm at the horizontal forearm to $\approx3.8$ cm near $\theta=90^\circ$. Note the identifiability: a single MVC measurement gives one equation $\tau=F_{\max}d_m$ in two unknowns and cannot separate force from moment arm; sweeping the angle breaks the degeneracy. Measuring $d_m$ directly from imaging is the more reliable route to the same quantity.

import numpy as np

rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
Fmax = 2000.0
th = np.radians(np.linspace(0, 80, 20))
d_true = 0.030 + 0.008*np.sin(th)
tau = Fmax*d_true + rng.normal(0, 0.8, th.size)   # noisy MVC torque
A = np.column_stack([np.ones_like(th), np.sin(th)])*Fmax
d0, d1 = np.linalg.lstsq(A, tau, rcond=None)[0]
print(f"d0={d0*100:.2f} cm, d1={d1:.4f} m")
print(f"d_m(0)={d0*100:.2f} cm, d_m(90)={(d0+d1)*100:.2f} cm")
# d0=3.02 cm, d1=0.0078 m ; d_m(0)=3.02, d_m(90)=3.79 cm

K10. To reduce elbow load you may either carry $2$ kg less or shorten the reach by $8$ cm. Compute the torque saved by each intervention as the base load $m_L$ varies, and find the crossover load at which shortening the reach overtakes shedding mass. Probes: a regime comparison with a crossover, guiding an intervention choice.

Show solution

At $\theta=0$, removing $\Delta m=2$ kg saves $g\,\Delta m\,r_L=6.87\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, independent of the base load. Shortening the reach by $\Delta r=8$ cm saves $g\,m_L\,\Delta r$, which grows with $m_L$. They are equal when $m_L=\Delta m\,r_L/\Delta r=8.75$ kg: for lighter loads shed mass, for heavier loads pull the load in. The crossover is the practical rule of thumb.

import numpy as np

g, r_L = 9.81, 0.35
save_mass = g*2*r_L               # constant in m_L
cross = 2*r_L/0.08                # where g*mL*0.08 == save_mass
print(f"mass-saving = {save_mass:.2f} N*m (all loads)")
print(f"crossover load = {cross:.2f} kg")
# mass-saving = 6.87 N*m ; crossover load = 8.75 kg

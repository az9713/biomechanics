0. Motivation - from a toolkit to a model

Sixteen modules have built a toolkit. Forces and free bodies (Module 1); bone as a beam and cartilage and tendon as biphasic and viscoelastic tissue (Modules 2, 4, 6); muscle as a chemo-mechanical actuator (Module 5); joints, standing, walking, running, and balance as mechanics under control (Modules 3, 7-10); reaching and coordination (Modules 11-12); the movements of an ordinary day (Module 13); aging and injury (Module 14); measurement and inverse dynamics (Module 15); and the continuum beneath it all (Module 16). Each was a piece. A capstone is what happens when you pick a real question and assemble the pieces into a model that answers it - and then check whether the answer is right.

This module is a set of such projects. Each takes a concrete question - why does standing sway? how large is the knee torque rising from a chair? will this fall break a hip? - and carries it through the full arc of a rigorous model: state the problem, choose the physical model and its biological basis, write the governing equations, list the assumptions, tabulate the parameters, simulate, plot, interpret, bound the errors, propose a validation, and name the extensions. The arc is the same every time, which is the point: modeling is a method, not a bag of tricks, and the method is the real deliverable of the course.

The projects are deliberately varied - a control problem, an inverse-dynamics estimate, three gait models, an impact-and-fracture calculation, and a catalogue of nine more - so that between them they exercise every module. Six are worked in full (Sections 2-7); nine more are specified as ready-to-build briefs (Section 8); and a closing section (Section 9) treats the part beginners skip and experts obsess over - validation and the honest report. First, the method.

1. The modeling method: a rigorous capstone template

A model is not a simulation that runs; it is an argument that a chosen abstraction answers a question to a stated accuracy. The argument has a fixed skeleton, and every capstone below fills it in. Stating the skeleton once turns the projects from demonstrations into exercises of one transferable method.

The capstone template (eleven elements). (1) Problem - the question, sharp enough to have a number for an answer. (2) Physical model - the idealisation (a pendulum, a spring, a beam) and why it is adequate. (3) Biological mechanism - what tissue or control realises it. (4) Equations - the governing law, derived or cited. (5) Assumptions - each idealisation, named. (6) Parameters - a table of values with sources and units. (7) Simulation - the numerical method and code. (8) Plots - the computed result. (9) Interpretation - what the number means. (10) Limitations and validation - where the model fails and how you would check it against data. (11) Extensions - the next question it opens.

Three principles run through the eleven and are worth stating as their own small results, because they are what separates a rigorous capstone from a plausible-looking one.

Principle 1.1 (the simplest adequate model). Among models that capture the mechanism the question turns on, prefer the one with the fewest parameters; add complexity only when a validation shows the simpler model fails. A model with more free parameters than the data constrains can fit anything and predicts nothing.
Principle 1.2 (dimensional soundness and limits). Every equation must be dimensionally consistent, and every result must reduce to a known answer in a limiting case (zero load, small angle, infinite stiffness). A model that passes both is not proven right, but a model that fails either is certainly wrong - the cheapest tests available, applied first.
Principle 1.3 (an answer without an error bar is not a result). A predicted number carries an uncertainty inherited from its parameters and its idealisations; the capstone must estimate it (by propagation or Monte Carlo, Module 15) and report it. The width of the error bar decides whether the answer can settle the question at all.

These are not pieties. Principle 1.1 is why the projects below reach for a one-parameter inverted pendulum before a fifty-muscle simulation; Principle 1.2 is the small-angle and static checks each capstone runs; Principle 1.3 is why every headline number comes with a range. With the template fixed, the projects begin - each a filled-in instance of it, and together a tour of the whole course.

2. Capstone I - quiet standing with delayed feedback

Problem. Why does a person standing "still" sway, and what sets the limit beyond which they fall? Physical model. the body as an inverted pendulum of mass $m$ and height $L$, balanced by an ankle torque from a proportional-derivative controller acting through a neural delay $\Delta$ (Modules 7, 10). Mechanism. vestibular, visual, and proprioceptive feedback (Module 10) drives ankle muscle (Module 5), but the signal arrives late.

Model 2.1 (delayed inverted pendulum). The lean angle $\theta$ obeys $$\boxed{\;I\ddot\theta=mgL\,\theta-\big[k_p\,\theta(t-\Delta)+k_d\,\dot\theta(t-\Delta)\big],\;}$$ with $I=mL^2$, gravitational toppling term $mgL\,\theta$ (unstable), and a delayed restoring torque of gains $k_p,k_d$. The undelayed system is stable when $k_p\gt mgL$; the delay erodes that stability, and beyond a critical $\Delta_c$ the closed loop oscillates and grows - a fall.

Assumptions: small angle (linearised), single ankle joint, constant gains, planar. Parameters: $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, $L=1.0\ \mathrm{m}$, $k_p=1.5\,mgL$, $k_d=1.5\,mgL$, delay $\Delta\in\{80,140\}\ \mathrm{ms}$. Validation: the stable sway magnitude (a few degrees) and the critical delay (~150 ms) match Module 10 and posturography data.

import numpy as np

g, L, m = 9.81, 1.0, 70.0
Iz, kp, kd = m*L*L, 1.5*m*g*L, 1.5*m*g*L
fs = 200.0
dt = 1/fs


def simulate(delay):
    n = int(delay/dt)
    th = np.zeros(1200)
    om = np.zeros(1200)
    th[0] = 0.05                     # small initial lean
    for k in range(1, len(th)-1):
        kk = max(0, k - n)           # delayed sample
        tq = kp*th[kk] + kd*om[kk]
        thdd = (m*g*L*th[k] - tq)/Iz
        om[k+1] = om[k] + thdd*dt
        th[k+1] = th[k] + om[k+1]*dt
    return th


print("80 ms peak lean = %.1f deg" % (np.abs(simulate(0.08)).max()*57.3))
print("140 ms peak lean = %.1f deg" % (np.abs(simulate(0.14)).max()*57.3))

Interpretation and limits. An $80\ \mathrm{ms}$ delay sits inside the stability island: the initial lean decays and the model stands. A $140\ \mathrm{ms}$ delay is past $\Delta_c$: the same lean grows into a fall (Fig. 2). Standing is thus not passive but an actively stabilised unstable equilibrium, and its most fragile parameter is the delay - which is exactly why the delay's rise with age (Module 14) predicts falls. The model omits the sensory noise that drives real sway (add it as a stochastic forcing, Module 10) and the hip strategy that supplements the ankle. Validation: compare the simulated sway spectrum and critical delay to force-plate posturography; extension: add noise and map the full stability island in the delay-gain plane.

3. Capstone II - knee torque during a sit-to-stand

Problem. How large is the knee extensor torque demanded by rising from a chair - the demand that thins to a failure with age (Module 13)? Physical model. a prescribed knee-extension trajectory driving an inverse-dynamics estimate of the joint torque (Module 15). Mechanism. the quadriceps (Module 5) extend the knee against the gravitational load of the head-arms-trunk (HAT).

Model 3.1 (inverse dynamics of the rise). With a measured knee angle $q(t)$, the net knee torque is $$\boxed{\;\tau_{\rm knee}(t)=I\ddot q+m_{\rm HAT}\,g\,d(q)+\tau_0,\;}$$ the sum of an inertial term $I\ddot q$, the gravitational load of the HAT of mass $m_{\rm HAT}$ acting through a posture-dependent moment arm $d(q)$, and a baseline. The torque is read off the prescribed motion - the inverse-dynamics pipeline of Module 15 run forward on synthetic kinematics.

Assumptions: planar, prescribed minimum-jerk knee trajectory over $T=1.5\ \mathrm{s}$, quasi-static gravity plus small inertial term. Parameters: $m_{\rm HAT}=48\ \mathrm{kg}$, moment arm $d\approx0.20\ \mathrm{m}$ at seat-off, $I\approx0.12\ \mathrm{kg\,m^2}$. Validation: the $\sim$1.5 N·m/kg peak matches instrumented sit-to-stand studies and Module 13's chair demand.

import numpy as np

g, T = 9.81, 1.5
t = np.linspace(0, T, 200)
s = t/T
q = (np.pi/2)*(1 - (10*s**3 - 15*s**4 + 6*s**5))   # knee extends
qdd = np.gradient(np.gradient(q, t), t)
m_hat, d, Iz = 48.0, 0.20, 0.12
tau = m_hat*g*d*np.sin(q) + 0.6*Iz*np.abs(qdd) + 0.15*m_hat*g*d
print("peak knee torque = %.0f N m (%.2f N m/kg)" % (tau.max(), tau.max()/70))

Interpretation and limits. The torque peaks near seat-off - deep knee flexion with the whole upper body cantilevered forward - at about $109\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, roughly $1.5\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ per kilogram of body mass (Fig. 3). This is the number Module 13 tracked as a reserve: when age-thinned quadriceps can no longer supply it, the rise fails without arms or momentum. The model prescribes the kinematics rather than predicting them and lumps the trunk into one mass; a full model would solve the whole multi-segment inverse dynamics from marker data (Module 15). Validation: compare to force-plate and motion-capture sit-to-stand torques; extension: add the momentum strategy (a forward trunk lean that trades a horizontal impulse for a lower peak torque).

4. Capstone III - walking as an inverted pendulum

Problem. Why does walking have its characteristic twin-peaked ground-reaction force, and what caps its speed? Physical model. the compass / inverted-pendulum gait: the body vaults over a stiff stance leg like a pendulum inverted (Module 8). Mechanism. the stance leg redirects the centre of mass along an arc; gravity and the leg's thrust make the vertical force rise, dip, and rise again.

Model 4.1 (inverted-pendulum vault and the Froude limit). The vertical ground reaction is $F=m(g+\ddot y_{\rm COM})$, and because the COM arcs over the foot the required centripetal force sets a speed limit: the leg can push (not pull), so $mg-\dfrac{mv^2}{L}\ge0$, i.e. $$\boxed{\;\mathrm{Fr}=\frac{v^2}{gL}\le1,\;}$$ the Froude number; walking becomes a run near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx0.5$ ($v\approx\sqrt{gL/2}\approx2.5\ \mathrm{m/s}$ for a $1\ \mathrm{m}$ leg).

Assumptions: rigid stance leg, point-mass COM on a circular arc, single support. Parameters: $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, leg $L=1.0\ \mathrm{m}$; force normalised to body weight (BW). Validation: the twin-peaked M-shape (peaks $\sim$1.1-1.3 BW, mid-stance dip below BW) and the $\mathrm{Fr}\approx0.5$ transition match measured gait.

Interpretation and limits. The vault produces the M-shaped vertical force of Fig. 4 (blue): a loading peak as the COM rises, a mid-stance dip below body weight as it crests the arc (centripetal unloading), and a push-off peak. The Froude limit explains why humans switch to running at a predictable speed and why leg length sets it. The rigid-leg model misses the knee flexion and the double-support phase; a spring-loaded leg (next capstone) is the running counterpart. Validation: compare the force trace and preferred-transition speed to treadmill gait data; extension: add the step-to-step transition cost (Module 8) to predict the metabolically optimal speed.

5. Capstone IV - running as a spring-mass system

Problem. Why is running's ground reaction a single large hump, and how big is it? Physical model. the spring-loaded inverted pendulum (SLIP): a point mass on a massless leg-spring that compresses and rebounds each stance (Module 9). Mechanism. the leg spring is the series elasticity of tendon (Module 6) storing and returning the landing energy.

Model 5.1 (spring-mass stance). During stance the leg spring of stiffness $k$ and rest length $\ell_0$ produces a vertical force $F=k\,(\ell_0-\ell)\cos\phi$ that rises to a single peak at mid-stance and returns to zero at toe-off, giving a stance impulse that reverses the vertical momentum. For human running the peak reaches $$\boxed{\;F_{\rm peak}\approx2.5\text{-}3\ \text{body weights},\;}$$ set by the flight time to be absorbed and the (short) ground-contact time.

Assumptions: massless linear leg-spring, symmetric stance, point-mass COM. Parameters: $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$; leg stiffness tuned so the peak is $\approx2.6$ BW; contact $\sim$0.2 s. Validation: the single-hump shape and $\sim$2.5-3 BW peak match running force-plate data (Module 9).

Figure 4 plots the running force (dark red) beside the walking force: the spring-mass bounce gives one hump peaking near $2.6$ body weights, more than double the walking peak, over a shorter, sharper contact. The single versus twin peak is the cleanest mechanical signature separating the two gaits, and the higher peak is why running loads bone and tendon much more heavily each step - the impact risk of Module 9. Interpretation: the leg-spring stiffness is a control choice, tuned to the surface and speed; a stiffer leg raises the peak but shortens contact. Limits/validation: the massless-spring SLIP omits the swing leg and damping (real tendons return $\sim$90%, not 100%, of the energy, Module 6); compare the modelled and measured peak and contact time. Extension: add a damping term and predict the metabolic cost of the lost energy.

6. Capstone V - jump and landing forces

Problem. How large is the force of a landing, and why does bending the knees soften it? Physical model. impulse-momentum: the landing must remove the downward momentum over the distance the joints flex (Module 9). Mechanism. eccentric muscle and tendon (Modules 5, 6) absorb the kinetic energy over the flexion distance.

Model 6.1 (landing force from cushioning distance). A body landing at speed $v=\sqrt{2gh}$ (from drop height $h$) and arrested over a COM displacement $d$ absorbs its kinetic energy as work $F_{\rm avg}\,d=\tfrac12mv^2$, so the peak force scales as $$\boxed{\;F_{\rm peak}\approx\frac{mv^2}{2d}+mg=\frac{mgh}{d}+mg.\;}$$ The force is inversely proportional to the cushioning distance $d$ - a hyperbola.

Assumptions: constant deceleration over $d$, rigid-body COM, vertical landing. Parameters: $m=70\ \mathrm{kg}$, drop $h=0.4\ \mathrm{m}$ ($v=2.8\ \mathrm{m/s}$), $d$ from $0.05$ to $0.30\ \mathrm{m}$. Validation: the stiff-vs-soft force ratio matches drop-landing force-plate studies.

import numpy as np

g, m, h = 9.81, 70.0, 0.40
v = np.sqrt(2*g*h)
for d in (0.05, 0.20):
    F = (m*v**2/(2*d) + m*g)/1000
    print("d=%.2f m -> peak force %.1f kN" % (d, F))

Interpretation and limits. A stiff, $5\ \mathrm{cm}$ landing spikes to $\sim$6 kN (nearly nine body weights); flexing to $20\ \mathrm{cm}$ quarters it to $\sim$2 kN (Fig. 5). The same energy spread over four times the distance is a quarter of the force - the entire rationale of landing technique, and of the eccentric strength that lets a joint flex under load without collapsing. The constant-deceleration model overestimates the smoothness of a real landing; the true force has a sharper transient. Validation: compare peak force and timing to instrumented drop landings; extension: replace the constant force with the spring-damper of the SLIP model and resolve the force transient.

7. Capstone VI - hip-fracture risk in a sideways fall

Problem. Will a sideways fall break a hip, and at what bone density does the risk cross over? Physical model. a margin: the fall-impact force against the femur's strength (Modules 2, 14). Mechanism. the femoral neck (Module 16's bone continuum) fails when the impact stress exceeds its strength, which falls as the square of bone density (Module 2).

Model 7.1 (fracture margin). The fall delivers a peak impact $F\approx v\sqrt{m_{\rm eff}k}\approx4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$ (Module 14), and the femoral strength scales as $S=S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^2$ (Module 2). Fracture occurs when the margin drops below one: $$\boxed{\;\frac{S}{F}=\frac{S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^2}{F}\lt 1\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \Big(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}\Big)_{\rm crit}=\sqrt{F/S_0}.\;}$$

Assumptions: impact and strength as lumped scalars, quasi-static failure, density-squared strength law. Parameters: young strength $S_0=7\ \mathrm{kN}$, impact $F\approx4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$. Validation: the $\sim$79% critical density and the hip-protector shift match the fragility-fracture data of Module 14.

Interpretation and limits. The margin crosses one at about $79\%$ of young bone density (Fig. 6): above it a sideways fall bruises, below it the identical fall fractures the hip. The squared strength law makes the margin fall steeply, so a modest density loss is dangerous out of proportion - and both levers of prevention (lower $F$ with a hip protector, raise $S$ with loading) follow directly. The lumped-scalar model hides the true stress field, which the continuum of Module 16 would resolve to find where the neck actually cracks. Validation: compare the critical density to clinical fracture thresholds; extension: replace the scalar strength with a finite-element femur model loaded in the fall configuration.

9. Validation, uncertainty, and the honest report

The element every beginner skips and every expert dwells on is the last: is the answer right, and how sure are we? A simulation that runs is not a validated model, and a number without an error bar is not a result (Principle 1.3). This section makes the closing discipline explicit, because it is what separates a capstone from a demonstration.

The validation ladder (climb as far as the data allow). (1) Internal checks - dimensional consistency and limiting cases (Principle 1.2): does the force vanish at zero load, the strain at zero stress, the torque reduce to $mgL$ in a static hold? (2) Face validity - is the number the right order of magnitude and sign (a knee torque of tens, not thousands, of newton-metres)? (3) Independent measurement - does it match data the model was not fitted to (a force plate, EMG timing, a known modulus)? (4) Cross-model agreement - does a second, independent model give the same answer (the force-plate and kinematic COM accelerations of Module 15)?

Uncertainty is the other half. A capstone's prediction inherits error from two sources - the parameters (a body-segment mass known to $\pm15\%$, Module 15) and the idealisations (a rigid leg, a lumped mass) - and both must be propagated to the output, by linear error propagation or Monte Carlo (Module 15). The result is a prediction with a band, and the band decides everything: a knee-torque estimate of $109\pm8\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ can settle whether a patient clears a threshold; one of $109\pm60\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ cannot, and the honest report says so.

The honest report, then, has a fixed close: the prediction, its error bar, the validation it passed, the assumptions that bound it, and the one experiment that would test it hardest. A capstone that ends this way has done the whole job - not just produced a number, but earned it and said how far to trust it. This is the standard the entire course has been building toward.

10. Problems and extensions

Jump to: Conceptual · Derivational · Computational · Diagnostics

Twelve problems - four on the method, four on the capstone models, four computational extensions - plus five diagnostics. Each carries a Probes note and a worked solution; the computational ones extend the projects above rather than re-running them.

Conceptual ↑ contents

C1 - the simplest adequate model. Why prefer a one-parameter inverted pendulum to a fifty-muscle simulation for the question "why does standing sway"? Probes: Principle 1.1; Section 1.
Solution
The sway question turns on one mechanism - delayed feedback stabilising an unstable equilibrium - which the inverted pendulum captures with a single delay parameter. A fifty-muscle model adds parameters the sway data cannot constrain, so it can fit anything and predicts nothing new about the mechanism; its extra fidelity answers a different question. Match the model's complexity to the question and the data, not to the anatomy.
C2 - the two error sources. A capstone's prediction has an error bar. What two distinct sources feed it, and which does averaging reduce? Probes: Principle 1.3; Section 9.
Solution
Random error (measurement noise, trial-to-trial variation) and systematic error (a body-segment parameter or an idealisation that is wrong the same way every time). Averaging many trials shrinks the random part but not the systematic part, which repeats identically; only measuring or identifying the parameter (Module 15), or improving the model, reduces the systematic error. An honest band separates the two.
C3 - one plot, two gaits. How does a single ground-reaction-force trace tell walking from running? Probes: gait signatures; Sections 4-5.
Solution
Walking is an inverted-pendulum vault: the vertical force is twin-peaked (loading and push-off) with a mid-stance dip below body weight as the centre of mass crests its arc. Running is a spring-mass bounce: a single hump peaking near 2.5-3 body weights, with the force zero during flight. The number of peaks and the peak magnitude separate the two mechanisms unambiguously.
C4 - why the fall breaks the hip. The same sideways fall bruises a young hip and fractures an old one. In the capstone margin model, what changed? Probes: the fracture margin; Section 7.
Solution
The impact force $F$ depends on the fall, not the bone, so it is the same; what changed is the strength $S=S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^2$, which falls as the square of bone density. The margin $S/F$ crosses one near $79\%$ density: above it the fall is a bruise, below it a fracture. The squared law makes a modest density loss dangerous out of proportion - a small drift in a parameter flips a threshold.

Derivational ↑ contents

D1 - the delayed-pendulum equation. Write the equation of motion for the delayed-feedback standing model and state its stability condition without delay. Probes: Model 2.1; Section 2.
Solution
Newton for the inverted pendulum: $I\ddot\theta=mgL\theta-[k_p\theta(t-\Delta)+k_d\dot\theta(t-\Delta)]$, with $I=mL^2$. Without delay ($\Delta=0$) it is $I\ddot\theta+k_d\dot\theta+(k_p-mgL)\theta=0$, a damped oscillator, stable iff $k_p\gt mgL$ (restoring stiffness beats gravity's toppling) and $k_d\gt0$. The delay erodes this by adding phase lag, so beyond a critical $\Delta_c$ the loop oscillates unstably.
D2 - the landing-force law. Derive $F_{\rm peak}\approx mgh/d+mg$ for a landing cushioned over distance $d$. Probes: Model 6.1; Section 6.
Solution
Falling from height $h$ gives impact speed $v=\sqrt{2gh}$, hence kinetic energy $\tfrac12mv^2=mgh$. Arresting over a COM displacement $d$ at a roughly constant force does work $F_{\rm avg}\,d$, which must absorb the kinetic energy plus the gravity work over $d$: $F_{\rm avg}\,d\approx mgh+mgd$, so $F_{\rm avg}\approx mgh/d+mg$. The peak is a small factor above the average; the key dependence is $F\propto1/d$ - a hyperbola in the cushioning distance.
D3 - the Froude speed limit. Derive the walking speed limit from the requirement that the stance leg can only push. Probes: Model 4.1; Section 4.
Solution
At mid-stance the COM moves on a circular arc of radius $L$ at speed $v$, needing centripetal force $mv^2/L$ directed toward the foot (downward). Gravity supplies $mg$ downward and the leg can only push up (not pull), so the net downward force available for the centripetal requirement is at most $mg$: $mv^2/L\le mg$, i.e. $\mathrm{Fr}=v^2/(gL)\le1$. Above it the COM would fly off the arc - the walk must become a run (transition near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx0.5$).
D4 - the fracture threshold. Derive the critical bone density at which a sideways fall fractures the hip. Probes: Model 7.1; Section 7.
Solution
Fracture at $S=F$: with $S=S_0(\rho/\rho_0)^2$ and impact $F$, $(\rho/\rho_0)_{\rm crit}=\sqrt{F/S_0}$. For $F\approx4.4\ \mathrm{kN}$ and young strength $S_0\approx7\ \mathrm{kN}$, $(\rho/\rho_0)_{\rm crit}=\sqrt{0.63}\approx0.79$ - a $21\%$ density loss suffices. A hip protector cutting $F$ by $\sqrt2$ lowers the threshold to $\approx0.66$, protecting a hip that has lost a third of its density.

Computational ↑ contents

Each extends a worked capstone by a sweep, an inverse, an optimisation, or an uncertainty analysis - not a re-run.

K1 - the critical delay (inverse). Find, by bisection on the standing simulation, the largest neural delay the balance loop tolerates, and compare to the human value. Probes: locating a stability boundary; Section 2.
Solution
Bisect the delay between a known-stable and a known-unstable value, simulating each and testing whether the lean stays bounded, until the interval closes on $\Delta_c$. For the gains of Capstone I the critical delay lands near $130$-$150\ \mathrm{ms}$, just above the human $\sim$100 ms - so a healthy stander sits inside the island with a modest margin, and the age-related delay increase of Module 14 eats exactly that margin. The bisection turns a yes/no simulation into a boundary value.
K2 - the leg-stiffness sweep (sensitivity). Sweep the running leg-spring stiffness and find how the peak force and contact time trade off. Probes: the SLIP parameter; Section 5.
Solution
Integrating the spring-mass stance across a range of leg stiffness $k$: a stiffer leg gives a higher, sharper force peak and a shorter ground-contact time; a softer leg spreads a lower peak over a longer contact. The peak scales roughly as $\sqrt{k}$ and the contact time as $1/\sqrt{k}$, so their product (the impulse) is fixed by the flight momentum to be reversed. The sweep shows leg stiffness is the runner's tuning knob between a jarring, economical stride and a soft, costly one.
K3 - the momentum strategy (optimisation). For the sit-to-stand, optimise the forward trunk lean that minimises the peak knee torque. Probes: trading horizontal for vertical demand; Section 3.
Solution
Parameterise the rise by the amount of forward trunk lean before seat-off and compute the peak knee torque for each; a forward lean moves the centre of mass over the feet before the knees extend, converting part of the vertical lift into a horizontal momentum transfer and lowering the peak knee demand. The optimum is an intermediate lean - too little leaves the torque high, too much risks a forward fall - reproducing the momentum strategy older adults adopt as their knee reserve shrinks (Module 13).
K4 - the knee-torque error bar (uncertainty). Monte Carlo the sit-to-stand knee-torque estimate under body-segment-parameter and timing uncertainty, and report the band. Probes: propagating uncertainty; Section 9, Module 15.
Solution
Draw the HAT mass and moment arm from their $\pm10$-$15\%$ ranges and the movement time from its variability, run the inverse-dynamics estimate on each draw, and collect the peak knee torque. The result is a distribution - a mean near $109\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ with a band of order $\pm15\%$, dominated by the segment-parameter uncertainty (Module 15's error budget). Whether that band clears a clinical threshold is the answer the honest report must give; a bare $109\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ hides it.

Five diagnostics ↑ contents

  1. Model choice. When should a capstone add a parameter?
    Solution
    Only when a validation shows the simpler model fails - never merely because more detail is available (Principle 1.1).
  2. The static check. What must any joint-torque model return for a limb held still and horizontal?
    Solution
    Exactly the gravity torque $mgL$, with no dynamic term - the cheapest limiting-case check (Principle 1.2).
  3. Gait peaks. How many vertical-force peaks per stance for walking, and for running?
    Solution
    Two for walking (an M-shape about a mid-stance dip), one for running.
  4. Cushioning. Doubling the landing flexion distance changes the peak force how?
    Solution
    Roughly halves it, since $F\propto1/d$ (Model 6.1).
  5. The honest close. What five things end a rigorous capstone report?
    Solution
    The prediction, its error bar, the validation passed, the bounding assumptions, and the sharpest experiment that would test it.

These twelve close the course's problem sets as they began - with questions that have numbers for answers, and answers that come with the evidence for trusting them. The capstones are where the toolkit becomes a practice; the method is the lasting result.

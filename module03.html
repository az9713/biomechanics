0. Motivation — why the knee bends one way and the shoulder dislocates

Sit down and watch your knee: it swings the shin forward and back through a wide arc, but try to twist the shin sideways or slide it left and right relative to the thigh and almost nothing happens. The knee behaves, to good approximation, as a one-axis hinge — and it does so while transmitting forces several times body weight every step. Now roll your shoulder: the hand can reach overhead, behind your back, across your chest, in almost any direction. The shoulder is the body's most mobile joint. It is also, by a wide margin, the most frequently dislocated. The same freedom that lets the arm explore space is the freedom for the joint to come apart.

This is the central trade-off of Module 3, and it is not anatomy trivia — it is a statement about constraints. A rigid body floating in space has six independent ways to move (three translations, three rotations). A joint is a device that removes some of those freedoms by physical contact: matching bony surfaces, taut ligaments, and the pressure of cartilage on cartilage. Each freedom removed is a constraint; the freedoms that remain are the joint's degrees of freedom. The knee removes five and keeps one; the shoulder removes only three and keeps three. Mobility and stability are the two sides of that ledger, and you cannot increase one without spending the other.

But a constraint is not free. To hold the shin from sliding sideways, the knee's surfaces and ligaments must actively push and pull — and the size of that hidden force is exactly what determines whether cartilage wears smoothly for eighty years or grinds into osteoarthritis. So this module answers three quantitative questions:

  • How many ways can a joint move? We count degrees of freedom precisely, as the dimension of configuration space minus the number of independent constraints (§1), and classify every major joint by that number (§2).
  • What force does the constraint carry? We write the equations of motion of a constrained body and show that each constraint comes with a Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$ that is the constraint force (§3). Applied to a real limb, this reveals that the joint reaction force is several times body weight even in quiet standing (§4), and turning that force into a contact pressure (§5) sets up the cartilage mechanics of Module 4.
  • Why trade mobility for stability at all? We quantify the trade-off and see why evolution built a deep, congruent socket at the hip (which almost never dislocates) and a shallow, mobile one at the shoulder (§6) — then build a working simulation of a constrained limb that computes its own joint forces (§7).

The mathematical engine throughout is constrained dynamics: the same Newton–Euler and Lagrangian mechanics from Module 1, now augmented with constraint equations and the multipliers that enforce them. The loads we feed in are the muscle and joint torques of Module 1; the contact pressures we get out are the inputs to Module 4. Joints are where the skeleton's freedom is rationed — and where its forces are concentrated.

Builds on Module 1 (forces, torques, free-body diagrams) and Module 2 (the loads bones and joints must carry).

1. Configuration space and degrees of freedom

To count how a joint can move, we first need precise language for where a body is. That language is the configuration.

Generalized coordinates

Definition 1.1 (generalized coordinates, configuration space). A set of numbers $q=(q_1,\dots,q_n)$ that fixes the position of every part of a system is a set of generalized coordinates. The space of all allowed $q$ is the configuration space $Q$, and $n=\dim Q$ is the number of coordinates.

The coordinates need not be Cartesian. For a hinged limb, angles are far more natural than $xy$-positions.

Example. A planar two-link arm is fixed completely by its two joint angles, $q=(\theta_1,\theta_2)$. Every pose of the arm is therefore a single point in the $(\theta_1,\theta_2)$ plane — and moving the arm draws a path through that plane.

Degrees of freedom

Definition 1.2 (degrees of freedom). The number of degrees of freedom (DOF) is the number of independent quantities that can be varied independently — the dimension of the set of reachable configurations.

A single rigid body, free in 3-D space, has 6 DOF: three translations and three rotations. Confined to a plane, only three survive — two translations and one rotation.

Constraints reduce the count

A joint, a contact, or a taut ligament forces the coordinates to obey an equation. Each such independent equation removes one freedom.

Definition 1.3 (holonomic constraint). A holonomic constraint is a relation $g(q)=0$ among the coordinates alone (not their velocities). With $n$ coordinates and $m$ independent holonomic constraints, the degrees of freedom are $$\boxed{\,f = n - m\,}\tag{1.1}$$

Constraining a free particle ($n=3$) to a surface adds one equation ($m=1$), leaving $f=2$. Pinning a planar body to the ground removes its two translations, leaving $f=1$ — a hinge.

Aside. Constraints on velocities that cannot be integrated into a relation among coordinates — e.g. a wheel rolling without slipping — are nonholonomic; they restrict motion without reducing $f$. We meet them only in passing; joints in this module are holonomic.

This single count is the spine of the module: classify every joint by how many freedoms it keeps (§2), then compute the force each removed freedom costs (§3).

2. A taxonomy of joints by degrees of freedom

Two bodies meeting freely share six relative freedoms (§1). A synovial joint — a freely movable joint where two cartilage-capped bones meet inside a fluid-filled capsule — of mobility $f$ removes $6-f$ of them by keeping its surfaces congruent. Every such joint falls into one of six classical types.

Why exactly these? Kinematic pairs

Each type is a way two surfaces can stay in contact while sliding — a lower kinematic pair. A cylindrical surface on a matching groove gives a single rotation (revolute → hinge); a sphere in a socket gives three (spherical → ball-and-socket).

Bone shape is the constraint: the trochlea of the humerus (the spool-shaped lower end of the upper-arm bone) admits one axis; the femoral head (the ball atop the thigh bone) is a sphere that admits three. Anatomy and kinematics are the same statement.

Counting a limb: the mobility formula

For an open chain (no loops), the freedoms simply add — the DOF of the end-effector (the free tip of the chain, e.g. the hand) is the sum of the joint mobilities:

Mobility of a serial chain. $$\boxed{\,f_{\text{end}}=\sum_i f_i\,}\tag{2.1}$$ For a mechanism with loops, use the Grübler–Kutzbach count $M=6(N-1)-\sum_i(6-f_i)$, where $N$ counts all bodies including ground.

The arm. Shoulder (3) + elbow (1) + forearm pronation (1) + wrist (2) $= 7$ DOF for the hand.

Placing the hand at a chosen position and orientation needs only 6. The arm has one to spare — it is kinematically redundant, which is why you can keep your hand flat on a table and still swing your elbow. We exploit this in §6 and Module 11.

Real joints are idealizations. "1 DOF" is a modeling choice, justified by the dominant motion. The knee is not a true hinge: as it extends it also rolls, glides a few millimetres, and rotates axially (the "screw-home"). We adopt the idealized constraints here because they capture the freedoms that carry the load; §7 shows what the idealization costs.

Each constraint in the table — every "$6-f$" — is a freedom the joint must actively prevent. Preventing it takes force. §3 computes that force.

3. Constraint forces and Lagrange multipliers

A joint earns its degrees of freedom by forbidding the others, and forbidding a motion takes a force. This section derives that force from first principles. The result is one of the most useful facts in mechanics: every holonomic constraint carries along a number — its Lagrange multiplier — and that number is the constraint force. For a joint, it is the joint reaction.

We build the result in three steps: first the direction a constraint force must point (virtual work), then the full equations of motion of a constrained body, then how to solve them for the multiplier. We then watch λ turn into a real, measurable tension in a swinging pendulum.

3.1 What direction must a constraint force point?

A holonomic constraint $g(q)=0$ confines the system to a surface in configuration space — the constraint surface. Any motion the system is allowed to make stays on that surface, so its velocity $\dot q$ is tangent to it.

The role of the constraint force is to prevent the system from leaving the surface — and nothing more. It does no work on motions that stay on the surface. This is d'Alembert's principle: constraint forces do no virtual work on any virtual displacement $\delta q$ consistent with the constraints.

A force that does no work along every tangent direction must be perpendicular to the surface. The directions perpendicular to $g(q)=0$ are spanned by the gradient rows of the constraint Jacobian $J_c=\partial g/\partial q$. Hence the constraint force has the form $J_c^{\mathsf T}\lambda$ for some vector $\lambda$ — one number per constraint.

3.2 The equations of motion of a constrained body

Start from the unconstrained dynamics. For generalized coordinates $q$, Lagrangian mechanics (Module 1) gives the Euler–Lagrange equations, which for a mechanical system always take the matrix form

$$M(q)\,\ddot q + c(q,\dot q) = Q,\tag{3.1}$$

where $M(q)$ is the (symmetric, positive-definite) mass matrix, $c(q,\dot q)$ collects the Coriolis, centrifugal and gravitational terms, and $Q$ is the generalized applied force (muscle torques, external loads).

Now add the constraints. By §3.1 the constraint contributes the generalized force $J_c^{\mathsf T}\lambda$, so the constrained system obeys:

Theorem 3.1 (constrained equations of motion). A mechanical system with mass matrix $M(q)$, bias term $c(q,\dot q)$, applied force $Q$, and holonomic constraints $g(q)=0$ evolves by $$\boxed{\;M(q)\,\ddot q + c(q,\dot q) = Q + J_c^{\mathsf T}\lambda,\qquad g(q)=0,\;}\tag{3.2}$$ where $J_c=\partial g/\partial q$ is the constraint Jacobian and $\lambda$ are the Lagrange multipliers. The term $J_c^{\mathsf T}\lambda$ is the generalized constraint (joint reaction) force.

The symbols, gathered:

3.3 Solving for the multiplier

Equation (3.2) has $n+m$ unknowns ($\ddot q$ and $\lambda$) but only $n$ dynamical equations. The missing $m$ equations come from the constraint itself: differentiate $g(q)=0$ twice in time. Once gives $J_c\dot q=0$; again gives the acceleration-level constraint

$$J_c\,\ddot q + \dot J_c\,\dot q = 0.\tag{3.3}$$

Equations (3.2) and (3.3) together are a single linear system in $(\ddot q,\lambda)$ — the classic saddle-point (KKT) form:

$$\begin{bmatrix} M & J_c^{\mathsf T}\\[2pt] J_c & 0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\ddot q\\[2pt] -\lambda\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}Q-c\\[2pt] -\dot J_c\,\dot q\end{bmatrix}.\tag{3.4}$$

Eliminating $\ddot q$ (solve the top block, substitute into the bottom) gives the multiplier in closed form:

Proposition 3.2 (the multiplier). $$\boxed{\;\lambda = -\bigl(J_c M^{-1} J_c^{\mathsf T}\bigr)^{-1}\bigl(J_c M^{-1}(Q-c)+\dot J_c\,\dot q\bigr).\;}\tag{3.5}$$ The matrix $J_c M^{-1} J_c^{\mathsf T}$ is invertible whenever the constraints are independent (full-rank $J_c$). The physical constraint force is then $J_c^{\mathsf T}\lambda$.

Two readings of (3.5). The $\dot J_c\dot q$ term is the force needed to keep the body curving along the constraint — the centripetal part. The $J_c M^{-1}(Q-c)$ term is the part of the applied and inertial forces that would push the body off the surface, which the constraint must cancel.

3.4 Worked example: the multiplier is the string tension

Nothing makes "λ is the constraint force" concrete like a case where we already know the answer. Take a plane pendulum: a bob of mass $m$ on a massless rod of length $L$, free to swing. We deliberately keep redundant Cartesian coordinates $q=(x,y)$ (with $y$ measured downward) and impose the rod as a constraint, rather than collapsing to the single angle $\theta$.

The constraint is "stay at distance $L$ from the pivot," written conveniently as

$$g(x,y)=\tfrac12\,(x^2+y^2-L^2)=0,\qquad J_c=\partial g/\partial q=(x,\;y).\tag{3.6}$$

The applied force is gravity, $Q=(0,\,mg)$, where $g$ here is the gravitational acceleration (distinct from the constraint function $g(q)$, which always appears with its argument) and the force points downward, i.e. along $+y$. The mass matrix is just $M=mI$, with $I$ the $2\times2$ identity. Theorem 3.1 gives the two scalar equations of motion

$$m\ddot x = \lambda x,\qquad m\ddot y = mg + \lambda y.\tag{3.7}$$

The constraint force is $\lambda\nabla g=\lambda(x,y)$ — radial, as the figure shows. Now solve for $\lambda$ using (3.3). Differentiating $g$ twice:

$$x\dot x + y\dot y = 0,\qquad x\ddot x + y\ddot y + \dot x^2+\dot y^2 = 0.\tag{3.8}$$

Substitute $\ddot x=\lambda x/m$ and $\ddot y = g+\lambda y/m$ from (3.7), and write $v^2=\dot x^2+\dot y^2$ for the speed:

$$x\Bigl(\frac{\lambda x}{m}\Bigr) + y\Bigl(g+\frac{\lambda y}{m}\Bigr) + v^2 = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \frac{\lambda}{m}(x^2+y^2) + g\,y + v^2 = 0.$$

On the circle $x^2+y^2=L^2$, so

$$\lambda = -\,\frac{m\,(g\,y + v^2)}{L^2}.\tag{3.9}$$

Finally substitute the geometry $y=L\cos\theta$ (bob below pivot) and $v^2=L^2\dot\theta^2$, and read off the magnitude of the radial constraint force, $T=|\lambda|\,\lVert\nabla g\rVert = |\lambda|\,L$:

Result. The Lagrange multiplier yields exactly the elementary string tension — $$\boxed{\;T = m g\cos\theta + m L\dot\theta^{2}\;}\tag{3.10}$$ gravity's component along the rod plus the centripetal term. Releasing from rest at $\theta_0$ and using energy conservation ($v^2=2gL(\cos\theta-\cos\theta_0)$) gives the compact form $T=mg\,(3\cos\theta-2\cos\theta_0)$.

So the abstract multiplier $\lambda$ is not bookkeeping — it is the force you would measure with a gauge on the rod. The animation below traces it: the red arrow is the tension $T$, growing to its maximum $2mg$ at the bottom of the swing (where it must both support the weight and supply centripetal force) and shrinking to $\tfrac12 mg$ at the turning points.

3.5 Why multipliers, when angles would do?

A pendulum has one degree of freedom; we could have used the single coordinate $\theta$ and never written a constraint. That route gives the motion $\ddot\theta=-(g/L)\sin\theta$ directly — but it makes the rod tension vanish from the equations entirely. Reduced coordinates buy simplicity at the price of hiding exactly the force we care about.

The multiplier method keeps the redundant coordinates and the constraint, and pays us back with $\lambda$ — the force the joint must carry. For the body this is decisive: a clinician cares about the load on the hip cartilage, not the hip angle. The next section turns this machinery on a real limb and extracts the joint reaction force, which we will find is several times body weight.

4. Joint reaction force: why a hinge carries far more than body weight

The constraint force $J_c^{\mathsf T}\lambda$ of §3 has a concrete name in the body: the joint reaction force (JRF), the force pressed across the cartilage surfaces where two bones meet. It is the load that cartilage must distribute, that wears it out, and that an implant must survive.

The central — and at first surprising — fact is that the JRF is set mostly by muscle, not by the external load. Muscles attach close to joints, on short moment arms, so to produce a given joint torque they must pull hard (the mechanical disadvantage of Module 1, §5). That large muscle force presses the joint surfaces together. The hand may hold a light cup, yet the joints carry a multiple of it.

4.1 Warm-up: the elbow

Recall the Module 1 example: a 70 kg adult holds a 5 kg load with the forearm horizontal, and the elbow flexors must pull with $F_m\approx 630\ \mathrm N$ on a 3 cm moment arm. What does the elbow joint itself carry?

Take the forearm as a free body. Three vertical forces act on it. The muscle pulls up with $F_m$. The combined weight pulls down, $W_{\text{tot}}=W_{\text{seg}}+W_{\text{load}}$, where $W_{\text{seg}}\approx 15\ \mathrm N$ is the weight of the forearm-plus-hand segment and $W_{\text{load}}=m_{\text{load}}\,g = 5\times 9.81\approx 49\ \mathrm N$ is the weight of the held 5 kg load (both carried over from Module 1), so $W_{\text{tot}}\approx 64\ \mathrm N$. Finally the humerus (the upper-arm bone) pushes on the forearm at the joint with the reaction $R$ we are after. Vertical balance gives

$$R = F_m - W_{\text{tot}} \approx 630 - 64 \approx 566\ \mathrm N.$$

So the elbow is compressed by $\approx 0.8$ times body weight while merely holding a grocery bag — and almost all of it (566 of the 630 N) is the muscle's doing, not the bag's. The load contributes 49 N; the joint carries twelve times that.

4.2 The hip in single-leg stance

The effect is largest at the hip, and it happens every step: during walking you spend much of the cycle balanced on one leg. To hold the pelvis level on a single support, the hip abductor muscles (gluteus medius and minimus — the muscles on the outer hip that pull the thigh away from the body's midline) must generate a large force, and that force, plus body weight, bears down on the femoral head.

Model the frontal plane (the head-on plane dividing the body into front and back halves) at the instant of single-leg stance. The femoral head — the ball at the top of the thigh bone — is the pivot $O$. Everything above the hip, namely the body weight $W$ less the swinging leg, $W_s\approx\tfrac{5}{6}\,W$, acts at the body's center of mass a horizontal distance $b$ to the medial side of $O$ (toward the body's midline). The abductors act at the greater trochanter (the bony bump on the outer thigh) a distance $a$ to the lateral side (away from the midline).

Two equilibrium equations finish it. Treating the forces as vertical (a standard simplification), moments about $O$ give the abductor force, and vertical force balance gives the reaction:

$$F_{ab}\,a = W_s\,b \;\Longrightarrow\; F_{ab}=W_s\frac{b}{a},\qquad R = W_s + F_{ab}.$$
Hip joint reaction (single-leg stance). $$\boxed{\;R = W_s\Bigl(1+\frac{b}{a}\Bigr).\;}\tag{4.1}$$ With $W_s=\tfrac56 W$, abductor arm $a\approx5\ \mathrm{cm}$, and weight arm $b\approx10\ \mathrm{cm}$: $R = \tfrac56 W\,(1+2)=2.5\,W$ — two-and-a-half times body weight, standing on one leg.

The lever ratio $b/a$ is the whole story. Because the abductors work at half the body-weight lever, they must pull at twice body weight, and the femoral head carries their pull plus the weight itself. Gait raises this further — the dynamic terms of §3 add inertial load, pushing peak hip JRF to roughly $3$–$5\,W$ while walking and running.

4.3 The clinical payoff: lean over the sore hip

Equation (4.1) says the JRF is governed by $b$, the distance from the hip to the body's center of mass. So move the center of mass over the hip — lean your trunk toward the stance side — and $b$ collapses, taking $F_{ab}$ and $R$ down with it.

This is exactly what people with a painful or weak hip do, often without being taught: they lurch the trunk over the affected leg at each step (a compensated Trendelenburg gait). It is not a quirk; it is (4.1) solved for comfort.

Why a cane in the opposite hand helps. A cane held on the sound side adds an upward support force on a long lever about the painful hip, contributing its own large counter-moment. Combined with a slight lean, it can cut hip JRF by a further $\sim$1 body weight — which is why the simplest aids produce outsized relief. The same arithmetic, run forward, also explains why carrying a load on the same side as a painful hip reduces its abductor demand, while carrying it on the opposite side worsens it.

4.4 Where this leads

The joint reaction force is the input to everything downstream. Spread it over the cartilage contact area and it becomes a contact pressure — the subject of §5, and the quantity that governs whether cartilage survives or degenerates into osteoarthritis (Module 4). Fed into the femur as an axial-plus-bending load, it sets the bone stresses of Module 2. The hidden multiplier of §3 is, quite literally, the load of a lifetime.

5. From reaction force to contact pressure

A bone does not feel a force; it feels a pressure — force spread over the area that actually touches. The joint reaction $R$ of §4 is delivered through a patch of cartilage (the smooth, load-bearing tissue capping the bone ends), and what governs whether that cartilage survives is not $R$ itself but $R$ divided by the contact area. This section makes that division precise, and shows that the body's two design choices — large, well-matched contact areas and a deliberately soft cartilage layer — are what keep the pressure survivable.

5.1 Mean contact pressure

Definition 5.1 (contact pressure). Pressure $p$ is force per unit area normal to a surface, in pascals ($\mathrm{Pa}=\mathrm{N\,m^{-2}}$; here we work in $\mathrm{MPa}=10^6$ Pa). If a joint reaction $R$ is shared over a contact area $A_c$, the mean contact pressure is $$\boxed{\;\bar p = \frac{R}{A_c}.\;}\tag{5.1}$$

The single most important word here is congruent: a joint whose surfaces match closely (like a ball seated in a snug socket) spreads load over a large $A_c$ and so runs at low pressure; an incongruent joint — surfaces that meet over a small patch — concentrates the same $R$ into a high pressure.

Worked number (hip). The single-leg-stance reaction was $R\approx 2.5\,W\approx 1715\ \mathrm N$ (§4). The hip's loaded cartilage contact area is roughly $A_c\approx 10\ \mathrm{cm^2}=1.0\times10^{-3}\ \mathrm m^2$, so $$\bar p = \frac{1715}{1.0\times10^{-3}} \approx 1.7\ \mathrm{MPa}.$$ A couple of atmospheres' worth of pressure, carried for a lifetime — comfortably below the level that damages cartilage, precisely because the hip is so congruent.

5.2 Pressure is not uniform: Hertz contact

Equation (5.1) gives the average. But two curved elastic bodies pressed together do not share load evenly: the pressure is highest at the centre of contact and falls to zero at the edge. The classical solution is Hertz contact theory, which we state for the case that matters here — a convex bone end (a near-sphere) bearing on its mating surface.

Definition 5.2 (effective radius and modulus). Two contacting bodies of radii $R_1,R_2$ and elastic properties $(E_1,\nu_1),(E_2,\nu_2)$ behave like a single equivalent contact with $$\frac{1}{R_{\text{eff}}}=\frac{1}{R_1}\pm\frac{1}{R_2},\qquad \frac{1}{E^*}=\frac{1-\nu_1^2}{E_1}+\frac{1-\nu_2^2}{E_2},$$ where the sign is $-$ for a ball in a concave socket. Here $E$ is Young's modulus (Module 2) and $\nu$ is Poisson's ratio — the fraction by which a material narrows sideways when stretched (near $0.5$ for nearly-incompressible cartilage). $R_{\text{eff}}$ measures how sharply curved the contact is; $E^*$ is the effective modulus, the combined stiffness of the pair.
Hertz contact (sphere). A normal force $F$ pressed through such a contact produces a circular patch of radius $a$ carrying a dome-shaped pressure that peaks at $p_0$ in the centre: $$\boxed{\;a=\Bigl(\tfrac{3FR_{\text{eff}}}{4E^*}\Bigr)^{1/3},\qquad p_0=\frac{3F}{2\pi a^2}=\Bigl(\tfrac{6F\,{E^*}^{2}}{\pi^3 R_{\text{eff}}^{2}}\Bigr)^{1/3}.\;}\tag{5.2}$$ The pressure profile is $p(r)=p_0\sqrt{1-(r/a)^2}$, and the peak exceeds the mean by exactly $\tfrac32$.

5.3 Why cartilage is soft on purpose

Look at how $E^*$ combines the two surfaces: $1/E^*$ adds the compliances, so the softer material dominates. Cartilage ($E\approx 10\ \mathrm{MPa}$) is about $1700$ times more compliant than bone ($E\approx 17\ \mathrm{GPa}$), so a cartilage-on-cartilage contact has $E^*\approx 6\ \mathrm{MPa}$ against $\approx 9\ \mathrm{GPa}$ for bone-on-bone.

By (5.2), $p_0\propto {E^*}^{2/3}$: a softer contact spreads load and lowers the peak. Running the same joint force through both:

This is only half of cartilage's trick. Because it is also porous and fluid-filled, much of the load is briefly carried by pressurized interstitial water rather than the solid matrix, lowering the stress on the collagen still further — the biphasic behaviour we develop quantitatively in Module 4.

5.4 Failure: when the contact shrinks

Read (5.2) the other way and you have the mechanics of joint degeneration. Anything that shrinks the contact area — a shallow dysplastic socket, a torn meniscus, an incongruence left by a mal-healed fracture, or early cartilage thinning — raises the peak pressure, because for a fixed force $p_0=3F/(2\pi a^2)\propto 1/a^2$.

Higher pressure damages and thins the cartilage, which shrinks the effective contact further, which raises the pressure again: a feedback loop that is one mechanical route into osteoarthritis. The animation shows the runaway — as the contact patch narrows, the pressure dome climbs past the damage threshold.

The chain of this module is now complete: a joint's freedoms (§1–§2) determine its constraints, the constraints carry a force (§3), the force is amplified into a joint reaction (§4), and that reaction becomes the contact pressure (§5) that the living tissue must survive. §6 returns to the mobility-versus-stability trade-off with these forces in hand; Module 4 takes the contact pressure into the biphasic mechanics of cartilage itself.

6. Stability versus mobility

We opened this module (§0) with a puzzle: the hip and the shoulder are both 3-DOF ball-and-socket joints — identical on the constraint ledger of §2 — yet the shoulder dislocates routinely and the hip almost never. The degree-of-freedom count cannot explain it. The answer, now that we can compute joint forces (§3–§5), is that stability is not about how many freedoms a joint has but about how stiffly it resists leaving the ones it forbids — and that stiffness is set by the shape of the socket.

Definition 6.1 (stability, dislocation). A joint is stable if, after a small displacement, internal forces restore it to its seated position. Dislocation is the failure of that restoration: the head (the ball) rides out of the socket entirely. Stability is therefore quantitative — it is the size of the destabilizing force the joint can resist before the head escapes.

6.1 Dislocation as climbing the rim

Model a ball-and-socket as a sphere held in a spherical cup. The joint reaction (§4) presses the head into the cup with a compressive force $N$ directed along the socket axis. A destabilizing force $F_t$ acts sideways, trying to slide the head out.

The head can only leave by riding up the cup wall to its rim. Let $\beta$ be the coverage angle — the angle from the load axis to the rim, i.e. how far the socket wraps around the head. The head stays seated as long as the resultant of $N$ and $F_t$ still points into the cup, that is, while it tilts by less than $\beta$. The resultant tilts by $\varphi=\arctan(F_t/N)$, so dislocation begins when $\varphi=\beta$:

Stability ratio. The sideways force needed to dislocate a ball-and-socket, and the dimensionless stability ratio $S$ (the clinically measured "glenohumeral stability ratio"), are $$\boxed{\;F_{\text{dislocate}} = N\tan\beta,\qquad S \equiv \frac{F_{\text{dislocate}}}{N}=\tan\beta.\;}\tag{6.1}$$ Stability scales with the socket's compressive load $N$ and with the tangent of its coverage angle $\beta$. A deeper socket (larger $\beta$) resists a proportionally larger destabilizing force.

6.2 The trade-off: coverage buys stability, costs range

Equation (6.1) makes the hip–shoulder difference quantitative: with coverage angles of $\beta\approx55^\circ$ (hip) and $\beta\approx22^\circ$ (shoulder), the stability ratios are $S=\tan55^\circ\approx1.4$ versus $S=\tan22^\circ\approx0.4$ — the hip is about $3.5\times$ harder to dislocate per unit of compressive load, before any soft tissue is counted.

But coverage is not free. The deeper the socket, the sooner the bony neck of the head strikes the rim and stops the motion. Stability and mobility pull in opposite directions along the single dial $\beta$:

6.3 Active stabilization: the shoulder's bargain

A stability ratio of $0.4$ sounds alarmingly low — yet most shoulders never dislocate. The shoulder buys back stability not with bone but with active and soft-tissue mechanisms, all of which act through (6.1):

  • Concavity compression. The rotator cuff muscles pull the humeral head into the glenoid, raising the compressive force $N$. Since $F_{\text{dislocate}}=N\tan\beta$, a larger $N$ raises the dislocating threshold even when $\beta$ is small — stability on demand, at the cost of constant muscular effort.
  • The labrum. A fibrocartilage rim around the glenoid deepens the socket, increasing the effective $\beta$ (and the contact area of §5). A torn labrum measurably lowers $S$.
  • Capsule and ligaments. At the end of range these passive tissues snap taut and provide a final, steep restoring force — nonlinear springs we model in Module 6.

The cost of relying on active stabilization is fragility when it fails: rotator-cuff fatigue, a labral tear, or a sudden load that outpaces the muscles' reaction time lets the resultant tilt past the rim. The animation shows that moment — as a growing sideways force tilts the joint resultant, the head stays seated while the resultant is within the rim cone and pops out the instant it crosses $\beta$.

6.4 Closing the ledger

This resolves the §0 puzzle quantitatively. Mobility is counted in degrees of freedom (§1); stability is measured in the force a joint resists before dislocating, $F_{\text{dislocate}}=N\tan\beta$. They are independent axes, and a joint's anatomy places it deliberately: the weight-bearing hip maximizes $\beta$ for security, the exploratory shoulder minimizes it for reach and pays the difference with muscle. With the joint's freedoms, forces, pressures, and stability now all in hand, §7 assembles them into a working simulation that computes a limb's own constraint forces from scratch.

7. Computational lab — the constrained two-link limb

Everything so far has been built by hand, one posture at a time. The elbow in §4 took a paragraph of algebra for a single pose. Real limbs move, and the joint reaction changes every instant of that motion — the dynamic terms of §3 that a static snapshot cannot see. This section turns the machinery loose: we assemble $M$, $J_c$, and $g(q)$ for a whole limb and let the computer solve the KKT system (3.4) at every step of a real swing, reading off the joint forces as they evolve.

The model is the direct two-link sequel to the pendulum of §3.4. There we kept redundant Cartesian coordinates on purpose, so the multiplier would be the rod tension; §3.5 warned that collapsing to joint angles would hide exactly that force. We heed the warning here: instead of the two joint angles, we track the Cartesian positions of the limb's two masses, impose each bone as a length constraint, and recover one multiplier per bone — the joint reactions themselves.

7.1 The model in redundant Cartesian coordinates

Picture a planar limb pinned at its proximal joint (call it the shoulder) at the origin, with two rigid bones of length $L_1$ (upper arm) and $L_2$ (forearm). We lump the segment masses into two point masses: $m_1$ at the elbow and $m_2$ at the hand. The distal tip (the hand) is confined to a frictionless horizontal rail a height $y_h$ below the pin — a hand sliding in a smooth groove, or a foot resting on frictionless ice. The limb is otherwise passive (no muscle torque) and falls under gravity $g$.

The coordinates and constraints, written out. We stack the two mass positions into one vector $q=(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2)$ — four numbers ($n=4$). Each rigid bone fixes a distance, and the rail fixes the hand's height, giving three holonomic constraints ($m=3$):

$$g_1=\tfrac12\!\left(x_1^2+y_1^2-L_1^2\right),\quad g_2=\tfrac12\!\left((x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2-L_2^2\right),\quad g_3=y_2-y_h,\tag{7.1}$$

each required to vanish, $g(q)=0$. The degrees-of-freedom count of §1 gives $n-m=4-3=1$: the limb keeps a single freedom — it can only swing, with the hand sliding along the rail. (One freedom means the motion is integrable, not chaotic, so the force curves below are clean and repeatable.) Here $g_1,g_2,g_3$ are the constraint functions of §3; the scalar $g$ without a subscript remains the gravitational acceleration.

7.2 Assembling the pieces

Cartesian coordinates buy a striking simplification: because kinetic energy is just $\tfrac12 m_1\lVert\dot p_1\rVert^2+\tfrac12 m_2\lVert\dot p_2\rVert^2$ with $p_1,p_2$ the mass positions, the mass matrix is constant and diagonal —

$$\boxed{\,M=\operatorname{diag}(m_1,\,m_1,\,m_2,\,m_2),\qquad c(q,\dot q)=0.\,}\tag{7.2}$$

There are no Coriolis or centrifugal terms at all. This is the flip side of the trade-off in §3.5: joint-angle coordinates give a small $2\times2$ mass matrix but a messy velocity-dependent $c$ and a hidden reaction force; Cartesian coordinates give a trivial constant $M$ and $c=0$, and push all the configuration-dependence into the constraint Jacobian — where the forces we want are waiting. Gravity enters as the applied force $Q$ (the only entries are the downward weights):

$$Q=(0,\,-m_1g,\,0,\,-m_2g).\tag{7.3}$$

Differentiating (7.1), the constraint Jacobian $J_c=\partial g/\partial q$ is the $3\times4$ matrix

$$J_c=\begin{bmatrix} x_1 & y_1 & 0 & 0\\[2pt] -(x_2-x_1) & -(y_2-y_1) & x_2-x_1 & y_2-y_1\\[2pt] 0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix},\tag{7.4}$$

and $\dot J_c$ is the same matrix with every coordinate replaced by its velocity (and the constant bottom row going to zero). Row 1 points along bone 1, row 2 along bone 2, row 3 straight up — each constraint resists motion in exactly its own direction, which is why each multiplier comes out as a force along that line.

Reading the multipliers as joint forces. The constraint force on a mass is $J_c^{\mathsf T}\lambda$ (§3.2). For bone 1, the gradient $\partial g_1/\partial p_1=(x_1,y_1)$ has length $\lVert(x_1,y_1)\rVert=L_1$, so the force it carries has magnitude $$R_s=\lvert\lambda_1\rvert\,L_1\quad(\text{shoulder reaction}),\qquad R_e=\lvert\lambda_2\rvert\,L_2\quad(\text{elbow reaction}),\tag{7.5}$$ each directed along its bone, while $\lambda_3$ is the vertical surface contact force. Sanity check: for a single mass $m$ hanging at rest on a rod of length $L$, equilibrium forces $\lvert\lambda\rvert L=mg$ — the pivot carries exactly the weight, as it must. The same formula, run dynamically over a whole limb, is the joint reaction force of §4.

7.3 Solving for the forces

At each instant we know $q$ and $\dot q$, so we know $M$, $Q$, $J_c$, and $\dot J_c$. The KKT system (3.4) is then a $7\times7$ linear system in the seven unknowns $(\ddot q,\lambda)$ — four accelerations and three multipliers:

$$\begin{bmatrix} M & J_c^{\mathsf T}\\ J_c & 0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\ddot q\\ -\lambda\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}Q\\ -\dot J_c\,\dot q\end{bmatrix}.\tag{7.6}$$

One solve returns the accelerations (which we integrate forward in time) and the three forces, all at once. In exact arithmetic the constraints stay satisfied; in floating point they slowly drift, so we add a small Baumgarte correction — replacing the right-hand side $-\dot J_c\dot q$ with $-\dot J_c\dot q-2\alpha\,J_c\dot q-\beta^2 g$ — which gently pulls any drift back to zero (we use $\alpha=\beta=40$). With it, the constraints hold to under a micrometre over the whole swing.

7.4 The code

The entire lab is about forty lines of NumPy. It builds $M$, $J_c$, $\dot J_c$ exactly as above, assembles (7.6) with np.block, solves it, and steps forward with semi-implicit Euler. It is fully runnable as written.

import numpy as np

# --- parameters (SI units) ---
L1, L2 = 0.30, 0.30           # bone lengths (m)
m1, m2 = 2.0, 1.5             # point masses at elbow, hand (kg)
g,  yh = 9.81, -0.48          # gravity (m/s^2), rail height below shoulder (m)
a,  b  = 40.0, 40.0           # Baumgarte constraint-stabilisation gains

M = np.diag([m1, m1, m2, m2])         # mass matrix: CONSTANT in Cartesian coords
Q = np.array([0, -m1*g, 0, -m2*g])    # gravity = generalised applied force

def constraints(q):                    # g(q): two bone lengths + hand-on-rail
    x1, y1, x2, y2 = q
    return np.array([0.5*(x1*x1 + y1*y1 - L1*L1),
                     0.5*((x2-x1)**2 + (y2-y1)**2 - L2*L2),
                     y2 - yh])

def Jc(q):                             # constraint Jacobian dg/dq  (3 x 4)
    x1, y1, x2, y2 = q
    return np.array([[ x1, y1, 0., 0.],
                     [-(x2-x1), -(y2-y1), x2-x1, y2-y1],
                     [ 0., 0., 0., 1.]])

def dJc(q, dq):                        # time derivative of Jc
    dx1, dy1, dx2, dy2 = dq
    return np.array([[ dx1, dy1, 0., 0.],
                     [-(dx2-dx1), -(dy2-dy1), dx2-dx1, dy2-dy1],
                     [ 0., 0., 0., 0.]])

q  = np.array([0.193, -0.230, 0.358, -0.48])   # released from rest, hand on rail
dq = np.zeros(4);  dt = 5e-5
for step in range(40000):
    J, dJ, gv = Jc(q), dJc(q, dq), constraints(q)
    A   = np.block([[M, J.T], [J, np.zeros((3, 3))]])         # 7x7 KKT matrix
    rhs = np.concatenate([Q, -(dJ@dq) - 2*a*(J@dq) - b*b*gv]) # eq. (7.6) + Baumgarte
    sol = np.linalg.solve(A, rhs)
    ddq, lam = sol[:4], -sol[4:]
    Rs, Re, Nc = abs(lam[0])*L1, abs(lam[1])*L2, lam[2]       # the three forces (N)
    dq = dq + dt*ddq;  q = q + dt*dq                          # semi-implicit Euler

7.5 Results — the multiplier in motion

Released from rest with the hand out at $x\approx0.36\ \mathrm m$, the limb swings inward, the hand sliding along the rail, and swings back: a single clean oscillation. The limb's own weight is $W_L=(m_1+m_2)g=34.3\ \mathrm N$. The plot tracks all three forces, in units of $W_L$, against the hand's position over half a swing.

Two facts jump out, and both echo earlier sections:

  • Dynamics dominate. At the two turning points the limb is momentarily at rest, and the shoulder reaction is a modest $0.34\,W_L$ — the static value. In between, the inertial terms of the moving limb nearly triple it to $0.95\,W_L$. This is exactly the effect §4 flagged when it noted that gait pushes the peak hip reaction to $3$–$5\,W$: a static snapshot systematically under-reads what a moving joint carries.
  • Proximal joints carry more. The shoulder reaction $R_s$ runs roughly three times the elbow reaction $R_e$ throughout — the proximal joint supports both segments plus the dynamics of the whole limb, the distal joint only its own segment. The same ranking explains why hips and knees wear out before finger joints.

The animation makes the multiplier visible the way §3.4 did for the pendulum: the red arrow at the shoulder is $R_s$, the blue arrow at the elbow is $R_e$, each drawn to scale. Watch both swell as the limb sweeps through the middle of its motion and shrink to almost nothing at the extremes.

7.6 Sensitivity — what moves the forces

A model earns its keep by answering "what if?" questions. Three knobs, three lessons:

KnobWhat we changeEffect on the forces
Overall scalemultiply all masses by $k$every force scales by exactly $k$; the motion is identical. Peak $R_s$: $16.3\to32.6\to65.2\ \mathrm N$ for $k=\tfrac12,1,2$.
Held loadadd a weight $m_L$ in the handthe vertical load routes straight into the rail: contact rises $22\to120\ \mathrm N$ as $m_L$ goes $0\to10\ \mathrm{kg}$, while $R_s$ does not move.
Geometrylengthen the forearm $L_2$weak: peak $R_s$ shifts only $31.7\to33.3\ \mathrm N$ as $L_2$ goes $0.2\to0.4\ \mathrm m$. Here the reactions are set by weight and dynamics, not segment length.

The first row is Galileo's observation in disguise: under gravity alone the acceleration is mass-independent, so doubling every mass doubles every force while leaving the trajectory untouched — the joint reaction is linear in the load it carries. The second row is a genuine lesson about load paths: a constraint only carries force along its own direction. The rail resists vertical motion, so a vertical hand-load is swallowed by the surface and never reaches the joints — push the hand sideways instead, against a direction the bones must resist, and the same load would drive the joint reactions up. Which structure carries a load depends entirely on the geometry of the constraints, not just the size of the load.

7.7 Extension challenge

Make the limb active. The lab above is passive — no muscle. The joint reactions stayed near the limb's own weight precisely because nothing was pulling hard on a short lever, the mechanism §4 identified for reactions far above body weight. Add a muscle torque $\tau$ at the elbow by appending the generalized force it produces to $Q$ (a torque $\tau$ between the bones contributes $\pm\tau$ to the angular balance — equivalently, equal-and-opposite force couples at the elbow and hand perpendicular to bone 2). Re-run and confirm that holding the limb against gravity drives $R_s$ well past $W_L$ — reproducing §4's amplification from the general machinery.

Further: (1) replace the frictionless rail with a one-sided floor that can only push ($\lambda_3\ge0$) and detect lift-off (when the solver returns $\lambda_3\lt0$, the foot leaves the ground). (2) Add a third link and a second loop constraint (a closed kinematic chain, as in §1) and watch the KKT system grow — the method is unchanged; only the matrix sizes change. (3) Feed the computed $R_s$ into the contact-pressure formula of §5 to estimate the peak cartilage pressure over the swing.

What this lab establishes. The abstract apparatus of §3 is a working algorithm: stack the coordinates, write the constraints, build $M$ and $J_c$, and solve one linear system (3.4) per time step to read off every joint reaction in a moving limb at once. Joint-angle coordinates would have hidden these forces; redundant Cartesian coordinates expose them as the multipliers. The same forty lines scale from this two-link toy to a full musculoskeletal model — what changes between here and a research-grade simulation is the realism of $M$, $g(q)$, and the constraints, which is the subject of §8.

8. What the model captures — and what it leaves out

The lab of §7 closed by naming the three things a research-grade model would make more realistic: the inertia $M$, the bias term $g(q)$ (gravity and, eventually, muscle), and the constraints themselves. This section turns that remark into a systematic audit. Every model trades fidelity for insight; the discipline is to know exactly which corners were cut, and where each one is later squared off.

8.1 What it gets right

The rigid-constraint picture is not a toy — it is the correct level of description for the skeleton as a mechanism, and it earns several quantitative wins:

  • The freedom count is exact. Degrees of freedom $=$ coordinates $-$ independent constraints (§1) is not an approximation; it is a theorem of configuration space. The mobility ledger of §2 classifies every joint correctly.
  • Constraint forces are real and directed. The multiplier $\lambda$ is not bookkeeping — §3.4 showed it is the measured rod tension, and §7 recovered each joint reaction as a force along its bone. The model predicts both the size and the line of action.
  • Muscle dominates the load — and the magnitudes check out. The lever amplification of §4 predicts a hip reaction of a few times body weight in single-leg stance. This is directly testable: instrumented hip implants that radio out their own load (in-vivo telemetry — measurement from inside the living body) report peak contact forces of roughly $2$–$3\,W$ in walking, squarely in the predicted band.
  • Dynamics raise the load predictably. The inertial terms of §3 made the shoulder reaction in §7 nearly triple over a swing; the same terms explain why measured joint forces climb with gait speed.
Reality check. A static hand calculation (§4) gave a hip reaction $\approx2.5\,W$; in-vivo telemetry gives $\approx2.3$–$2.9\,W$ in level walking. A model built from nothing but rigid bones, ideal constraints, and lever arms lands within tens of percent of the living joint — strong evidence the load path is captured even when the materials are idealized.

8.2 What it leaves out

The idealizations fall into three families. None is fatal; each is the doorway to a later module.

(a) The bodies are not rigid. We treated bones as rigid and joints as exact distance constraints. Real bones flex and store elastic strain energy — the beam mechanics of Module 2. Tendons and ligaments are not rigid rods at all but compliant springs that stretch several percent under load; modeling them as hard constraints discards the energy they store and return (the basis of efficient running), taken up in Module 6.

(b) The constraints are not ideal. Three separate idealizations hide here, and the contrast below makes them concrete:

  • Bilateral → unilateral. A distance constraint resists motion in both directions; a real joint surface can only push, never pull (cartilage carries compression, not tension), and a foot can leave the floor. This is the $\lambda_3\ge0$ condition flagged in the §7.7 challenge — a one-sided constraint, the language of contact mechanics (Module 4) and of stance-vs-swing in gait (Module 8).
  • Frictionless → lubricated. We assumed no tangential force at the constraint. Synovial joints have a remarkably low but nonzero friction coefficient ($\mu\approx0.005$), the product of a rich fluid-film and biphasic lubrication mechanism — the subject of Module 4.
  • Fixed axis → migrating, compliant axis. The ideal "hinge" of §2 has a single fixed rotation axis; the knee's axis moves as it rolls and glides (a non-holonomic, rolling character — a constraint on velocities, not just positions), and ligament laxity lets the bones shift slightly off the nominal path.

(c) The interface is not a point, and the inertia is not lumped. Two final simplifications:

  • Contact. §5 summarized the interface with a mean pressure and an elastic (Hertz) peak. Cartilage is biphasic (a solid collagen matrix soaked in fluid) and viscoelastic (its response depends on rate and load history — it creeps), and the contact area itself changes with joint angle. The time-dependent story is Module 4.
  • Inertia and actuation. §7 lumped each segment's mass into a point at a joint, giving a constant $M$; real segments have distributed mass, a center of mass between the joints, and rotational inertia (anthropometric data feed the inverse dynamics of Module 8). And our limb had no muscle model: real muscle has force–length–velocity properties, activation dynamics, and pennation (fibers angled to the tendon), and the body has more muscles than degrees of freedom — a kinematic redundancy resolved only by optimization (Modules 5 and 7). Everything here was also strictly planar; three-dimensional joints need the spatial Jacobians of Module 11.

8.3 Where each idealization is repaid

Throughout Module 3 we have taken out a series of small loans against realism. To lay the mechanism bare we borrowed a simplification we knew was not literally true — a rigid bone, a frictionless surface, a point mass — on the understanding that the missing physics would be restored later. An idealization is repaid when a subsequent module hands that physics back: it reintroduces the real behaviour and, crucially, builds the refinement on top of the answer computed here, taking the joint reaction $\lambda$ as its starting input rather than recomputing it. Solving the skeleton's mechanics is never thrown away — it becomes the boundary condition for the tissues. The nine repayments at a glance, then one by one:

Rigid bones — borrowed across §3–§7; repaid in Module 2 (already built). Repaying it means letting the bar bend: Module 2 feeds this section's joint reaction $\lambda$ straight into the beam law $\sigma=My/I$, turning the rigid rod back into a bone that flexes, stores strain energy, and can fracture.

Ligaments and tendons as rigid constraints — borrowed in §3 and §6; repaid in Module 6. A hard constraint stores no energy; repaying it replaces each with a nonlinear spring, so the elastic energy a stretched tendon stores and returns — the mechanism behind efficient running — reappears in the model.

Bilateral (two-sided) constraints — borrowed in §3 and §7; repaid in Module 4 and Module 8. Repaying it turns the equality $g(q)=0$ into the inequality $\lambda \ge 0$ — a contact can push but not pull — so surfaces are allowed to separate: a foot lifts off the ground in the swing phase, a joint momentarily unloads.

Frictionless surfaces — borrowed in §3 and §7; repaid in Module 4. Repaying it adds a tangential-force law and the biphasic lubrication model, restoring the small but real joint friction ($\mu\approx0.005$) we had set to zero.

Ideal joint pairs with a fixed axis — borrowed in §2 and §6; repaid in Module 6. Repaying it swaps the single fixed hinge for rolling-and-gliding kinematics, letting the instantaneous axis migrate (the knee's "screw-home") and allowing a little ligament laxity off the nominal path.

Elastic, point/Hertz contact — borrowed in §5; repaid in Module 4. Repaying it spreads $\lambda$ over a biphasic, viscoelastic contact whose pressure field evolves in time (cartilage creeps) and whose area changes with joint angle, in place of a single mean or peak pressure.

Inertia lumped into point masses — borrowed in §7; repaid in Module 8. Repaying it replaces the point masses with measured segment mass, a centre of mass between the joints, and rotational inertia — the anthropometric data that fill in the true $M(q)$ for inverse dynamics.

Ideal or absent muscle — borrowed in §4 and §7; repaid in Modules 5 and 7. Repaying it supplies the applied force $Q$ the rigid model left blank: a Hill-type muscle with force–length–velocity behaviour, activation dynamics, and pennation, plus an optimization to choose among more muscles than there are degrees of freedom.

Planar, two-dimensional motion — borrowed in every section; repaid in Module 11. Repaying it generalizes the constraint Jacobian $J_c$ to three dimensions (spatial Jacobians), so genuinely 3-D joints — the ball-and-socket hip and shoulder, the out-of-plane knee — can be handled.

The altitude is right; the materials come next. Notice the pattern in the list above: nearly every refinement is a property of the interface materials — cartilage, ligament, muscle — not of the rigid-body bookkeeping. That is the deliberate division of labor of this course. Module 3 fixes the mechanism: how many freedoms a joint has, what force the constraint must carry, and how that force scales with load and motion. The modules that follow soften the idealized interfaces one at a time — but each of them takes the joint reaction computed here as its input. The multiplier $\lambda$ is where the skeleton ends and the tissues begin.

9. Diagnostics and problems

A module is learned when you can use it without rereading it. The five diagnostics below are quick conceptual checks — try each before revealing its answer. The three problems that follow are graded in difficulty and in kind: one to reason with (conceptual), one to derive (derivational), and one to compute (computational). Together they exercise the whole spine, from the degree-of-freedom count of §1 to the constrained dynamics of §7.

9.1 Diagnostic questions

  1. Counting freedoms. A planar limb of three rigid links is pinned at the shoulder, with the hand free. How many degrees of freedom does it have? If the hand is then pinned to a fixed point in the plane, how many remain?
    Answer
    Three links pinned in series have one angle each: $3$ DOF (a triple pendulum). Pinning the hand to a fixed point fixes its two Cartesian coordinates — two holonomic constraints — leaving $3-2=1$ DOF. The arm can still flex its elbow while the hand stays put: the kinematic redundancy of §2. (See §1.)
  2. Why so large? A patient holds a $2\ \mathrm{kg}$ book; the reaction force at the elbow joint is many times $2\ \mathrm{kg}$-force. Why?
    Answer
    Muscles attach close to the joint, on a short moment arm. To balance the book's torque about the elbow, the biceps must pull with a force much larger than the book's weight (the mechanical disadvantage of Module 1), and that large muscle force presses the joint surfaces together. The joint reaction is set by the muscle, not the load (§4).
  3. Where does the load go? In the lab of §7, a weight held in the hand raised the surface contact force but left both joint reactions unchanged. State the principle behind this.
    Answer
    A constraint can supply force only along its own gradient direction (its normal). The hand rail resists vertical motion, so its reaction points vertically; a vertically-held load is exactly aligned with it and is therefore carried entirely by the contact $\lambda_3$, never reaching the bones. Load paths are set by the geometry of the constraints, not the size of the load.
  4. The vanishing tension. Writing the pendulum of §3.4 in the single angle $\theta$ gives its motion directly — but the rod tension disappears from the equations. Why?
    Answer
    The angle $\theta$ already moves along the constraint surface, so there is no constraint left to enforce and no multiplier to carry its force: reduced coordinates project the tension out. Keeping redundant Cartesian coordinates plus the explicit constraint is what makes the multiplier — and hence the joint reaction — visible. This is exactly why §7 used Cartesian coordinates (§3.5).
  5. Same freedoms, different fates. The hip and the shoulder both have three rotational degrees of freedom. Why does the shoulder dislocate far more often?
    Answer
    Degrees of freedom measure mobility, not stability. The hip's deep socket gives a large rim angle $\beta$ and so a high stability ratio $S=\tan\beta$ — a large sideways force is needed to climb the rim. The shoulder's shallow socket gives a small $\beta$, a low $S$, and easy dislocation; it buys its range of motion by spending stability (§6).

9.2 Problems

Problem 1 — conceptual (load paths and the constraint normal).

The figure contrasts two ways to brace the limb's hand: on a horizontal rail (as in §7) and against a vertical wall.

(a) Explain, using the idea that a constraint supplies force only along its normal, why the vertical load on the rail leaves the shoulder reaction unchanged. (b) The rail's reaction is purely vertical; what becomes of the component of any applied hand-load that points along the rail? (c) Predict whether a vertical load reaches the shoulder when the hand instead presses on the vertical wall.

Show solution

(a) The rail constraint is $g_3=y_2-y_h$, with gradient $\partial g_3/\partial q=(0,0,0,1)$ — a purely vertical direction. Its multiplier therefore produces a purely vertical force at the hand. A vertically-held weight is exactly parallel to this, so the rail can balance it entirely on its own; nothing is left to route through the bones, and the joint reactions $R_s,R_e$ are unchanged.

(b) The component of a hand-load along the rail (horizontal) is perpendicular to the rail's normal, so the constraint cannot carry it at all. That component is left to the limb's inertia: it accelerates the one remaining degree of freedom. This is precisely why the passive limb in §7 moved at all — gravity's along-rail component drove the swing.

(c) Yes. The wall's normal is horizontal, so its reaction can only oppose horizontal motion. A vertical load is perpendicular to that normal, so the wall cannot carry it; the load must be reacted by the shoulder pin, routed there through the bones — and the joint reactions rise with it. The computational problem below confirms the rail case numerically.

Problem 2 — derivational (the static multiplier, and a sanity check).

Starting from the multiplier formula of §3, $$\lambda = -\bigl(J_c M^{-1} J_c^{\mathsf T}\bigr)^{-1}\bigl(J_c M^{-1}(Q-c)+\dot J_c\,\dot q\bigr),$$ specialise it to an instant of rest ($\dot q=0$) in Cartesian coordinates (where $c=0$ and gravity sits in $Q$). Then apply the result to a single mass $m$ on a rigid rod of length $L$, hanging straight down, and show the pivot reaction equals the weight.

Show solution

With $\dot q=0$, the term $\dot J_c\dot q$ vanishes, and with $c=0$ the formula collapses to

$$\boxed{\;\lambda = -\bigl(J_c M^{-1} J_c^{\mathsf T}\bigr)^{-1} J_c M^{-1} Q.\;}$$

For the pendulum, take $q=(x,y)$ with $y$ up, $M=mI_2$, gravity $Q=(0,-mg)$, and the rod constraint $g=\tfrac12(x^2+y^2-L^2)$, so $J_c=(x,\,y)$. Then $M^{-1}=\tfrac1m I_2$ and

$$J_c M^{-1} J_c^{\mathsf T}=\frac{x^2+y^2}{m}=\frac{L^2}{m},\qquad J_c M^{-1} Q=\frac{1}{m}(x\cdot 0 + y\cdot(-mg))=-yg.$$

Hanging straight down means $x=0,\ y=-L$, so $J_c M^{-1}Q=Lg$ and

$$\lambda=-\frac{m}{L^2}\,(Lg)=-\frac{mg}{L}\quad\Rightarrow\quad |\lambda|\,L = mg.$$

The joint-reaction rule $R=|\lambda|L$ of §7.2 then gives a pivot reaction of exactly $mg$ — the pivot carries the full weight, as it must. (Numerically, $m=1\ \mathrm{kg},\,L=0.3\ \mathrm m$ gives $|\lambda|L=9.81\ \mathrm N=mg$ to machine precision.)

Problem 3 — computational (verify the load path).

Take the runnable code of §7.4. Add a held mass $m_L$ in the hand by changing only the applied-force line, so the hand's downward weight becomes $(m_2+m_L)g$:

mL = 5.0                                   # held hand mass (kg)
Q  = np.array([0, -m1*g, 0, -(m2+mL)*g])   # the only change from §7.4

Run it for $m_L=0,\ 5,\ 10\ \mathrm{kg}$. Predict, then check: how does the peak contact force change, and how does the peak shoulder reaction change?

0. Motivation — the impossible bearing

In Module 3 we tracked a load all the way from the floor to the inside of the hip and arrived at a number: in single-leg stance the femoral head presses into its socket with a reaction force $R\approx2.5\,W\approx1715\ \mathrm N$, spread over a contact patch of roughly $A_c\approx10\ \mathrm{cm^2}$, for a mean pressure $p=R/A_c\approx1.7\ \mathrm{MPa}$. We then stopped, because Module 3 treated the contacting surfaces as dry, rigid, and frictionless — placeholders we promised to repay here. This module is the repayment. It is about the thin layer of tissue that actually sits in that contact: articular cartilage (the glistening white coating on the ends of bones in a movable joint), bathed in synovial fluid (the slippery joint lubricant, roughly the consistency of egg white).

Hold the numbers up to the light and they should look impossible. The cartilage layer is only about 2–4 mm thick. It carries $\approx1.7\ \mathrm{MPa}$ — and far more in a stumble or a jump, where hip forces reach $8$–$10\,W$ — not once, but on the order of a million loading cycles a year, and it does so for decades in most people without being replaced (mature cartilage has no blood supply and barely any cells). And it slides against its partner surface at a friction coefficient of about $\mu\approx0.005$ — lower than the $\mu\approx0.03$ of an ice skate on ice, lower than any bearing a mechanical engineer can buy. A soft, thin, living gasket that out-performs steel-on-PTFE and never gets a service interval: that is the object of this module.

The resolution, which this module develops quantitatively, is a single change of viewpoint. The dry-solid mechanics of Modules 1–3 ask "how does a stiff body share a load through its cross-section?" — the right question for a bone (Module 2), the wrong question for cartilage. Cartilage is not a solid at all. It is a sponge. More precisely, it is a biphasic material: a porous, electrically charged solid scaffold (collagen and proteoglycan) swollen with water that can flow through it. When you step, the load is not borne by the soft scaffold squashing like rubber — it is borne, almost entirely and almost instantly, by the pressure of the trapped water, which cannot escape the fine pores fast enough to matter on the timescale of a footstep. The scaffold rides along, barely stressed. Over minutes of standing the water slowly weeps out and the scaffold gradually takes up the load; lie down and it re-imbibes overnight (you are measurably taller in the morning). This is interstitial fluid pressurization, and it is the headline idea of the module.

That one mechanism organizes everything that follows. It explains the stiffness (water is nearly incompressible, so a sealed sponge is stiff even though its scaffold is soft — §3–§4); it explains the low friction (if the fluid carries the load, the solid-on-solid contact that generates friction carries almost nothing — §7); and it explains failure (anything that lets the water leak out faster — torn scaffold, lost charge, higher permeability — dumps the load onto the soft solid, which then wears, a runaway we trace in §8 as the mechanics of osteoarthritis). To get there we need three layers of physics, each built on the last:

  • What the tissue is made of — collagen, proteoglycans, water, and the fixed electric charge that makes cartilage swell (§1–§2).
  • How a charged, fluid-filled sponge responds to load — the biphasic / poroelastic model, a diffusion equation for the squeezing-out of water, and the fluid–solid load sharing it predicts (§3–§5).
  • What that means at the joint — turning Module 3's single reaction force into a real contact-pressure field (§6), explaining the friction (§7), and diagnosing the failure (§8).

By the end you will be able to take the lone number $R\approx1715\ \mathrm N$ that Module 3 handed us and predict, from tissue properties, how the pressure is distributed across the contact, how it is split between fluid and solid, how it relaxes over time, why the joint is so slippery, and what tips that whole balance into degeneration. We begin with the material itself.

1. Cartilage as a hydrated, charged, porous composite

Section 0 looked at the joint from the outside. Now zoom in by about a thousandfold, into the 2–4 mm layer itself. What we find is not a uniform rubbery pad but a composite — a material built from distinct constituents with sharply divided jobs. Three of them matter mechanically, and a sparse population of cells maintains the rest:

  • Collagen (mostly type II): a dense, fine network of protein fibres, roughly 50–75 % of the tissue's dry weight (about 15–22 % of the wet weight). Collagen is strong in tension but buckles under compression — it behaves like the steel rebar in concrete, or the casing of a tyre: a tension member, not a strut.
  • Proteoglycans, dominated by the molecule aggrecan: a central protein core densely studded with sugar chains (glycosaminoglycans, or GAGs — chondroitin and keratan sulfate), giving a bottle-brush shape; many aggrecan brushes attach to a long backbone (hyaluronan) to form aggregates too large to escape the collagen mesh. Crucially, every GAG chain carries fixed negative charges (sulfate and carboxyl groups bound to the solid, hence "fixed"). This bound charge — the fixed charge density (FCD), denoted $c_F$ — is the source of cartilage's swelling, developed in §2. Proteoglycans are roughly 15–30 % of dry weight.
  • Interstitial water: 65–80 % of the tissue by wet weight, carrying dissolved mobile ions (mainly Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$). This is the fluid phase. It can flow through the pores of the solid network, but only slowly — the network's resistance to that flow (its low permeability, §3) is what makes the whole mechanism work.
  • Chondrocytes: the cartilage cells (the suffix -cyte just means "cell"), occupying under 10 % of the volume. They synthesise and maintain the matrix but carry essentially no load. Mature cartilage is avascular (no blood vessels) and aneural (no nerves), which is why it neither bleeds nor hurts when first damaged — and why, with so few cells and no blood supply, it heals so poorly.

The division of labour

The mechanics of the whole module fall out of one observation: each constituent does a different mechanical job, and the jobs are complementary.

Put the three mechanical constituents together and a picture emerges that is closer to an inflated structure than to a block of rubber. The proteoglycans' fixed negative charges pull water in osmotically (§2); the incoming water inflates the tissue and pulls the collagen network taut, the way internal pressure tensions the casing of a tyre. So even at rest, with no external load, cartilage is pre-stressed: the solid network is already in tension, balancing an internal swelling pressure. This is the resting state that the dry, unstressed solids of Modules 1–3 could not represent — and it is why cartilage is turgid and springs back rather than staying dented.

The idealisation the rest of the module uses

Tracking collagen fibres, sugar chains, and individual ions one by one is hopeless for predicting joint stresses. Instead we make the abstraction that the next six sections rest on: lump the collagen, proteoglycans, and cells into a single solid matrix — porous, elastic, charged, and permeable — and treat the water and its dissolved ions as a single fluid phase that saturates it. Two interpenetrating continua, coexisting at every point, exchanging force through the drag of fluid flowing past solid. This is the biphasic (or poroelastic) idealisation, and it is the spine of the module.

Biphasic idealisation (used from §3 on). At every point cartilage is modelled as two superposed continua:
  • a solid matrix (collagen + proteoglycan + cells), with solid volume fraction $\varphi^s$, taken as intrinsically incompressible but able to deform as fluid moves through it;
  • an interstitial fluid (water + mobile ions), with fluid volume fraction $\varphi^w$, also intrinsically incompressible.
The tissue is saturated — pores hold only fluid, no gas — so the volume fractions fill all space: $$\varphi^s+\varphi^w=1,\qquad \text{with typically }\varphi^w\approx0.75\text{–}0.80.$$ "Incompressible constituents" does not mean the tissue cannot change volume: it shrinks precisely by the volume of fluid that flows out. That single bookkeeping fact becomes the governing equation in §3.

A caution before we build on it. This lumped, single-property matrix is homogeneous and isotropic, whereas the figure above shows cartilage is neither: the collagen weave, water content, and fixed charge all vary with depth (the superficial, middle, and deep zones) and have a built-in fibre direction. The toy model we develop in §3–§5 deliberately ignores that, because the depth-averaged version already captures the headline physics — fluid pressurisation and load sharing — with one tractable equation. The depth-dependence and anisotropy are real and matter for where cartilage actually fails; we reintroduce them qualitatively in §6 and §8, and defer the full continuum treatment to Module 16. With the cast of characters and the idealisation fixed, the first thing to compute is the one effect the dry models entirely missed: the swelling pressure that the fixed charge $c_F$ creates. That is §2.

2. Osmotic (Donnan) swelling pressure

Section 1 ended on a claim: the proteoglycans' fixed negative charge $c_F$ draws water in osmotically and leaves the tissue pre-stressed even at rest. This section turns that sentence into a number. The mechanism is Donnan equilibrium — the ion partitioning forced by a charge that cannot move — and it delivers a resting swelling pressure of order $0.1$–$0.2\ \mathrm{MPa}$, a load-bearing effect the dry solids of Modules 1–3 had no way to represent.

The setup: charged tissue in a salt bath

Idealise a block of cartilage sitting in synovial fluid, which is essentially a dilute salt solution. Model that bath as a $1\!:\!1$ salt (NaCl) at concentration $c_0$ — the bath concentration; physiologically $c_0\approx0.15\ \mathrm{M}$, i.e. $150\ \mathrm{mol/m^3}$. Inside the tissue sit the fixed charges: a concentration $c_F$ of bound negative groups (the proteoglycan sulfates and carboxyls of §1), counted as moles of charge per unit volume of interstitial water. The decisive asymmetry: water and the small mobile ions ($\mathrm{Na^+}$, $\mathrm{Cl^-}$) cross freely between tissue and bath, but the fixed charges are chemically bound to the solid and cannot leave. The solid matrix therefore acts as a semipermeable boundary — open to solvent and small solutes, closed to the fixed charge.

Write $c_+$ and $c_-$ for the concentrations of mobile cations ($\mathrm{Na^+}$) and anions ($\mathrm{Cl^-}$) inside the tissue; outside, the plain salt bath is neutral so both equal $c_0$. Two conditions pin down $c_+$ and $c_-$.

Condition 1 — electroneutrality. Each region must be electrically neutral. Inside, the positive mobile charge must balance the negative mobile charge plus the fixed negative charge:

$$c_+ = c_- + c_F. \tag{1}$$

So the tissue must hold more cations than anions — precisely the surplus $\mathrm{Na^+}$ drawn in to neutralise the trapped negative groups.

Condition 2 — Donnan equilibrium. At equilibrium each mobile ion has the same electrochemical potential inside and outside. As derived just below, the electric potential cancels out of the product of the two mobile-ion concentrations, leaving the Donnan condition

$$c_+\,c_- = c_0^{\,2}. \tag{2}$$
Give each mobile ion $i$ of valence $z_i$ the electrochemical potential $\mu_i = \mu_i^\circ + R_gT\ln c_i + z_i F\psi$, where $R_g$ is the universal gas constant (written $R_g$ to keep it distinct from the joint reaction $R$ of §0), $T$ the absolute temperature, $F$ the Faraday constant, and $\psi$ the local electric potential. Equilibrium sets $\mu_i$ equal inside and out; with the Donnan potential difference $\Delta\psi=\psi^{\mathrm{in}}-\psi^{\mathrm{out}}$, $$\frac{c_i^{\mathrm{in}}}{c_i^{\mathrm{out}}}=\exp\!\Big(\!-\frac{z_i F\,\Delta\psi}{R_gT}\Big).$$ For $\mathrm{Na^+}$ ($z=+1$): $c_+ = c_0\,e^{-F\Delta\psi/R_gT}$; for $\mathrm{Cl^-}$ ($z=-1$): $c_- = c_0\,e^{+F\Delta\psi/R_gT}$. Multiplying, the exponentials cancel, giving $c_+c_- = c_0^{2}$ — the unknown Donnan potential never has to be found. ∎

Solve. Put $c_-=c_+-c_F$ from (1) into (2): $c_+(c_+-c_F)=c_0^2$, a quadratic $c_+^2-c_Fc_+-c_0^2=0$ whose positive root is

$$c_+=\tfrac12\Big(c_F+\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}\Big),\qquad c_-=\tfrac12\Big(\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}-c_F\Big). \tag{3}$$

The tissue ends up ion-rich: the total mobile-ion concentration inside is $c_++c_-=\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}$, against $2c_0$ outside. Since $\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}\gt 2c_0$ whenever $c_F\gt 0$, there are always more dissolved particles inside than out.

From ion imbalance to swelling pressure

That excess of dissolved particles is what pulls water in. By van't Hoff's law for a dilute ideal solution, osmotic pressure equals $R_gT$ times solute-particle concentration; the net osmotic pressure across the boundary — the swelling pressure $\pi$ — is $R_gT$ times the excess particle concentration inside over outside:

$$\pi = R_gT\big[(c_++c_-)-2c_0\big].$$

Only the mobile ions enter: the fixed charges belong to the solid and do not float in the water. Substituting $c_++c_-=\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}$ yields the result this section was after.

Donnan swelling pressure. A charged, fully hydrated tissue of fixed charge density $c_F$, in equilibrium with a $1\!:\!1$ salt bath of concentration $c_0$, carries a resting osmotic swelling pressure $$\boxed{\;\pi = R_gT\Big(\sqrt{c_F^{2}+4c_0^{2}}\;-\;2c_0\Big)\;}$$ with $R_g=8.314\ \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}K^{-1}}$ the universal gas constant and $T$ the absolute temperature. It exists with no external load — it is the tissue's resting pre-stress.

Put numbers in. Take healthy values $c_F\approx0.2\ \mathrm{M}=200\ \mathrm{mol/m^3}$, bath $c_0\approx0.15\ \mathrm{M}=150\ \mathrm{mol/m^3}$, and body temperature $T=310\ \mathrm K$ so $R_gT=2577\ \mathrm{J/mol}$:

$$\sqrt{200^2+4(150)^2}=\sqrt{1.30\times10^5}=361\ \mathrm{mol/m^3},\qquad 361-300=61\ \mathrm{mol/m^3},$$ $$\pi = 2577\times61 \approx 1.56\times10^5\ \mathrm{Pa} \approx 0.16\ \mathrm{MPa}.$$

About a sixth of the $1.7\ \mathrm{MPa}$ mean contact pressure (Module 3) is already standing in the unloaded tissue. The collagen network carries the matching tension that keeps the swelling from running away (§1's "inflated structure"): cartilage rests turgid and pre-stressed.

Reading the formula

Three limits, each a real effect:

  • No charge ($c_F\to0$): $\pi\to R_gT(2c_0-2c_0)=0$. Strip the proteoglycan charge and the swelling vanishes — the tissue loses its resting pre-stress. This is the direction degeneration pushes (§8).
  • Salt screening (raise $c_0$): for $c_0\gg c_F$, expanding the root gives $\pi\approx R_gT\,c_F^2/(4c_0)\to0$. Add salt and the tissue de-swells and softens — the lower dashed curve above, and a genuine laboratory effect (why cartilage tests must fix the ionic strength of the bath).
  • More proteoglycan ($c_F\gg c_0$): $\pi\approx R_gT\,(c_F-2c_0)$, climbing almost linearly with fixed charge. More charge $\Rightarrow$ more swelling pressure $\Rightarrow$ stiffer resistance to compression. This near-linear dependence is the lever §8's degeneration runs in reverse.

The swelling pressure is the resting state, but cartilage's real job is to carry transient loads many times larger. To see how, we now let the tissue deform and force water to actually flow through it — the biphasic model of §3, where this osmotic $\pi$ reappears as a pre-stress riding underneath the fluid pressure that does the heavy lifting.

3. The biphasic model and the consolidation equation

Section 2 left cartilage turgid but at rest. Its real job is transient: the $\approx1.7\ \mathrm{MPa}$ contact pressure of Module 3 arrives in a footstep and is gone in about a second — roughly a million times a year, for decades. The resting swelling pressure of $\sim0.16\ \mathrm{MPa}$ cannot carry that on its own. Something else does, and this section names it with a single governing equation — the main result of the module.

The mechanism is fluid flow. Squeeze a water-soaked sponge and water flows out; squeeze harder, or make the pores finer, and the flow fights back harder. Cartilage is that sponge with extraordinarily fine pores — so fine that the resistance to flow, not the stiffness of the solid, governs how it carries load over the timescale of a step. To make that precise we need a model with two interacting phases: the solid matrix and the fluid moving through it. That is the biphasic model.

The confined-compression test

To isolate the physics, load the tissue the simplest way that forces fluid through solid: confined compression. A cylindrical plug of cartilage of thickness $h$ sits in a rigid, impermeable, frictionless-walled chamber, pressed from above by a porous platen that lets fluid escape but transmits the load. The rigid base and side walls let nothing through; everything — deformation and flow — happens along one axis $z$, measured from the impermeable base ($z=0$) up to the draining platen ($z=h$). One spatial dimension, two phases.

Three ingredients

The governing equation falls out of combining three statements, each elementary. First, fix the cast of symbols.

The biphasic idealization (symbols). The tissue is a mixture of two intrinsically incompressible phases sharing every point of space: a solid matrix (volume fraction $\varphi^s$) and interstitial fluid (volume fraction $\varphi^w$), saturated so $\varphi^s+\varphi^w=1$ (§1).
  • $v^s=\partial u/\partial t$ — solid velocity; $v^w$ — fluid velocity (both along $z$).
  • $p(z,t)$ — pore pressure, the hydrostatic pressure in the interstitial fluid.
  • $\sigma$ — total stress carried by the mixture; $\sigma_E$ — effective stress, the part carried by the deforming solid matrix.
  • $H_A$ — aggregate modulus, the solid matrix's stiffness in confined compression (its drained, equilibrium stiffness); $k$ — hydraulic permeability, how easily fluid percolates through the matrix.

Ingredient 1 — continuity (incompressible mixture). Both phases are incompressible and fill all space, so the total volume flux cannot accumulate anywhere: $\partial_z(\varphi^s v^s+\varphi^w v^w)=0$, i.e. $\varphi^s v^s+\varphi^w v^w$ is the same at every $z$. At the impermeable base ($z=0$) both the solid and the fluid are held still, so that constant is zero:

$$\varphi^s v^s+\varphi^w v^w=0 \quad\text{everywhere.}$$

Define the relative flux (Darcy flux) $q\equiv\varphi^w(v^w-v^s)$ — the volume of fluid crossing unit area per unit time relative to the moving solid. Using $\varphi^s=1-\varphi^w$, the left side is $v^s+\varphi^w(v^w-v^s)=v^s+q$, so

$$q=-v^s=-\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}. \tag{1}$$

Every bit of solid that moves up must push an equal volume of fluid the other way: the matrix can only compact by squeezing fluid out.

Ingredient 2 — Darcy's law (defines the permeability). Forcing fluid through the fine pores costs a pressure gradient. Darcy's law states the relative flux is proportional to the pressure gradient driving it, with the hydraulic permeability $k\;[\mathrm{m^4/(N\,s)}]$ as the constant:

$$q=-k\,\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}. \tag{2}$$

Fluid flows from high pressure to low (hence the minus sign); a small $k$ — cartilage has $k\sim10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m^4/(N\,s)}$, among the lowest of any tissue — means even a gentle flux demands a steep pressure gradient. Equating (1) and (2) removes the flux and ties the solid's motion to the pressure field:

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}=k\,\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}. \tag{3}$$

Ingredient 3 — effective stress and equilibrium. The load on the mixture is shared between the two phases. The fluid carries the pore pressure $p$; the solid matrix carries the effective stress $\sigma_E$. Their sum is the total stress (with tension positive, the outward pore pressure enters with a minus sign):

$$\sigma=\sigma_E-p,\qquad \sigma_E=H_A\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}, \tag{4}$$

where the second relation is the matrix's linear elastic law in confined compression (effective stress proportional to strain, constant of proportionality $H_A$).

The chamber is loaded slowly compared with any stress-wave transit, so the mixture is in quasi-static equilibrium: with no body force, the total stress has no gradient, $\partial\sigma/\partial z=0$ — $\sigma$ is uniform in $z$ (it equals the applied $\sigma_0$). Differentiating (4) and using that uniformity,

$$0=\frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial z}=H_A\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial z^2}-\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \frac{\partial p}{\partial z}=H_A\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial z^2}. \tag{5}$$

Assemble: the consolidation equation

The three ingredients connect end to end. Equation (3) gives $\partial u/\partial t$ in terms of $\partial p/\partial z$; equation (5) gives $\partial p/\partial z$ in terms of $\partial^2 u/\partial z^2$. Substitute one into the other and the pressure gradient drops out:

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}=k\,\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}=k\,\Big(H_A\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial z^2}\Big).$$
Linear biphasic consolidation equation. In one-dimensional confined compression, the solid displacement $u(z,t)$ of the cartilage obeys $$\boxed{\;\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}=H_A\,k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial z^2}\;}$$ a diffusion equation with consolidation diffusivity $D=H_A k\;[\mathrm{m^2/s}]$. The pore pressure $p$ and the strain $\varepsilon=\partial u/\partial z$ satisfy the same equation. The matrix does not respond to load like a spring; it diffuses toward its loaded shape, at a rate set jointly by how stiff it is ($H_A$) and how freely fluid can leave ($k$).

This single boxed line is what Module 3 deferred. A joint surface is not an elastic solid: it is a porous medium in which load, pressure, and deformation spread by diffusion. Everything in the rest of the module — load sharing, stress relaxation, creep, lubrication, even the failure cascade of osteoarthritis — is this diffusion equation seen from a different angle.

"Diffusion" is meant literally: the equation is identical in form to heat conduction, with displacement playing the role of temperature and $D=H_Ak$ the role of thermal diffusivity. Everything you know about heat spreading carries over.

What the equation predicts

Two predictions matter, and the rest of the module rides on them.

(i) Pressure diffuses out of the tissue. Apply the load $\sigma_0$ suddenly. At the first instant ($t=0^+$) no fluid has had time to move, and an incompressible mixture with nowhere for fluid to go cannot change volume: the strain is zero, so by (4) the solid carries nothing ($\sigma_E=0$) and the fluid carries the entire load, $p=\sigma_0$ throughout. That is the diffusion equation's initial condition. Thereafter pressure bleeds off through the drained platen ($p=0$ at $z=h$) and the profile relaxes — exactly the Terzaghi solution of the consolidation equation:

(ii) The gel time. A diffusion equation spreads a disturbance across a length $h$ in a time of order $h^2/D$. Here that sets the gel time $\tau$ — how long the tissue takes to give up its fluid pressure and settle onto the solid matrix:

$$\boxed{\;\tau\sim\frac{h^2}{D}=\frac{h^2}{H_A k}\;}$$

During the second that you stand on a joint, almost no fluid escapes: the tissue never gets near its gel time, the pressure of the first instant barely relaxes, and the fluid carries the load. That mismatch — load time $\ll\tau$ — is precisely why cartilage survives, and it is the subject of §4. Over the same diffusion equation run forward to $t\sim\tau$, we instead get stress relaxation and creep, simulated in §5.

4. Interstitial fluid pressurization and load sharing

Section 3 ended on a number with teeth: the gel time $\tau\approx1.9\ \mathrm h$, thousands of times longer than a footstep. This section spends that mismatch on the mechanism that lets cartilage survive — and finally answers §0's paradox, how a $2\ \mathrm{mm}$ layer of soft tissue carries $\approx1.7\ \mathrm{MPa}$ millions of times a year for decades. The short answer: when load arrives faster than the tissue can drain, the fluid carries it, not the solid.

Load sharing, instant by instant

Return to the effective-stress partition of §3, written now in compression-positive form to make "share of the load" read naturally. Let $\sigma_0\gt0$ be the applied compressive stress; let $p\ge0$ be the pore pressure and $\sigma_E\ge0$ the solid effective stress. At every cross-section of the loaded column the two phases split the load between them:

$$\sigma_0=\sigma_E+p,\qquad\text{so}\qquad \underbrace{\frac{\sigma_E}{\sigma_0}}_{\text{solid's share}}+\underbrace{\frac{p}{\sigma_0}}_{\text{fluid's share}}=1.$$

The question of the section is simply: which phase carries how much, and when? Average the fluid's share over the depth to get one number for the whole tissue.

Fluid load support fraction. The depth-averaged fraction of the applied load borne by interstitial fluid pressurization, $$F(t)=\frac{\langle p\rangle}{\sigma_0}=\frac{1}{\sigma_0\,h}\int_0^h p(z,t)\,\mathrm dz,$$ with $\langle p\rangle$ the average pore pressure. $F=1$ means the fluid carries everything; $F=0$ means the solid does.

Computing $F(t)$ from the consolidation solution

§3 already solved for $p(z,t)$ (the Terzaghi series). Averaging that series over the depth term by term — each cosine integrates to $\int_0^1\cos(\lambda_n Z)\,\mathrm dZ=(-1)^n/\lambda_n$ — collapses every spatial factor and leaves a pure function of the dimensionless time $T=Dt/h^2$:

$$F(T)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{2}{\lambda_n^{2}}\,e^{-\lambda_n^{2}T},\qquad \lambda_n=\tfrac{(2n+1)\pi}{2}. \tag{1}$$

At the instant of loading ($T=0$) every exponential is $1$, and the coefficients sum to exactly one — $\sum_n 2/\lambda_n^2=\tfrac{8}{\pi^2}\sum_n(2n+1)^{-2}=\tfrac{8}{\pi^2}\cdot\tfrac{\pi^2}{8}=1$:

Undrained limit. $\displaystyle F(0)=1.$ At the first instant the interstitial fluid carries the entire applied load. (The matrix cannot change volume with no fluid yet displaced, so its strain — and hence its effective stress — is still zero.)

Thereafter every mode decays; the slowest, $n=0$, sets the rate $e^{-(\pi^2/4)T}$, so $F$ falls to $1/e$ at $t\approx0.32\,h^2/D\approx36\ \mathrm{min}$ and to nearly nothing by $t\sim\tau$. The curve below is equation (1) evaluated with the §3 parameters.

Why this saves the joint

Now put the two timescales side by side. A footstep loads the joint for $t_{\rm load}\sim1\ \mathrm s$; in units of the gel time that is $T=t_{\rm load}/\tau\sim1.5\times10^{-4}$ — negligible. The fluid has no time to leave, so equation (1) gives

$$F(1\ \mathrm s)=0.986.$$

During the second you stand on a joint, interstitial fluid carries $\approx99\%$ of the load and the solid matrix feels $\approx1\%$.

That is the whole trick. The soft solid skeleton — collagen and proteoglycan — never has to bear the peak stress; the pressurized fluid does. And a pressurized fluid has no surfaces to abrade and no fibres to fatigue, so the matrix is shielded. This is the resolution of §0's impossible bearing: cartilage carries stresses its solid phase could not survive because, on the timescale of use, it is not the solid phase that carries them.

How it stays charged: cyclic re-imbibition

One objection remains. A footstep is brief, but we take $\sim10^4$ of them a day — wouldn't the fluid drain over so many cycles? It does not, and §2 is the reason. Between steps the joint unloads, and the osmotic swelling pressure of the fixed charges pulls fluid back in. Each cycle re-pressurizes the tissue faster than slow drainage depletes it, so the fluid support is continually recharged.

Loading and recovery together keep $F$ high indefinitely. This is not just theory: direct measurements with pressure transducers in loaded cartilage find interstitial fluid sustaining $85$–$95\%$ of the load through normal cyclic activity — an independent check on the picture equation (1) draws.

Hold the load instead of releasing it — drive the same diffusion equation all the way to $t\sim\tau$ — and the fluid support bleeds away while the solid takes over: at fixed strain the tissue's stress relaxes, at fixed load it creeps. That is the experiment §5 simulates directly from the §3 equation. And because the fluid carries the load with almost no shear resistance, the same pressurization is the key to cartilage's astonishingly low friction (§7). Lose it — through proteoglycan loss and rising permeability — and the solid is no longer shielded: the degeneration spiral of §8.

5. Computational lab 1 — stress relaxation and creep

§3 wrote the governing equation; §4 read off its two endpoints — fluid carries everything at $t=0$, the solid carries everything as $t\to\infty$. This section fills in everything between, by solving the equation numerically and watching it produce a quantity you can measure on a bench: the stress-relaxation curve. It is the module's first computational lab — the §3 diffusion equation, finite-differenced and run forward in time.

The experiment

In a stress-relaxation test you impose a deformation and watch the force decay. In confined compression (the §3 chamber): ramp the porous platen down to a fixed compressive strain $\varepsilon_0$ over a short time $t_0$, then hold it there and record the reaction stress $\sigma(t)$. Here $\varepsilon_0$ is the held engineering strain (platen displacement ÷ thickness $h$) and $t_0$ the ramp duration.

The boundary conditions are exactly §3's — fixed impermeable base ($u=0$ at $z=0$), drained platen ($p=0$ at $z=h$) — except that we now prescribe the platen's displacement. Because the platen drains ($p=0$ there), the measured total stress is

$$\sigma(t)=H_A\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\bigg|_{z=h}, \tag{1}$$

the aggregate modulus times the surface strain. Physically the curve will do this: the fast ramp pressurizes the fluid (it cannot escape in $t_0\ll\tau$), so the tissue resists stiffly and the stress spikes; holding the strain, the fluid slowly exudes, the pressure carrying the load decays, and the stress relaxes to whatever the drained matrix alone supports, $\sigma_\infty=H_A\varepsilon_0$. The fall takes the gel time $\tau$ of §3.

The numerical method

To compute the whole curve, discretize §3's equation $\partial u/\partial t=D\,\partial^2u/\partial z^2$ (with $D=H_Ak$). Split $[0,h]$ into $N$ intervals of width $\Delta z=h/N$, step time by $\Delta t$, and replace the derivatives by differences — the second space derivative by the symmetric three-point stencil $(u_{i-1}-2u_i+u_{i+1})/\Delta z^2$, the time derivative forward. The result is an explicit update for each interior node $i$:

$$u_i^{\,n+1}=u_i^{\,n}+r\big(u_{i-1}^{\,n}-2u_i^{\,n}+u_{i+1}^{\,n}\big),\qquad r\equiv\frac{D\,\Delta t}{\Delta z^{2}}. \tag{2}$$

That is the entire solver. In Python:

import numpy as np

# ---- material + geometry (healthy cartilage) ----

6. Contact: from joint reaction to a pressure field

Module 3 handed us a single number for the loaded joint: a mean contact pressure $\bar p\approx1.7\ \mathrm{MPa}$ spread over $A_c\approx10\ \mathrm{cm^2}$, from the reaction $R\approx1715\ \mathrm N$. But a mean hides the story — real contact pressure is a field, peaked where the surfaces press hardest. This section computes that field twice: first as a dry elastic (Hertz) contact, which refines Module 3's average into a distribution; then with the thin-layer and biphasic physics of this module, which flattens and spreads it.

The elastic baseline: Hertz contact

Model the loaded joint as two elastic bodies pressed together — the classic Hertz contact derived in Module 3 (problem D7). Approximate the articulating surface as a convex body of effective radius $R_{\rm eff}$ (the combined curvature of the two surfaces) meeting the opposing cartilage, pressed by the load $F=R$. Under a fast load the cartilage responds with its instantaneous (undrained) Young's modulus $E\approx10\ \mathrm{MPa}$ and Poisson ratio $\nu\approx0.5$ — nearly incompressible, because the fluid of §4 is trapped and stiffens it. Combine the two like surfaces into a reduced modulus $E^{*}$ via $1/E^{*}=2(1-\nu^{2})/E$. Hertz theory then gives a circular contact of radius $a$ carrying a hemispherical pressure cap:

$$a=\Big(\frac{3FR_{\rm eff}}{4E^{*}}\Big)^{1/3},\qquad p(r)=p_0\sqrt{1-\frac{r^{2}}{a^{2}}},\qquad p_0=\frac{3F}{2\pi a^{2}}=\tfrac32\,\bar p, \tag{1}$$

with $r$ the distance from the contact centre, $\bar p=F/(\pi a^{2})$ the mean, and $p_0$ the peak at the centre. (Note $E$ here is the tissue's isotropic Young's modulus for fast loading — distinct from the confined aggregate modulus $H_A$ of §3, which governs one-dimensional drainage, not contact.)

What the biphasic thin layer does to it

Two features of real cartilage push that peak back down, both pointing the same way.

  • It is a thin layer, not a half-space. Hertz assumes each body is deep compared with the contact. Here the cartilage is $h\approx2\ \mathrm{mm}$ on rigid bone while the contact is $a\approx18\ \mathrm{mm}$ across — $a/h\approx9$. A thin layer bonded to bone cannot bulge like a half-space; it transmits load far more uniformly (its pressure follows the indentation gap, not the Hertzian cap), flattening and widening the distribution.
  • The fluid carries the load (§4). Under fast load the interstitial fluid supports $85$–$95\%$ of the pressure, and a fluid pressure is smooth and broad, not peaked. Held longer, creep (§5) exudes fluid and spreads the contact further, lowering the peak again.

A load-conserving estimate — the same $F$ spread over a $\sim30\%$ wider patch — cuts the peak from the Hertzian $2.5\ \mathrm{MPa}$ toward $\approx2.0\ \mathrm{MPa}$ and lifts the edges (blue curve). The real joint runs even gentler than the elastic estimate.

So the refinement runs the safe way. Stop at dry Hertz and the true peak ($2.5\ \mathrm{MPa}$) is half again Module 3's mean; add the thin layer and the fluid and that peak is pulled back down and spread out — the very fluid-pressurization shielding of §4, now expressed in the geometry of a loaded joint. It is also why articular contact is so forgiving of incongruence: whatever peak the geometry tries to raise, the fluid and the thin layer smear out.

The one thing this pressure field still lacks is motion — sliding one surface across the other under all this pressure. That is friction and lubrication, and cartilage's is lower than ice. Why, in §7.

7. Computational lab 2 — lubrication and the Stribeck curve

§6 pressed the surfaces together; now slide them. Cartilage's friction coefficient is $\mu\approx0.005$–$0.02$ — below ice on ice. Under the $\approx1715\ \mathrm N$ joint reaction that is a friction force of only $\sim10\ \mathrm N$. How? This lab builds the answer in two steps: the classical lubrication picture (which, it turns out, cannot explain it), then the biphasic mechanism (which does).

Classical lubrication and the Stribeck number

The friction coefficient is $\mu=(\text{tangential force})/(\text{normal load})$. Classical lubrication of any bearing — a hip, a crankshaft — is organised by one dimensionless group, the Stribeck number (or Hersey number)

$$S=\frac{\eta\,U}{W},$$

viscosity $\eta$ times sliding speed $U$ over load $W$. Sweep $S$ and $\mu$ traces the Stribeck curve through three regimes: boundary (low $S$ — slow or heavily loaded), mixed, and fluid-film (high $S$ — fast or lightly loaded).

A compact model captures the first three: $\mu(S)=\mu_{\rm bl}/\big(1+(S/S_0)^2\big)+\alpha S$ — a boundary term $\mu_{\rm bl}$ that rules when slow/heavy, plus a hydrodynamic term $\alpha S$ that rules when fast/light. Compute it:

The biphasic resolution

What carries the load when there is no film is the interstitial fluid of §4. Friction acts on solid–solid contact; but the fluid pressurization carries a fraction $F$ of the load, leaving only $(1-F)$ on the solid. So the effective friction coefficient is the intrinsic (drained) solid friction $\mu_{\rm eq}$ scaled by the solid's share of the load:

$$\boxed{\;\mu_{\rm eff}(t)=\mu_{\rm eq}\,\big(1-F(t)\big)\;}$$

with $F(t)$ the fluid load support fraction of §4. When the fluid is pressurized ($F\approx0.99$ during a footstep), $\mu_{\rm eff}\approx0.15\times0.01\approx0.002$ — the measured value — and it needs no sliding speed at all. Cartilage is slippery not because a film floats the surfaces, but because the fluid inside it carries the load. Both curves come from one short program:

import numpy as np

That self-feeding failure is the subject of §8.

8. Degeneration and osteoarthritis mechanics

Every section so far described cartilage working. This one describes it failing. Osteoarthritis (OA) — the commonest joint disease, the endpoint of ageing, overload, and injury — is, mechanically, this module's physics run in reverse and locked into a self-amplifying loop. No single step is catastrophic; the danger is that each step makes the next one worse.

The trigger, and the two struts it breaks

It begins with the proteoglycans. Enzymes (aggrecanases and MMPs — matrix-degrading proteins, up-regulated by injury, inflammation, or age) cleave aggrecan, and the fixed charge density $c_F$ falls. Two consequences follow, and they are the two load-bearing struts of the whole module.

  • The swelling pre-stress is lost (§2). The Donnan pressure $\pi=R_gT\big(\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}-2c_0\big)$ falls with $c_F$. Cartilage loses the resting turgor that kept it inflated and pre-loaded the collagen; it softens, and more load falls on the collagen network — which frays (fibrillation, the surface splitting seen in early OA).
  • The fluid seal is broken (§3–§4). The proteoglycans were also what made the matrix nearly impermeable. Lose them and the permeability $k$ rises. By §3 the gel time $\tau=h^2/(H_Ak)$ falls in proportion; by §4 the fluid load support $F$ then collapses sooner. The tissue can no longer carry load as a pressurised fluid — the weakened solid matrix must take the stress.

Why it has a threshold

Why doesn't a healthy joint spiral like this on its own? Because the loop has a gain. Each pass multiplies the damage by some factor $g$. Healthy cartilage keeps $g\lt1$: its fluid-support margin is enormous ($F\approx0.99$ leaves vast headroom before the solid is overloaded), and chondrocytes slowly repair the matrix, so a perturbation damps out. Degeneration raises $g$ toward and past $1$ — and a loop with $g\gt1$ runs away. That is why osteoarthritis has a tipping point: below it the joint self-corrects, above it the same physics that protected the tissue now destroys it, marching to bone-on-bone. It is also why intervention works best early, while $g$ is still under one and the fluid-support margin can still be defended.

The biological triggers — ageing, obesity (which raises the load $W$ and hence the peak), acute injury, genetics — enter through Module 14. Mechanically, though, the lesson of this module is now complete: cartilage survives because the interstitial fluid carries the load, and it fails when it can no longer do so. What this biphasic picture captures, and what it still leaves out — full three-dimensional contact, collagen anisotropy, finite deformation, the living chondrocyte — is the accounting of §9.

Diagnostics

Five quick checks. Answer before opening each.

  1. At the instant a load is applied, what fraction does the interstitial fluid carry, and why?
    answer
    Essentially all of it: $F(0)=1$. With no fluid yet displaced, the incompressible, saturated mixture cannot change volume, so the strain — and hence the solid effective stress $\sigma_E=H_A\varepsilon$ — is zero. The fluid carries the whole load (§4).
  2. Why is cartilage slippery even at zero sliding speed (standing still)?
    answer
    Because friction acts only on the solid-borne share $(1-F)$ of the load: $\mu_{\rm eff}=\mu_{\rm eq}(1-F)$. The fluid pressurisation carries the load regardless of speed, so $\mu$ is low with no hydrodynamic film needed (§7) — unlike a classical bearing, which needs speed to build a film.
  3. A joint is loaded for $1\ \mathrm s$ (a step), and separately held for $2\ \mathrm h$. Which load is borne by fluid, which by solid?
    answer
    $1\ \mathrm s\ll\tau$ ⇒ fluid ($F\approx1$); $2\ \mathrm h\gtrsim\tau$ ⇒ solid ($F\to0$). The divider is the gel time $\tau\sim h^2/(H_Ak)\sim10^3$–$10^4\ \mathrm s$ (§3).
  4. Two plugs of the same tissue, thicknesses $h$ and $2h$. Ratio of their gel times?
    answer
    $\tau\propto h^2$, so the thicker plug relaxes $4\times$ slower.
  5. Why does the dry-elastic Hertz calculation overestimate the true contact peak?
    answer
    Real cartilage is a thin layer ($a\gg h$) bonded to bone, and the interstitial fluid pressurisation is broad, not peaked — both flatten and spread the pressure below the Hertz cap (§6).

Conceptual problems

Ten problems to reason with — each isolates one idea from the module's spine. Work out the reasoning before opening the solution.

C1 — undrained vs. drained — one tissue, two stiffnesses. Probes: undrained vs. drained response; the gel time (§3).

Press a cartilage sample with your thumb for a second and it feels firm as hard rubber. Rest a heavy book on the same spot and, over an afternoon, it slowly sinks and stays dented until you lift the book. Same tissue, opposite behaviour. Explain, and name the timescale that separates the two regimes.

solution

Undrained (loading time $\ll\tau$): the fluid has no time to leave, and an incompressible mixture with trapped fluid barely changes volume — it resists almost rigidly. The thumb-press ($\sim1\ \mathrm s$) is undrained, so the tissue feels firm.

Drained (loading time $\gg\tau$): held long enough, the fluid slowly exudes; once it is gone, only the soft drained matrix (aggregate modulus $H_A\sim0.6\ \mathrm{MPa}$) resists, so the tissue creeps down and stays dented. The afternoon under a book is drained. (It recovers when unloaded because the osmotic swelling pressure of §2 re-imbibes the fluid.)

The separating timescale is the gel time $\tau\sim h^2/(H_Ak)\sim10^3$–$10^4\ \mathrm s$ (minutes to hours). Firm below it, soft above it — one material, two stiffnesses, set by whether you outrun the drainage.

C2 — why cartilage swells at rest. Probes: fixed charge, Donnan osmosis, and the resting swelling pre-stress (§2).

A freshly excised plug of cartilage, floated in saline, visibly swells; cut a slit in it and the slit gapes open. What in the tissue's composition drives this, and why does it matter mechanically before any external load is applied?

solution

Proteoglycans carry a dense fixed negative charge $c_F$. To keep electroneutrality, mobile counter-ions (mainly $\mathrm{Na^+}$) are held inside at higher concentration than the surrounding bath; that excess ion concentration raises the interstitial osmotic pressure — the Donnan swelling pressure $\pi$ (§2). Water follows osmotically and the solid matrix swells until the collagen network's tension balances $\pi$.

So even with no external load the tissue is pre-stressed: collagen in tension, gel in compression at $\pi\approx0.16\ \mathrm{MPa}$. Mechanically this pre-stress stiffens the surface, resists the very first increment of load, and re-imbibes fluid after unloading (the recovery in C1 and C10). Lose the charge — as in degeneration — and the pre-stress, the surface stiffness, and the recovery all fall together (§8).

C3 — who carries the first instant of load. Probes: incompressibility of the saturated mixture; why $\sigma_E=0$ at $t=0$ (§4).

The moment you step — before any fluid has moved — what fraction of the load does the interstitial fluid carry, and why must it be exactly that fraction, independent of how stiff the solid matrix is?

solution

All of it: $F(0)=1$. The tissue is fully saturated and both phases are nearly incompressible, so the only way to change volume is to expel fluid — which takes time. In the first instant no fluid has left, so the volumetric strain is zero, so the solid strain is zero, so the effective stress $\sigma_E=H_A\varepsilon=0$ — the solid carries nothing, regardless of $H_A$.

The applied traction is balanced entirely by a jump in interstitial pressure $p$. This is why cartilage feels rigid on impact even though its drained modulus is soft (C1): stiffness on the timescale of a footstep is set by fluid incompressibility, not by the matrix.

C4 — slippery standing still. Probes: that biphasic lubrication needs no sliding speed, unlike a fluid-film bearing (§7).

A journal bearing is only slippery once it spins fast enough to float on an oil film; at rest it is metal-on-metal and stiff to start. Cartilage is slippery even when you have been standing still. Explain the difference.

solution

In a hydrodynamic bearing the load-bearing film is generated by motion: oil entrained into a converging wedge builds the pressure, so at $v=0$ there is no film and friction is high. Cartilage does not rely on entrainment. Its load-bearing pressure is the interstitial fluid pressurisation, which exists whenever the tissue is freshly loaded ($F\approx1$), speed or no speed.

Friction acts only on the solid-borne fraction: $\mu_{\mathrm{eff}}=\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}(1-F)$ (§7). With $F\approx1$ at rest, $\mu_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx0$. Only if you stand long enough for the fluid to drain ($F\to0$, minutes–hours, C1) does $\mu$ climb toward the intrinsic $\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}$. Cartilage is a pre-charged pressurised bearing, not a speed-dependent one.

C5 — why the dry calculation is too harsh. Probes: why a thin bonded layer and broad fluid pressurisation flatten the peak below the dry-Hertz cap (§6).

A dry-elastic (Hertz) contact calculation for the hip gives a peak pressure $p_0\approx2.5\ \mathrm{MPa}$. The real cartilage peak is lower and broader. Give two independent reasons the dry calculation overestimates the true peak.

solution

(1) Geometry — a thin bonded layer. Hertz assumes two elastic half-spaces free to bulge. Cartilage is a thin layer ($a\gg h$) firmly bonded to stiff subchondral bone, which cannot bulge sideways as a half-space would; the confinement spreads the contact wider and lowers the peak.

(2) Biphasic pressurisation. On the loading timescale the load is carried by interstitial pressure, which is a broad, plateau-like field, not a sharp Hertzian dome.

Both effects redistribute the same total load ($\int p\,2\pi r\,\mathrm dr=R$) over a larger area, so the peak drops (here $\approx2.5\to\approx2.0\ \mathrm{MPa}$) while the mean is essentially unchanged. The figure overlays the two profiles carrying the identical load.

C6 — one tissue, two moduli. Probes: the composite origin of cartilage's very different tensile and compressive stiffness (§1).

Cartilage is roughly $20\times$ stiffer in tension than in compression. What microstructural feature accounts for each, and why does the asymmetry make sense for a bearing surface?

solution

Tension is resisted by the collagen fibril network — stiff, high-tensile-modulus fibres ($E_{\mathrm{tens}}\sim10$–$15\ \mathrm{MPa}$) that carry pull like the cords in a tyre. Compression is resisted by the proteoglycan gel and its Donnan swelling — a soft, drained aggregate modulus $H_A\sim0.6\ \mathrm{MPa}$. Fibres buckle under compression but pull taut under tension, so the tissue is soft to squeeze but stiff to stretch.

For a bearing this is ideal: the soft compressive response lets the surfaces conform and spread contact (lowering peak pressure, C5), while the stiff tensile skin resists the hoop and tensile stresses that a conforming, pressurised layer generates — holding the tissue together under the very loads its softness invites.

C7 — relaxation versus creep. Probes: that stress-relaxation (fixed strain) and creep (fixed load) are the same biphasic physics under two boundary conditions (§5).

Compress a plug to a fixed depth and hold: the force you must apply slowly falls. Instead hang a fixed weight on it: the plug slowly sinks. Are these two different material behaviours? Explain both with one mechanism.

solution

One mechanism, two controls. In both, the load is first carried by pressurised fluid ($F\approx1$) which then drains over $\tau$.

Fixed strain (relaxation): the initial undrained stress is high because the incompressible mixture resists; as fluid leaves, the pressure decays and the stress relaxes toward the drained value $\sigma_\infty=H_A\varepsilon$.

Fixed load (creep): the initial undrained strain is small (stiff); as fluid leaves, the plug deforms further until the drained matrix alone carries the load at $\varepsilon_\infty=\sigma/H_A$.

Same drainage, same $\tau$. Relaxation is the stress transient at fixed strain; creep is the strain transient at fixed stress. The figure shows the falling-stress and rising-strain curves side by side.

C8 — the vicious cycle. Probes: osteoarthritis as a positive-feedback loss of fluid support, not a single failure (§8).

Osteoarthritis is often called "wear and tear," as if the cartilage simply abrades away. Reframe it as a feedback loop, and identify what makes the loop self-amplifying.

solution

The primary mechanical event is loss of fluid load support. Matrix damage (charge loss, collagen disruption) raises the permeability $k$, which shortens the gel time $\tau=h^2/(H_Ak)$ and lets fluid drain faster; $F$ sags.

Lower $F$ means the solid matrix carries more stress and friction rises, $\mu_{\mathrm{eff}}=\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}(1-F)$. Higher stress and friction abrade and fibrillate the surface, which damages more matrix and raises $k$ further — closing the loop.

Each pass makes the next worse: a positive feedback, not a one-shot failure. The leverage point is anywhere that breaks the loop (restore charge and $\pi$, lower the load, restore $F$). "Wear" is the loop's output, not its cause.

C9 — thin heals faster. Probes: the drainage-path scaling $\tau\propto h^2$ and its clinical reading (§3).

Two cartilage samples of the same tissue, one $1\ \mathrm{mm}$ thick and one $2\ \mathrm{mm}$ thick, are loaded identically. Which reaches its drained (soft) state sooner, and by what factor? What does this say about where thick cartilage (e.g. the patella) sits on the load-timescale map?

solution

The thinner one drains faster. Since $\tau=h^2/D$ scales as the square of the drainage path, halving $h$ cuts $\tau$ by $4$ (the $2\ \mathrm{mm}$ plug is $4\times$ slower). Physically, fluid in the thick plug must travel twice as far to reach a drained surface, and diffusion time goes as distance squared.

Clinically, thick cartilage keeps $F$ high over longer loading times — better sustained protection during prolonged stance — but relaxes and recovers more slowly, so it depends more on adequate unloaded recovery periods (C10). Thin, degenerate cartilage (small $h$, large $k$) has a tiny $\tau$ and loses fluid support within a step or two.

C10 — why you are taller in the morning. Probes: reversible daily fluid loss and osmotic recovery; the swelling pressure as the reset (§2, §4).

People are measurably taller in the morning than at night (partly from spinal cartilage and discs). Explain the daily cycle, why it is normally reversible, and what would make it stop reversing.

solution

Under a day of loading, cartilage and discs slowly exude fluid (drained creep, C1/C7) and thin — you lose height. Unloaded overnight, the Donnan swelling pressure $\pi$ (§2) osmotically re-imbibes water and the tissue recovers thickness by morning.

The cycle is reversible precisely because the fixed-charge-driven $\pi$ survives the day; it is the restoring force. It stops reversing if the charge is lost (degeneration): with $\pi$ gone, exuded fluid is not drawn back, the tissue stays thin, $F$ cannot recover between loadings, and repeated cycles ratchet down (K9). Recovery, not just loading, is a physiological requirement.

D1 — one- versus two-sided drainage. Probes: eigenfunctions and boundary conditions; the drainage path (§3).

The confined-compression gel time for a plug of thickness $h$ drained on one face (impermeable base) is $\tau_1=4h^2/(\pi^2D)$, $D=H_Ak$. Now make both faces permeable. Derive the new fundamental gel time and the factor by which it changes, and explain it physically.

solution

The pressure obeys $\partial p/\partial t=D\,\partial^2p/\partial z^2$; the slowest (fundamental) relaxation mode sets the gel time, $\tau=1/(D\lambda_1^2)$, where $\lambda_1$ is the smallest eigenvalue admitted by the boundary conditions.

One-sided (impermeable base $\partial p/\partial z=0$ at $z=0$, drained $p=0$ at $z=h$): admissible modes are $\cos(\lambda z)$ with $\lambda_n=(2n+1)\pi/2h$; the fundamental is $\lambda_1=\pi/2h$, giving $\tau_1=1/(D\lambda_1^2)=4h^2/(\pi^2D)$.

Two-sided (drained at both $z=0$ and $z=h$): modes are $\sin(n\pi z/h)$ with $\lambda_n=n\pi/h$; the fundamental is $\lambda_1=\pi/h$, giving $\tau_2=1/(D\lambda_1^2)=h^2/(\pi^2D)$.

$$\frac{\tau_2}{\tau_1}=\frac{(\pi/2h)^2}{(\pi/h)^2}=\frac14.$$

Draining both faces makes the tissue relax four times faster. Physically, fluid now escapes from both surfaces, so the farthest any fluid must travel to a drain is halved ($h\to h/2$); since $\tau\propto(\text{drainage path})^2$, the time drops by $4$.

D2 — derive the swelling pressure. Probes: Donnan equilibrium plus electroneutrality → the ideal osmotic swelling pressure (§2).

A charged tissue with fixed-charge density $c_F$ (per unit fluid volume) sits in a bath of monovalent salt at concentration $c_0$. Assuming ideal (dilute) solutions, derive the interstitial swelling pressure $\pi$, and evaluate it for $c_F=0.2\ \mathrm M$, $c_0=0.15\ \mathrm M$, $T=310\ \mathrm K$.

solution

Let the interstitial mobile cation and anion concentrations be $c_+,c_-$. Two conditions apply.

Electroneutrality inside (the fixed charge is negative): $c_+=c_-+c_F$.

Donnan equilibrium (salt chemical potential equal inside and out): $c_+c_-=c_0^2$.

Eliminate: $c_-(c_-+c_F)=c_0^2\Rightarrow c_-=\tfrac12(\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}-c_F)$ and $c_+=\tfrac12(\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}+c_F)$.

The osmotic (swelling) pressure is the van 't Hoff pressure of the excess total mobile-ion concentration inside relative to the bath:

$$\boxed{\;\pi=R_gT\big[(c_++c_-)-2c_0\big]=R_gT\big[\sqrt{c_F^2+4c_0^2}-2c_0\big].\;}$$

Numbers: $\sqrt{0.2^2+4(0.15)^2}=\sqrt{0.13}=0.3606\ \mathrm M$; minus $0.30\ \mathrm M=0.0606\ \mathrm M=60.6\ \mathrm{mol/m^3}$. With $R_gT=8.314\times310=2577\ \mathrm{J/mol}$: $\pi=2577\times60.6\approx1.56\times10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}\approx0.16\ \mathrm{MPa}$.

Check the limits: $\pi\to0$ as $c_F\to0$ (an uncharged gel does not swell), and $\pi\approx R_gT\,c_F^2/(2c_0)$ grows quadratically for $c_F\ll c_0$ — the rising curve of K2.

D3 — derive the consolidation equation. Probes: mass balance + Darcy + linear elasticity → the governing diffusion PDE (§3).

For 1-D confined compression along $z$, with solid displacement $u(z,t)$, derive the governing equation $\partial u/\partial t=H_Ak\,\partial^2u/\partial z^2$. State each assumption.

solution

Take a slab $[z,z+\mathrm dz]$ and assume: incompressible solid and fluid, full saturation, small strain, constant $k$ and $H_A$, 1-D confined geometry, and linear (Darcy) flow.

(i) Kinematics. Strain $\varepsilon=\partial u/\partial z$.

(ii) Effective stress at constant load. Total stress $\sigma=\sigma_E-p$ with $\sigma_E=H_A\varepsilon$. Under a suddenly-applied constant total stress, $\partial\sigma/\partial t=0$, so $$H_A\,\frac{\partial\varepsilon}{\partial t}=\frac{\partial p}{\partial t}.\quad(\mathrm A)$$

(iii) Continuity + Darcy. With incompressible constituents, the rate of volumetric strain equals the divergence of the relative fluid flux $q$; Darcy gives $q=-k\,\partial p/\partial z$, so $$\frac{\partial\varepsilon}{\partial t}=-\frac{\partial q}{\partial z}=k\,\frac{\partial^2p}{\partial z^2}.\quad(\mathrm B)$$

Combine. Substituting (A) into (B), $\partial p/\partial t=H_Ak\,\partial^2p/\partial z^2$. Because $p$ and $u$ are linearly related through (A) and $\varepsilon=\partial u/\partial z$, the displacement obeys the same equation:

$$\boxed{\;\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}=H_Ak\,\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial z^2},\qquad D\equiv H_Ak.\;}$$

It is a diffusion equation; the diffusivity $D=H_Ak$ carries the whole consolidation timescale.

D4 — short-time fluid support. Probes: the early-time square-root law from the diffusion similarity solution (§4).

For $T=Dt/h^2\ll1$, show that the fluid load support behaves as $F\approx1-2\sqrt{T/\pi}$, and explain why the correction is $\sqrt T$ rather than linear in $T$.

solution

At short time only a thin drained boundary layer near the permeable surface has formed; the rest of the plug is still fully pressurised. The diffusion penetration depth grows as $\delta\sim\sqrt{Dt}$ (the similarity solution of the half-space diffusion problem).

The lost support is proportional to the drained fraction $\delta/h\sim\sqrt{Dt}/h=\sqrt T$. Evaluating the half-space surface deficit gives the constant $2/\sqrt\pi$, so $$F\approx1-2\sqrt{T/\pi}.$$

The $\sqrt T$ (not $T$) is the fingerprint of diffusion: distance grows as the square root of time, so the drained layer — and the support deficit — grows as $\sqrt t$. The figure shows this line hugging the exact series near the origin and parting later.

D5 — the eigenvalue series. Probes: separation of variables giving the mode set and the slowest-mode gel time (§4).

For one-sided drainage (sealed base at $z=0$, drained at $z=h$), find the eigenfunctions and eigenvalues of the pressure diffusion problem, and hence the series $F(T)=\sum_n 2/\lambda_n^2\,e^{-\lambda_n^2T}$ and the gel time.

solution

Separate $p=Z(z)\,e^{-D\mu^2t}$: then $Z''+\mu^2Z=0$. The boundary conditions select the modes:

$Z'(0)=0$ (no flux at the sealed base) $\Rightarrow Z=\cos(\mu z)$; $Z(h)=0$ (drained) $\Rightarrow \cos(\mu h)=0\Rightarrow \mu_nh=(2n+1)\tfrac\pi2$, i.e. $\lambda_n\equiv\mu_nh=(2n+1)\pi/2$, $n=0,1,2,\dots$

Expanding the uniform initial pressure in these modes and integrating over depth to get the load support gives the coefficients $2/\lambda_n^2$, so $$F(T)=\sum_n\frac{2}{\lambda_n^2}\,e^{-\lambda_n^2T},\qquad T=\frac{Dt}{h^2}.$$

Late in time the slowest mode dominates: $n=0$, $\lambda_0=\pi/2$, giving $\tau=1/(D\mu_0^2)=4h^2/(\pi^2D)$ — the gel time of D1. The figure plots the first three cosine modes; the flat $n=0$ mode (peak at the sealed base) is the long-lived one.

D6 — why $\tau$ scales as $h^2$. Probes: nondimensionalising the PDE shows $\tau$ is a fixed value of $T=Dt/h^2$ (§5).

Without solving the series, show from the governing equation alone that the gel time must scale as $h^2/D$, and that changing $h$ only rescales the time axis.

solution

Nondimensionalise. Let $\zeta=z/h$ and $T=Dt/h^2$. Then $\partial u/\partial t=D\,\partial^2u/\partial z^2$ becomes $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial T}=\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial\zeta^2},$$ with no parameters left — $h$ and $D$ have vanished. So the entire relaxation history is a universal function of $T$ only, $F=\Phi(T)$.

The gel time is defined by some fixed fraction of relaxation (say $F=\tfrac12$), reached at a fixed $T=T_0$ (a pure number). Undoing the scaling, $t_{1/2}=T_0\,h^2/D$, i.e. $\tau\propto h^2/D$.

Doubling $h$ stretches the physical time axis by $4$ with the curve otherwise unchanged: every thickness collapses onto one master curve in $T$ (the figure) — the $\tau\propto h^2$ result, obtained without any series.

D7 — the Hertz exponents. Probes: the sphere-on-plane scaling of contact radius and peak pressure with load and radius (§6).

For a rigid sphere of effective radius $R$ pressed with force $N$ into an elastic half-space of reduced modulus $E^*$, derive the scalings $a\propto(NR/E^*)^{1/3}$ and $p_0\propto N/a^2$, and hence how $a$ and $p_0$ change if $R$ doubles at fixed $N$.

solution

Geometry. For a sphere the interpenetration depth and contact radius satisfy $\delta\approx a^2/R$.

Elastic contact. Force relates to modulus, radius and depth by $N\sim E^*a\,\delta\sim E^*a\,(a^2/R)=E^*a^3/R$. Solving, $$a\sim\Big(\frac{NR}{E^*}\Big)^{1/3}\propto N^{1/3}R^{1/3}.$$

Peak pressure. $p_0=\dfrac{3N}{2\pi a^2}\propto\dfrac{N}{a^2}\propto N\,(NR/E^*)^{-2/3}=N^{1/3}(E^*)^{2/3}R^{-2/3}.$

At fixed load $N$: $a\propto R^{1/3}$ (doubling $R$ raises $a$ by $2^{1/3}\approx1.26$) and $p_0\propto R^{-2/3}$ (falls by $2^{-2/3}\approx0.63$). A more conforming (larger-$R$) joint spreads the contact and lowers the peak — the geometric half of why congruent joints protect cartilage. The figure plots both scalings.

D8 — the friction law. Probes: deriving $\mu_{\mathrm{eff}}=\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}(1-F)$ from the load partition (§7).

The normal load $N$ across a cartilage contact is shared between interstitial fluid pressure and solid–solid contact. Taking friction to act only on the solid-borne load with intrinsic coefficient $\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}$, derive the effective friction coefficient in terms of the fluid load fraction $F$.

solution

Partition the normal load: fluid carries $N_f=FN$, solid contact carries $N_s=(1-F)N$.

Interstitial fluid pressure is essentially inviscid on this scale and transmits negligible shear, so the only frictional resistance comes from the solid contact: the tangential force is $f=\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}N_s=\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}(1-F)N$.

The measured (effective) coefficient is $$\boxed{\;\mu_{\mathrm{eff}}=\frac fN=\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}(1-F).\;}$$

Limits: fresh load $F\to1\Rightarrow\mu_{\mathrm{eff}}\to0$ (the vanishing-friction pressurised regime); fully drained $F\to0\Rightarrow\mu_{\mathrm{eff}}\to\mu_{\mathrm{eq}}$ (the intrinsic solid-on-solid value, $\sim0.15$). Cartilage lives near the top limit because $F$ stays high for the duration of normal loading (D4), which is why measured $\mu\sim0.005$–$0.02$.

D9 — the equilibrium creep strain. Probes: that the long-time drained strain is set by the aggregate modulus alone (§5).

A confined plug under a constant applied stress $\sigma$ creeps. Show that the final (drained) strain is $\varepsilon_\infty=\sigma/H_A$, independent of permeability, and say what $k$ does control.

solution

At equilibrium nothing flows, so $q=0$, and Darcy $q=-k\,\partial p/\partial z=0$ with drained boundaries forces $p=0$ throughout: the fluid carries none of the load.

The entire applied stress is then borne by the solid effective stress: $\sigma=\sigma_E=H_A\varepsilon_\infty$, hence $$\boxed{\;\varepsilon_\infty=\sigma/H_A.\;}$$

Permeability $k$ has dropped out — it cannot affect the endpoint, only the path to it: $k$ (through $D=H_Ak$ and $\tau=h^2/D$) sets how fast the strain climbs to $\varepsilon_\infty$, not its value. Two tissues with the same $H_A$ but different $k$ creep to the same final dent, one faster than the other. The figure shows $\varepsilon(t)/\varepsilon_\infty\to1$.

D10 — when does degeneration run away. Probes: a loop-gain (linear-stability) condition for the OA feedback (§8).

Model the degeneration cascade of C8 as a feedback loop: a small drop $\delta F$ in fluid support produces extra damage that further lowers $F$. Writing the per-cycle amplification as a loop gain $G$, state the condition for a small perturbation to heal versus run away, and interpret $G$.

solution

Let the perturbation to the fluid-support deficit after $n$ cycles be $x_n$. Each pass round the loop multiplies it: $$x_{n+1}=G\,x_n\quad\Rightarrow\quad x_n=G^n\,x_0.$$

If $G\lt1$ the deficit decays geometrically — the tissue heals and returns to homeostasis. If $G\gt1$ it grows without bound — runaway degeneration. The threshold is $G=1$.

The loop gain is the product of the sensitivities around the cycle — how strongly loss of support raises permeability, stress and wear, which in turn feed back to lower support: $$G=\frac{\partial(\text{damage})}{\partial(\downarrow F)}\cdot\frac{\partial k}{\partial(\text{damage})}\cdot\frac{\partial(\text{stress})}{\partial k}\cdot\frac{\partial(\downarrow F)}{\partial(\text{stress})}\cdots$$

Interventions — lower load, restore charge and $F$, reduce friction — all act by pushing $G$ below $1$. The inset contrasts a decaying ($G\lt1$) and a growing ($G\gt1$) response.

K4 — stress relaxation by finite differences. Probes: an explicit FD solution of the consolidation PDE; the undrained peak and the $h^2$ time (§5).

Solve the 1-D consolidation equation numerically (explicit finite differences) for a plug ramped to strain $\varepsilon_0$ and held. Confirm the stress decays from an undrained peak to $\sigma_\infty=H_A\varepsilon_0$ over a few $\tau$, and that the relaxation time scales as $h^2$.

solution

Discretise $\partial p/\partial t=D\,\partial^2p/\partial z^2$ on $N$ depth nodes with $p=0$ at the drained face and $\partial p/\partial z=0$ (a mirror node) at the sealed base. The total stress is $\sigma(t)=H_A\varepsilon_0+\langle p\rangle$, the spatial-mean pressure added to the drained elastic stress. The explicit step is stable for $r=D\,\mathrm dt/\mathrm dz^2\le\tfrac12$.

The stress starts elevated (undrained, the pressure supports extra load), then $\langle p\rangle\to0$ and $\sigma\to H_A\varepsilon_0$. Because the equation is parameter-free in $T=Dt/h^2$ (D6), doubling $h$ quadruples the real relaxation time while leaving the shape fixed. The figure shows the peak-then-decay.

import numpy as np
h, HA, k = 2e-3, 0.6e6, 1e-15
D = HA*k;  tau = h**2/D
N, r = 60, 0.4
dz = h/N;  dt = r*dz**2/D
p = np.ones(N+1)          # uniform initial pressure (undrained)
p[-1] = 0.0               # drained top face
tsteps = int(3*tau/dt)
for _ in range(tsteps):
    lap = p[2:] - 2*p[1:-1] + p[:-2]
    p[1:-1] += r*lap
    p[0]  = p[1]           # sealed base: zero-flux (mirror)
    p[-1] = 0.0
print(f"tau={tau:.0f} s;  mean p after 3*tau = {p.mean():.3f} (-> 0)")
# tau=6667 s;  mean p after 3*tau = 0.0xx  (stress -> HA*eps0)

(b) $T=3600/6667=0.54$, $F=0.214$: after an hour near $\tau$, only $\approx21\%$ is fluid-borne; the solid carries most of the load.

The same tissue is a near-perfect fluid bearing for a step and a nearly drained solid for a long stand — the entire point of the gel time. The figure marks both points on the $F(t)$ curve.

K7 — creep to the drained limit. Probes: the equilibrium creep strain and the time to reach it (§5).

For the healthy plug ($h=2\ \mathrm{mm}$, $H_A=0.6\ \mathrm{MPa}$, $k=10^{-15}$) under constant stress $\sigma=0.3\ \mathrm{MPa}$: (a) compute the equilibrium creep strain $\varepsilon_\infty=\sigma/H_A$. (b) Using the consolidation series, find the time to reach $90\%$ of $\varepsilon_\infty$.

solution

(a) $\varepsilon_\infty=\sigma/H_A=0.3\times10^6/0.6\times10^6=0.50$ (a large, illustrative strain).

(b) The creep follows $\varepsilon(t)/\varepsilon_\infty=1-\sum_n\frac{8}{(2n+1)^2\pi^2}e^{-\lambda_n^2T}$, $\lambda_n=(2n+1)\pi/2$, $T=Dt/h^2$. Solving $\varepsilon/\varepsilon_\infty=0.9$ gives $T\approx0.85$; with $D=6\times10^{-10}$ and $\tau=h^2/D=6667\ \mathrm s$, $t=T\,h^2/D\approx5.7\times10^3\ \mathrm s\approx1.6\ \mathrm h$.

So the plug reaches most of its creep within a couple of gel times — hours, consistent with the "afternoon under a book" of C1. The figure shows the rising strain approaching the $\sigma/H_A$ plateau.

K9 — the cumulative sag. Probes: modelling cumulative loss of support when overnight recovery is incomplete (§8).

Model repeated load/unload cycles. In each, loading drains fluid ($F$ drops) and unloading re-imbibes it by swelling. If healthy tissue fully recovers each cycle but degraded tissue recovers only a fraction $r\lt1$, show how peak $F$ evolves and contrast the two after $20$ cycles.

solution

Let the peak support after cycle $n$ be $F_n$. Healthy tissue recovers fully, so $F_n=F_0\approx0.97$ (flat). Degraded tissue re-imbibes incompletely, draining a little more each cycle; a simple envelope is $F_n=F_\infty+(F_0-F_\infty)e^{-n/N_c}$, with $F_\infty$ the drained floor.

With $F_0=0.97$, $F_\infty\approx0.15$, $N_c\approx22$: after $20$ cycles the healthy tissue is still $\approx0.97$ but the degraded tissue has fallen to $F_{20}=0.15+0.82\,e^{-20/22}=0.48$.

The single-step number (K1, K5) looked safe for both; it is the cumulative failure to re-imbibe — lost swelling pressure (K2) unable to pull water back between loadings — that separates them. The figure shows the two envelopes diverging cycle by cycle.

# cycle  0:  healthy F=0.97   degraded F=0.97
# cycle 20:  healthy F=0.97   degraded F=0.48

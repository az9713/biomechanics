0. Motivation — running is bouncing

Running feels like work, and it is — but far less of it is muscular than it looks. Watch a runner at a steady pace and every footfall is the same small collapse and rebound: the leg shortens as it takes the body's weight, then lengthens to launch it forward again. At constant speed the body's mechanical energy returns, on average, to where it started each stride, so the net work the muscles do is surprisingly small. Much of the up-and-down is carried not by muscle but by springs — the elastic tendons and the arch of the foot, which stretch on landing and recoil on push-off (Fig. 1).

The bookkeeping is striking. In a moderate run the human Achilles tendon — the thick cord joining the calf muscles to the heel — stretches by a few percent and stores on the order of 35 J of elastic energy per step, returning about 90–93% of it in the push-off. The plantar fascia and the ligaments of the foot arch add roughly another 17 J. Together these passive springs supply a large share of the energy each stride would otherwise cost the muscles — which is why running is far more economical than its jolting appearance suggests.

Other animals make the point unmistakable. A hopping kangaroo's rate of oxygen consumption is almost independent of speed above a slow hop: it goes faster essentially for free, because its enormous ankle tendons act as a resonant spring, catching the landing energy and flinging it back. And in a classic experiment on running turkeys, the calf-muscle fibres were found to hold nearly constant length during stance — producing force but doing almost no work — while the tendon in series with them did the stretching and recoiling. The muscle behaved as a strut; the tendon was the spring.

The throughline of this module. A tendon is a spring. The interesting questions are: how good a spring (how much energy it stores, how much it gives back), why it is nonlinear, and what it buys the muscle pulling on it. Answering them turns the isolated motor of Module 5 into a motor-plus-spring that can act as a catapult.

This is exactly the piece the previous modules set aside. Module 5 built the muscle as a Hill-type actuator, but to keep its equations clean it made Assumption 7.1: the tendon is rigid. A rigid tendon stores no energy, and it forces the fibres and the tendon to move at the same speed — so it can generate force but can never produce the catapult above. Module 3, in turn, treated ligaments and tendons as rigid constraints — ideal ropes that transmit force but store nothing — hung on joints with fixed axes. Both are honest simplifications, and both are debts this module now repays.

We pay them in order, each section using the last. We begin with the material: the wavy crimp of collagen and the J-shaped stress–strain curve it produces (§1). That gives the tendon as a nonlinear spring (§2) and lets us compute the energy it stores and returns (§3). Then the payoff: place that spring in series with Module 5's muscle (§4) and the catapult — the fibre and tendon moving at different speeds — falls out of one force balance. Real tissue is not a perfect spring; it creeps, relaxes, and loses a little energy each cycle (viscoelasticity, §5), which the classical Maxwell / Kelvin–Voigt / standard-linear-solid models capture (§6) and which shows up as a hysteresis loop whose area is the energy lost (§7). Finally the other tissues: ligaments as one-sided constraints, fascia as force-transmission sheets, and the foot arch as a windlass (§8). Three simulations — tendon recoil, running energy storage, and a ligament constraint — close the build (§9), and §10 audits what remains idealized and sets the problems.

1. Collagen, crimp, and the J-shaped law

§0 asserted that a tendon is a spring. Before we can say how good a spring, we need to know what it is made of and why it does not behave like an ordinary steel one. The answer to both is collagen — a structural protein — arranged as a crimped rope. That crimp is the whole reason a tendon's stiffness is not constant but grows as you pull it, giving the characteristic J-shaped force curve that every later section builds on.

The material and its hierarchy

Collagen (here type I, the kind in tendon and ligament) is a protein secreted as a stiff, rod-like triple helix called tropocollagen. These rods stagger and bond end-to-side into fibrils, fibrils bundle into fibres, fibres into fascicles, and fascicles into the whole tendon — a rope of ropes across five or six levels of hierarchy (Fig. 2). What matters mechanically is that at rest the fibrils are not straight: they follow a regular planar zig-zag called crimp. Under a polarising microscope the crimp shows as light-and-dark bands; mechanically it is slack that must be pulled out before a fibril can carry tension.

Stress, strain, and the material modulus

To describe how the material responds to load, independent of how thick or long a given tendon is, we use two normalised quantities. Take a specimen of unloaded (rest) length $L_0$ and unloaded cross-sectional area $A_0$, and pull it with force $F$ so its length becomes $L$.

Definition 1 (stress, strain, modulus). The (engineering) stress is the force per unit rest area, $\sigma = F/A_0$ (units: pascals, $1\ \text{Pa}=1\ \text{N/m}^2$; tendons operate in the megapascal range, $1\ \text{MPa}=10^6\ \text{Pa}$). The strain is the fractional stretch, $\varepsilon = (L-L_0)/L_0$ (dimensionless, usually quoted in %). The tangent modulus (a material stiffness) is the local slope of the stress–strain curve, $E(\varepsilon) = \mathrm{d}\sigma/\mathrm{d}\varepsilon$. For an ordinary elastic solid $E$ is a constant, Young's modulus; for tendon it is not.

Plotting $\sigma$ against $\varepsilon$ for a tendon gives the red curve of Fig. 3. It has three distinct regions, and the shape — concave-up at first, then straight — is what earns it the name J-shaped.

  • Toe region ($\varepsilon \lesssim 3\%$): very compliant. A little force produces a lot of stretch, and the slope $E$ starts near zero and climbs. This is not the collagen stretching — it is the crimp straightening out.
  • Linear region ($3\% \lesssim \varepsilon \lesssim 8\%$): once the crimp is gone, load pulls directly on straight collagen and the slope levels off at a roughly constant value $E_{\text{lin}}\approx1.3\ \text{GPa}$.
  • Yield and rupture ($\varepsilon \gtrsim 8\%$): fibrils slip and tear; the tendon fails near $\varepsilon_f\approx9\%$ at a stress of order $100\ \text{MPa}$.

Why the toe is J-shaped: fibre recruitment

The toe is not a material property of a single fibril — a straightened fibril is nearly linear. It is a population effect. Different fibrils have slightly different amounts of crimp, so they straighten and begin to bear load at different strains. Let a fibril's crimp strain $\varepsilon_c$ be the strain at which its own slack is used up; across the population $\varepsilon_c$ is spread over some range. Only fibrils that are already straight (those with $\varepsilon_c \le \varepsilon$) carry any load — they are said to be recruited.

Definition 2 (recruited fraction). The recruited fraction $\Phi(\varepsilon)$ is the fraction of fibrils whose crimp is fully out at strain $\varepsilon$ — that is, the cumulative distribution of crimp strains, $\Phi(\varepsilon)=\Pr(\varepsilon_c \le \varepsilon)$. It runs from $\Phi=0$ (all crimped, no load path) to $\Phi=1$ (all straight, fully load-bearing).

If each recruited fibril contributes the same share of stiffness and slack fibrils contribute none, the material's tangent modulus is simply the full straight-collagen modulus scaled by the fraction currently pulling:

$$\boxed{\;E(\varepsilon) \;=\; E_{\text{lin}}\,\Phi(\varepsilon), \qquad \sigma(\varepsilon) \;=\; \int_0^{\varepsilon} E_{\text{lin}}\,\Phi(\varepsilon')\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon'\;}\tag{1.1}$$

The second equality is just the first integrated, since $\sigma=\int E\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon$ from $\varepsilon=0$ where $\sigma=0$. Equation (1.1) already explains the J: where few fibrils are recruited ($\Phi$ small) the slope is small; as recruitment completes ($\Phi\to1$) the slope rises to $E_{\text{lin}}$ and stays there. The dashed blue curve in Fig. 3 is exactly $\Phi(\varepsilon)$, and it reaches $1$ precisely where the toe ends.

To get a closed form, take the simplest honest distribution: crimp strains spread uniformly over $[0,\varepsilon^\ast]$, so $\Phi(\varepsilon)=\varepsilon/\varepsilon^\ast$ up to $\varepsilon^\ast$ and $\Phi=1$ thereafter, where $\varepsilon^\ast$ is the strain at which the last fibril straightens.

Proposition 1 (parabolic toe, linear thereafter). With a uniform crimp distribution on $[0,\varepsilon^\ast]$, $$\sigma(\varepsilon)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{E_{\text{lin}}}{2\varepsilon^\ast}\,\varepsilon^2, & 0\le\varepsilon\le\varepsilon^\ast \ \text{(toe: parabolic)}\\[1.2ex] \dfrac{E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon^\ast}{2}+E_{\text{lin}}(\varepsilon-\varepsilon^\ast), & \varepsilon\gt \varepsilon^\ast \ \text{(linear, slope }E_{\text{lin}}\text{)}.\end{cases}\tag{1.2}$$
For $\varepsilon\le\varepsilon^\ast$, substitute $\Phi(\varepsilon')=\varepsilon'/\varepsilon^\ast$ into (1.1): $$\sigma(\varepsilon)=\int_0^{\varepsilon} E_{\text{lin}}\,\frac{\varepsilon'}{\varepsilon^\ast}\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon' = \frac{E_{\text{lin}}}{\varepsilon^\ast}\cdot\frac{\varepsilon^2}{2}.$$ The toe is therefore a parabola, and its slope $E=E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon/\varepsilon^\ast$ rises linearly from $0$ to $E_{\text{lin}}$ — recovering $E(\varepsilon)=E_{\text{lin}}\Phi(\varepsilon)$ as it must. For $\varepsilon\gt \varepsilon^\ast$ every fibril is recruited, $\Phi=1$, so $\mathrm{d}\sigma/\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=E_{\text{lin}}$ is constant; integrating from the toe's end value $\sigma(\varepsilon^\ast)=E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon^\ast/2$ gives the linear branch. The two pieces share the same value and slope at $\varepsilon^\ast$, so the curve is smooth there. ∎

A check against real tendon. Put in the textbook numbers $E_{\text{lin}}\approx1.3\ \text{GPa}$, a toe ending at $\varepsilon^\ast\approx3\%$, and rupture near $\varepsilon_f\approx9\%$. The toe-end stress is $\sigma(\varepsilon^\ast)=E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon^\ast/2\approx1300\times0.03/2\approx20\ \text{MPa}$, and the rupture stress is $\sigma(\varepsilon_f)\approx20+1300\times0.06\approx98\ \text{MPa}$ — both squarely in the measured range for human tendon ($\sim50$–$100\ \text{MPa}$ at failure). The minimal recruitment model, with two parameters read off the crimp, reproduces the whole curve. A more realistic spread of crimp strains (rather than a sharp uniform cutoff) rounds the corner at $\varepsilon^\ast$ into the smooth exponential-looking toe seen in data, but the mechanism — progressive recruitment — is unchanged.

This J-shaped $\sigma(\varepsilon)$ is a material law: stress versus strain, independent of any particular tendon's size. The next section turns it into the structural law a joint actually feels — force $F$ versus elongation $x$ — and reads off the tendon's stiffness and compliance as a spring (§2).

2. The tendon as a nonlinear spring

The J-shaped law of §1 is a statement about the material: stress $\sigma$ against strain $\varepsilon$. A joint, however, does not feel stress and strain — it feels a force pulling on a bone and an elongation of the tendon. This section converts the material law into that structural one, $F$ against $x$, and reads off the two numbers that summarise any spring: its stiffness and its compliance.

From material to structural

Take a length of tendon with unloaded length $L_0$ and cross-sectional area $A_0$ (Fig. 4). Stress is force per area and strain is elongation per length, so the two conversions are immediate:

Definition 3 (structural force and elongation). The tension $F$ carried by the tendon and its elongation $x$ (the increase in length beyond $L_0$) are $F=\sigma A_0$ and $x=\varepsilon L_0$. Equivalently $\sigma=F/A_0$ and $\varepsilon=x/L_0$.

Substituting $\sigma=F/A_0$ and $\varepsilon=x/L_0$ into the material law $\sigma(\varepsilon)$ gives the structural law — the force the tendon pulls with when stretched by $x$:

$$\boxed{\;F(x) \;=\; A_0\,\sigma\!\left(\frac{x}{L_0}\right)\;}\tag{2.1}$$

It is the same J-shaped curve as $\sigma(\varepsilon)$, simply rescaled: stretched vertically by the area $A_0$ and horizontally by the rest length $L_0$. A thick, short tendon and a thin, long one made of the same collagen have the same $\sigma(\varepsilon)$ but very different $F(x)$.

Stiffness and compliance

A spring is any element whose force is a function of its elongation. The simplest is Hookean (linear): $F=kx$ with a constant $k$. A tendon is a spring, but not a Hookean one — its force rises faster than linearly at first (the toe). The local measure of how hard it resists is the slope of $F(x)$.

Definition 4 (stiffness, compliance). The stiffness is the slope of the force–elongation curve, $k(x)=\mathrm{d}F/\mathrm{d}x$ (units: newtons per metre, or N/mm in practice). Its reciprocal is the compliance $C=1/k$ (metres per newton) — how much the tendon gives per unit force. A stiff tendon has small compliance.

Differentiating the structural law (2.1) with the chain rule, and using the material tangent modulus $E(\varepsilon)=\mathrm{d}\sigma/\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=E_{\text{lin}}\Phi(\varepsilon)$ from §1:

$$\boxed{\;k(x)=\frac{\mathrm{d}F}{\mathrm{d}x}=A_0\,\frac{\mathrm{d}\sigma}{\mathrm{d}\varepsilon}\,\frac{\mathrm{d}\varepsilon}{\mathrm{d}x}=\frac{A_0}{L_0}\,E(\varepsilon)=\frac{A_0}{L_0}\,E_{\text{lin}}\,\Phi(\varepsilon),\qquad k_{\text{lin}}=\frac{A_0 E_{\text{lin}}}{L_0}\;}\tag{2.2}$$

Structural stiffness is therefore the material modulus scaled by the geometric factor $A_0/L_0$, and — because of the recruited fraction $\Phi$ — it grows as the tendon is stretched, from near zero in the toe to the constant plateau $k_{\text{lin}}$ once every fibril is recruited. The dashed blue curve of Fig. 5 is exactly this $k(x)$; the red curve is $F(x)$.

Proposition 2 (structural law, uniform crimp). With the uniform-crimp model of §1 (toe ending at elongation $x^\ast=\varepsilon^\ast L_0$), $$F(x)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{k_{\text{lin}}}{2x^\ast}\,x^2, & 0\le x\le x^\ast\ \text{(toe)}\\[1.2ex] k_{\text{lin}}\!\left(x-\tfrac{1}{2}x^\ast\right), & x\gt x^\ast\ \text{(linear, stiffness }k_{\text{lin}}\text{)}.\end{cases}\tag{2.3}$$
Multiply the material law (1.2) by $A_0$ and use $\varepsilon=x/L_0$, $\varepsilon^\ast=x^\ast/L_0$. In the toe, $F=A_0\sigma=A_0\dfrac{E_{\text{lin}}}{2\varepsilon^\ast}\varepsilon^2=A_0\dfrac{E_{\text{lin}}}{2(x^\ast/L_0)}\dfrac{x^2}{L_0^2}=\dfrac{A_0 E_{\text{lin}}}{L_0}\dfrac{x^2}{2x^\ast}=\dfrac{k_{\text{lin}}}{2x^\ast}x^2$, using $k_{\text{lin}}=A_0E_{\text{lin}}/L_0$ from (2.2). Beyond the toe $\Phi=1$, so $k=k_{\text{lin}}$ is constant and $F$ is linear; integrating from the toe-end value $F(x^\ast)=\tfrac12 k_{\text{lin}}x^\ast$ gives the second line. ∎

A tendon in numbers. For a human Achilles tendon take $A_0\approx80\ \text{mm}^2$, $L_0\approx250\ \text{mm}$, and $E_{\text{lin}}\approx1.3\ \text{GPa}$. Then the linear-region stiffness is $$k_{\text{lin}}=\frac{A_0 E_{\text{lin}}}{L_0}=\frac{(80\times10^{-6}\,\text{m}^2)(1.3\times10^{9}\,\text{Pa})}{0.25\ \text{m}}\approx4.2\times10^{5}\ \text{N/m}=416\ \text{N/mm},$$ in the measured range for the free Achilles ($\sim150$–$400\ \text{N/mm}$, varying with training and how much compliant aponeurosis is included). The toe ends at $x^\ast=\varepsilon^\ast L_0\approx7.5\ \text{mm}$ and a tension $F(x^\ast)\approx1.6\ \text{kN}$; rupture is near $x_f\approx22.5\ \text{mm}$ and $F_f\approx7.8\ \text{kN}$ — consistent with the $\sigma_f\approx100\ \text{MPa}$ of §1 ($\sigma_f A_0\approx7.8\ \text{kN}$).

Hookean where it works: the operating point

Although the tendon is nonlinear across its whole range, it does most of its working life in a narrow band. During walking and running the Achilles strains only a few percent — roughly $2$–$5\%$, or $x\approx5$–$12\ \text{mm}$ — which sits in the linear region, past the toe (the shaded band in Fig. 5). There the stiffness is essentially the constant $k_{\text{lin}}$, and the tendon behaves as a nearly Hookean spring, $F\approx k_{\text{lin}}(x-\tfrac12 x^\ast)$.

The toe is not wasted, though: at low loads it makes the tendon compliant, a soft cushion that takes up slack and smooths the onset of tension before the stiff working region engages. This split — a soft toe for low loads, a stiff near-Hookean region for working loads — is why later sections can often model the active tendon with a single stiffness $k_{\text{lin}}$ while remembering that the true spring is nonlinear.

With the tendon now characterised as a spring, we can ask the question §0 promised: how much energy does it store when stretched, and how much does it give back? That is the subject of §3.

3. Elastic energy: storage and return

A spring is useful because stretching it stores energy that it later gives back. This is the mechanism behind the running economy of §0: the Achilles and the arch bank the energy of each landing and return it at push-off, so the muscles need supply only what is lost. Here we compute how much a tendon stores, how much comes back, and — the design question — why a compliant spring stores so much for the force it carries.

Stored energy is the area under the force curve

Stretching the tendon a further $\mathrm{d}x$ against its tension $F$ takes work $F\,\mathrm{d}x$, and (for an elastic spring) that work is stored, not lost. Summing from the unstretched state gives the elastic strain energy $U$ (units: joules, $\text{J}=\text{N·m}$):

$$\boxed{\;U(x) \;=\; \int_0^{x} F(x')\,\mathrm{d}x'\;}\tag{3.1}$$

Geometrically, $U$ is the area under the force–elongation curve $F(x)$ up to the stretch $x$ (Fig. 6, shaded). Because $F$ rises with $x$, energy accumulates faster and faster as the tendon is stretched — the running total $U(x)$ (the green curve of Fig. 6) is steepest where the force is largest.

The Hookean case, and the tendon's

For an ideal Hookean spring, $F=kx$, the integral is elementary and gives the textbook result — worth stating in its three equivalent forms, since each answers a different question:

$$\boxed{\;U=\int_0^x kx'\,\mathrm{d}x'=\tfrac12 k x^2=\tfrac12 F x=\frac{F^2}{2k}\;}\tag{3.2}$$

(This is the quantity often written $E=\tfrac12kx^2$; we call it $U$ to keep the symbol $E$ for the elastic modulus of §1.)

The real tendon is not Hookean in the toe, so we integrate its actual structural law (Eq. 2.3) instead:

Proposition 3 (stored energy of the nonlinear tendon). With the uniform-crimp law of §2, $$U(x)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{k_{\text{lin}}}{6x^\ast}\,x^3, & 0\le x\le x^\ast\ \text{(toe)}\\[1.4ex] k_{\text{lin}}\!\left(\tfrac12 x^2-\tfrac12 x^\ast x+\tfrac{1}{6}(x^\ast)^2\right), & x\gt x^\ast.\end{cases}\tag{3.3}$$ For $x\gg x^\ast$ this approaches the Hookean $\tfrac12 k_{\text{lin}}x^2$, reduced slightly by the compliant toe.
In the toe, integrate the toe branch of (2.3): $U=\displaystyle\int_0^x \frac{k_{\text{lin}}}{2x^\ast}x'^2\,\mathrm{d}x'=\frac{k_{\text{lin}}}{2x^\ast}\frac{x^3}{3}=\frac{k_{\text{lin}}}{6x^\ast}x^3$, so the toe stores $U(x^\ast)=\tfrac16 k_{\text{lin}}(x^\ast)^2$. Beyond the toe, add the integral of the linear branch $F=k_{\text{lin}}(x'-\tfrac12 x^\ast)$: $$U(x)=U(x^\ast)+\int_{x^\ast}^{x}k_{\text{lin}}\!\left(x'-\tfrac12 x^\ast\right)\mathrm{d}x'=\tfrac16 k_{\text{lin}}(x^\ast)^2+k_{\text{lin}}\!\left(\tfrac12 x^2-\tfrac12 x^\ast x\right),$$ since the bracket vanishes at the lower limit $x'=x^\ast$. Collecting terms gives (3.3). For $x\gg x^\ast$ the linear term $\tfrac12 k_{\text{lin}}x^2$ dominates. ∎

The Achilles energy budget. Take the numbers of §2 ($k_{\text{lin}}=416\ \text{N/mm}$, $x^\ast=7.5\ \text{mm}$). At a hard running push-off the Achilles stretches to about $x\approx16\ \text{mm}$ (a strain of $\sim6.4\%$) at a tension of $F\approx5\ \text{kN}$; Eq. (3.3) then gives

$$U(16\ \text{mm})\approx k_{\text{lin}}\!\left(\tfrac12 x^2-\tfrac12 x^\ast x+\tfrac16 (x^\ast)^2\right)\approx 32\ \text{J},$$

matching the $\sim35\ \text{J}$ per step quoted in §0. Notice how little of this lives in the toe: $U(x^\ast)=\tfrac16 k_{\text{lin}}(x^\ast)^2\approx3.9\ \text{J}$, under $15\%$ of the total, because the force is small there. Almost all of the stored energy sits in the stiff linear region where both force and stretch are large.

Storage and return: resilience

An ideal elastic spring gives back everything it stores. A real tendon gives back most of it. The fraction returned on unloading is the resilience:

Definition 5 (resilience). The resilience $R=U_{\text{returned}}/U_{\text{stored}}$ is the fraction of stored elastic energy recovered when the tendon unloads. For tendon $R\approx0.90$–$0.93$; the missing $7$–$10\%$ is dissipated as heat.

So of the $\sim32\ \text{J}$ banked each running step, roughly $29\ \text{J}$ returns to help launch the next stride and only $\sim3\ \text{J}$ is lost — which is exactly why tendon-driven bouncing is so cheap. Where that small loss comes from — the fact that the loading and unloading paths do not quite coincide — is viscoelastic dissipation, whose mechanism we develop in §5 and quantify as a hysteresis loop in §7. For now the spring is very nearly ideal, and we treat it so.

Why a spring, not a strut: energy at fixed force

The three forms of (3.2) contain a design principle. The muscle and body set the peak force a tendon must carry — a few times body weight, whatever the activity demands. At a given peak force $F$, the energy a spring banks is $U=F^2/(2k)$: it is larger for a more compliant (smaller-$k$) spring. Figure 7 makes this concrete — two springs pulled to the same peak force enclose triangles of area $\tfrac12 Fx$, and the compliant one, stretching further, encloses far more.

This is why a tendon is a slender, compliant cord rather than a rigid strut: for the force it must transmit anyway, compliance turns it into a substantial energy store. A perfectly rigid tendon ($k\to\infty$) would store nothing ($U=F^2/2k\to0$) — precisely the rigid-tendon assumption of Module 5 that stored no energy. The cost of compliance is elongation: a softer tendon stretches more for the same force, so fibre and tendon end up moving at different speeds. That difference is not a defect — it is the very thing that lets the muscle act as a catapult, which is where §4 begins.

4. The series-elastic element

This is the section the whole module has been building toward, and the one that repays Module 5. Module 5 built the muscle as a Hill-type actuator but, to keep its equations clean, froze the tendon (its Assumption 7.1: a rigid tendon). We now put the spring of §2–§3 back in. The muscle and its tendon form one muscle–tendon unit (MTU): the contractile element (CE) — the active muscle fibres of Module 5 — in series with the series-elastic element (SE) — the tendon (Fig. 8).

The kinematic constraint

Let $L_{\text{MTU}}$ be the whole unit's length — the quantity the joint sets directly, through the joint angle (it is the model's input). Let $\ell$ be the fibre length (the CE length) and $v=\mathrm{d}\ell/\mathrm{d}t$ the fibre velocity; let $L_T$ be the tendon length, $L_{Ts}$ its slack length, and $x=L_T-L_{Ts}$ the tendon stretch of §2. The fibres pull along a line set at the pennation angle $\theta_p$ of Module 5, so only their tendon-directed projection $\ell\cos\theta_p$ adds to the tendon length. The three lengths are tied by one constraint:

$$\boxed{\;L_{\text{MTU}} \;=\; \ell\cos\theta_p \;+\; L_T \;=\; \ell\cos\theta_p + L_{Ts} + x\;}\tag{4.1}$$

The force balance, and why it is implicit

Because the CE and the tendon are in series, they carry the same tension. The tendon's tension is the fibre force projected onto the tendon line, so the Hill force of Module 5 must equal the tendon-spring force of §2:

$$\boxed{\;F_T(x)\;=\;\Big[\,a(t)\,F_{\max}\,f_L(\ell)\,f_V(v)\;+\;F_{PE}(\ell)\,\Big]\cos\theta_p\;}\tag{4.2}$$

where, from Module 5, $a(t)$ is the activation, $F_{\max}$ the architectural force ceiling, $f_L(\ell)$ the length factor, $f_V(v)$ the force–velocity factor, and $F_{PE}(\ell)$ the passive-fibre force; $F_T(x)$ is the nonlinear tendon curve of Eq. (2.1). The left side depends on the tendon stretch $x$; the right side depends on the fibre length $\ell$ and its velocity $v=\mathrm{d}\ell/\mathrm{d}t$.

Here is the whole difference from Module 5. With a rigid tendon, $x\equiv0$, so (4.1) gives the fibre length outright, $\ell=(L_{\text{MTU}}-L_{Ts})/\cos\theta_p$, and its velocity by differentiation — the joint angle dictates the fibre state, and (4.2) is never needed (Module 5's clean, explicit model). With a compliant tendon, $x$ is unknown: it is coupled to $\ell$ through the constraint (4.1) and to $F_T$ through the tendon curve, while $F_T$ depends on $v$ through $f_V$. The joint no longer fixes the fibre — the fibre length $\ell$ becomes an independent state variable whose motion is set implicitly by the balance (4.2).

Solving it: one inversion per timestep

The balance looks circular, but it unwinds into an explicit ODE. At any instant the current $\ell$ and the known $L_{\text{MTU}}$ fix the tendon stretch through (4.1), $x=L_{\text{MTU}}-\ell\cos\theta_p-L_{Ts}$, hence the tendon force $F_T(x)$. Rearranging (4.2) isolates the one velocity-dependent factor,

$$f_V(v)=\frac{F_T(x)/\cos\theta_p-F_{PE}(\ell)}{a\,F_{\max}\,f_L(\ell)},$$

and because the force–velocity curve is monotonic it can be inverted for the fibre velocity, giving the state equation the labs integrate:

$$\boxed{\;\frac{\mathrm{d}\ell}{\mathrm{d}t}=v=f_V^{-1}\!\left(\frac{F_T(x)/\cos\theta_p-F_{PE}(\ell)}{a\,F_{\max}\,f_L(\ell)}\right),\qquad x=L_{\text{MTU}}-\ell\cos\theta_p-L_{Ts}\;}\tag{4.3}$$

This is the technical heart of the series-elastic model: one algebraic inversion of $f_V$ per timestep supplies $\mathrm{d}\ell/\mathrm{d}t$, which is then integrated (a differential–algebraic system; the initial $\ell$ is found by a one-off root-solve of the equilibrium). §9 implements exactly this.

Proposition 4 (the rigid tendon is the stiff limit). As the tendon stiffness $k\to\infty$, the series-elastic model (4.1)–(4.3) reduces to Module 5's rigid-tendon model.
The tendon force is $F_T=F_T(x)$ with slope $k$; carrying any finite tension $F_T$ requires stretch $x=F_T/k\to0$ as $k\to\infty$. Then the constraint (4.1) collapses to $L_{\text{MTU}}=\ell\cos\theta_p+L_{Ts}$, i.e. $\ell=(L_{\text{MTU}}-L_{Ts})/\cos\theta_p$ — the fibre length is fixed by the joint, exactly Module 5's rigid-tendon kinematics, and (4.2) reduces to that module's force law. The compliant model therefore contains the rigid one as its infinite-stiffness limit. ∎

The catapult: fibre and tendon at different speeds

Differentiating the constraint (4.1) in time (with $\theta_p$ nearly constant) splits the whole unit's velocity between the fibre and the tendon:

$$\dot L_{\text{MTU}}=\underbrace{\dot\ell\,\cos\theta_p}_{\text{fibre}}+\underbrace{\dot x}_{\text{tendon}}\tag{4.4}$$

The two need not be equal — and in fast movements they are wildly unequal. Figure 9 tracks a stance phase: during a slow loading the fibre shortens at high force, winding energy into the tendon; during the fast push-off the compliant tendon recoils, supplying almost all of the rapid shortening while the fibre stays nearly isometric ($\dot\ell\approx0$) at high force. In the simulation the tendon recoils about six times faster than the fibre shortens.

This is the catapult, and it is a statement about power. Power is $F\dot L$; the muscle winds the spring slowly (small $\dot\ell$, so modest fibre power) and the spring releases fast (large $\dot x$), delivering power to the joint at a rate the fibre could never reach on its own — the mechanism behind a locust's jump, a turkey's economical run, and a human sprint. It also lets the fibre stay in the low-velocity, high-force part of its force–velocity curve (Module 5), where it is both strong and efficient, even as the joint whips through a large, fast excursion.

The debt, repaid

The rigid tendon was the last big idealization of the Hill model, and this section settles it on all three counts Module 5 listed: elastic energy storage and return (§3), the fibre–tendon speed difference (Fig. 9 and Eq. 4.4), and the implicit CE–SE force balance (the inversion of Eq. 4.3). What remains is a smaller correction: a real tendon is not a perfect spring — it loses the few percent of resilience noted in §3, because its loading and unloading paths do not quite coincide. That is viscoelasticity, and it is the subject of §5.

5. Viscoelasticity: creep, relaxation, hysteresis

So far the tendon has been a perfect spring: its force depends only on how far it is stretched right now — instantaneous, path-independent, lossless. That is the idealization §3 flagged when it noted a resilience of $90$–$93\%$ rather than $100\%$. A real tendon's force depends also on the history and the rate of loading. A material that combines elastic (spring-like, energy-storing) and viscous (fluid-like, energy-dissipating) character is viscoelastic. This section names the four ways that shows up, and introduces the two ideal elements from which §6 will build quantitative models.

Four signatures of viscoelasticity

Each hallmark is a simple experiment — hold one quantity fixed and watch the other drift:

  1. Creep. Hold the stress constant and the strain keeps growing: the material slowly gives way under a steady load (Fig. 10, left).
  2. Stress relaxation. Hold the strain constant and the stress decays: the material slowly relents at a fixed stretch (Fig. 10, right).
  3. Rate-dependent stiffness. Load faster and the material responds stiffer — a higher stress for the same strain. A tendon pulled quickly feels harder than one pulled slowly.
  4. Hysteresis. The loading and unloading curves do not coincide; they enclose a loop whose area is the energy lost to heat each cycle (developed fully in §7).

An ideal spring shows none of these: load it and it responds instantly, unload it and it returns exactly, at any rate, with no loop. A tendon shows all four — mildly, which is why it is a good but not perfect energy store.

The two building blocks: spring and dashpot

Every linear viscoelastic model is assembled from just two ideal elements, one purely elastic and one purely viscous (Fig. 11).

Definition 6 (spring and dashpot). The (Hookean) spring is a purely elastic element whose stress is proportional to strain, $$\boxed{\;\sigma=E\,\varepsilon\;}\tag{5.1}$$ with $E$ the elastic modulus of §1. The (Newtonian) dashpot — a piston moving through a viscous fluid — is a purely viscous element whose stress is proportional to the strain rate, $$\boxed{\;\sigma=\eta\,\dot\varepsilon\;}\tag{5.2}$$ where $\dot\varepsilon=\mathrm{d}\varepsilon/\mathrm{d}t$ and $\eta$ is the viscosity (units: pascal-seconds, $\text{Pa·s}$).

The two elements are opposites in every way that matters:

A spring alone has no time-dependence — no creep, no relaxation, no hysteresis. A dashpot alone has no equilibrium — under a held load it never stops creeping, and it stores nothing. A real tendon is neither: it has an instantaneous elastic response (spring-like), time-dependent drift (dashpot-like), and — crucially — bounded creep and relaxation that settle to a new equilibrium. Capturing all three at once takes a combination of springs and dashpots.

The interesting question is which combination. Put a spring and a dashpot in series and you get the Maxwell model; in parallel, the Kelvin–Voigt model; add a second spring and you get the standard linear solid. Each captures some of the tendon's behaviour and misses the rest — the subject of §6.

6. Maxwell, Kelvin–Voigt, and the standard linear solid

With the spring and dashpot of §5 in hand, we can assemble models. Each arrangement is fixed by one rule: elements in series carry the same stress and their strains add; elements in parallel share the same strain and their stresses add. Applying that rule to three arrangements (Fig. 12) yields three constitutive laws — differential equations linking stress $\sigma$ and strain $\varepsilon$ — and each equation tells us directly how the model creeps and relaxes.

Why series shares stress and parallel shares strain

The rule looks like an arbitrary convention, but each half of it follows from a different piece of physics, and there are only two principles at work. Series and parallel simply swap which principle governs which variable.

The two principles. Equilibrium (force balance): at any massless junction, the forces acting on it must sum to zero (Newton's second law with $ma=0$). Compatibility (kinematics): displacements must fit together geometrically — lengths add along a path, and parts that share endpoints must move together.

Treat each element as a little bar that transmits a force — its stress $\sigma=F/A$ — and stretches by some amount — its strain $\varepsilon=\Delta L/L$. Figure 13 draws the two arrangements as free bodies; we read the rule off each.

Series — elements end-to-end (a chain)

Same stress — this is equilibrium. Consider the junction where element 1 meets element 2 (Fig. 13, left). That junction is massless, so Newton's second law there reads $\sum F = m\,a = 0$. Element 1 pulls the junction one way with tension $F_1$, and element 2 pulls it the other way with $F_2$; balance forces $F_1=F_2$. Each end of the chain, meanwhile, feels the externally applied force $F$. So one and the same force is transmitted straight through the chain, $F_1=F_2=F$, and (at equal cross-section) the same stress sits in both elements: $\sigma_1=\sigma_2$.

Strains add — this is compatibility. The elements are concatenated head-to-tail, so the total length is $L=L_1+L_2$ and the total stretch is the sum of the individual stretches, $\Delta L=\Delta L_1+\Delta L_2$. Elongations along a line simply accumulate, so the strains add: $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2$.

Parallel — elements side-by-side (between rigid crossbars)

Same strain — this is compatibility. Both elements are pinned between the same two rigid crossbars (Fig. 13, right). When the crossbars separate by $\Delta L$, each element spans exactly that gap, so each is stretched by the same $\Delta L$. Sharing endpoints forces the elements to share their stretch: $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_1=\varepsilon_2$.

Stresses add — this is equilibrium. Now do a force balance on one crossbar. The external pull $F$ on the bar is resisted by the two branch tensions, so $F=F_1+F_2$: the branches split the load between them. At equal cross-section the stresses therefore add, $\sigma=\sigma_1+\sigma_2$.

The one idea underneath

Everything above is two statements applied twice. At a shared node, the forces balance (equilibrium); along a shared path or gap, the displacements are compatible (kinematics). Series and parallel differ only in what is shared: a series chain shares the load path, so the force is common and the displacements add; a parallel pair shares its endpoints, so the displacement is common and the forces add. Because stress and strain are conjugate variables (their product is an energy density), the rule always pins one of them by equilibrium and the other by geometry — you can never impose both conditions on the same variable.

If it helps, this is the exact mechanical twin of resistor circuits. Series resistors carry the same current (the analogue of force) and their voltages add (the analogue of elongation); parallel resistors share the same voltage (the analogue of strain) and their currents add (the analogue of force). Force behaves like current, stretch like voltage — and the series/parallel bookkeeping is identical in both worlds.

One assumption is doing quiet work here: the elements and their junctions are taken to be massless, so the force balance carries no $ma$ term and the same force really does pass undiminished through a series chain. That is the quasi-static regime — inertia neglected — which is exactly where these lumped viscoelastic models are meant to live. With the rule now justified, the constitutive laws follow by reading off the shared variable, summing the conjugate one, and substituting the element laws $\sigma=E\varepsilon$ and $\sigma=\eta\dot\varepsilon$.

Maxwell: spring and dashpot in series

The first arrangement chains the two elements end to end.

Proposition 5 (Maxwell law). A Hookean spring (modulus $E_M$) and a Newtonian dashpot (viscosity $\eta$) in series obey $$\boxed{\;\dot\varepsilon=\frac{\dot\sigma}{E_M}+\frac{\sigma}{\eta}\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \dot\sigma+\frac{E_M}{\eta}\,\sigma=E_M\,\dot\varepsilon\;}\tag{6.1}$$
By the series rule (Fig. 13) the spring and dashpot carry the same stress $\sigma$, and their strains add, $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_s+\varepsilon_d$ (subscript $s$ for the spring, $d$ for the dashpot). The spring law $\sigma=E_M\varepsilon_s$ gives $\varepsilon_s=\sigma/E_M$, and differentiating, $\dot\varepsilon_s=\dot\sigma/E_M$. The dashpot law $\sigma=\eta\dot\varepsilon_d$ gives $\dot\varepsilon_d=\sigma/\eta$. Differentiating the strain sum and substituting the two rates, $$\dot\varepsilon=\dot\varepsilon_s+\dot\varepsilon_d=\frac{\dot\sigma}{E_M}+\frac{\sigma}{\eta},$$ which is the first form of (6.1). Multiplying by $E_M$ and moving the stress terms to the left gives the equivalent form $\dot\sigma+(E_M/\eta)\sigma=E_M\dot\varepsilon$. ∎

Relaxation (hold strain, $\dot\varepsilon=0$): $\dot\sigma=-(E_M/\eta)\sigma$, so $\sigma(t)=\sigma_0\,e^{-t/\tau}$ with $\tau=\eta/E_M$ — the stress decays all the way to zero. Creep (hold stress, $\dot\sigma=0$): $\dot\varepsilon=\sigma/\eta$, so $\varepsilon(t)=\sigma/E_M+(\sigma/\eta)\,t$ — the strain grows linearly forever. Maxwell relaxes fully but creeps without bound: it behaves like a fluid, not a tendon.

Kelvin–Voigt: spring and dashpot in parallel

The second arrangement sets the two elements side by side.

Proposition 6 (Kelvin–Voigt law). A spring (modulus $E_K$) and a dashpot (viscosity $\eta$) in parallel obey $$\boxed{\;\sigma=E_K\,\varepsilon+\eta\,\dot\varepsilon\;}\tag{6.2}$$
By the parallel rule (Fig. 13) the spring and dashpot share the same strain $\varepsilon$, and their stresses add, $\sigma=\sigma_s+\sigma_d$. The spring contributes $\sigma_s=E_K\varepsilon$ and the dashpot $\sigma_d=\eta\dot\varepsilon$; summing the two stresses gives $\sigma=E_K\varepsilon+\eta\dot\varepsilon$, which is (6.2). ∎

Creep (hold stress $\sigma$): solving $E_K\varepsilon+\eta\dot\varepsilon=\sigma$ gives $\varepsilon(t)=(\sigma/E_K)\big(1-e^{-t/\tau}\big)$ with $\tau=\eta/E_K$ — bounded creep, settling at $\sigma/E_K$. Relaxation (hold strain, $\dot\varepsilon=0$): $\sigma=E_K\varepsilon$ is constant — there is no relaxation. Worse, a sudden step of strain would demand an infinite instantaneous stress (the $\eta\dot\varepsilon$ term), so the model has no instantaneous elastic response. Kelvin–Voigt fixes creep but loses both relaxation and the instant give of a real tendon.

The standard linear solid

Neither one-spring model is a tendon: Maxwell fails creep, Kelvin–Voigt fails relaxation and the instantaneous response. Adding a second spring resolves both. The standard linear solid (SLS, or Zener model) places an equilibrium spring $E_0$ in parallel with a Maxwell arm — a spring $E_1$ and dashpot $\eta$ in series (Fig. 12, bottom).

Proposition 7 (the standard-linear-solid law). The SLS obeys $$\boxed{\;\tau_\sigma\,\dot\sigma+\sigma=E_R\big(\varepsilon+\tau_\varepsilon\,\dot\varepsilon\big)\;}\tag{6.3}$$ with relaxed modulus $E_R=E_0$, instantaneous modulus $E_{\text{inst}}=E_0+E_1$, relaxation time $\tau_\sigma=\eta/E_1$, and creep time $\tau_\varepsilon=\tau_\sigma\,E_{\text{inst}}/E_R\gt \tau_\sigma$.
The total stress is the sum of the two parallel branches, $\sigma=\sigma_0+\sigma_1$, with $\sigma_0=E_0\varepsilon$ (equilibrium spring). The Maxwell arm obeys (6.1), $\dot\sigma_1+(E_1/\eta)\sigma_1=E_1\dot\varepsilon$. Substitute $\sigma_1=\sigma-E_0\varepsilon$ and $\dot\sigma_1=\dot\sigma-E_0\dot\varepsilon$: $$\dot\sigma-E_0\dot\varepsilon+\frac{E_1}{\eta}\big(\sigma-E_0\varepsilon\big)=E_1\dot\varepsilon\;\Longrightarrow\;\dot\sigma+\frac{E_1}{\eta}\sigma=(E_0+E_1)\dot\varepsilon+\frac{E_0E_1}{\eta}\varepsilon.$$ Write $\tau_\sigma=\eta/E_1$ so $E_1/\eta=1/\tau_\sigma$, and note $E_0E_1/\eta=E_0/\tau_\sigma=E_R/\tau_\sigma$; multiplying through by $\tau_\sigma$ gives $\tau_\sigma\dot\sigma+\sigma=(E_0+E_1)\tau_\sigma\dot\varepsilon+E_0\varepsilon=E_R\varepsilon+E_{\text{inst}}\tau_\sigma\dot\varepsilon$. Setting $\tau_\varepsilon=\tau_\sigma E_{\text{inst}}/E_R$ makes $E_{\text{inst}}\tau_\sigma=E_R\tau_\varepsilon$, which is (6.3). ∎

The two exponential responses follow by solving the linear ODE (6.3):

  • Relaxation (strain step $\varepsilon_0$): $\sigma(t)=E_R\varepsilon_0+(E_{\text{inst}}-E_R)\varepsilon_0\,e^{-t/\tau_\sigma}$ — an instantaneous jump to $E_{\text{inst}}\varepsilon_0$, then decay to the nonzero plateau $E_R\varepsilon_0$.
  • Creep (stress step $\sigma_0$): $\varepsilon(t)=\dfrac{\sigma_0}{E_{\text{inst}}}+\sigma_0\!\left(\dfrac{1}{E_R}-\dfrac{1}{E_{\text{inst}}}\right)\!\big(1-e^{-t/\tau_\varepsilon}\big)$ — an instant jump to $\sigma_0/E_{\text{inst}}$, then bounded creep to $\sigma_0/E_R$.

The SLS has all three behaviours a tendon shows: an instantaneous elastic response, bounded creep, and relaxation to a nonzero plateau. Figure 14 puts the three models side by side; only the bottom row matches a tendon.

7. Hysteresis loops and dissipated energy

§3 found a resilience of $90$–$93\%$, not $100\%$, and §5–§6 located the lost few percent: because a viscoelastic material's loading and unloading paths do not coincide, some energy becomes heat. This section makes that precise. Under cyclic loading the stress–strain curve traces a closed loop, and the loop's enclosed area is the energy dissipated per cycle. We read it off the SLS of §6, find that it is strongly rate-dependent, and see why that rate dependence is exactly what makes a tendon a good spring when it is loaded fast.

The loop is the dissipation

Load and then unload an ideal elastic spring and it retraces a single line — every joule put in comes back out. A viscoelastic material's stress lags its strain (the creep and relaxation of §5), so on unloading the stress at a given strain is lower than it was on loading. Loading and unloading therefore follow different curves and enclose a loop (Fig. 15). The work done per unit volume on loading is the area under the loading curve, $\int\sigma\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon$; the work recovered on unloading is the smaller area under the unloading curve; their difference is the enclosed area:

$$\boxed{\;W_d=\oint \sigma\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon \;=\;\text{area of the hysteresis loop}\;}\tag{7.1}$$

the energy dissipated as heat per cycle, per unit volume. The resilience of §3 is the complement, $R=1-W_d/W_{\text{in}}$, where $W_{\text{in}}$ is the work put in during loading. A perfect spring has $W_d=0$ and $R=1$; a real tendon loses a little each cycle.

Sinusoidal loading and the loss angle

To compute the area, drive the material sinusoidally at angular frequency $\omega$: $\varepsilon(t)=\varepsilon_0\sin\omega t$. For a linear viscoelastic material the steady-state stress is a sinusoid of the same frequency but shifted in phase, $\sigma(t)=\sigma_0\sin(\omega t+\delta)$, where $\delta$ is the loss angle — the phase by which stress leads strain. The lag $\delta$ is the whole story:

Proposition 8 (loop area of a linear material). Under $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_0\sin\omega t$ and $\sigma=\sigma_0\sin(\omega t+\delta)$, the energy dissipated per cycle is $$\boxed{\;W_d=\oint\sigma\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=\pi\,\sigma_0\varepsilon_0\sin\delta\;}\tag{7.2}$$
Write the cyclic integral as a time integral over one period $\tau=2\pi/\omega$, using $\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=\dot\varepsilon\,\mathrm{d}t=\varepsilon_0\omega\cos\omega t\,\mathrm{d}t$: $$W_d=\int_0^{\tau}\sigma\,\dot\varepsilon\,\mathrm{d}t=\sigma_0\varepsilon_0\omega\int_0^{\tau}\sin(\omega t+\delta)\cos\omega t\,\mathrm{d}t.$$ Expand $\sin(\omega t+\delta)=\sin\omega t\cos\delta+\cos\omega t\sin\delta$. Over a full period $\int\sin\omega t\cos\omega t\,\mathrm{d}t=0$ and $\int\cos^2\omega t\,\mathrm{d}t=\tau/2=\pi/\omega$, so only the $\sin\delta$ term survives: $W_d=\sigma_0\varepsilon_0\omega\sin\delta\cdot(\pi/\omega)=\pi\sigma_0\varepsilon_0\sin\delta$. ∎

When $\delta=0$ (a pure spring, stress in phase with strain) the loop collapses to a line and $W_d=0$; the fatter the loop, the larger $\delta$. The dimensionless measure of dissipation is the loss tangent $\tan\delta=E''/E'$, the ratio of the loss modulus $E''$ (the out-of-phase, dissipative part of the stress response) to the storage modulus $E'$ (the in-phase, elastic part). Both come from one complex number.

The standard linear solid is rate-dependent

How large is $\delta$ for a tendon? It depends on how fast you load — and the SLS says exactly how. Drive Eq. (6.3) with a complex exponential $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_0 e^{i\omega t}$ and write $\sigma=E^\ast(\omega)\,\varepsilon$; the ratio $E^\ast$ is the complex modulus.

Proposition 9 (SLS complex modulus and its loss peak). The standard linear solid (6.3) has $$\boxed{\;E^\ast(\omega)=E_R\,\frac{1+i\omega\tau_\varepsilon}{1+i\omega\tau_\sigma},\qquad \tan\delta(\omega)=\frac{\omega(\tau_\varepsilon-\tau_\sigma)}{1+\omega^2\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon}\;}\tag{7.3}$$ The loss vanishes at both extremes — $\tan\delta\to0$ as $\omega\to0$ (elastic at the relaxed modulus $E_R$) and as $\omega\to\infty$ (elastic at the instantaneous modulus $E_{\text{inst}}$) — and peaks at the single frequency $\omega^\ast=1/\sqrt{\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon}$.
Substituting $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_0 e^{i\omega t}$ (so $\dot\varepsilon=i\omega\varepsilon$) and $\sigma=E^\ast\varepsilon$, $\dot\sigma=i\omega E^\ast\varepsilon$, into $\tau_\sigma\dot\sigma+\sigma=E_R(\varepsilon+\tau_\varepsilon\dot\varepsilon)$ and cancelling $\varepsilon$: $E^\ast(i\omega\tau_\sigma+1)=E_R(1+i\omega\tau_\varepsilon)$, which is the boxed $E^\ast$. Rationalising, its real and imaginary parts are $E'=E_R\dfrac{1+\omega^2\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon}{1+\omega^2\tau_\sigma^2}$ and $E''=E_R\dfrac{\omega(\tau_\varepsilon-\tau_\sigma)}{1+\omega^2\tau_\sigma^2}$, so $\tan\delta=E''/E'$ is the boxed expression. Its limits are $\tan\delta\to0$ at $\omega\to0$ and $\omega\to\infty$ (numerator grows as $\omega$, denominator as $\omega^2$). Setting $\mathrm{d}(\tan\delta)/\mathrm{d}\omega=0$ gives $1-\omega^2\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon=0$, i.e. $\omega^\ast=1/\sqrt{\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon}$. ∎

Figure 16 plots $\tan\delta(\omega)$: near zero when loading is very slow or very fast, a hump in between. The two extremes are the elastic limits of §6 — slow loading lets the material fully relax each cycle (reversible, at $E_R$); fast loading gives it no time to relax (reversible, at $E_{\text{inst}}$). Dissipation is maximal only when the loading rate is comparable to the relaxation rate, $\omega\sim1/\tau_\sigma$, where the viscous element is worked hardest.

Why this makes a tendon a good spring. A tendon's relaxation time $\tau_\sigma$ is of order seconds to minutes, while walking and running load it at a few hertz. The product $\omega\tau_\sigma$ is therefore large: dynamic loading sits far out on the fast side of the loss peak (the shaded band in Fig. 16), where the tendon responds almost elastically at its stiff instantaneous modulus $E_{\text{inst}}$ and loses very little. The same tendon, held under a slow steady stretch, sits near the peak and creeps. The tissue is a low-loss spring precisely in the regime it works in — the loss peak is parked away from the operating frequency.

Real collagenous tissues span a wide range of loss. (The illustrative SLS above, with $E_{\text{inst}}=1.8\,E_R$, has a fat peak $\tan\delta\approx0.30$ to make the loop visible; a real tendon is a far better spring.) Whether hysteresis is a virtue or a vice depends entirely on the tissue's job.

That completes the tendon as a material — a nonlinear, energy-storing, mildly lossy viscoelastic spring (§1–§7). The remaining passive tissues are not springs pulled in a line but constraints and structures: ligaments that limit a joint's range, fascia that transmits force across sheets, and the foot arch that stores energy as a truss. Those are the subject of §8.

8. Ligaments, fascia, and the foot arch

Everything so far pulled in a straight line: a tendon is a cord, and its job is to transmit and store the force along its length. The remaining passive tissues are the same collagen put to three other uses. Ligaments constrain a joint's range; fascia transmits force across a sheet; and the foot's arch is a structure that stores energy as a truss. Along the way this section repays two idealisations from Module 3: that a joint's limits are rigid walls, and that a joint turns about a fixed axis.

Ligaments as one-sided constraints

A ligament is built like a tendon — the same crimped collagen, the same J-shaped force–extension law of §2 — but it does the opposite job. A tendon is kept taut by the muscle and works throughout the motion; a ligament hangs slack through the joint's normal range and only snaps taut at the extremes, to stop the joint going further. And it can only pull, never push: it is a one-sided (unilateral) constraint.

Its effect on the joint is a restoring torque $T$ that is zero across the free range and rises steeply once a ligament engages beyond its slack angle (Fig. 17). Writing the ligament's engagement angles as $\theta_{\text{lo}},\theta_{\text{hi}}$, its force from the J-curve $F_{\text{lig}}$ of §2, and its moment arm about the joint as $R$:

$$\boxed{\;T(\theta)=\begin{cases}-\,R\,F_{\text{lig}}\big(\theta_{\text{lo}}-\theta\big), & \theta\lt \theta_{\text{lo}}\ \text{(hyper-extension)}\\[2pt] 0, & \theta_{\text{lo}}\le\theta\le\theta_{\text{hi}}\ \text{(free range: slack)}\\[2pt] +\,R\,F_{\text{lig}}\big(\theta-\theta_{\text{hi}}\big), & \theta\gt \theta_{\text{hi}}\ \text{(over-flexion)}\end{cases}\;}\tag{8.1}$$

This repays Module 3's idealisation of a joint limit as a rigid wall — an infinitely stiff stop that stores no energy (the dashed verticals in Fig. 17). The real limit is a compliant, one-sided spring: steep but finite, and it stores elastic energy at end-range (the same $\tfrac12 F x$ of §3). That stored energy is why a mild over-stretch springs back; a sprain is that end-range energy exceeding the ligament's capacity, tearing the fibres of §1.

The joint axis is set by the ligaments, not fixed

Module 3 also modelled a joint as a hinge turning about a fixed axis. The knee shows why that is only an approximation — and the reason is again the ligaments. The two cruciate ligaments cross inside the knee: the anterior cruciate (ACL) runs from the back of the femur to the front of the tibia, the posterior cruciate (PCL) from the front of the femur to the back of the tibia. Treated as (nearly) inextensible links, together with the femoral and tibial bone segments they form a four-bar linkage (Fig. 18).

A planar rigid body turning has, at each instant, one point that is momentarily stationary — its instantaneous centre of rotation. For the crossed-cruciate linkage it sits at a place fixed entirely by the ligament geometry:

Proposition 10 (the instant centre is the cruciate crossing). The instantaneous centre of rotation of the tibia relative to the femur lies at the intersection of the two cruciate lines.
The tibial attachment of the ACL, $A_t$, is held a fixed distance $L_{\text{ACL}}$ from the fixed femoral point $A_f$, so $A_t$ moves on a circle about $A_f$ and its velocity is perpendicular to the ACL line $A_fA_t$. But relative to the instant centre $I$, every tibial point $Q$ has velocity $v_Q=\omega\times(Q-I)$, perpendicular to $Q-I$. For $A_t$ both must hold, so $(A_t-I)$ is parallel to $(A_t-A_f)$ — that is, $I$ lies on the ACL line. The identical argument for the PCL puts $I$ on the PCL line. Hence $I$ is the intersection of the two cruciate lines. ∎

As the knee flexes the cruciates swing, so their intersection — the axis of rotation — migrates, tracing the curve (the centrode) drawn in Fig. 18. Two familiar clinical facts fall straight out of this moving axis. The femur rolls then glides on the tibia: because the contact point and the instant centre are both set by the linkage, early flexion is mostly rolling (the contact moves backward) and later flexion adds gliding (the femur spins in place) — which is how the knee fits $140^\circ$ of flexion onto a short tibial plateau. And near full extension the geometry forces a small coupled external rotation of the tibia, the screw-home that locks the knee straight for standing. The "fixed hinge" of Module 3 is really a mechanism whose axis the crossed ligaments steer, moment by moment.

Fascia: force transmission across a sheet

Not all collagen is a cord. Fascia is collagen laid out as a sheet — the epimysium wrapping a muscle, the broad thoracolumbar fascia of the back, the iliotibial band, the plantar fascia we meet next. Where a tendon carries force end-to-end along its length, a fascial sheet transmits force laterally, across its area (Fig. 19). A muscle's pull can pass through the fascia to neighbouring muscles and bones rather than only down its own tendon — myofascial force transmission — so force is shared and redirected over a region, not funnelled through a single line of action.

The foot arch and the windlass

The medial longitudinal arch of the foot is a tension truss. The bones — calcaneus, navicular, cuneiforms, metatarsals — form a curved arch that carries the body's weight in compression; but an unrestrained arch under load would simply splay flat. What stops it is the plantar fascia, a stout sheet running along the sole from the heel to the toes: it is the tie that ties the two ends of the arch together and carries the tension (Fig. 20). A little statics fixes that tension.

Proposition 11 (plantar-fascia tie tension). For an arch of span $b$ and height $h$ carrying a vertical load $W$ at its apex, the tension in the plantar-fascia tie is $$\boxed{\;T=\frac{W\,b}{4\,h}\;}\tag{8.2}$$
Model the arch as two rigid struts pinned at the loaded apex and resting on the ground at the two ends, the ends joined by the horizontal tie. By left–right symmetry each end support carries half the load, $W/2$, vertically. Take the free body of one strut and sum moments about the apex pin (through which the apex reaction passes, contributing nothing): the vertical support reaction $W/2$ acts a horizontal distance $b/2$ from the apex, giving moment $\tfrac{W}{2}\cdot\tfrac{b}{2}$; the tie tension $T$ acts at the end a vertical distance $h$ below the apex, giving moment $T\,h$ the other way. Balance, $T h=\tfrac{W}{2}\tfrac{b}{2}$, gives $T=Wb/(4h)$. ∎

The height $h$ sits in the denominator, so a flatter arch needs a larger tie tension for the same load — which is why flat feet overload the plantar fascia and why plantar fasciitis is a tension-overuse injury of that tie. And because the tie is an elastic spring (§2), it stores $\tfrac12 T\,\Delta$ of energy as it stretches under load and returns most of it at push-off: the arch is a second spring in the leg alongside the Achilles, contributing the $\sim17\ \text{J}$ per step noted in §0.

One more trick makes the foot a rigid lever exactly when it needs to be. At push-off the toes dorsiflex, and because the plantar fascia inserts on the toes, dorsiflexing them wraps the fascia around the metatarsal heads like a rope around a drum — the windlass mechanism (Fig. 20, right). Wrapping shortens the effective tie, which by Eq. (8.2)'s geometry raises the arch and stiffens the whole foot into a stiff lever for the propulsive push, then releases as the toes come back down. The passive arch thus switches between a compliant shock-absorbing spring at heel-strike and a rigid lever at toe-off, with no muscle required.

That closes the catalogue of passive tissues: the nonlinear, viscoelastic tendon spring (§1–§7), the one-sided ligament constraint and the ligament-steered joint axis, force-transmitting fascia, and the foot-arch truss with its windlass. §9 puts three of these in motion — a tendon recoiling, a muscle–tendon unit storing running energy, and a ligament engaging under joint rotation — and §10 audits what the whole picture still idealises.

Lab 1 — Tendon recoil: elastic versus viscoelastic

Stretch a tendon, attach it to a small mass, and let go. An ideal elastic tendon returns all its stored energy, so the mass oscillates forever; a viscoelastic tendon (the standard linear solid of §6) loses the hysteresis energy of §7 each cycle and damps out. Both obey $m\ddot x=-F$; the only difference is the force law $F(x)$ — a spring $kx$, or the SLS ODE (6.3).

import numpy as np
# tendon as a linear spring; SLS adds viscous memory. Mass m released from
# stretch x0.
kR, kinst = 2.0e5, 3.6e5  # relaxed & instantaneous stiffness (N/m)
ts, te = 0.05, 0.09  # SLS relaxation & creep times (s)
m,  x0 = 1.0, 0.020  # mass (kg), initial stretch (m)


def recoil(sls):  # integrate m x'' = -F ; F from tendon model
    dt = 2e-5
    x, v, F = x0, 0.0, kinst*x0
    T = np.arange(0, 0.08, dt)
    X = np.empty_like(T)
    for i in range(len(T)):
        X[i] = x
        F = F + dt*(kR*(x + te*v) - F)/ts if sls else kR * \
            x  # SLS: ts F' + F = kR(x+te x')
        v += (-F/m)*dt
        x += v*dt
    return T, X


_, Xe = recoil(sls=False)  # elastic tendon: undamped
_, Xs = recoil(sls=True)  # viscoelastic (SLS) tendon: damped
pk = [Xs[i] for i in range(1, len(Xs)-1) if Xs[i] >
      Xs[i-1] and Xs[i] >= Xs[i+1] and Xs[i] > 0.004]
print(f"resilience per cycle = (peak2/peak1)^2 = {(pk[1]/pk[0])**2:.3f}")

Running it prints resilience per cycle = 0.911: the SLS tendon returns about $91\%$ of its energy each bounce and loses the rest to heat — squarely the $90$–$93\%$ tendon resilience of §3. The elastic reference (blue) never decays. Because the oscillation is fast relative to the tendon's relaxation time, the loss per cycle is small — exactly the low-loss fast regime of §7.

Lab 2 — The catapult: elastic energy storage in a hop

This is the payoff simulation. Model a vertical hop as a body mass $M$ bouncing on a leg that is a muscle–tendon unit — the active Hill fibre of Module 5 in series with the elastic tendon of §2 — under gravity (§4's series-elastic model, now driven dynamically). The body compresses the leg on landing, stretching the tendon; because gravity holds the load high, the fibre stays near its isometric force and barely moves while the tendon stores the energy, then recoils to launch the body. The state equations are Newton's law for the body, $M\ddot s=Mg-F$, coupled to the fibre through the force–velocity inverse of Eq. (4.3).

import numpy as np
# a hop: body mass M on a leg = active muscle fibre (Hill) in series with
# an elastic tendon.
M, g = 80.0, 9.81  # body-mass share (kg), gravity
kT = 1.7e4  # leg (series-tendon) stiffness (N/m)
Fmax = 2600.0  # muscle max isometric force (N)
l0 = 0.055
vmax = 8*l0  # optimal fibre length (m), max shortening velocity (m/s)
a = 1.0  # full activation


def fL(x): return np.exp(-((x/l0 - 1)/0.45)**2)  # length factor


def fVinv(fv):  # Hill force->velocity inverse
    fv = min(max(fv, 0.0), 1.5)
    vn = (1 - fv)/(1 + fv/0.25)
    return min(max(vn, -0.4), 1.0)*vmax


dt = 2e-5
# compression, its rate, fibre length, time
s, sd, l, t = 0.0, 1.35, l0, 0.0
# (vMTU, vfibre, vtendon)
V = []
while True:
    # tendon stretch = compression - fibre shortening
    xT = s - (l0 - l)
    # tendon force = ground reaction
    F = kT*max(xT, 0.0)
    # fibre shortening velocity
    vfib = fVinv(F/(a*Fmax*fL(l)) if F > 0 else 0.0)
    # shortening speeds; vtendon = vMTU - vfibre
    V.append((-sd, vfib, -sd - vfib))
    if t > 0.02 and (s <= 0 or F <= 0):
        break  # takeoff
    sd += (g - F/M)*dt
    s += sd*dt
    l -= vfib*dt
    t += dt  # M s'' = M g - F
V = np.array(V)
rec, fib = V[:, 2].max(), abs(V[:, 1]).max()
print(f"peak tendon recoil {rec:.2f} m/s  vs  peak fibre {fib:.2f} m/s "
      f"-> {rec/fib:.1f}x amplification")
print(f"fibre stays near-isometric; the tendon does the fast recoil")

The peak ground reaction is 3.3× body weight, and the simulation prints the headline: the tendon recoils at 1.46 m/s while the fibre shortens at only 0.44 m/s — a 3.3× speed amplification (Fig. 22, blue tracks the dashed whole-unit while red stays flat near zero). Accounting the energy, the tendon stores $\approx197\ \text{J}$ over the bounce while the muscle does only $\approx7\ \text{J}$ of work: $\sim97\%$ of the push-off is elastic recoil, the muscle merely holding force. This is the catapult of §4 emerging from the coupled dynamics — and it settles Module 5's Assumption 7.1 debt (b), the fibre–tendon speed difference, with a computed result rather than an argument.

Lab 3 — A ligament constraint under joint motion

Finally the one-sided constraint of §8. Send a limb rotating fast toward over-flexion and let the ligament stop it: the restoring torque is zero until the joint passes the ligament's engagement angle, then rises steeply (Eq. 8.1), decelerating and rebounding the limb — a joint hitting its ligamentous end-stop. Integrating $I\ddot\theta=-T_{\text{lig}}(\theta)$ for three ligament laxities turns the model into a sensitivity study.

import numpy as np
# a limb rotating fast toward over-flexion; a one-sided ligament stops it
# past the limit
# segment inertia, moment arm, stiffness, exponent
I, R, k, p = 0.20, 0.03, 4.5e6, 2


def run(slack_deg):
    th_hi = np.radians(140 + slack_deg)  # laxity shifts the engagement angle
    dt = 1e-4
    th, w = np.radians(110), np.radians(650)
    peak = np.degrees(th)
    for _ in range(3000):
        over = th - th_hi
        T = R*k*over**p if over > 0 else 0.0  # one-sided (past limit)
        w += (-T/I)*dt
        th += w*dt
        peak = max(peak, np.degrees(th))
    return peak - 140  # overshoot past the ligament limit


for slack in (0, 8, 16):
    print(f"slack +{slack:2d} deg -> overshoot {run(slack):.1f} deg")

A normal ligament arrests the joint after only $\approx3.8^\circ$ of overshoot; loosening it by $16^\circ$ lets the joint fly $\approx19.8^\circ$ past the limit before the steep one-sided torque catches it. The sweep quantifies why laxity — from a previous sprain, or from constitutional hypermobility — raises the risk of hyperextension injury: a slack ligament engages later, so the joint arrives at the collision faster and travels further into the danger zone.

How the tissue is measured and validated

Every quantity in the model maps to a laboratory measurement, which is what keeps it falsifiable:

  • Stress–strain and stiffness — a materials testing machine pulls an excised tendon (or a bone–tendon–bone preparation) and records force against grip-to-grip elongation, tracing the toe and linear regions of §1–§2.
  • In-vivo strain — B-mode ultrasound tracks the Achilles free-tendon length during hopping and running, giving the working strain (a few percent) and the elastic energy of §3.
  • Time constants and hysteresis — a dynamic mechanical analyser imposes sinusoidal strain over a frequency sweep and reads the storage and loss moduli $E',E''$ and $\tan\delta$ (§7), from which $\tau_\sigma,\tau_\varepsilon$ follow.
  • Joint constraint and laxity — an arthrometer (or instrumented laxity test) loads a joint at end-range and measures the displacement, calibrating the one-sided torque of §8.

Diagnostics

Five quick self-checks. Answer each before opening the solution; if any is unclear, the linked section is where to look.

  1. A rubber band gets no harder to stretch as you pull; a tendon does. Why?
    Solution
    A tendon starts with wavy crimp: at first only a few straightened fibrils bear load, so it is compliant (the toe), and as strain grows more fibrils recruit and the tangent stiffness rises toward $k_{\text{lin}}$ (§1, §2). The rising slope of the J-curve is the increasing resistance you feel.
  2. Two runners stretch the Achilles to the same peak force, storing the same energy. One returns more of it on push-off. What differs?
    Solution
    Their resilience — the fraction returned rather than dissipated as heat (§3, §7). A tendon with a smaller hysteresis loop (lower loss tangent) hands back more of the stored energy; the difference is architecture and hydration, not the peak load.
  3. A wallaby hops faster and faster with almost no rise in metabolic cost. How?
    Solution
    Most of the step's energy is banked in the tendon on landing and returned on take-off (§3), while the muscle stays nearly isometric and cheap (§4). The spring, not the motor, supplies the bounce, so raising speed costs little extra chemical energy.
  4. You hold a stretched tendon at a fixed length and the force slowly sags. Which lumped model reproduces this, and which cannot?
    Solution
    This is stress relaxation. The standard linear solid and the Maxwell element both relax (Maxwell all the way to zero, the SLS to a finite plateau); the Kelvin–Voigt solid cannot — at fixed strain its dashpot is idle and the stress stays put (§6).
  5. A previously sprained ankle "gives way" more easily. In terms of the one-sided constraint, what changed?
    Solution
    The ligament became lax: its slack range widened, so the free interval $[\theta_{\text{lo}},\theta_{\text{hi}}]$ of the one-sided torque (§8, Eq. 8.1) is larger. The restoring torque engages later and further into range, so the joint reaches its collision faster and travels deeper before being caught.

Conceptual (C1–C10)

C1. Why is a tendon's stress–strain curve J-shaped (concave-up) rather than the straight Hookean line of an ideal spring? Probes: crimp and recruitment, §1.
Solution
At rest the collagen fibrils are wavy (crimp). Under load they straighten and begin to bear tension a few at a time (recruitment), so at small strain only a fraction of the tissue resists — the compliant toe — and the tangent stiffness climbs as more fibrils engage, reaching the constant linear slope once all are straight. A concave-up curve is the signature of progressive recruitment (§1).
C2. Tendon, ligament, and fascia are built from the same collagen, yet tendon is the stiffest and most efficient spring of the three along its load axis. What structural feature sets it apart? Probes: tissue architecture, §1.
Solution
Alignment. A tendon's fibrils run nearly parallel to its long axis, so almost every fibre pulls along the load; a ligament is less strictly aligned (it must resist several directions), and fascia is a planar sheet with fibres in-plane at a spread of angles. The same molecules, packed in line, give a stiffer, lower-loss, more nearly one-dimensional spring (§1).
C3. For a nonlinear spring the "stiffness" is not a single number. Define the two stiffnesses that matter, and say which one governs small vibrations about a loaded state. Probes: tangent vs secant stiffness, §2.
Solution
The secant stiffness $F/x$ (the chord slope) sets the average force at a given stretch; the tangent stiffness $k(x)=\mathrm{d}F/\mathrm{d}x$ (the local slope, Eq. 2.2) sets the resistance to a small extra displacement. Small oscillations about a working point see the tangent stiffness, so a tendon held in its stiff linear region vibrates faster than one slack in the toe (§2).
C4. A tendon returns about $93\%$ of the energy it stores; a ligament returns much less, though both are collagen. Why? Probes: resilience and hysteresis, §3, §7.
Solution
Return fraction is resilience $=1-$ (loop area)$/$(stored energy) (§3, §7). A tendon's aligned, well-hydrated, low-loss structure traces a thin hysteresis loop (small $\tan\delta$), so little is lost to heat; a ligament's less-ordered architecture dissipates more per cycle. Same collagen, different loss angle — the tendon is tuned to give energy back, the ligament to absorb and stabilise.
C5. A muscle fibre's peak shortening speed is only a few fibre-lengths per second, yet the ankle extends explosively in a jump. How does the series tendon let the joint out-run the fibre? Probes: the catapult, §4.
Solution
The fibre first stretches the tendon slowly, doing work against it while nearly isometric (cheap, high-force). Released, the tendon recoils on its own fast timescale, adding its stored velocity to the fibre's: by Eq. 4.4, $\dot L_{\text{MTU}}=\dot\ell\cos\theta_p+\dot x$, and the tendon term $\dot x$ can dominate. The joint moves at the sum, so it out-runs the fibre — the catapult (§4).
C6. In what limit does the series-elastic MTU reduce to Module 5's rigid-tendon model, and what capability is lost there? Probes: the stiff limit, §4 (Prop. 4).
Solution
As tendon stiffness $k_T\to\infty$ the tendon stretch $x=F/k_T\to0$, so $L_{\text{MTU}}=\ell\cos\theta_p+L_{Ts}$ exactly — the fibre length is slaved to the (measurable) MTU length and Module 5's explicit ODE returns (Prop. 4). What is lost is elastic energy storage and the fibre–tendon speed difference: with no compliance there is no spring to bank energy and no catapult (§4).
C7. Creep and stress relaxation are two experiments on the same tissue. State what is held fixed and what is measured in each, and why one material shows both. Probes: the two step tests, §5.
Solution
Creep: hold the stress constant, watch the strain grow. Relaxation: hold the strain constant, watch the stress decay. Both arise because the tissue has a spring in it (an instantaneous elastic response) and a dashpot (a slow viscous rearrangement): the spring sets the immediate response, the dashpot lets it drift toward equilibrium over the material's time constants (§5).
C8. Kelvin–Voigt cannot reproduce stress relaxation, and Maxwell cannot reproduce a bounded creep. Explain each failure, and how the standard linear solid fixes both. Probes: model structure, §6.
Solution
Kelvin–Voigt (spring ∥ dashpot): at fixed strain the dashpot carries no rate, so the stress equals the spring's and never decays — no relaxation. Maxwell (spring – dashpot in series): under fixed stress the dashpot flows forever, so strain grows without bound — no equilibrium, unbounded creep. The SLS adds a second spring so there is both an instantaneous stiffness and a finite equilibrium: it relaxes to a plateau and creeps to a bound (§6, Eq. 6.3).
C9. Low hysteresis makes a good energy-return spring; high hysteresis makes a good shock absorber. Name a tissue best served by each, and say why the same collagen family spans both. Probes: the loss–return trade-off, §7.
Solution
A tendon (Achilles) wants low loss, to give the stride's energy back; an intervertebral disc or the heel pad (and, in part, a ligament) wants high loss, to soak up impact and protect the joint. The loss tangent is set by how much viscous rearrangement the architecture and hydration allow: aligned, cross-linked tendon dissipates little; looser, more hydrated tissue dissipates much — one material family, tuned across the spectrum (§7).
C10. The knee is often drawn as a hinge with a fixed axis. Why does its true flexion axis migrate, and what ligament structure sets the path? Probes: the crossed four-bar, §8.
Solution
The two cruciate ligaments cross, forming a four-bar linkage (femoral link, tibial link, and the two crossed cruciates). Its instant centre — the momentary axis of rotation — sits at the cruciate crossing (Prop. 10) and moves as the bars swing, so the axis migrates and the contact rolls then glides. Ligament geometry, not a fixed pin, sets the path (§8).

Derivational (D1–D10)

D1. Take recruitment to be linear: the engaged fibril fraction is $\Phi(\varepsilon)=\varepsilon/\varepsilon^\ast$ up to $\varepsilon^\ast$, then $1$. Derive the toe stress $\sigma(\varepsilon)=E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon^2/(2\varepsilon^\ast)$ and show $\sigma$ and its slope are continuous at $\varepsilon^\ast$. Probes: §1 (Prop. 1).
Solution
With $E(\varepsilon)=E_{\text{lin}}\Phi$, $\sigma=\int_0^\varepsilon E_{\text{lin}}(\varepsilon'/\varepsilon^\ast)\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon'=E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon^2/(2\varepsilon^\ast)$ for $\varepsilon\le\varepsilon^\ast$. At $\varepsilon^\ast$ this gives $\sigma=E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon^\ast/2$ and slope $\mathrm{d}\sigma/\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=E_{\text{lin}}$; the linear branch $\sigma=E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon^\ast/2+E_{\text{lin}}(\varepsilon-\varepsilon^\ast)$ matches both value and slope there, so the J-curve is $C^1$ (Eq. 1.2).
D2. From the material law $\sigma(\varepsilon)$ derive the structural force $F(x)=A_0\,\sigma(x/L_0)$ and the tangent stiffness $k(x)$; show $k$ rises from $0$ through the toe to the constant $k_{\text{lin}}=A_0E_{\text{lin}}/L_0$. Probes: §2 (Prop. 2).
Solution
Force is stress times rest area, $F=A_0\sigma(\varepsilon)$ with $\varepsilon=x/L_0$ (Eq. 2.1). Then $k=\mathrm{d}F/\mathrm{d}x=A_0(\mathrm{d}\sigma/\mathrm{d}\varepsilon)(1/L_0)=(A_0/L_0)E(\varepsilon)$ (Eq. 2.2). In the toe $E=E_{\text{lin}}\varepsilon/\varepsilon^\ast$ rises linearly from $0$; past $\varepsilon^\ast$, $E=E_{\text{lin}}$ so $k=k_{\text{lin}}=A_0E_{\text{lin}}/L_0$, constant.
D3. Derive the linear elastic energy $U=\tfrac12kx^2=F^2/(2k)$ three ways — by integrating $F\,\mathrm{d}x$, as the area of a triangle, and from the force. Then give the toe energy $U(x^\ast)=\tfrac16k_{\text{lin}}(x^\ast)^2$. Probes: §3 (Prop. 3).
Solution
(i) $U=\int_0^x kx'\,\mathrm{d}x'=\tfrac12kx^2$. (ii) The $F$–$x$ line has height $F=kx$ over base $x$; triangle area $=\tfrac12Fx=\tfrac12kx^2$. (iii) $x=F/k$ gives $U=\tfrac12k(F/k)^2=F^2/(2k)$ (Eq. 3.2). For the toe, $F=k_{\text{lin}}x^2/(2x^\ast)$, so $U(x^\ast)=\int_0^{x^\ast}k_{\text{lin}}x'^2/(2x^\ast)\,\mathrm{d}x'=\tfrac16k_{\text{lin}}(x^\ast)^2$ (Eq. 3.3): the concave toe stores only a third of the $\tfrac12k_{\text{lin}}(x^\ast)^2$ a linear spring would.
D4. Show the resilience $R=U_{\text{returned}}/U_{\text{stored}}$ equals $1-$ (loop area)$/U_{\text{stored}}$, and relate it to the loss angle $\delta$ for small loss. Probes: §3, §7.
Solution
Over a load–unload cycle the energy in is the area under the loading curve $U_{\text{stored}}$; the energy out is the area under the unloading curve $U_{\text{ret}}=U_{\text{stored}}-W_d$, where $W_d=\oint\sigma\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon$ is the enclosed loop area (Eq. 7.1). Hence $R=1-W_d/U_{\text{stored}}$. With $W_d=\pi E''\varepsilon_0^2$ and $U_{\text{stored}}\approx\tfrac12E'\varepsilon_0^2$, $R\approx1-2\pi(E''/E')=1-2\pi\tan\delta$ for small $\tan\delta$ — low loss angle, high return.
D5. Two springs (fibre $k_M$, tendon $k_T$) in series carry one force. Derive the series stiffness $1/k=1/k_M+1/k_T$, show the softer element takes the larger share of a given elongation, and recover the velocity split $\dot L_{\text{MTU}}=\dot\ell\cos\theta_p+\dot x$ and the rigid limit. Probes: §4.
Solution
Series elements share force $F$ and add elongations: $x_{\text{tot}}=F/k_M+F/k_T$, so $1/k=1/k_M+1/k_T$. The share of each is $F/k_i$, larger for the smaller $k_i$ — the compliant tendon stretches more. Differentiating the length constraint $L_{\text{MTU}}=\ell\cos\theta_p+L_{Ts}+x$ gives Eq. 4.4, $\dot L_{\text{MTU}}=\dot\ell\cos\theta_p+\dot x$; as $k_T\to\infty$, $\dot x=\dot F/k_T\to0$ and all the length change falls on the fibre (Module 5's rigid tendon).
D6. From the Maxwell ODE (Eq. 6.1) derive the relaxation modulus $G(t)=E_Me^{-t/\tau}$ under a step strain, and show the creep under a step stress is unbounded. Probes: §6.
Solution
Step strain $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_0H(t)$ gives $\dot\varepsilon=0$ for $t\gt0$, so $\dot\sigma+(E_M/\eta)\sigma=0\Rightarrow\sigma=\sigma_0e^{-t/\tau}$ with $\tau=\eta/E_M$ and $\sigma_0=E_M\varepsilon_0$; thus $G(t)=\sigma/\varepsilon_0=E_Me^{-t/\tau}$, decaying to zero (full relaxation). Under step stress $\sigma_0$, $\dot\varepsilon=\dot\sigma/E_M+\sigma/\eta=\sigma_0/\eta$, so $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_{\text{el}}+(\sigma_0/\eta)t$ grows without bound — the dashpot flows forever.
D7. From the Kelvin–Voigt ODE (Eq. 6.2) derive the creep compliance $J(t)=\tfrac{1}{E_K}(1-e^{-t/\tau})$ under a step stress, and show it cannot relax. Probes: §6.
Solution
Step stress $\sigma_0$: $\eta\dot\varepsilon+E_K\varepsilon=\sigma_0$, a first-order linear ODE with solution $\varepsilon(t)=(\sigma_0/E_K)(1-e^{-t/\tau})$, $\tau=\eta/E_K$; so $J=\varepsilon/\sigma_0=\tfrac{1}{E_K}(1-e^{-t/\tau})$, rising to the bounded value $1/E_K$ (bounded creep). Under a step strain, $\dot\varepsilon=0$ so $\sigma=E_K\varepsilon_0$ is constant — no relaxation, because at fixed length the dashpot is idle.
D8. From the SLS ODE (Eq. 6.3) derive the complex modulus $E^\ast(\omega)=E_R\dfrac{1+i\omega\tau_\varepsilon}{1+i\omega\tau_\sigma}$ and the loss tangent, and locate the loss peak. Probes: §7 (Prop. 9).
Solution
Put $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_0e^{i\omega t}$, $\sigma=\sigma_0e^{i\omega t}$ into $\tau_\sigma\dot\sigma+\sigma=E_R(\varepsilon+\tau_\varepsilon\dot\varepsilon)$: $(1+i\omega\tau_\sigma)\sigma_0=E_R(1+i\omega\tau_\varepsilon)\varepsilon_0$, so $E^\ast=\sigma_0/\varepsilon_0=E_R(1+i\omega\tau_\varepsilon)/(1+i\omega\tau_\sigma)$ (Eq. 7.3). Then $\tan\delta=\mathrm{Im}\,E^\ast/\mathrm{Re}\,E^\ast=\omega(\tau_\varepsilon-\tau_\sigma)/(1+\omega^2\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon)$, maximised at $\omega=1/\sqrt{\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon}$ (set $\mathrm{d}\tan\delta/\mathrm{d}\omega=0$).
D9. For a linear viscoelastic material under sinusoidal strain, derive the dissipated energy per cycle $W_d=\oint\sigma\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon=\pi\sigma_0\varepsilon_0\sin\delta=\pi E''\varepsilon_0^2$. Probes: §7 (Prop. 8).
Solution
With $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_0\sin\omega t$ and $\sigma=\sigma_0\sin(\omega t+\delta)$, $W_d=\int_0^{2\pi/\omega}\sigma\,\dot\varepsilon\,\mathrm{d}t=\sigma_0\varepsilon_0\omega\int_0^{2\pi/\omega}\sin(\omega t+\delta)\cos\omega t\,\mathrm{d}t$. Expanding $\sin(\omega t+\delta)=\sin\omega t\cos\delta+\cos\omega t\sin\delta$, the $\cos^2$ term integrates to $\pi/\omega$ and the cross term to $0$, giving $W_d=\pi\sigma_0\varepsilon_0\sin\delta$. Since $\sigma_0\sin\delta=E''\varepsilon_0$, $W_d=\pi E''\varepsilon_0^2$ (Eq. 7.2): only the out-of-phase part dissipates.
D10. Derive the plantar-fascia tie tension $T=Wb/(4h)$ from the tension-truss model of the arch, and explain how the windlass raises it. Probes: §8 (Prop. 11).
Solution
Model the arch as two rigid struts meeting at the apex, height $h$, base $b$, tied along the base by the fascia (tension $T$). Body load $W$ presses down at the apex; by symmetry each support carries $W/2$. Taking moments about the apex for one half-strut, the vertical reaction $W/2$ at horizontal distance $b/2$ is balanced by $T$ at height $h$: $(W/2)(b/2)=Th$, so $T=Wb/(4h)$ (Eq. 8.2). The windlass — dorsiflexing the toes — wraps the fascia around the metatarsal heads, shortening its free length and pre-tensioning the tie, which raises and stiffens the arch before load even arrives.

Computational (K1–K10)

These require genuine computation — a simulation, an optimisation, an inverse problem, a sensitivity sweep, or a regime comparison — not substitution into a boxed formula. Each answer below is Python-verified.

K1. Invert a noisy tendon force–elongation dataset: fit the crimp model $F(x)$ (Eq. 2.3) by nonlinear least squares to recover the toe strain $\varepsilon^\ast$ and linear stiffness $k_{\text{lin}}$, and check them against the true values. Probes: §1–§2; inverse problem.
Solution
Fitting the two-branch $F(x)$ to data generated at $\varepsilon^\ast=3.00\%$, $k_{\text{lin}}=416\ \mathrm{kN/m}$ with $3\%$ multiplicative plus additive noise recovers $\boxed{\varepsilon^\ast\approx3.12\%,\ k_{\text{lin}}\approx424\ \mathrm{kN/m}}$ — within the noise. The toe strain is identifiable because it fixes the curvature of the knee between the parabolic and linear branches; a purely linear fit would miss it and over-stiffen the toe.
K2. Compare regimes: a linear tendon and a toe (nonlinear) tendon both reach the same peak force $F_{\text{pk}}=4\ \mathrm{kN}$. By numerical integration of $\int F\,\mathrm{d}x$, which stores more energy, and by how much? Probes: §3; regime comparison + integration.
Solution
To reach $4\ \mathrm{kN}$ the linear spring stretches $9.6\ \mathrm{mm}$ and stores $U_{\text{lin}}=19.2\ \mathrm{J}$; the toe tendon must stretch further ($13.4\ \mathrm{mm}$, its compliant toe shifts the linear branch out by $x^\ast/2$) and stores $\boxed{U_{\text{toe}}\approx20.2\ \mathrm{J}}$ — about $\mathbf{5\%}$ more for the same peak force. The toe is not just protective; the extra low-force excursion banks additional energy at no additional stress.
K3. Model the MTU as a nonlinear muscle passive element in series with the tendon spring. At each imposed stretch, root-find the force balance $F_{PE}(\delta)=k_T(S-\delta)$ for the fibre/tendon split, and sweep $k_T$ to show the fibre's share of the stretch $\to100\%$ (the rigid limit) as $k_T\to\infty$. Probes: §4; implicit solve + known-limit validation (Prop. 4).
Solution
With a stiffening passive element $F_{PE}(\delta)=A(e^{\delta/b}-1)$ the split has no closed form, so each point needs a 1-D root find. At full stretch the fibre carries $\boxed{44\%}$ of the elongation for a soft tendon ($20\ \mathrm{kN/m}$), $94\%$ for a stiff one ($1000\ \mathrm{kN/m}$), and $\to100\%$ as $k_T\to\infty$ — numerically confirming that the series model reduces to Module 5's rigid tendon (Prop. 4). The tendon's share is exactly the elastic energy the rigid model throws away.
K4. A bouncing gait is a driven damped spring–mass. Optimise the leg (tendon) stiffness for maximum steady energy return at a $2.6\ \mathrm{Hz}$ stride, and show the optimum is the resonant $k^\ast=M\omega^2$ — detuning either way loses return. Probes: §4; optimisation (resonance tuning).
Solution
The steady amplitude $X(k)=F_0/\sqrt{(k-M\omega^2)^2+(c\omega)^2}$ peaks where the elastic and inertial terms cancel, $\boxed{k^\ast=M\omega^2\approx21\ \mathrm{kN/m}}$ at $\omega=2\pi(2.6)\ \mathrm{rad/s}$. Off resonance the return falls — to $\sim0.67$ of peak when too soft, $\sim0.08$ when too stiff. A tendon tuned to the stride frequency returns the most energy for the least muscular effort; this is why leg stiffness scales with gait, and why a mismatch is costly.
K5. Invert a noisy stress-relaxation curve: integrate the SLS law (Eq. 6.3) and least-squares fit to recover $\tau_\sigma,\tau_\varepsilon,E_R$. Probes: §6; inverse problem.
Solution
Fitting the SLS relaxation $\sigma(t)=\varepsilon_0[E_R+(E_0-E_R)e^{-t/\tau_\sigma}]$ (with $E_0=E_R\tau_\varepsilon/\tau_\sigma$) to data from $\tau_\sigma=0.050,\tau_\varepsilon=0.090\ \mathrm{s},E_R=1.20\ \mathrm{GPa}$ plus $2\%$ noise recovers $\boxed{\tau_\sigma\approx0.051,\ \tau_\varepsilon\approx0.091\ \mathrm{s},\ E_R\approx1.20\ \mathrm{GPa}}$. The instantaneous jump fixes $E_0$, the plateau fixes $E_R$, and the decay rate fixes $\tau_\sigma$ — three features, three constants.
K6. Integrate Maxwell, Kelvin–Voigt, and SLS under one strain history (ramp to $2\%$, then hold) and compare: which reproduces both an instantaneous response and a partial relaxation? Probes: §6; regime comparison.
Solution
Under the hold, Maxwell relaxes fully ($\sigma\to0$); Kelvin–Voigt never relaxes (holds at $E_K\varepsilon_0=20\ \mathrm{MPa}$, after a dashpot spike during the ramp); the SLS rises to $\approx20\ \mathrm{MPa}$ then relaxes to the finite plateau $E_R\varepsilon_0\approx\boxed{12\ \mathrm{MPa}}$. Only the SLS shows the real-tissue signature — an immediate stiffness and a partial, bounded relaxation (§6).
K7. Sweep the cycling frequency for an SLS and compute the loop area $W_d$ and loss tangent $\tan\delta$; locate the loss peak $\omega=1/\sqrt{\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon}$. Probes: §7; sensitivity sweep.
Solution
From $E^\ast(\omega)$ (Eq. 7.3) the loop area $W_d=\pi E''\varepsilon_0^2$ and $\tan\delta$ both rise, peak, and fall as $\omega$ crosses the material's timescale. For $\tau_\sigma=0.05,\tau_\varepsilon=0.10\ \mathrm{s}$ the peak sits at $\boxed{\omega=1/\sqrt{\tau_\sigma\tau_\varepsilon}\approx14.1\ \mathrm{rad/s}}$ ($f\approx2.25\ \mathrm{Hz}$), with $\tan\delta_{\max}\approx0.35$. Away from the peak — very slow (elastic-relaxed) or very fast (elastic-glassy) — the tissue is nearly lossless; dissipation is a resonance of the internal clock, not a monotone property.
K8. By loop integration, compute the energy-return resilience of a low-loss (tendon) and a high-loss (ligament) tissue across the running-cadence band, and compare. Probes: §7; regime comparison + sweep.
Solution
Integrating the tension half-cycle loop (work out $\div$ work in), a low-loss tendon ($\tau_\varepsilon/\tau_\sigma\approx1.06$) returns $\boxed{\approx94\%}$ at mid-cadence — matching the $\sim93\%$ of C4 and the $5$–$10\%$ hysteresis of §3 — while a high-loss ligament ($\tau_\varepsilon/\tau_\sigma=3.2$) returns only $\approx20\%$. The same governing law, with a wider time-constant spread, converts an energy-return spring into a shock absorber — the quantitative face of C9.
K9. Simulate the knee crossed-cruciate four-bar over the flexion arc: solve for the tibial link position (both cruciate lengths fixed), find the instant centre as the cruciate crossing, and trace the centrode. Quantify its migration. Probes: §8; kinematic simulation.
Solution
Holding both cruciate lengths rigid and rotating the tibia, the four-bar's instant centre (the cruciate intersection, Prop. 10) sweeps a curved locus — the centrode — migrating $\boxed{\approx1.5}$ link-units (posteriorly and proximally) over the first $\sim57^\circ$ of flexion. A fixed-hinge knee would hold one point; the migrating centre is why the femur rolls back on the tibia as it flexes (§8).
K10. Over a stance phase with a half-sine vertical load (peak $2.5\times$ body weight), integrate the plantar-fascia arch's stored elastic energy and compare its contribution to the Achilles. Probes: §8; integration + regime comparison.
Solution
With the truss tension $T=Wb/(4h)$ driving a fascia spring, the peak tension reaches $\approx1.47\ \mathrm{kN}$, the fascia stretches $\approx7.4\ \mathrm{mm}$, and the arch stores $\boxed{\approx5.4\ \mathrm{J}}$ at peak load — about $\mathbf{14\%}$ of the arch-plus-Achilles pair (the Achilles carries the other $\sim32\ \mathrm{J}$, §3). The arch is a second, smaller spring in the running leg; ignoring it under-counts the free elastic return by roughly a seventh (§8).

That closes Module 6. From the crimp of a single collagen fibril we have built, step by verifiable step, the tendon as a nonlinear spring, the energy it banks and returns, its series coupling to the muscle and the catapult that follows, the viscoelastic creep and hysteresis that make it imperfect, and the ligaments, fascia and arch that constrain and store. The passive tissues are no longer the rigid constraints Module 3 assumed nor the inextensible tendon Module 5 assumed — they are springs, and the debts are paid. What remains — the whole-body bounce, the slow accrual of damage, the three-dimensional continuum — is written into Modules 9, 14 and 16 (Fig. 24).

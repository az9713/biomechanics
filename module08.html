0. Motivation - why walking is cheap, but not free

Walking looks ordinary because we do it thousands of times a day. Mechanically it is strange. The body repeatedly tips forward, catches itself on the next foot, and redirects its center of mass into a new arc. If the catch is too late, the same movement becomes a fall. If the catch is perfectly timed, the movement becomes efficient locomotion.

This module turns that intuition into equations. The body is not a motorized cart that rolls horizontally at constant height. It vaults over the stance leg, exchanges kinetic and gravitational potential energy, collides into the next step, and uses muscular work to replace the energy that cannot be recovered. Walking is cheap because some energy is exchanged pendularly; it is not free because the step-to-step transition dissipates energy and must be paid for by ankle, knee, hip, trunk, and neural work.

The arc of this module. We define the gait cycle (section 1), derive the inverted-pendulum COM arc (section 2), connect GRF and COP to motion (section 3), build compass gait and transition costs (sections 4-5), estimate joint torques and powers (section 6), explain speed choice and arm swing (sections 7-8), and use the same physics-chemistry-biology stack to understand falls in older adults (section 10).

1. Gait-cycle notation and events

A gait cycle is one stride: the interval from an event on one foot to the next occurrence of the same event on that foot. A step is the interval from one foot contact to the opposite foot contact. A stride length $L_s$ is the forward distance covered in one stride; a step length $L$ is half of that distance for symmetric walking, so $L_s=2L$. The cadence $c$ is steps per unit time.

Proposition 1.1 (speed bookkeeping). For symmetric walking with step length $L$ and cadence $c$, the mean forward speed is $$v=cL.$$ If cadence is reported in steps per minute, $v=c_{\rm min}L/60$.
During one step the body advances $L$. If $c$ steps occur per second, distance per second is $cL$. The stride version is the same statement because one stride advances $2L$ and occurs at frequency $c/2$. ∎
EventMechanical meaningWhy it matters

Double support means both feet are in contact; single support means only one foot is in contact. Walking has double support. Running does not: it replaces double support with aerial flight. That difference is why this module is pendular and Module 9 becomes spring-mass.

2. The inverted-pendulum COM arc

In single support, the stance leg is often well a\approximated as a nearly rigid link of length $\ell$ from the contact point to the COM. Let $\theta$ be the leg angle from vertical, positive forward. The COM coordinates relative to the stance foot are

$$x=\ell\sin\theta,\qquad y=\ell\cos\theta.$$

When $\theta=0$, the COM is highest. At the beginning or end of the step, $|\theta|$ is larger and the COM is lower. If the leg angle sweeps from $-\alpha$ to $+\alpha$, the vertical rise from step edge to midstance is exact.

Proposition 2.1 (pendular vault height). The COM rise during stance is $$\Delta h=\ell(1-\cos\alpha).$$
At midstance, $y_{\rm mid}=\ell$. At either edge, $y_{\rm edge}=\ell\cos\alpha$. Their difference is $\ell-\ell\cos\alpha$. ∎

If no work is done, the mechanical energy $E=\frac12 Mv^2+Mgy$ is nearly conserved across the middle of stance. The body slows as it rises and speeds up as it falls. This is the pendular exchange that makes walking economical — and, exactly as in Module 7, the natural rate of the leg-length pendulum is $\omega_0=\sqrt{g/\ell}$.

Proposition 2.2 (pendular speed limit and the Froude number). Because the stance foot can only push, the ground reaction is compressive, and at midstance the vertical balance forces the speed to obey $v^2\le g\ell$. The dimensionless group $$\mathrm{Fr}=\frac{v^2}{g\ell}$$ therefore cannot exceed $1$ in pure vaulting; walking gives way to running well before that, near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx\tfrac12$.
At midstance the COM sweeps horizontally on a circular arc of radius $\ell$ about the stance foot, so it carries a centripetal acceleration $v^2/\ell$ directed downward, toward the foot. Newton's second law in the vertical direction is $$Mg-N=M\frac{v^2}{\ell},$$ with $N\ge0$ the vertical ground reaction — the foot can push up but cannot pull the body down. Solving, $N=M\!\left(g-\dfrac{v^2}{\ell}\right)$. The contact constraint $N\ge0$ then requires $v^2\le g\ell$, i.e. $\mathrm{Fr}\le1$. At $\mathrm{Fr}=1$ the ground reaction vanishes at midstance and the vault turns ballistic, so any faster gait must leave the ground — that is running (Module 9). Humans switch earlier, near $\mathrm{Fr}\approx\tfrac12$: with $\ell\approx0.9$ m this is $v\approx\sqrt{0.5\,g\ell}\approx2.0\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, where the growing push-off and swing costs overtake the pendular saving. ∎
Dynamic similarity. Because $\mathrm{Fr}=v^2/(g\ell)$ is the only dimensionless speed in the vaulting model, geometrically similar legged bodies walk alike at equal $\mathrm{Fr}$, not at equal absolute speed — a mouse, a human, and (were it pendular) a sauropod share one gait chart in Froude units. This is why $\mathrm{Fr}$, not $v$, predicts the walk–run transition across species and body sizes.

3. Ground reaction force and COP trajectory in walking

In Module 7, the center of pressure (COP) was the point inside the foot where the vertical ground reaction could be taken to act. In walking, the same point becomes a moving record of the stance foot's loading. At heel strike the COP starts near the posterior heel, travels forward along the foot during stance, and leaves near the forefoot or toes.

Let $\mathbf r_{\rm com}$ be the COM position, $M$ total body mass, and $\mathbf F_{\rm grf}$ the ground reaction force. Newton's second law gives

Proposition 3.1 (GRF is COM acceleration plus weight). During stance, if air resistance is negligible, $$\mathbf F_{\rm grf}=M\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm com}+Mg\mathbf e_y,$$ where $\mathbf e_y$ points upward.
The external forces on the whole body are ground reaction and weight: $\mathbf F_{\rm grf}-Mg\mathbf e_y=M\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm com}$. Rearranging gives the result. ∎

The COP tells where that resultant acts; the GRF tells which acceleration it produces. A force plate measures both, but the equations explain why the two are not redundant: location sets moment arms, while vector magnitude sets COM acceleration.

4. Compass gait model

The simplest walking model that has alternating feet is the compass gait model. It has two rigid legs of length $\ell$ joined at a hip mass. During single support, one leg is pinned at the stance foot and the other swings forward. At heel strike, the leading foot becomes the new stance foot and the old stance leg becomes the trailing leg. That relabeling is not bookkeeping trivia; it is where collision loss enters.

Let $\theta$ be the stance-leg angle from vertical and $\phi$ the swing-leg angle from vertical. In the simplest point-mass version, the single-support equation for the hip angle is the inverted-pendulum equation

$$\ddot\theta=\frac{g}{\ell}\sin\theta+\frac{u}{M\ell^2},$$

where $u$ is a generalized actuation term. The gravity term carries the $+\sin\theta$ sign of Module 7's unstable inverted pendulum: standing fought this toppling, walking lets it run, catching the fall on the swing leg. A passive walker sets $u=0$ and walks only if slope and collision geometry supply a stable limit cycle (section 9).

The relabeling at heel strike is where mechanical energy actually leaves the model, and it is an impulsive collision we can solve exactly.

Proposition 4.1 (heel-strike impact map). For the point-mass compass walker with inter-leg angle $2\alpha$ at contact, the instantaneous heel-strike collision scales the angular velocity by $\cos 2\alpha$: $$\dot\theta^{+}=\dot\theta^{-}\cos 2\alpha,$$ so a fraction $\sin^2 2\alpha$ of the kinetic energy is dissipated at every step.
Heel strike is instantaneous and perfectly inelastic: the leading foot sticks and becomes the new pivot while the trailing foot releases. The impulsive ground reaction acts at the new contact point, so it has no moment about that point, and the angular momentum of the hip mass about the new contact is conserved across the collision. Just before impact the mass moves at speed $v^-=\ell\dot\theta^-$ perpendicular to the trailing (old stance) leg. The new stance leg makes angle $2\alpha$ with the old one, so the direction perpendicular to the new leg is rotated by $2\alpha$ from $v^-$, and the velocity component that produces rotation about the new foot is $v^-\cos 2\alpha$. Hence the angular momentum about the new contact is $$H^-=M\ell\,(v^-\cos 2\alpha)=M\ell^2\dot\theta^-\cos 2\alpha \quad\text{before},\qquad H^+=M\ell^2\dot\theta^+\quad\text{after},$$ and conservation $H^-=H^+$ gives $\dot\theta^+=\dot\theta^-\cos 2\alpha$. The kinetic-energy ratio is $(\dot\theta^+/\dot\theta^-)^2=\cos^2 2\alpha$, so the lost fraction is $1-\cos^2 2\alpha=\sin^2 2\alpha$ — precisely the redirection loss computed energetically in section 5. ∎

5. Step-to-step transition cost

The inverted-pendulum arc saves energy within a step, but consecutive arcs point in different directions. At the end of one step the COM velocity is directed along the old arc; at the beginning of the next, the velocity must align with the new arc. The redirection costs work.

Proposition 5.1 (redirection loss and its step-length scaling). Redirecting the COM velocity of magnitude $v$ by the inter-leg angle $2\alpha$ through the inelastic heel strike dissipates $$E_{\rm loss}=\tfrac12 Mv^2\sin^2(2\alpha)\;\approx\;\tfrac12 Mv^2\Big(\frac{L}{\ell}\Big)^2\quad(\text{small }\alpha),$$ so the cost per step grows as $v^2L^2$ and the cost per unit distance as $v^2L$: long, fast steps are expensive.
By the heel-strike impact map (section 4, Prop 4.1), the collision scales the speed by $\cos 2\alpha$, so the kinetic energy drops from $\tfrac12 Mv^2$ to $\tfrac12 Mv^2\cos^2 2\alpha$ and the dissipated energy is $$E_{\rm loss}=\tfrac12 Mv^2\big(1-\cos^2 2\alpha\big)=\tfrac12 Mv^2\sin^2 2\alpha.$$ For a step of length $L$ the leg sweeps from $-\alpha$ to $+\alpha$ with $\sin\alpha=(L/2)/\ell$, so for small angles $2\alpha\approx L/\ell$ and $\sin^2 2\alpha\approx (L/\ell)^2$, giving the stated scaling. Dividing by the step length $L$ shows the cost of transport from transitions rises as $v^2L$. ∎

The redirection must be paid for somehow, but how it is paid changes the bill. The key result is that spreading the redirection over more, gentler impulses is cheaper than one hard collision.

Proposition 5.2 (why pre-emptive push-off is cheap). Splitting a velocity redirection of total angle $\beta$ into $n$ equal sequential impulses reduces the collision loss from $\tfrac12 Mv^2\beta^2$ to $\tfrac12 Mv^2\beta^2/n$. Consequently a trailing-leg push-off that does the first half of the redirection as positive work, immediately before the heel-strike collision does the second half, cuts the powered cost of the transition to a fraction of the collision-only value.
One inelastic redirection by $\beta$ costs $\tfrac12 Mv^2\sin^2\beta\approx\tfrac12 Mv^2\beta^2$. Replacing it by $n$ equal redirections of $\beta/n$ each costs $n\cdot\tfrac12 Mv^2(\beta/n)^2=\tfrac12 Mv^2\beta^2/n$ — the loss falls as $1/n$ because the dissipated component is quadratic in the per-impulse angle. Push-off supplies the first redirection as positive muscle work (an impulse along the trailing leg) rather than a dissipative collision, so the heel strike is left only the residual angle to destroy. The optimal pre-emptive push-off (impulse along the trailing leg the instant before contact, matched to the collision) is the classic simplest-walker result: it powers the same transition with one-quarter of the work required if the collision is left to dissipate and hip work repays it afterward. ∎

This is why ankle power in late stance (section 6) is not merely propulsion: it is collision management, timed just before the opposite heel strikes so the transition costs as little as the geometry allows.

6. Joint torques and joint powers

Inverse dynamics estimates the net joint torques required to produce measured motion under measured external forces — cashing the promise made in Module 3 that measured segment inertias would later drive a gait torque estimate.

Proposition 6.1 (segmental inverse dynamics). For a planar body segment of moment of inertia $I$ and angular acceleration $\ddot\theta$, acted on by a known external moment $M_{\rm ext}$ (gravity, the ground reaction, and the already-solved distal joint), the net proximal joint torque is $$\boxed{\;\tau=I\ddot\theta-M_{\rm ext}\;}.$$ Applied foot-first up the chain, it yields the ankle, knee, and hip torques in turn.
Euler's rotational equation for the segment is $I\ddot\theta=\sum M=\tau+M_{\rm ext}$, the sum of the proximal joint torque $\tau$ and every other moment $M_{\rm ext}$; solving gives $\tau=I\ddot\theta-M_{\rm ext}$. Because each segment's distal end carries the joint reaction of the segment below it, the recursion starts at the free (foot) end, where the only external load is the measured ground reaction of section 3, and each solved joint supplies the $M_{\rm ext}$ for the next segment up. ∎

Solving foot-first up the chain gives the ankle, knee, and hip moments. The power at joint $j$ is then a signed rate of work.

Joint power. If $\tau_j$ is the net internal joint torque and $\dot\theta_j$ is the joint angular velocity, $$P_j=\tau_j\dot\theta_j.$$ Positive $P_j$ means the joint is doing net mechanical work on the body; negative $P_j$ means the joint is absorbing work.

Walking has a recognizable power signature: the ankle produces a large positive burst in late stance, the knee absorbs energy after heel strike and during swing control, and the hip alternates between powering the trunk and controlling limb motion.

7. Speed, cadence, stride length, and cost

A walker can increase speed by increasing cadence, increasing step length, or both. Neither is free. Long steps increase the redirection angle $\alpha$ and hence the collision cost of section 5 ($\propto v^2\sin^2 2\alpha$). Very high cadence increases swing work, muscle activation frequency, and the joint work of section 6. Preferred walking speed emerges from this tradeoff, and the cost of transport it minimises is the observable of the second lab in section 11.

A minimal cost proxy can be written as

$$C(v,L)=A\,v^2\sin^2\!\left(2\arcsin\frac{L}{2\ell}\right)+B\left(\frac{v}{L}\right)^2+C_0,$$

where the first term represents step-to-step transition cost, the second penalizes high cadence $c=v/L$, and $C_0$ is baseline cost. This proxy is not a full metabolic model. Its purpose is to make the optimum unavoidable: one cannot minimize transition cost and cadence cost at the same step length.

8. Arm swing and whole-body angular momentum

Arm swing is often described as passive, but passive does not mean irrelevant. The legs exchange angular momentum with the trunk each step. If the arms did not swing, the trunk would have to absorb more yaw and roll momentum, requiring more muscular stabilization. Arm swing gives the body a low-cost moving counterweight.

Proposition 8.1 (angular-momentum bookkeeping). About the whole-body COM, the angular momentum satisfies $$\dot{\mathbf L}_G=\sum \mathbf M_G^{\rm ext}.$$ If external moments about the COM are small over part of the stride, arm and leg angular momenta must a\approximately cancel within the body.
This is Euler's angular-momentum balance for the whole body about its COM. With small net external moment, $\dot{\mathbf L}_G\approx0$, so internal segment angular momenta trade against each other. ∎

This is why tying the arms down changes the gait: the legs still swing, but the missing counter-swing must be made up by the trunk and hip muscles.

9. Passive dynamic walking and stability

A passive dynamic walker can walk downhill with no motors. It is not violating energy conservation. Gravity supplies the energy lost at each collision, and the walker settles into a stable limit cycle: one stride maps the state back to itself.

Let $\mathbf z_n=(\theta_n,\dot\theta_n)$ be the state just after heel strike on step $n$. One stride defines a map

$$\mathbf z_{n+1}=\mathcal P(\mathbf z_n;\gamma),$$

where $\gamma$ is the slope angle. A periodic gait is a fixed point $\mathbf z^*=\mathcal P(\mathbf z^*;\gamma)$, and its local stability is determined by the eigenvalues of the Jacobian $D\mathcal P(\mathbf z^*)$. If their magnitudes are less than one, nearby steps converge back to the gait — the walker recovers from a small disturbance with no control at all. We do not need the full map to find which slope sustains the gait, though: an energy balance fixes it.

Proposition 9.1 (passive limit-cycle energy balance). A periodic compass gait on a slope $\gamma$ requires the gravitational energy released per step to equal the heel-strike loss, $$MgL\sin\gamma=\tfrac12 Mv^2\sin^2 2\alpha,$$ which for small angles selects a mid-stance speed $v^2\approx g\ell\,\dfrac{\sin\gamma}{\alpha}$: faster on steeper slopes, and impossible on the level.
Over one step the configuration returns to itself, so the mechanical energy is unchanged step to step. Only two things change it: gravity does positive work $MgL\sin\gamma$ as the COM descends the slope by $L\sin\gamma$ over a step of length $L$, and the heel strike dissipates $E_{\rm loss}=\tfrac12 Mv^2\sin^2 2\alpha$ (section 5, Prop 5.1). Periodicity demands they balance, $MgL\sin\gamma=\tfrac12 Mv^2\sin^2 2\alpha$. Substituting $L\approx2\ell\alpha$ and $\sin 2\alpha\approx2\alpha$ gives $Mg\,(2\ell\alpha)\sin\gamma=\tfrac12 Mv^2(2\alpha)^2$, hence $v^2\approx g\ell\sin\gamma/\alpha$. For $\ell=0.9$ m, a step half-angle $\alpha\approx0.3$ rad and a gentle slope $\gamma\approx0.05$ rad ($3^\circ$), this gives $v\approx1.2\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ — a realistic walking speed from gravity alone. On the level ($\gamma=0$) the only solution is $v=0$: gravity supplies nothing, so the losses cannot be replaced and steady walking must inject exactly $\tfrac12 Mv^2\sin^2 2\alpha$ per step by active push-off. ∎
Passive walking lesson. A gait can be mechanically self-stabilizing over a finite basin (the Jacobian eigenvalues sit inside the unit circle), and on a slope gravity even pays the running cost — but level-ground human walking still needs muscles: without slope or actuation, the heel-strike loss of Prop 5.1 drains the energy and the walker halts.

10. When walking fails: older-adult falls and post-fall decline

A fall is not simply "bad balance." For older adults, the causal stack is best read from the bottom upward: chemistry/materials set the tissue and signaling substrate; biology turns that substrate into sensing, force, reaction-time, and control capacity; physics is the visible gait event when the available control margin is no longer enough for the perturbation. Physics is therefore not separate from aging biology and chemistry, but it is still the layer that decides the actual motion, impact energy, and contact path. After the fall, biology and chemistry decide whether recovery restores function or whether pain, inflammation, fear, and unloading drive decline.

Chemistry/materials layer: the aging substrate

Chemistry helps explain both why the fall becomes more likely and why it becomes more injurious. Low vitamin D and disturbed calcium-parathyroid hormone balance can reduce muscle performance and bone mineralization. Chronic low-grade inflammation promotes catabolism, worsens muscle quality, and biases remodeling away from robust tissue maintenance. Diabetes-related glycation and microvascular injury can damage peripheral nerves, degrading sensation before the fall. Sedatives, anticholinergics, antihypertensives, alcohol, dehydration, and electrolyte disturbances alter receptor signaling, alertness, blood pressure regulation, and reaction time. At impact, the same chemistry/material state sets the failure threshold: bone mineral density, trabecular architecture, collagen cross-link quality, and remodeling balance determine whether a hip, wrist, vertebra, or skull survives the mechanical energy delivered by the fall.

Biology layer: the weakened control system

The pre-fall biology is the loss of reserve in the control loop. Sarcopenia reduces muscle cross-sectional area, especially fast force-producing capacity, so the corrective step and hip strategy develop too little impulse too late. Motor-unit remodeling makes activation coarser; tendon and muscle stiffness changes reduce power transfer; vestibular, visual, and proprioceptive signals become noisier; peripheral neuropathy makes the foot-ground state less certain; and executive load matters because walking while turning, talking, or scanning a curb is a dual-task control problem. The result is not merely "weakness." It is a slower, noisier, lower-authority feedback controller operating with less mechanical margin.

Physics layer: the visible fall event

The physics question is whether the extrapolated COM can be brought back inside a recoverable base of support before the body rotates or translates too far. Older adults often walk with shorter steps and lower speed as a rational stability strategy, but several variables shrink the recovery margin at once: reduced toe clearance increases trip probability; slower reaction time delays corrective stepping; weaker hip abductors and ankle plantarflexors reduce the impulse that can redirect the COM; and impaired sensation delays detection of where the foot and ground are.

Fall-margin inequality. A step can recover a forward perturbation only if the required capture location is within feasible step reach $x_s^{\max}$: $x_{\rm com}+\frac{\dot x_{\rm com}}{\omega_0}\le x_s^{\max},\qquad \omega_0=\sqrt{g/h_{\rm com}}.$ Aging can reduce the right side by strength/range limits and increase the left side by delayed response.

Biology after the fall: why health often declines afterward

The post-fall decline is not psychological weakness. It is a coupled biological cascade. Pain reduces loading; unloading accelerates sarcopenia and bone loss; fear of falling narrows activity; less activity worsens cardiovascular reserve, insulin sensitivity, balance calibration, and muscle power; hospitalization adds sleep disruption, inflammation, infection risk, and rapid deconditioning. The next fall then becomes more likely. A single mechanical event can become a systems-biology transition to a lower functional state.

12. Problems, diagnostics, and limitations

Conceptual problems

C1. Identify which part of a walking step is controlled falling and which part is catching. Probes: mechanism before terminology.
Solution
The fall is the descent after midstance toward the next contact; the catch is the leading-foot collision and loading response that redirects the COM.
C2. Explain why walking has double support but running has flight. Probes: gait classification.
Solution
Walking keeps at least one foot on the ground and has a double-support exchange. Running has an aerial interval, so its mechanics are spring-mass rather than inverted-pendulum dominated.
C3. Why is midstance not the most energetically expensive part of walking? Probes: exchange versus loss.
Solution
In ideal pendular motion, kinetic and potential energy exchange at midstance with little net loss. The expensive part is redirecting velocity between steps.
C4. Why does the COP move from heel to toe in stance? Probes: foot loading.
Solution
As load transfers from heel strike through midfoot to forefoot push-off, the pressure-weighted average of contact pressure moves forward.
C5. Explain why long steps can be costly even if cadence is low. Probes: transition angle.
Solution
Longer steps increase the angle between successive inverted-pendulum arcs, increasing collision/redirection loss.
C6. Why does ankle push-off reduce heel-strike loss? Probes: timing of work.
Solution
It redirects the COM before the leading leg collision, leaving a smaller incompatible velocity component for heel strike to dissipate.
C7. What does positive joint power mean? Probes: sign convention.
Solution
Positive $P=\tau\dot\theta$ means the joint's net torque adds mechanical energy; negative power means it absorbs energy.
C8. Why can arm swing reduce walking cost? Probes: angular momentum.
Solution
The arms swing opposite the legs, reducing trunk angular momentum that muscles would otherwise need to control.
C9. Why is passive dynamic walking not free on level ground? Probes: energy source.
Solution
The passive walker relies on downhill gravitational work. On level ground, muscles must replace heel-strike losses.
C10. Why do older-adult falls require physics, chemistry, and biology together? Probes: stack reasoning.
Solution
Physics explains loss of stability and impact; chemistry/materials explain fracture and tissue tolerance; biology explains sarcopenia, fear, inflammation, and deconditioning after the event.

Derivational problems

D1. Derive $v=cL$ for symmetric walking. Probes: gait bookkeeping.
Solution
Each step advances $L$ and there are $c$ steps per second, so speed is distance/time $v=cL$.
D2. Derive $\Delta h=\ell(1-\cos\alpha)$ for an inverted-pendulum stance arc. Probes: COM geometry.
Solution
The COM height is $\ell\cos\theta$; compare edge $\theta=\alpha$ to midstance $\theta=0$.
D3. Derive the Froude number by nondimensionalizing $v^2$ with $g\ell$. Probes: dynamic similarity.
Solution
$g\ell$ has units $\mathrm{m^2s^{-2}}$, the same as $v^2$, so $v^2/(g\ell)$ is the natural dimensionless speed.
D4. Starting from Newton's law, derive $\mathbf F_{\rm grf}=M\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm com}+Mg\mathbf e_y$. Probes: whole-body force balance.
Solution
Sum external forces: $\mathbf F_{\rm grf}-Mg\mathbf e_y=M\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm com}$, then rearrange.
D5. Derive the compass-gait relabeling condition at heel strike. Probes: event maps.
Solution
The old swing foot becomes the new stance pivot, so pre-impact swing-leg angle becomes post-impact stance-leg angle with sign convention changed; velocities map through the impact constraint.
D6. Derive the redirection loss $\frac12 Mv^2\sin^2(2\alpha)$. Probes: collision geometry.
Solution
The component perpendicular to the new admissible tangent is $v\sin(2\alpha)$; an inelastic collision removes its kinetic energy.
D7. Derive $P_j=\tau_j\dot\theta_j$ from work rate. Probes: rotational power.
Solution
Rotational work is $dW=\tau_j d\theta_j$. Divide by $dt$ to get $P_j=\tau_j\dot\theta_j$.
D8. Derive a first-order condition for optimal step length in a cost proxy $C(L)=a\sin^2(2\arcsin(L/2\ell))+b/L^2$. Probes: optimum, not substitution.
Solution
Differentiate $C$ with respect to $L$ and set $dC/dL=0$; transition and cadence costs scale oppositely, so an interior optimum can exist.
D9. Derive the capture inequality $x+\dot x/\omega_0\le x_s^{\max}$. Probes: fall margin.
Solution
For the linear inverted pendulum, the divergent component is $x+\dot x/\omega_0$. A feasible step must place support beyond that component, so the maximum feasible step reach $x_s^{\max}$ must exceed it.
D10. Show why passive downhill walking needs slope work to equal collision loss over a cycle. Probes: limit-cycle energy.
Solution
On a periodic gait, total mechanical energy repeats each step. Therefore gravitational work from descent plus actuator work must equal impact and frictional losses. Passive means actuator work is zero, so slope work pays the losses.

Computational problems

K1. Simulate COM height over one step for $\ell=0.95$ m and $\alpha=12^\circ,16^\circ,20^\circ$. Which step angle produces the largest pendular energy exchange? Probes: numerical geometry.
Solution
Compute $\Delta h=\ell(1-\cos\alpha)$ for each angle. The largest angle gives the largest rise: numerically, with $\ell=0.95$ m, $\Delta h=2.1,\,3.7,\,5.7$ cm and $Mg\Delta h\approx14,\,25,\,39$ J for $12^\circ,16^\circ,20^\circ$, so the $20^\circ$ step exchanges the most energy.
K2. Sweep walking speed from 0.6 to 2.0 m/s and compute $\mathrm{Fr}$ for leg lengths 0.8, 0.95, and 1.1 m. Probes: dynamic similarity.
Solution
Use $\mathrm{Fr}=v^2/(g\ell)$. At $v=1.3$ m/s, $\mathrm{Fr}=0.22,\,0.18,\,0.16$ for $\ell=0.8,\,0.95,\,1.1$ m; the short-legged walker sits at a higher Froude number for the same speed, and reaches the walk–run range ($\mathrm{Fr}\approx0.5$) sooner.
K3. Optimize step length in the cost proxy in section 7 for $v=1.3$ m/s. Probes: optimization.
Solution
Evaluate the proxy on a grid of feasible $L$ or solve $dC/dL=0$ numerically. With the section-7 form (transition cost against a cadence term $B(v/L)^2$), the grid minimum at $v=1.3$ m/s sits near $L^{*}\approx0.6$ m — close to the preferred step length; too-long steps raise transition loss, too-short steps raise cadence cost.
K4. Compare transition loss before and after a push-off impulse that reduces the redirection angle by 25 percent. Probes: intervention effect.
Solution
Apply the loss $\propto\sin^2(\text{angle})$ twice. Cutting the redirection angle to $0.75\times$ multiplies the loss by $0.75^2=0.56$, a $44\%$ reduction — a large saving from a modest pre-emptive push-off.
K5. From synthetic ankle torque and angular velocity curves, integrate positive ankle work over stance. Probes: power integration.
Solution
Compute $P(t)=\tau(t)\dot\theta(t)$ and integrate only $P^{+}$ by the trapezoid rule. For the synthetic terminal-stance burst this gives $\int P^{+}\,dt\approx15.8$ J of positive ankle work per step.
K6. Add noise to joint angle data, finite-difference it twice, and observe how inverse-dynamics torque changes. Probes: measurement sensitivity.
Solution
Second derivatives amplify noise, so unfiltered kinematics produce large torque artifacts. This forwards to Module 15.
K7. Sweep cadence and step length combinations that produce the same speed. Which combinations minimize the cost proxy? Probes: redundancy in gait choice.
Solution
Constrain $cL=v$ and evaluate the proxy over feasible $L$. The minimum lands near $L^{*}\approx0.6$ m (cadence $c\approx2.2$ steps/s at $v=1.3$ m/s): the redundancy in $(L,c)$ is broken because transition cost rises with $L$ while cadence cost falls with $L$.
K8. Model arm swing as reducing trunk angular-momentum amplitude by 30 percent. Estimate the change in a quadratic stabilization cost. Probes: angular-momentum cost.
Solution
If cost is proportional to amplitude squared, a 30 percent amplitude reduction gives cost multiplier $0.7^2=0.49$.
K9. Iterate a one-dimensional stride map $z_{n+1}=az_n+b$ for different $a$. Which values are stable? Probes: limit-cycle stability.
Solution
The fixed point is stable when $|a|\lt1$; perturbations decay geometrically. Iterating confirms it: $a=0.6$ and $a=0.9$ converge to the fixed point, while $a=1.15$ diverges — the passive-gait Jacobian eigenvalue must lie inside the unit circle (section 9).
K10. Sweep reaction delay, step reach, and COM speed in the fall-margin inequality. Which variable has the strongest effect on loss of recoverability? Probes: older-adult fall sensitivity.
Solution
Compute $x+v\Delta+v/\omega_0$ against $x_{\rm max}^{\rm step}$. Numerically the margin's elasticity is $\approx0.88$ to speed versus $\approx0.35$ to reaction delay and $\approx0.30$ to step reach: speed dominates because it enters both the reaction drift $v\Delta$ and the divergent term $v/\omega_0$.

Diagnostics

  1. If someone says walking is free because energy is exchanged pendularly, ask where heel-strike loss went.
  2. If a gait figure has arrows but no foot, COP, COM, or event labels, it is not yet a gait figure.
  3. If inverse dynamics reports torque without angular velocity, it cannot report joint power.
  4. If an older-adult fall explanation names only "balance," it is missing tissue chemistry and recovery biology.
  5. If a passive walker is drawn on level ground without actuation, it must eventually stop.

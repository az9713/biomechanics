0. Motivation - the number you never measured

Every module of this course has been written in the currency of joint torque. Module 1 balanced the moment a biceps must supply to hold a weight; Module 5 turned neural drive into muscle force and then into joint torque; Modules 8, 11, and 13 read off the ankle, knee, hip, and shoulder torques of walking, reaching, and rising from a chair. In every case the torque was given - a number we computed from a model, or asked a simulation to produce. But no instrument measures joint torque. You cannot put a sensor on a knee. What you can measure is where the body is, how hard it presses on the ground, and how loudly its muscles fire - and from those, and a model, you must infer the torque.

This module builds that inference, honestly and end to end. It is the bridge between the mechanical models of the whole course and the data a laboratory or a phone actually collects. The task has three hard parts, and the module is organised around them.

First, the sensors do not measure the state you want. A camera records the two-dimensional pixel position of a marker; a force plate records three force components and a point of application; an inertial measurement unit (IMU - a small chip pairing an accelerometer and a gyroscope) records linear acceleration and angular rate in its own tilting frame; electromyography (EMG) records a voltage that rises with muscle activation but is not force. None of these is a joint angle, and none is a torque. Section 1 lays out this measurement chain - what each instrument records, and the observation model that maps the body's true state onto it.

Second, the operation that converts position into the dynamics - differentiation - amplifies noise catastrophically. Inverse dynamics needs accelerations, and acceleration is the second time-derivative of position. Differentiating a noisy signal is the single most dangerous step in all of biomechanics: as we will prove in Section 3, the variance of a finite-difference derivative grows like the noise variance divided by the time step squared, so halving the sampling interval to sharpen a derivative quadruples the noise variance in it. Sections 3 and 4 build the defence - smoothing, and a principled choice of how much - and show there is an optimal amount of smoothing that trades bias against variance.

Third, the answer is only as trustworthy as its error bars. A torque curve with no uncertainty is a claim without evidence. Sections 8 and 9 propagate the measurement noise, the body-segment-parameter uncertainty, and the model's own inconsistency through the pipeline, so the output is a torque with a quantified error - and show how to identify the model's parameters from data and validate the whole chain against an independent measurement.

The spine is the pipeline of Fig. 1, built left to right: the instruments (Section 1), the planar body model and its inertial parameters (Section 2), smoothing and differentiation (Sections 3-4), the centre of mass and its force-plate check (Section 5), the inverse-dynamics recursion that is the heart of the module (Section 6), the assembled pipeline on synthetic data (Section 7), the error analysis (Section 8), and parameter identification and validation (Section 9). Four labs and a thirty-problem set close it. By the end, the torques the course has spent fourteen modules using will be numbers you know how to produce - and how far to trust.

1. The measurement chain: what each instrument records

The object of the whole module is the body's configuration over time. For a planar rigid-body model (Section 2) that configuration is a vector of joint angles $\mathbf q(t)=(q_1,\dots,q_n)$, and the dynamics also need its first and second time-derivatives, the joint angular velocities $\dot{\mathbf q}$ and accelerations $\ddot{\mathbf q}$. No sensor delivers $\mathbf q$ directly. Each instrument returns some function of the state, corrupted by noise; recovering $\mathbf q$ from those readings is an estimation problem, and naming the map from state to reading precisely is the first step.

Definition 1.1 (observation model). Let the body's state at time $t$ be $\mathbf x(t)$ (positions, and where relevant velocities). An instrument returns a reading $$\mathbf y(t)=\mathbf h\big(\mathbf x(t)\big)+\boldsymbol\varepsilon(t),$$ where $\mathbf h$ is the (generally nonlinear) observation map fixed by the instrument's physics and geometry, and $\boldsymbol\varepsilon(t)$ is measurement noise with zero mean and covariance $\mathbf R=\operatorname{cov}(\boldsymbol\varepsilon)$. Estimation is the inverse problem: recover $\mathbf x$ (hence $\mathbf q,\dot{\mathbf q},\ddot{\mathbf q}$) from the readings $\mathbf y$ given $\mathbf h$ and the noise statistics.

The instruments of movement science differ in what their $\mathbf h$ is, and in how noisy and how direct they are. The five that matter are collected in Fig. 2 and the table that follows.

Three features of this table organise the rest of the module. The force plate is the odd one out - and the most valuable. It measures a force directly and precisely, whereas every other instrument measures a position (or, for the IMU, an acceleration) that must be processed into the dynamics. That is why inverse dynamics is anchored at the foot: the one place a clean force is known is the ground contact, and the recursion (Section 6) marches up the body from there. The IMU is the one device that measures acceleration without differentiating - a genuine advantage - but it pays for it with bias drift that corrupts any attempt to integrate back to position, the mirror image of the differentiation problem. EMG never enters the torque calculation. It tells you which muscle is active and when, which is invaluable for validating a result or resolving the redundancy of Section 9, but because the map from activation to force depends on length, velocity, and fatigue (Module 5), surface EMG is not a calibrated force sensor. Throughout, we keep the instruments and the model separate: the instrument supplies $\mathbf y$; the model supplies $\mathbf h$ and the dynamics; estimation joins them.

2. The planar marker model and body-segment parameters

To turn marker positions into joint angles, and joint angles into torques, we need a model of the body as a linked chain of rigid segments with known inertias. This module uses the simplest model that still captures the mechanics of the sagittal-plane movements the course has studied - squatting, a step, a lift - the planar rigid-body model: rigid segments joined by pin (revolute) joints, all moving in one vertical plane. Everything generalises to three dimensions, but the planar case exposes every idea - segment angles, differentiation, the inverse-dynamics recursion, error propagation - without the bookkeeping of 3-D rotations.

Definition 2.1 (planar segment and its markers). A rigid segment $i$ (say the shank) is a body of mass $m_i$, with its centre of mass at a point $\mathbf c_i$ and a moment of inertia $I_i$ about that centre (for planar motion, a single scalar). Its orientation is a single segment angle $\theta_i$, the angle its long axis makes with the horizontal. Skin markers are fixed points on the segment; the vector between the proximal marker (nearer the trunk) and the distal marker (farther) points along the segment, so $$\theta_i=\operatorname{atan2}\big(y_{\rm dist}-y_{\rm prox},\;x_{\rm dist}-x_{\rm prox}\big),$$ recovering the segment angle from two measured marker positions. A joint angle is a difference of adjacent segment angles, e.g. the knee flexion $q_{\rm knee}=\theta_{\rm thigh}-\theta_{\rm shank}$.

The geometry recovers the angles. The dynamics needs more: each segment's mass, the location of its centre of mass along its length, and its moment of inertia. These body-segment parameters (BSP) are not measured on the subject - you cannot weigh a living person's shank - but are estimated from anthropometric regression tables built from cadaver studies and volumetric scans, which express each segment's mass as a fraction of body mass, its centre-of-mass location as a fraction of segment length, and its inertia through a radius of gyration.

Definition 2.2 (body-segment parameters). For segment $i$ of a person of total mass $M$ and with segment length $L_i$, the anthropometric model gives $$m_i=f_i\,M,\qquad c_i=\rho_i\,L_i,\qquad I_i=m_i\,(k_i L_i)^2,$$ where $f_i$ is the segment's mass fraction, $\rho_i$ the centre-of-mass fraction (measured from the proximal joint), and $k_i$ the radius-of-gyration fraction (both dimensionless multipliers of $L_i$). The parameters $f_i,\rho_i,k_i$ are read from a table (below); $M$ and $L_i$ are measured on the subject. Because these come from population regressions, they carry an uncertainty - typically $\sim$10-20% on $m_i$ and $I_i$ - that Section 8 propagates into the torque estimate.

3. Noise, smoothing, and why differentiation is dangerous

Inverse dynamics needs velocities and accelerations, and the only way to get them from marker positions is to differentiate. This is the pipeline's most treacherous step, because differentiation is a high-pass operation: it multiplies each frequency component by that frequency, so it amplifies exactly the fast, small wiggles that measurement noise is made of. To see how badly, model the measurement first.

Definition 3.1 (sampled signal with noise). A joint angle is sampled at times $t_k=k\,\Delta t$ (sampling interval $\Delta t=1/f_s$ for sampling rate $f_s$; here $k$ is the integer time-sample index, distinct from the gyration fraction $k_i$ of Definition 2.2), giving readings $$q_k=q(t_k)+\varepsilon_k,\qquad \varepsilon_k\ \text{i.i.d., }\ \mathbb E[\varepsilon_k]=0,\ \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_k)=\sigma^2,$$ with the noise samples $\varepsilon_k$ independent across time and of variance $\sigma^2$. A finite-difference derivative is any fixed linear combination of the $q_k$; its noise is the same linear combination of the $\varepsilon_k$.
Proposition 3.1 (finite differences amplify noise as a power of $1/\Delta t$). Under Definition 3.1, the central-difference velocity and second-difference acceleration, $$\dot q_k=\frac{q_{k+1}-q_{k-1}}{2\,\Delta t},\qquad \ddot q_k=\frac{q_{k+1}-2q_k+q_{k-1}}{\Delta t^2},$$ have noise variances $$\boxed{\;\operatorname{Var}(\dot q_k)=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\,\Delta t^2},\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\ddot q_k)=\frac{6\,\sigma^2}{\Delta t^4}.\;}$$ So the velocity noise scales as $1/\Delta t$ and the acceleration noise as $1/\Delta t^2$: faster sampling makes the raw derivatives noisier, not cleaner.
A finite difference is a linear combination $\sum_j a_j q_{k+j}$; its noise part is $\sum_j a_j\varepsilon_{k+j}$. Because the $\varepsilon$ are independent with variance $\sigma^2$, the variance of a weighted sum is $\sigma^2\sum_j a_j^2$ (cross terms vanish since $\operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon_i,\varepsilon_j)=0$ for $i\neq j$). For the central velocity the weights are $a_{+1}=+\tfrac1{2\Delta t}$, $a_{-1}=-\tfrac1{2\Delta t}$, so $\sum a_j^2=\tfrac1{4\Delta t^2}+\tfrac1{4\Delta t^2}=\tfrac1{2\Delta t^2}$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\dot q_k)=\sigma^2/(2\Delta t^2)$. For the second difference the weights are $\tfrac1{\Delta t^2}(1,-2,1)$, so $\sum a_j^2=\tfrac1{\Delta t^4}(1+4+1)=\tfrac6{\Delta t^4}$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\ddot q_k)=6\sigma^2/\Delta t^4$. ∎

The numbers are sobering. Take a marker-derived angle with a modest $\sigma=0.006\ \mathrm{rad}$ (about $0.3^\circ$) sampled at $f_s=100\ \mathrm{Hz}$ ($\Delta t=0.01\ \mathrm{s}$), tracking a $1\ \mathrm{Hz}$, $0.30\ \mathrm{rad}$ motion. The true velocity peaks at $A\,2\pi f_0=1.9\ \mathrm{rad/s}$ and the true acceleration at $A(2\pi f_0)^2=11.8\ \mathrm{rad/s^2}$. But Proposition 3.1 gives a raw-velocity noise of $\sigma/(\sqrt2\,\Delta t)=0.42\ \mathrm{rad/s}$ - a fifth of the signal - and a raw-acceleration noise of $\sigma\sqrt6/\Delta t^2=147\ \mathrm{rad/s^2}$, which is twelve times the true peak acceleration. The raw second derivative is not a noisy version of the answer; it is noise. Figure 3 shows the first derivative failing already.

The remedy follows from the same frequency picture. The signal lives at low frequencies (a $1\ \mathrm{Hz}$ motion, joint kinematics rarely above $\sim$6-10 Hz even in running); the noise is spread across all frequencies up to the Nyquist limit $f_s/2$. A low-pass filter that passes frequencies below a cutoff $f_c$ and rejects those above it therefore removes most of the noise while keeping the signal - provided $f_c$ is set above the motion's real bandwidth and below the noise-dominated high frequencies. In biomechanics the standard choice is a Butterworth filter (a maximally flat low-pass response), run forward and backward over the recorded data so its phase lag cancels (a "zero-lag" or dual-pass filter) - essential here, because a torque estimate delayed by even a few samples is misaligned with the force plate. Filter the angle first, then differentiate: the blue curve in Fig. 3 is exactly this, and it recovers the true velocity from the same data that gave the useless gold curve. How low to set $f_c$ is the subject of the next section.

4. Differentiation done right: the optimal cutoff

Filtering is not free. A cutoff low enough to kill the noise also attenuates the fastest genuine parts of the motion - the sharp reversal at the bottom of a squat, the heel-strike transient - so the smoothed signal is biased: systematically flattened. Set the cutoff too low and this bias dominates; set it too high and the noise of Section 3 dominates. The error as a function of cutoff is therefore a competition between two opposing terms, and the honest goal is not "no noise" but minimum total error.

Proposition 4.1 (bias-variance decomposition and an interior optimum). Let $\hat{\dot q}(f_c)$ be the velocity estimate obtained by low-pass filtering at cutoff $f_c$ and differentiating. Its mean-square error decomposes as $$\boxed{\;\mathrm{MSE}(f_c)=\underbrace{B(f_c)^2}_{\text{bias: signal removed}}+\underbrace{V(f_c)}_{\text{variance: noise passed}},\;}$$ where the bias $B(f_c)$ (the true motion attenuated above $f_c$) decreases with $f_c$, and the passed-noise variance $V(f_c)$ increases with $f_c$. If both are smooth with $B^2$ convex-decreasing and $V$ increasing from zero, the total has a unique interior minimum at the cutoff $f_c^\star$ where the marginal signal recovered equals the marginal noise admitted, $\dfrac{d(B^2)}{df_c}=-\dfrac{dV}{df_c}$.
Write the estimate as truth plus two independent departures: $\hat{\dot q}=\dot q + b(f_c) + \nu(f_c)$, where $b=\mathbb E[\hat{\dot q}]-\dot q$ is the deterministic distortion the filter imposes on the noise-free signal (it grows as more of the true spectrum is cut, i.e. as $f_c$ falls), and $\nu$ is the zero-mean random part from the passed noise. Since the noise is independent of the signal, $\mathbb E[b\,\nu]=0$, so $\mathrm{MSE}=\mathbb E[(b+\nu)^2]=b^2+\mathbb E[\nu^2]=B^2+V$ with $B^2=\lVert b\rVert^2$ and $V=\operatorname{Var}(\nu)$. As $f_c\to\infty$ no signal is removed ($B^2\to0$) but all noise passes ($V\to$ the raw value of Proposition 3.1); as $f_c\to0$ the reverse. With $B^2$ decreasing and convex and $V$ increasing from $0$, the derivative $\tfrac{d}{df_c}(B^2+V)$ moves from negative (at small $f_c$, $B^2$ falling fast) to positive (at large $f_c$, $V$ rising), crossing zero once at the stationary point $f_c^\star$ where $(B^2)'=-V'$, a minimum. ∎

Figure 4 is this proposition computed for the Section 3 signal: the bias falls and the variance rises with $f_c$, and their sum bottoms out near $f_c^\star\approx7\ \mathrm{Hz}$ - comfortably above the $1\ \mathrm{Hz}$ motion and its low harmonics, comfortably below the noise floor. The practical question is how to find $f_c^\star$ without knowing the true signal. The standard answer is residual analysis (Winter's method): filter at a range of cutoffs, and for each compute the root-mean-square residual between raw and filtered signal. At high $f_c$ the residual is pure noise and falls linearly toward zero as $f_c$ rises; where the residual curve departs upward from that noise line is the cutoff at which the filter has begun removing signal, and that knee is taken as the chosen cutoff. It locates a cutoff in the operating band of Fig. 4 from the data alone, without knowing the true signal.

With clean velocities and accelerations in hand, the pipeline can turn to the two quantities inverse dynamics actually consumes: the centre of mass (Section 5) and, through it, the joint torques (Section 6).

5. Estimating the centre of mass and validating with the force plate

Two quantities complete the inputs to inverse dynamics: the whole-body centre of mass (COM), whose motion the ground reaction must explain, and an independent check that the kinematics and the forces are mutually consistent. Both come from one idea - Newton's second law applied to the whole body - and it gives the module its most valuable diagnostic.

The whole-body COM is the mass-weighted average of the segment centres. Each segment's COM position $\mathbf r_{c,i}$ is fixed on the segment by Definition 2.2 (a fraction $\rho_i$ along it from the proximal marker), so from the markers and the anthropometric table the body COM is

Definition 5.1 (whole-body centre of mass). For segments of mass $m_i=f_iM$ with centres $\mathbf r_{c,i}$, $$\mathbf r_{\rm COM}=\frac{\sum_i m_i\,\mathbf r_{c,i}}{\sum_i m_i}=\sum_i f_i\,\mathbf r_{c,i},$$ the total mass $M$ cancelling because the mass fractions sum to one ($\sum_i f_i=1$). Differentiating twice gives the COM acceleration $\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm COM}$ from the kinematics alone.

Now the check. The force plate measures the ground-reaction force $\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}$ directly and precisely. Newton's second law for the whole body ties it to the COM acceleration - and because the two are measured by completely different instruments (cameras versus a force plate), their agreement is a genuine, assumption-free validation.

Proposition 5.1 (force-plate validation and the dynamic residual). The only external forces on the body are gravity and the ground reaction, so the whole-body COM obeys $$\boxed{\;M\,\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm COM}=\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}-M\mathbf g\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm COM}=\frac{\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}}{M}-\mathbf g.\;}$$ The left side is computed from the kinematics (Definition 5.1, twice differentiated); the right side from the force plate. Their difference, the dynamic residual $\mathbf d=\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm COM}^{\rm kin}-(\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}/M-\mathbf g)$, is zero for a perfect measurement and otherwise quantifies the total error of the kinematic pipeline against the near-noiseless force.
Treat the body as the union of its segments and sum Newton's second law $m_i\ddot{\mathbf r}_{c,i}=\sum\mathbf F_i^{\rm ext}$ over all segments. The forces acting on any segment are gravity $m_i\mathbf g$, the ground reaction (nonzero only on the foot in contact), and the internal joint reaction forces from neighbouring segments. By Newton's third law each internal joint force appears twice, equal and opposite, so all internal forces cancel in the sum. What remains is $\sum_i m_i\ddot{\mathbf r}_{c,i}=\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}+\sum_i m_i\mathbf g=\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}-M\mathbf g$ (taking $\mathbf g$ downward). The left side is $M\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm COM}$ by Definition 5.1 differentiated twice. Dividing by $M$ gives the boxed identity; the residual is the measured mismatch between its two independently obtained sides. ∎

Figure 5 shows the check on a squat: the vertical COM acceleration from the cameras and from the force plate track each other to within a residual of about $0.13\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ on a signal peaking near $1.6\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ - the pipeline is consistent to a few per cent. When the residual is not small, it localises the problem: a residual that grows with acceleration points to body-segment-parameter error (wrong masses), while a jittery residual points to under-smoothed kinematics. The same identity underlies the classical reaction-board and double-integration methods of finding the COM, and its per-joint generalisation - internal forces cancel, external forces drive the motion - is exactly the bookkeeping the inverse-dynamics recursion of the next section performs one segment at a time.

6. Inverse dynamics: the Newton-Euler recursion

Inverse dynamics is the payoff: given the measured kinematics, the segment inertias, and the ground reaction, compute the net torque each joint must have produced. The method is to isolate one segment at a time as a free body and write Newton's and Euler's laws for it. Each segment has two unknowns we want - the force and the moment transmitted across its proximal joint - and everything else in its equations is known if we start where a force is known: the foot, pressing on the force plate. So the recursion marches distal to proximal, foot → shank → thigh, each segment handing the next the reaction it must react against.

Definition 6.1 (free body of one planar segment). Segment $i$ has mass $m_i$, centre-of-mass acceleration $\mathbf a_{c,i}$ (from Sections 3-4), moment of inertia $I_i$, and angular acceleration $\alpha_i$. At its distal joint act a known force $\mathbf F_d$ and moment $M_d$ (the ground reaction for the foot, or the reaction from the segment below for the rest); at its proximal joint act the unknown force $\mathbf F_p$ and net muscle moment $M_p$ (the joint moments carry a joint subscript; the bare $M$ used elsewhere is total body mass). Gravity $m_i\mathbf g$ acts at the centre of mass. Let $\mathbf r_d$ and $\mathbf r_p$ be the position vectors from the segment's centre of mass to its distal and proximal joints.
Proposition 6.1 (the inverse-dynamics recursion). Newton's and Euler's laws for segment $i$ solve for the proximal force and joint moment: $$\boxed{\;\mathbf F_p=m_i\,\mathbf a_{c,i}-m_i\mathbf g-\mathbf F_d,\qquad M_p=I_i\,\alpha_i-M_d-(\mathbf r_d\times\mathbf F_d)-(\mathbf r_p\times\mathbf F_p).\;}$$ Initialise at the foot with $\mathbf F_d=\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}$ and $M_d=\mathbf r_{\rm COP}\times\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}$ (the moment of the ground reaction about the foot's centre of mass, $\mathbf r_{\rm COP}$ the vector to the centre of pressure). By Newton's third law the proximal reaction of segment $i$ is the distal load of segment $i{+}1$: $\mathbf F_d^{(i+1)}=-\mathbf F_p^{(i)}$, $M_d^{(i+1)}=-M_p^{(i)}$. The net joint torque at each joint is the $M_p$ computed there.
Newton's second law for the segment sums the forces to $m_i\mathbf a_{c,i}$: $\mathbf F_p+\mathbf F_d+m_i\mathbf g=m_i\mathbf a_{c,i}$, which rearranges to $\mathbf F_p=m_i\mathbf a_{c,i}-m_i\mathbf g-\mathbf F_d$. Euler's law about the centre of mass sums the moments to $I_i\alpha_i$: the two joint moments $M_p$ and $M_d$, plus the moments of the two joint forces about the centre of mass, $\mathbf r_p\times\mathbf F_p$ and $\mathbf r_d\times\mathbf F_d$ (gravity acts at the centre of mass and contributes no moment). Thus $M_p+M_d+\mathbf r_p\times\mathbf F_p+\mathbf r_d\times\mathbf F_d=I_i\alpha_i$, giving $M_p=I_i\alpha_i-M_d-(\mathbf r_d\times\mathbf F_d)-(\mathbf r_p\times\mathbf F_p)$. Because $\mathbf F_p$ is already known from the Newton step, the moment equation has the single unknown $M_p$. The third-law coupling passes the solved proximal load down as the next segment's distal load, propagating the known ground reaction up the chain. ∎

A concrete instance closes the loop. Take the shank of the $M=70\ \mathrm{kg}$ subject from Section 2 ($m=3.3\ \mathrm{kg}$, $I=0.048\ \mathrm{kg\,m^2}$, length $0.40\ \mathrm{m}$) at an instant of a squat where the measured shank COM acceleration is $\mathbf a_c=(0.4,\,1.2)\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ and angular acceleration $\alpha=3.0\ \mathrm{rad/s^2}$, with a distal (ankle) reaction $\mathbf F_d=(15,\,690)\ \mathrm{N}$ and ankle moment $M_d=-8\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ handed up from the foot. The Newton step gives the knee reaction $\mathbf F_p=m\mathbf a_c-m\mathbf g-\mathbf F_d=(3.3\cdot0.4,\,3.3\cdot1.2+3.3\cdot9.81)-(15,690)=(-13.7,\,-653.7)\ \mathrm{N}$ - the thigh pushes down and slightly back on the shank, chiefly to support the load above the knee (with a smaller component decelerating the descent). The Euler step then returns the net knee moment $M_p=I\alpha-M_d-(\mathbf r_d\times\mathbf F_d)-(\mathbf r_p\times\mathbf F_p)$ once the segment's pose fixes the moment arms $\mathbf r_p,\mathbf r_d$ (the vectors from the centre of mass to the knee and ankle, of length $0.17$ and $0.23\ \mathrm{m}$) - the very knee torque Module 13 used to analyse the chair rise, now produced from measured motion rather than assumed. Assembling this recursion into code, run on synthetic data whose true torques we know, is the subject of Section 7.

7. The measurement-to-torque pipeline

The pieces now assemble into one procedure. Given marker trajectories and a synchronised force plate, the estimate of a joint torque runs left to right through Fig. 1:

  1. Markers → angles. Segment angles by Definition 2.1, joint angles as their differences.
  2. Filter. Low-pass each angle at the cutoff $f_c^\star$ from a residual analysis (Section 4), zero-lag.
  3. Differentiate. Twice, for $\dot{\mathbf q}$ and $\ddot{\mathbf q}$, on the filtered angles.
  4. Inertias. Segment masses, COM locations, and moments of inertia from the anthropometric table (Section 2).
  5. Kinematics of the centres. Each segment's COM position and acceleration from the joint kinematics.
  6. Recursion. Newton-Euler from the foot (with the measured ground reaction) up the chain (Section 6).
  7. Output. The net joint torque at each joint - with an uncertainty band from Section 8.

The only honest way to test such a pipeline is on data whose answer is known. We forward-simulate a movement: pick a joint trajectory $q(t)$, choose the true torque from the equation of motion (here a single gravity-loaded segment, $\tau_{\rm true}=I\ddot q+mgL\sin q$), add realistic marker noise, and then run the inverse pipeline to see whether it returns $\tau_{\rm true}$. Figure 7 is that test, and it makes the module's central warning concrete.

With filtering, the recovered torque tracks the truth to a root-mean-square error of $0.08\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ on a $3.3\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ signal - a faithful estimate. Run on the same noisy data without the filter, the estimate has an error of $6.8\ \mathrm{N\,m}$, twice the signal itself: the torque is buried in differentiation noise. The pipeline works only because Sections 3 and 4 tamed the derivative. The core of the code is short enough to read in full.

import numpy as np
from scipy.signal import butter, filtfilt


def smooth(x, fc, fs):
    b, a = butter(2, fc/(fs/2))       # 2nd-order Butterworth
    return filtfilt(b, a, x)          # forward-backward: zero lag


def deriv(x, dt):                     # central difference
    d = np.gradient(x, dt)
    return d


def joint_torque(q, fs, Iz, m, g, L, fc):
    dt = 1.0/fs
    qf = smooth(q, fc, fs)            # 1-2. filter the angle
    qd = deriv(qf, dt)               # 3.  velocity
    qdd = deriv(qd, dt)              # 3.  acceleration
    return Iz*qdd + m*g*L*np.sin(qf)  # 4-7. inverse dynamics


# forward truth -> add noise -> invert -> compare
t = np.arange(0, 2, 0.01)
q_true = 0.40*np.sin(2*np.pi*0.8*t)
q_meas = q_true + np.random.normal(0, 0.006, t.size)
tau = joint_torque(q_meas, 100, 0.05, 4.0, 9.81, 0.25, 6.0)

This is the whole idea in twenty lines: the general planar case (Section 6) replaces the last line with the Newton-Euler recursion over several segments, and adds the force plate as the foot's distal load, but the shape - filter, differentiate, apply dynamics - is identical. What it does not yet report is how far to trust the number, which is the next section.

8. Error and uncertainty propagation

A torque estimate without an error bar is not a measurement. Two kinds of error enter the pipeline, and they behave oppositely under filtering. Random error - marker noise - is broadband and is largely removed by the smoothing of Section 4; what survives is a residual variance. Systematic error - a body-segment parameter that is 15% too large because the subject is not the cadaver-average of the table - is a fixed scaling that no amount of filtering touches, because it is not noise. Quantifying both, and finding which dominates, is the job of error propagation.

Proposition 8.1 (variance of the torque estimate). Write the single-joint torque as $\tau=I\ddot q+mgL\sin q$. For small, independent errors in the inputs, the estimate's variance is the sum of squared sensitivities times input variances, $$\boxed{\;\operatorname{Var}(\tau)\approx\Big(\tfrac{\partial\tau}{\partial I}\Big)^2\!\operatorname{Var}(I)+\Big(\tfrac{\partial\tau}{\partial m}\Big)^2\!\operatorname{Var}(m)+\Big(\tfrac{\partial\tau}{\partial\ddot q}\Big)^2\!\operatorname{Var}(\ddot q)+\cdots\;}$$ with $\partial\tau/\partial I=\ddot q$, $\partial\tau/\partial m=gL\sin q$, $\partial\tau/\partial\ddot q=I$. Independent sources add in quadrature; a systematic (correlated) error instead adds its full bias to every time sample.
Linearise $\tau=\tau(I,m,\ddot q,\dots)$ about the nominal inputs: a small perturbation gives $\delta\tau=\tfrac{\partial\tau}{\partial I}\delta I+\tfrac{\partial\tau}{\partial m}\delta m+\tfrac{\partial\tau}{\partial\ddot q}\delta\ddot q+\cdots$. Taking the variance and using that the input errors are mutually independent (so all cross-covariances vanish), $\operatorname{Var}(\tau)=\sum_j(\partial\tau/\partial x_j)^2\operatorname{Var}(x_j)$, the quadrature sum. The partials follow by differentiating $\tau=I\ddot q+mgL\sin q$ term by term. If an error is instead systematic - the same $\delta I$ on every frame - it is not random across samples: it shifts the whole torque curve by $\ddot q\,\delta I$, a bias that quadrature does not describe and filtering cannot remove. ∎

The quadrature formula is exact only for small, independent, Gaussian errors; when the errors are large or the model nonlinear, the honest tool is Monte Carlo - draw many samples of the noisy markers and the uncertain parameters, run the whole pipeline on each, and read the spread of the output. Figure 8 does this for the synthetic joint.

The uncertainty band (Fig. 8, left) is the estimate's honest width; the error budget (right) says where it comes from. Here the $15\%$ body-segment-parameter uncertainty contributes far more RMS error ($0.41\ \mathrm{N\,m}$) than the marker noise ($0.09$) or even a poorly chosen cutoff ($0.31$), because it is a systematic scaling the filter cannot remove. This ranking is the practical payoff of the whole analysis: it says that to sharpen this torque estimate you should measure the subject's inertias better before you buy a faster camera - and it motivates estimating those parameters from the data itself, the subject of Section 9.

9. Parameter identification and model validation

Section 8 found the dominant error to be the body-segment parameters, taken from a population table rather than measured on the subject. But the data contain information about them: the same force plate that validated the COM (Section 5) constrains the masses, because a wrong mass makes the kinematic and force-plate accelerations disagree. Parameter identification turns that disagreement into an estimate - choose the parameters that make the model most consistent with the measurements.

Proposition 9.1 (least-squares parameter identification). Let a model output depend linearly on an unknown parameter vector $\boldsymbol\beta$ through $\mathbf y_{\rm model}=\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta$, and let $\mathbf y_{\rm meas}$ be the corresponding measurements. The parameters minimising the squared residual $\lVert\mathbf y_{\rm meas}-\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta\rVert^2$ satisfy the normal equations $$\boxed{\;\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf A\,\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}=\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf y_{\rm meas}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}=(\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf A)^{-1}\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf y_{\rm meas},\;}$$ the value at which the residual is orthogonal to every column of $\mathbf A$.
The squared residual $R(\boldsymbol\beta)=(\mathbf y_{\rm meas}-\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta)^{\!\top}(\mathbf y_{\rm meas}-\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta)$ is a convex quadratic in $\boldsymbol\beta$. Its gradient is $\nabla R=-2\mathbf A^{\!\top}(\mathbf y_{\rm meas}-\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta)$; setting it to zero gives $\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf A\,\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}=\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf y_{\rm meas}$. Since $\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf A$ is positive definite when $\mathbf A$ has independent columns, the stationary point is the unique minimum, and $\nabla R=0$ says exactly that the residual $\mathbf y_{\rm meas}-\mathbf A\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}$ is orthogonal to the column space of $\mathbf A$ - no direction in the model can reduce it further. ∎

Applied here, the residual is the dynamic inconsistency of Proposition 5.1: sweep a mass-scaling parameter and find the value that minimises the mean-square gap between the kinematic and force-plate COM accelerations (Fig. 9). More generally, the measured ground reaction and the measured kinematics are almost never perfectly consistent - the model is planar, the parameters approximate, the markers move on the skin - so their residual is nonzero. Residual-reduction methods (RRA) make the smallest adjustment to the masses, COM locations, or kinematics that drives the residual to zero, producing a dynamically consistent trajectory before the joint torques are read off.

Identification is not validation. A model can be tuned to fit its own residual and still be wrong, so the estimate must be checked against something it was not fitted to. Three independent checks are standard, in increasing strength: known static poses (hold a limb still and horizontal - the pipeline should return exactly the gravity torque $mgL$, with no dynamics); EMG timing (a muscle's activation should precede and share the sign of the torque it produces - Section 1 - so a computed knee-extensor torque during a period of silent quadriceps is a red flag); and the force-plate cross-check of Proposition 5.1, which uses an instrument the torque calculation did not. When a torque survives identification and these validations, it has earned the trust the rest of this course placed in it. The number the fourteen previous modules assumed is, at last, a measurement - with a value, an error bar, and a chain of evidence behind it.

11. Problems, diagnostics, and limitations

Jump to: Conceptual · Derivational · Computational · Diagnostics · Limitations · Repayment

Thirty problems - ten conceptual, ten derivational, ten computational - plus five diagnostics. Each carries a Probes note and a collapsible worked solution; computational solutions quote numbers the code produces.

Conceptual ↑ contents

C1 - the number you never measured. The course spent fourteen modules computing joint torques. Why can no instrument measure one directly, and what must replace the measurement? Probes: state vs observation; Section 0.
Solution
Torque is an internal generalised force; instruments report externally observable quantities - marker positions, contact forces, muscle voltages - none of which is a torque. What replaces the measurement is inference: a model (the observation map plus the equations of motion) combined with the observable data lets you solve for the torque as an inverse problem. The torque is computed, never read, so it inherits the error of both the data and the model.
C2 - the odd instrument out. Of the five instruments, why is the force plate both the exception and the most valuable for inverse dynamics? Probes: force vs position measurement; Section 1.
Solution
Every other instrument measures a position (or, for the IMU, an acceleration) that must be differentiated or integrated into the dynamics; the force plate measures a force directly and with very low noise. Since inverse dynamics is anchored where a force is known, the clean ground reaction is the boundary condition that starts the recursion - the one place the chain touches a measured kinetic quantity rather than a processed kinematic one.
C3 - invisible noise, visible ruin. Position noise too small to see in the angle wrecks the acceleration. Why? Probes: differentiation as a high-pass operation; Section 3.
Solution
Differentiation multiplies each frequency component by its frequency, so it amplifies the high-frequency content where broadband noise lives while barely touching the low-frequency signal. The angle is signal-dominated (low frequency) and looks clean; its second derivative is noise-dominated (high frequency) because the same noise has been multiplied by frequency twice. The eye judges the angle; the dynamics need its derivatives.
C4 - faster is worse. Buying a faster camera (smaller $\Delta t$) makes the raw finite-difference acceleration noisier. Explain the paradox. Probes: the $1/\Delta t^2$ scaling; Section 3.
Solution
By Proposition 3.1 the second-difference acceleration noise variance is $6\sigma^2/\Delta t^4$, so its standard deviation scales as $1/\Delta t^2$: halving $\Delta t$ quadruples the noise variance (doubles the amplitude). A faster camera samples the same spatial noise over a shorter interval, and dividing a fixed position error by a smaller time step inflates the derivative. Speed helps the signal bandwidth but hurts the raw derivative; the resolution is not slower sampling but proper smoothing.
C5 - not as smooth as possible. Why is there a best cutoff frequency, rather than "smooth until it looks clean"? Probes: bias vs variance; Section 4.
Solution
Smoothing trades two errors. Too little and noise floods the derivative (high variance); too much and the filter attenuates the real motion (high bias). The total mean-square error is their sum (Proposition 4.1), a U with an interior minimum. "As smooth as possible" walks off the low-cutoff end into large bias - a torque whose peaks are flattened - which looks clean but is systematically wrong. The goal is the minimum, found by residual analysis.
C6 - why start at the foot. The Newton-Euler recursion runs distal to proximal, beginning at the foot. Why there, and why that direction? Probes: the known boundary condition; Section 6.
Solution
Each segment's equations have two unknowns - the proximal joint force and moment - and are solvable only if the distal load is already known. The one segment with a measured distal load is the foot, whose distal "load" is the force-plate ground reaction. Solving it yields the ankle load, which by Newton's third law is the shank's distal load, and so on up the chain. The direction is forced by where the known force is.
C7 - two instruments, one truth. Why does agreement between the kinematic and force-plate COM accelerations validate the pipeline, when both could be wrong? Probes: independence of the instruments; Section 5.
Solution
The two estimates come from physically independent instruments (cameras vs a force plate) and independent error sources (marker noise and body-segment parameters vs plate calibration). Newton's second law says they must equal the same acceleration, so their agreement is unlikely to be a coincidence of two independent errors cancelling. A large residual, conversely, flags that at least one side has an error - a genuine, assumption-light check.
C8 - the error filtering cannot touch. Why does a $15\%$ error in a segment mass survive any amount of smoothing? Probes: systematic vs random error; Section 8.
Solution
Filtering removes error that is random across time (noise, spread over frequency); a wrong mass is a fixed multiplier applied identically to every frame, so it is not noise and has no high-frequency signature to remove. It biases the whole torque curve by a constant factor. The only cures are measuring or identifying the parameter (Section 9), not smoothing - which is why the error budget ranks body-segment parameters above marker noise.
C9 - the instrument that stays outside. EMG measures the muscles that make the torque, yet never enters the torque calculation. Why - and what is it for? Probes: activation vs force; Section 1, Section 9.
Solution
Surface EMG tracks muscle activation, but the map from activation to force depends on the muscle's length, velocity, and fatigue (Module 5), so EMG is not a calibrated force sensor and cannot be inserted into the dynamics. Its value is as an independent check on timing and sign: a computed extensor torque during a period of silent extensors is a contradiction, so EMG validates the inverse-dynamics result and helps resolve muscle redundancy without ever supplying a number to it.
C10 - the pose that checks everything. Holding a limb still and horizontal is the simplest validation of the whole pipeline. Why is it so powerful? Probes: the static limit; Section 9.
Solution
At rest all accelerations are zero, so the inverse-dynamics equation collapses to the pure gravity torque $mgL$ about the joint - a value fixed by known anthropometry and geometry alone, with no differentiation involved. If the pipeline returns anything else in a static hold, the error is in the parameters or the geometry, not the smoothing, isolating one class of error cleanly. It is the estimation analogue of checking a scale reads zero when empty.

Derivational ↑ contents

D1 - segment angle from markers. From proximal and distal marker positions, derive the segment angle and the knee joint angle, and state when the formula is ill-conditioned. Probes: the observation map; Section 2.
Solution
The vector from proximal to distal marker is $(\Delta x,\Delta y)=(x_d-x_p,\,y_d-y_p)$; its direction is $\theta=\operatorname{atan2}(\Delta y,\Delta x)$, using atan2 to cover all four quadrants. The knee angle is the difference of adjacent segment angles, $q_{\rm knee}=\theta_{\rm thigh}-\theta_{\rm shank}$. It is ill-conditioned when the markers are close together (small separation $d=\sqrt{\Delta x^2+\Delta y^2}$): marker noise then produces a large angular error, $\delta\theta\approx\delta(\text{position})/d$ - the reason markers are placed far apart on a segment.
D2 - velocity noise. Derive the noise variance of the central-difference velocity. Probes: variance of a linear combination; Section 3.
Solution
Write $\dot q_k=(q_{k+1}-q_{k-1})/(2\Delta t)$; its noise is $(\varepsilon_{k+1}-\varepsilon_{k-1})/(2\Delta t)$. With independent $\varepsilon$ of variance $\sigma^2$, the variance of a weighted sum is $\sigma^2\sum a_j^2=\sigma^2[(1/2\Delta t)^2+(1/2\Delta t)^2]=\sigma^2/(2\Delta t^2)$. The cross term vanishes because $\operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon_{k+1},\varepsilon_{k-1})=0$. Hence $\operatorname{Var}(\dot q_k)=\sigma^2/(2\Delta t^2)$ (Proposition 3.1).
D3 - acceleration noise. Derive the noise variance of the second-difference acceleration and compare its growth to the velocity's. Probes: repeated differencing; Section 3.
Solution
$\ddot q_k=(q_{k+1}-2q_k+q_{k-1})/\Delta t^2$ has weights $(1,-2,1)/\Delta t^2$, so $\sum a_j^2=(1+4+1)/\Delta t^4=6/\Delta t^4$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\ddot q_k)=6\sigma^2/\Delta t^4$. Its standard deviation scales as $1/\Delta t^2$, versus $1/\Delta t$ for the velocity: each extra derivative multiplies the noise growth by another factor of $1/\Delta t$, which is why acceleration is the most fragile quantity in the pipeline.
D4 - bias-variance. Show that the mean-square error of a smoothed-then-differentiated estimate splits into a bias-squared and a variance term. Probes: independence of signal distortion and noise; Section 4.
Solution
Write the estimate as $\hat{\dot q}=\dot q+b+\nu$, with $b=\mathbb E[\hat{\dot q}]-\dot q$ the deterministic distortion and $\nu$ the zero-mean random part. Then $\mathrm{MSE}=\mathbb E[(b+\nu)^2]=b^2+2b\,\mathbb E[\nu]+\mathbb E[\nu^2]$; since $\mathbb E[\nu]=0$ and $b$ is deterministic, the cross term drops and $\mathrm{MSE}=b^2+\operatorname{Var}(\nu)=B^2+V$. Bias grows as the cutoff falls (more signal removed), variance grows as it rises (more noise passed); their sum has an interior minimum (Proposition 4.1).
D5 - the centre of mass. Derive the whole-body COM from the segment centres and show the total mass cancels. Probes: mass-weighted mean; Section 5.
Solution
By definition $\mathbf r_{\rm COM}=(\sum_i m_i\mathbf r_{c,i})/(\sum_i m_i)$. Writing $m_i=f_iM$ with mass fractions $f_i$, the total mass $M$ factors out of numerator and denominator: $\mathbf r_{\rm COM}=(M\sum_i f_i\mathbf r_{c,i})/(M\sum_i f_i)=\sum_i f_i\mathbf r_{c,i}$, using $\sum_i f_i=1$. The COM position thus depends only on the mass fractions, not the absolute mass - which is why identifying total mass (Lab D) needs the dynamics, not the geometry.
D6 - the force-plate identity. Derive $M\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm COM}=\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}-M\mathbf g$ by summing over segments. Probes: internal forces cancel; Section 5.
Solution
Sum Newton's law $m_i\ddot{\mathbf r}_{c,i}=\sum\mathbf F^{\rm ext}_i$ over all segments. External forces are gravity $m_i\mathbf g$, the ground reaction (only on the foot), and internal joint forces; each internal force appears twice (action-reaction) and cancels in the sum. The left side is $M\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm COM}$; the right is $\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}+\sum_i m_i\mathbf g=\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}-M\mathbf g$. This is Proposition 5.1, the basis of the force-plate cross-check.
D7 - the Newton step. Derive the proximal joint force on a segment from Newton's second law. Probes: free-body force balance; Section 6.
Solution
The forces on the segment are the proximal reaction $\mathbf F_p$, the known distal reaction $\mathbf F_d$, and gravity $m_i\mathbf g$. Newton's second law sums them to $m_i\mathbf a_{c,i}$: $\mathbf F_p+\mathbf F_d+m_i\mathbf g=m_i\mathbf a_{c,i}$, so $\mathbf F_p=m_i\mathbf a_{c,i}-m_i\mathbf g-\mathbf F_d$. With $\mathbf a_{c,i}$ from the kinematics, the inertia from anthropometry, and $\mathbf F_d$ from the segment below, the proximal force is fully determined (Proposition 6.1).
D8 - the Euler step. Derive the net proximal joint moment about the segment centre of mass. Probes: moment balance including force moments; Section 6.
Solution
Euler's law about the centre of mass sums all moments to $I_i\alpha_i$: the two joint moments $M_p$ and $M_d$, and the moments of the two joint forces, $\mathbf r_p\times\mathbf F_p$ and $\mathbf r_d\times\mathbf F_d$ (gravity acts at the COM, no moment). Thus $M_p+M_d+\mathbf r_p\times\mathbf F_p+\mathbf r_d\times\mathbf F_d=I_i\alpha_i$, giving $M_p=I_i\alpha_i-M_d-(\mathbf r_d\times\mathbf F_d)-(\mathbf r_p\times\mathbf F_p)$. Since $\mathbf F_p$ is already known from D7, this has the single unknown $M_p$, the net joint torque.
D9 - torque error bars. Derive the variance of the single-joint torque estimate and its sensitivity coefficients. Probes: linear error propagation; Section 8.
Solution
Linearise $\tau=I\ddot q+mgL\sin q$: $\delta\tau=\ddot q\,\delta I+gL\sin q\,\delta m+I\,\delta\ddot q+\cdots$. Taking the variance with independent errors kills the cross-covariances, leaving $\operatorname{Var}(\tau)=\ddot q^2\operatorname{Var}(I)+(gL\sin q)^2\operatorname{Var}(m)+I^2\operatorname{Var}(\ddot q)+\cdots$, the quadrature sum of Proposition 8.1. The partials $\partial\tau/\partial I=\ddot q$, $\partial\tau/\partial m=gL\sin q$, $\partial\tau/\partial\ddot q=I$ show the inertial term dominates during fast movement (large $\ddot q$) and the gravitational term during slow, extended postures.
D10 - least squares. Derive the normal equations for the parameter that minimises the model residual. Probes: setting the gradient to zero; Section 9.
Solution
Minimise $R(\boldsymbol\beta)=\lVert\mathbf y-\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta\rVert^2=(\mathbf y-\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta)^{\!\top}(\mathbf y-\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta)$. The gradient is $\nabla R=-2\mathbf A^{\!\top}(\mathbf y-\mathbf A\boldsymbol\beta)$; setting it to zero gives $\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf A\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}=\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf y$, so $\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}=(\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf A)^{-1}\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf y$. Positive-definiteness of $\mathbf A^{\!\top}\mathbf A$ (independent columns) makes it the unique minimum, and $\nabla R=0$ states the residual is orthogonal to the model's column space (Proposition 9.1).

Computational ↑ contents

Each requires a sweep, an inverse solve, a simulation, or a regime comparison - not substitution into a boxed formula. Numbers quoted are those the code produces.

K1 - raw versus filtered noise across sampling rates (sensitivity + regime). Sweep the sampling rate from 50 to 400 Hz and compute the acceleration noise two ways - raw finite difference, and after low-pass filtering at a fixed cutoff - and explain the opposite conclusions they reach about buying a faster camera. Probes: the raw scaling law versus the filter decoupling it; Section 3, Section 4.
Solution
The raw acceleration noise $\sigma\sqrt6/\Delta t^2$ climbs from $37\ \mathrm{rad/s^2}$ at $50\ \mathrm{Hz}$ to $2360$ at $400\ \mathrm{Hz}$ - a $64\times$ rise, the $1/\Delta t^2$ law of Proposition 3.1, and the naive conclusion that a faster camera is worse. But run the same data through a fixed $7\ \mathrm{Hz}$ low-pass before differentiating and the acceleration error stays essentially flat, $2$-$5\ \mathrm{rad/s^2}$ across the whole range - $10$ to $500\times$ smaller than the raw value and nearly independent of sampling rate. The non-obvious result the sweep surfaces: once you filter, the camera speed barely affects the acceleration noise (the filter, not $f_s$, sets it), so faster sampling is now free - it sharpens the signal bandwidth at no noise cost. The raw law says "slower is better"; the filtered pipeline reverses it.
K2 - finding the cutoff two ways (optimisation + inverse). On one synthetic trial, find the cutoff by residual analysis (no truth used) and by minimising the true velocity error, and compare. Probes: data-driven vs truth-driven optima; Section 4.
Solution
Residual analysis (Lab A) locates the knee at $\approx4\ \mathrm{Hz}$ without using the truth; minimising the RMS velocity error against the known signal (Fig. 4) bottoms near $7\ \mathrm{Hz}$. They do not coincide exactly - the data-free knee is a few Hz lower - but both lie well above the $1\ \mathrm{Hz}$ motion and below the noise floor, the same operating band. The residual knee is slightly conservative for the velocity, yet lands squarely on the torque optimum (Lab B, $\approx4\ \mathrm{Hz}$) - apt, since the torque is the quantity we ultimately want. Residual analysis is thus a usable truth-free surrogate, biased toward the extra smoothing the higher derivatives need.
K3 - three cutoffs for the torque (regime). Recover the joint torque at an over-smoothed, an optimal, and an under-smoothed cutoff, and compare the errors. Probes: the U-shaped torque error; Section 7, Lab B.
Solution
Sweeping the cutoff and comparing recovered torque to truth, the RMS torque error is $U$-shaped and bottoms near $4\ \mathrm{Hz}$: at $1.5\ \mathrm{Hz}$ (over-smoothed) the peaks are flattened and the error is large; at $4\ \mathrm{Hz}$ it is minimal ($\sim0.1\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ on a $3.3\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ signal); at $20\ \mathrm{Hz}$ (under-smoothed) the differentiation noise returns and the error climbs again. The three regimes make concrete that neither extreme is acceptable and the optimum for the second-derivative-based torque lies below the velocity optimum of K2.
K4 - recover the torque, with and without filtering (simulation). From noisy synthetic markers, run the full pipeline both ways and quantify the error against the known torque. Probes: the payoff of smoothing; Section 7.
Solution
Forward-simulate $\tau_{\rm true}=I\ddot q+mgL\sin q$, add $\sigma=0.006\ \mathrm{rad}$ marker noise, and invert. The filtered pipeline recovers the torque to an RMS error of $0.08\ \mathrm{N\,m}$; the identical data run without filtering gives $6.8\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ - twice the $3.3\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ signal itself. The unfiltered estimate is not a degraded answer but noise: the simulation shows the smoothing step is not a refinement but a precondition for any usable torque.
K5 - reading the dynamic residual (simulation + regime). Introduce a mass error and a smoothing error separately, and show the dynamic residual distinguishes them. Probes: diagnosing the pipeline; Section 5.
Solution
Compute the force-plate residual $\mathbf d=\ddot{\mathbf r}_{\rm COM}^{\rm kin}-(\mathbf F_{\rm GRF}/M-\mathbf g)$ under two faults. A $15\%$ mass error makes the residual scale with the acceleration (largest at peak movement, a coherent shape), while under-smoothing makes it a high-frequency jitter independent of the motion. The two signatures are distinguishable by eye and by their frequency content, so the residual is not just a pass/fail number but a diagnostic that localises the fault - the practical use of Proposition 5.1.
K6 - the two-segment recursion (simulation). Run the Newton-Euler recursion over foot and shank from a measured ground reaction up to the knee moment, and compare to the known truth. Probes: the recursion in code; Section 6.
Solution
Initialise with the ground reaction as the foot's distal load and march up: solve the foot for the ankle load, pass its negative to the shank, and solve the shank for the knee force and moment (Proposition 6.1). On synthetic data whose true torques are known, the recovered knee moment matches the truth to within the differentiation error of K4. The exercise shows the recursion is a short loop, and that each joint's estimate depends on every measurement below it - so an error at the foot propagates all the way up.
K7 - the error budget (sensitivity). Monte Carlo the pipeline under marker noise and a $15\%$ body-segment-parameter uncertainty, and rank the sources of torque error. Probes: where the uncertainty comes from; Section 8.
Solution
Running the pipeline over many random draws, the RMS torque error from each source alone is $0.41\ \mathrm{N\,m}$ for the body-segment parameters, $0.31$ for a poorly chosen cutoff, and $0.09$ for marker noise. The parameter error dominates because it is systematic - a scaling the filter cannot remove. The budget answers a design question quantitatively: to sharpen this estimate, measure the subject's inertias, do not buy a faster camera - the opposite of the naive instinct.
K8 - identify the mass (inverse). Recover total body mass from the dynamic residual across growing spans of a trial, and study the convergence. Probes: least-squares identification; Section 9, Lab D.
Solution
Minimising $\mathrm{RMS}(\ddot y^{\rm kin}-(F_y/M-g))$ over $M$ (Proposition 9.1) returns the mass at which the two instruments agree. With few frames the estimate scatters by several kilograms; by the full trial it settles onto the true $70\ \mathrm{kg}$. Convergence is driven by acceleration content - at rest $F_y=Mg$ constrains only the product, so a static trial cannot identify $M$, while a dynamic one (a jump) identifies it in a fraction of the frames. The inverse problem is well-posed only when the motion excites it.
K9 - the noise-versus-parameter crossover (sensitivity). Vary the marker noise and the body-segment-parameter uncertainty independently and find where each dominates the torque error. Probes: two error regimes; Section 8.
Solution
Torque error from marker noise grows with $\sigma$ but is suppressed by filtering; error from body-segment parameters grows linearly with their uncertainty and is unfiltered. Sweeping both, the marker-noise contribution overtakes the parameter contribution only for implausibly large $\sigma$ (several degrees) or a very well-known subject ($\lt 5\%$ parameter uncertainty). For typical values ($\sigma\sim0.3^\circ$, parameters $\sim15\%$) the parameters dominate, locating the operating point firmly in the systematic-error regime and confirming K7's ranking is not incidental to the particular numbers.
K10 - spending a measurement budget (optimisation). Given a fixed budget divisible between reducing marker noise and measuring subject-specific inertias, find the allocation that minimises torque error. Probes: allocating effort to the dominant source; Section 8, Section 9.
Solution
Model the total torque error as the quadrature sum $\sqrt{V_{\rm noise}(\sigma)+B_{\rm bsp}^2(u)}$ and let the budget buy either smaller $\sigma$ or smaller parameter uncertainty $u$ at given marginal rates. Because the parameter term enters as an un-filterable bias and starts as the larger contributor ($0.41$ vs $0.09\ \mathrm{N\,m}$), the first units of budget bought on subject-specific inertias reduce total error far more than the same spent on markers; the optimum tips toward markers only once the parameter uncertainty has been driven below the noise floor. The allocation confirms quantitatively the qualitative lesson of K7 - fix the dominant, systematic source first.

Five diagnostics ↑ contents

  1. The high-pass nature of differentiation. Why does differentiating amplify noise more than the signal?
    Solution
    Because it scales each frequency by its value, boosting the high-frequency noise and leaving the low-frequency signal nearly untouched.
  2. Zero-lag filtering. Why filter forward and backward rather than once?
    Solution
    A single pass imposes a phase lag that misaligns the torque with the synchronised force plate; running the filter both ways cancels the lag exactly.
  3. The static check. What torque should the pipeline return for a limb held still and horizontal?
    Solution
    Exactly the gravity torque $mgL$, since all accelerations are zero; any other value flags a parameter or geometry error.
  4. Anchoring the recursion. Could inverse dynamics start at the head instead of the foot?
    Solution
    Only if a force were measured there; the recursion must begin where a distal load is known, which is the foot on the force plate (or a free distal end with zero load).
  5. Systematic vs random. Which error does averaging many trials reduce, and which does it not?
    Solution
    Averaging reduces random marker noise (it cancels across trials) but not a systematic body-segment-parameter error, which repeats identically every trial.

Limitations - what this pipeline idealises

The planar inverse-dynamics pipeline is a deliberate simplification. Each idealisation is a real effect it omits, repaid elsewhere in the course or left as a known caveat.

Planar motionall motion in one sagittal planereal movement is three-dimensional; the full method uses 3-D rotations and a $3\times3$ inertia tensor, but every idea here carries over.
